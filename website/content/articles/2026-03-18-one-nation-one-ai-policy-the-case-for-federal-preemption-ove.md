On March 11, the federal-state AI governance war entered a new phase. Two deadlines embedded in President Trump's [December 2025 AI Executive Order](https://www.paulhastings.com/insights/client-alerts/president-trump-signs-executive-order-challenging-state-ai-laws) came due simultaneously: the Commerce Department was required to publish a comprehensive review identifying state AI laws deemed "overly burdensome" or conflicting with federal policy, and the FTC was required to issue a policy statement on when state laws compelling alterations to AI outputs may be preempted under Section 5 of the FTC Act. Meanwhile, the [DOJ's AI Litigation Task Force](https://www.mondaq.com/unitedstates/new-technology/1755166/march-2026-federal-deadlines-that-will-reshape-the-ai-regulatory-landscape) — operational since January — stands ready to file constitutional challenges against state laws that exceed their jurisdictional reach.

The question these mechanisms are designed to answer is the right one. It is just being asked too late.

## The Patchwork Is Already Here

State legislators have not been waiting for Washington. As of this month, lawmakers in 45 states have already introduced [1,561 AI-related bills](https://www.multistate.ai/artificial-intelligence-ai-legislation) in the 2026 legislative session alone — a staggering acceleration from the 1,208 introduced across all 50 states in 2025 and 635 in 2024. The legislative proliferation spans every conceivable domain: hiring and employment (New York's [RAISE Act](https://www.kslaw.com/news-and-insights/new-state-ai-laws-are-effective-on-january-1-2026-but-a-new-executive-order-signals-disruption)), algorithmic discrimination (Colorado's [SB 205](https://www.wsgr.com/en/insights/2026-year-in-preview-ai-regulatory-developments-for-companies-to-watch-out-for.html)), transparency and disclosure, healthcare AI, insurance scoring, and law enforcement.

Each law reflects the genuine concerns of its state's legislators. But taken together, they constitute what the [U.S. Chamber of Commerce has called](https://www.uschamber.com/technology/the-hidden-cost-of-50-state-ai-laws-a-data-driven-breakdown) a "compliance fragmentation tax" — a structural cost imposed on every American AI developer that wants to operate at national scale. That tax is not assessed equally. Large incumbents — the Googles and Microsofts with armies of regulatory counsel — absorb it. Startups and mid-sized AI companies are buried by it.

The numbers are unambiguous. [ENGINE's analysis](https://www.engine.is/news/category/how-2025-state-legislative-sessions-grew-the-ai-patchwork-and-what-it-means-for-startups) of the AI regulatory landscape finds that mandatory independent third-party audits "would create a costly barrier to entry" with no established audit ecosystem to draw on. The privacy law analog is instructive: [a Cornell and Bocconi University study](https://www.kiteworks.com/regulatory-compliance/state-ai-regulation-patchwork-compliance/) found Fortune 500 companies spent an average of $15.8 million each on initial GDPR compliance. The 50-state AI patchwork is tracking toward a similar trajectory — one that only entrenched incumbents can absorb.

This is the perverse core of the patchwork problem: laws designed to protect consumers from concentrated AI power are structured to *entrench* that concentration. The startup that might have challenged today's dominant foundation model providers is instead navigating contradictory disclosure mandates across 14 jurisdictions before it ships a single product.

## Misreading the 99-1 Vote

Last summer, the Senate voted [99-1 to strip a 10-year moratorium](https://time.com/7299044/senators-reject-10-year-ban-on-state-level-ai-regulation-in-blow-to-big-tech/) on state AI regulation from the "One Big Beautiful Bill." Consumer groups and state legislators celebrated; headlines declared that Big Tech's preemption push had been defeated. This reading is almost entirely backward.

The moratorium failed not because the Senate endorsed the current patchwork, but because legislators — including Republican governors who [sent a bipartisan letter opposing the moratorium](https://www.ncsl.org/press-room/details/ncsl-applauds-senates-99-1-vote-to-remove-ai-moratorium) — recognized that a blunt decade-long freeze, with no replacement federal framework, was the wrong instrument. Senator Blackburn, who co-authored the amendment to kill the moratorium, was explicit: "Until Congress passes federally preemptive legislation like the Kids Online Safety Act and an online privacy framework, we can't block states from making laws that protect their citizens."

Read that carefully. The objection is not that federal preemption is wrong. The objection is that a moratorium without replacement legislation is wrong. The 99-1 vote is a starting gun for federal legislation — not a stop sign. Congress cleared the field for a federal framework. It has not yet built one.

## The FTC Pathway and the Litigation Task Force

The Trump administration's legal architecture, however imperfect, is moving in the right direction with the instruments available. The FTC's Section 5 preemption theory — that state laws compelling AI developers to alter "truthful outputs" to mitigate bias effectively mandate deceptive conduct — is legally creative. [As Ropes & Gray has noted](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/03/examining-the-landscape-and-limitations-of-the-federal-push-to-override-state-ai-regulation), its ultimate reach is uncertain. But the underlying logic is strategically sound: using existing federal consumer protection authority to clear conflicting state mandates is faster than waiting for comprehensive legislation.

The AI Litigation Task Force is the sharper instrument. Targeting state laws that impose extraterritorial obligations — California effectively regulating how a developer in Austin trains a model used in Ohio — is grounded in [Dormant Commerce Clause doctrine](https://www.jenner.com/en/news-insights/publications/client-alert-trump-administration-issues-executive-order-in-attempt-to-preempt-state-ai-laws) that has long barred states from projecting their regulatory preferences beyond their borders. These are not novel constitutional arguments. They are precisely the kind of targeted, judicially reviewable challenges that should accompany any serious federal preemption strategy.

Pursue both tracks simultaneously. Do not wait for Congress to act before filing the first constitutional challenge.

## What Brussels Got Right (And Wrong)

Critics of federal preemption often invoke the EU AI Act as a cautionary tale about what unified federal AI governance looks like. It is, in fact, the argument for preemption — not against it.

The EU AI Act is a compliance catastrophe in the making. Independent analyses estimate [initial compliance costs of $8–15 million for large enterprises](https://techpinions.com/why-the-eus-ai-act-is-about-to-become-every-enterprises-biggest-compliance-challenge/) deploying high-risk AI, with penalties reaching €35 million or 7% of global turnover. The European Commission is already [proposing to delay high-risk AI obligations](https://www.orrick.com/en/Insights/2025/11/The-EU-AI-Act-6-Steps-to-Take-Before-2-August-2026) until 2027 because the law outran its own implementation infrastructure. This is not a model to replicate.

But the lesson is not "therefore, no federal framework." The lesson is that **jurisdictional coherence** — one compliance regime, one set of definitions, one enforcement authority — creates clearer paths than fragmented multi-layer regimes, even when the unified rules are imperfect. Companies operating across the EU, however frustrated by the AI Act's substantive requirements, face a single compliance universe. American AI companies today face 50 competing ones, with definitions of "chatbot," "high-risk," and "automated decision" diverging across jurisdictions in ways that carry [no technical or policy rationale](https://compliancehub.wiki/is-2026-the-year-of-the-chatbot-bill-a-state-by-state-ai-legislation-roundup/).

Take the EU's jurisdictional model. Reject its substantive architecture.

## The Competitive Clock Is Running

Every month of patchwork-as-usual is a month of competitive disadvantage. China's AI development operates under a unified national framework designed to accelerate deployment, not fragment it. While American AI startups hire compliance counsel to parse Colorado's algorithmic impact assessment requirements against California's training data disclosure rules, Chinese developers are iterating. [U.S. AI leadership is a strategic imperative](https://www.justsecurity.org/128568/expert-roundup-emerging-tech-trends-2026/); jurisdictional incoherence is how you lose it.

The case for federal preemption is not "no rules for AI." It is "one coherent set of rules, enforced consistently, that lets American AI compete globally." A light federal floor — narrow, targeted, enforced through existing authorities — is more protective of consumers than 50 vague, inconsistent mandates that produce litigation theater rather than meaningful accountability.

The March 11 deadlines have passed. The Commerce review is in. The FTC statement is on record. The Litigation Task Force is operational. The 99-1 vote has cleared the runway. Congress now has every reason — and no more excuses — to act.
