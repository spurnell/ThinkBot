On May 22, 2026, Google filed its [111-page opening brief](https://www.courthousenews.com/google-urges-dc-circuit-to-overturn-search-monopoly-remedies/) with the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit, asking the court to overturn the remedies order issued in this case. That order — celebrated by the DOJ as delivering ["significant remedies"](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/department-justice-wins-significant-remedies-against-google) against a proven search monopolist — compels Google to share its proprietary search index and user-interaction data with rival search engines, prohibits exclusive distribution deals, and extends its reach into Google's nascent Gemini AI products. Oral arguments are not yet scheduled, but the briefing window is live and the stakes are generational.

This piece takes no position on the liability finding. Judge Mehta's conclusion that Google illegally maintained its search monopoly through exclusive default-placement agreements — billions of dollars annually paid to Apple, Mozilla, and Android OEM partners to foreclose rival distribution — is defensible antitrust law. The District of Columbia Circuit will evaluate that holding on the merits; our concern here is narrower and more urgent.

The remedies order is wrong. Not wrong about Google specifically — wrong as a matter of antitrust doctrine, innovation economics, and judicial scope. The exclusive-distribution ban is the surgically appropriate remedy for the conduct that was actually adjudicated. The data-sharing mandate goes further, addressing a theory of harm that was never tried, never proven, and — given the dramatic market disruption already underway from AI-native search entrants — may not describe a durable competitive problem at all. The DC Circuit should affirm the conduct remedies and vacate the data-sharing requirements, guided by its own quarter-century-old precedent in *United States v. Microsoft Corp.*

## I. What the Liability Finding Actually Adjudicated

The monopoly finding in *United States v. Google LLC* rested on a specific theory: that Google paid for exclusivity in default search placement on mobile and desktop browsers, thereby locking rivals out of the distribution channels through which most users encounter a search engine for the first time. The Apple deal — roughly [$20 billion annually](https://money.usnews.com/investing/news/articles/2026-05-22/google-appeals-us-court-ruling-on-search-monopoly) to remain the default on Safari — was the centerpiece. Mozilla, wireless carriers, and Android OEM agreements completed the picture.

This is a coherent theory of exclusionary conduct. Revenue-based exclusivity arrangements that foreclose rival distribution can harm competition by denying rivals the query volume they need to train and improve their ranking algorithms. Judge Mehta was not wrong to find that this conduct crossed the Sherman Act Section 2 line.

But notice what the theory does *not* say. It does not say Google's search index is too good. It does not say Google's proprietary ranking algorithms are an essential facility that rivals have a right to access. It does not say that information asymmetry in behavioral data is itself exclusionary conduct. Those theories might be interesting policy arguments for a different proceeding. They were not adjudicated in this one.

## II. The *Microsoft* Precedent: Remedies Must Track the Violation

The DC Circuit's own governing precedent on this precise question is [*United States v. Microsoft Corp.*, 253 F.3d 34 (D.C. Cir. 2001)](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/F3/253/34/576095/). The en banc court vacated Judge Jackson's structural breakup order not because Microsoft had done nothing wrong, but because the remedy was disproportionate to the adjudicated violation and had been imposed without a proper evidentiary hearing on remedies. The Circuit was unambiguous: remedies must be tethered to the proven violation and tailored to fit the wrong they are meant to cure. A court cannot adjudicate one form of exclusionary conduct and then order remedies addressing an entirely different market structure problem.

*Microsoft* is controlling here — and it is, critically, the DC Circuit's own precedent. The liability finding in *Google* established that exclusive distribution deals were the mechanism of unlawful monopoly maintenance. The logical, causally linked remedy is to ban those deals — which Judge Mehta did. That remedy survives *Microsoft* scrutiny because the causal nexus is clean: remove the exclusive arrangements, restore the distribution competition that rivals were denied, and allow the market to determine whether Google's search quality can retain users absent the contractual lock-in.

The data-sharing mandate fails the *Microsoft* causal-nexus test. The theory animating compelled data sharing is that Google's behavioral dataset — its accumulated trove of click-and-query signals — is a scale advantage that rivals cannot replicate, thereby entrenching Google's ranking superiority independent of any distribution deal. That may or may not be true as an economic matter. But it was not what was tried. Judge Mehta did not adjudicate whether Google's data advantage constitutes exclusionary conduct; he adjudicated whether Google's payment-for-exclusivity contracts crossed Section 2's line. The [Congressional Research Service's analysis](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11362) of the remedies order notes that the district court "endorsed behavioral remedies" — but the data-sharing component is not a behavioral constraint on the adjudicated conduct. It is a structural intervention in a market dynamic that was never litigated.

## III. Compelled Sharing Inverts the Innovation Incentive

Even setting aside the causal-nexus problem, the data-sharing mandate is bad antitrust policy on first principles. The Supreme Court addressed the tension between compelled access and innovation incentives directly in [*Verizon Communications Inc. v. Law Offices of Curtis V. Trinko, LLP*, 540 U.S. 398 (2004)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/540/398/). Justice Scalia's opinion for the Court was unambiguous: "Compelling such firms to share the source of their advantage is in some tension with the underlying purpose of antitrust law, since it may lessen the incentive for the monopolist, the rival, or both to invest in those economically beneficial facilities." The Court went further, warning against "the uncertain virtue of forced sharing" and noting that the opportunity to earn monopoly returns — even temporarily — "is what attracts 'business acumen' in the first place; it induces risk taking that produces innovation and economic growth."

*Trinko* was a statutory case about the Telecommunications Act, not a direct holding on Sherman Act remedies. But its logic applies with full force here. Google's search index is the product of more than two decades of crawling infrastructure, PageRank engineering, and continuous quality investment. Its behavioral dataset — the click-and-query signals the district court's technical committee will now package for "qualified competitors" — represents hundreds of billions of user interactions accumulated through constant refinement of what users actually want from search. A court order requiring that investment to be handed to rivals at government-determined terms tells every future technology firm that sufficient market success is a trigger for compelled sharing. That signal corrodes exactly the investment incentives that antitrust law is supposed to protect.

The [Knight-Georgetown Institute](https://kgi.georgetown.edu/research-and-commentary/without-a-payment-ban-what-can-we-expect-from-the-us-v-google-data-sharing-remedies/) has noted that the practical competitive benefit of the data-sharing mandate depends heavily on implementation details the district court left to a technical committee — a body whose membership, timeline, and data-access protocols remain undefined. This is not a minor administrative gap. The distance between "Google shares click data" and "rivals derive meaningful ranking improvements" involves significant engineering, institutional capacity, and time. If the competitive benefit is uncertain and long-dated, and the innovation disincentive effect is immediate and certain, the error-cost calculus runs against the mandate.

## IV. The Market Is Already Solving the Problem the Remedies Target

Perhaps the most powerful argument against the data-sharing mandate is the simplest one: the market is already doing what the remedy is supposed to accomplish, without any court intervention.

AI-native search entrants have built competitive answer engines from scratch — without access to Google's index, without Google's click-stream data, and without any of the assets the district court is now ordering Google to share. Perplexity AI [processed roughly 780 million search queries monthly as of mid-2025](https://www.demandsage.com/perplexity-ai-statistics/) — a figure that more than doubled over the prior year and has continued to climb — while growing its monthly active user base from 22 million to over 45 million. ChatGPT Search has scaled rapidly enough that OpenAI has reported [over one billion web searches in a single week](https://www.demandsage.com/chatgpt-statistics/) during peak usage periods. Microsoft Copilot, integrated into Bing's infrastructure, represents a well-capitalized incumbent challenger. Together, AI-native platforms are pulling high-value query types — research, learning, commercial investigation — away from traditional search in ways that would have seemed implausible when the Google liability case was filed.

None of these entrants needed Google's search index. They built their own retrieval systems, trained large language models on web-scale corpora, and created user experiences that are attracting users who once had no reason to leave Google. Google's own appeal brief [makes this point directly](https://www.courthousenews.com/google-urges-dc-circuit-to-overturn-search-monopoly-remedies/): rival AI platforms are "already succeeding as wildly as any technology in human history without any need to free-ride on Google's success."

If that characterization is even approximately correct — and the market trajectory suggests it is — the remedies order has misdiagnosed the competitive bottleneck. The data-sharing mandate was designed to help search rivals replicate Google's ranking quality by bootstrapping off its behavioral data. But the most dynamic competitors in the next generation of search are not replicating Google's architecture; they are building a different one. Ordering Google to share an index optimized for ten blue links does not obviously help companies building generative answer engines. The competitive relevance of the mandated data may be declining faster than the technical committee can operationalize the sharing regime.

This is a textbook application of the dynamic competition argument that courts should take seriously in fast-moving technology markets. A remedy designed around a static snapshot of competitive conditions in 2024 may be actively counterproductive by the time it is implemented in 2027 or 2028. The DC Circuit in *Microsoft* recognized exactly this risk — the danger that antitrust remedies in technology markets overshoot a competitive problem that market forces are already resolving.

## V. The Privacy Hazard the Court Did Not Model

The remedies order requires Google to share user-interaction data — specifically, [click-and-query data](https://www.searchenginejournal.com/googles-antitrust-ruling-what-the-remedies-mean-for-search-seo-and-ai-assistants/555086/) reflecting which links users click, how long they hover, and what search terms preceded each interaction — with a roster of qualified competitors to be defined by the technical committee. This is among the most sensitive behavioral data that a technology company collects. A user's search history, correlated with their click behavior, maps their health concerns, financial anxieties, political interests, and personal relationships with greater intimacy than almost any other data type.

The district court's order delegates the data protection question to the technical committee — a body that does not yet exist, whose security standards have not been specified, and whose members will have no obligation to match Google's own security infrastructure and incident-response capabilities. This is not a marginal oversight. The compelled sharing of a sensitive behavioral dataset across multiple organizations with varying security postures creates an aggregation risk that the court never analyzed. Each qualified competitor that receives the data becomes an additional attack surface; a breach at any one of them exposes the behavioral histories of millions of users who searched on Google with no knowledge their data would be transmitted to third parties.

The Federal Trade Commission's [September 2024 staff report on commercial surveillance](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2024/09/ftc-staff-report-finds-large-social-media-video-streaming-companies-have-engaged-vast-surveillance) documented the compounding dangers of exactly this kind of aggregated behavioral data moving across organizations — finding that even nominally non-sensitive data, when combined and transferred, can expose health concerns, political interests, and personal relationships in ways that harm consumers and national security alike. It is a profound irony that a remedies order designed to constrain one of the world's most security-capable data custodians would require distributing that same sensitive data to smaller organizations whose security practices have never been audited. The privacy community has been notably absent from the remedies debate; its voice belongs here.

## VI. The Gemini Extension: Importing Monopoly Logic Into a Competitive Market

The remedies order extends its reach to Google's Gemini AI products — prohibiting exclusive arrangements for Gemini app distribution and subjecting AI-related conduct to the same oversight framework as traditional search. This is the most legally vulnerable element of the order, and the one the DC Circuit should find easiest to vacate on scope grounds.

Gemini operates in a generative AI market that Google does not monopolize. OpenAI's ChatGPT, Anthropic's Claude, Meta's Llama-based products, Microsoft's Copilot, and dozens of smaller competitors offer directly substitutable AI assistant experiences. The generative AI market is structurally competitive in ways that the general search market — with its high query volume, network effects, and distribution dependencies — arguably was not in the period Judge Mehta examined. Extending antitrust remedies from a monopolized market (general search) into an adjacent, competitive market (generative AI) without an independent liability finding in that market is a significant jurisdictional overreach.

The district court's theory appears to be that Gemini could leverage Google's search monopoly to entrench AI dominance — a vertical extension-of-monopoly concern. But vertical extension claims require their own evidentiary foundation: evidence that Google is actually using search leverage to foreclose AI competition, and that such foreclosure is causing competitive harm in the AI market. That case was not made at trial. The [Winston & Strawn review](https://www.winston.com/en/blogs-and-podcasts/competition-corner/antitrust-remedies-in-united-states-v-google-ai-and-the-evolving-search-market) of the remedies decision confirms that the court's approach was to treat GenAI products "like Search wherever they function in a similar fashion" — a formulation that, however well-intentioned, transplants the monopoly logic of a market Google dominated into one where it plainly does not.

The DC Circuit should apply its *Microsoft* causal-nexus principle directly: if the liability finding was about search distribution deals, the remedy's scope extends no further than search distribution. Gemini is a different product in a different, unconcentrated market. It does not belong inside a remedial decree designed to address exclusionary contracts for traditional search defaults.

## The Right Scope of Remedy

None of this analysis requires the DC Circuit to disturb the liability finding. Judge Mehta's conclusion that exclusive default-placement agreements violated Section 2 was grounded in evidence; the appellate question on liability is whether that conclusion was legally and factually supportable, and that is for the court to assess on its own terms.

On remedies, the analysis is cleaner. The distribution bans — prohibiting Google from paying for exclusive search defaults on browsers, mobile operating systems, and carrier agreements — are the causally appropriate response to the adjudicated violation. They address the conduct that foreclosed distribution, they are easily monitored, and they do not require the court to second-guess Google's investment in its search infrastructure. They are, in short, antitrust remedies: targeted cures for specific anticompetitive conduct.

The data-sharing mandate, the syndication requirements, and the Gemini-extension provisions are something different. They are regulatory interventions in the structure and information architecture of the search market, justified not by what was adjudicated at trial but by a broader theory of what a more competitive search market ought to look like. That is industrial policy. It is legitimate as a legislative choice; it is not legitimate as a judicial remedy for a specific Sherman Act violation.

The DC Circuit built the causal-nexus doctrine in *Microsoft* precisely to prevent this category error. A generation later, the same court has the opportunity to apply its own precedent — and to hold that antitrust remedies in technology markets must be calibrated to the violation, not to the aspirations of the government's competition policy agenda.
