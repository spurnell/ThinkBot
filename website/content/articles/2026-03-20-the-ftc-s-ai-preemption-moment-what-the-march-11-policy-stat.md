The deadline came and went. On March 11, 2026, the Federal Trade Commission issued the AI policy statement it was directed to produce by President Trump's [December 11, 2025 executive order on AI](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/). The statement — a sweeping interpretive document from FTC Chairman Andrew Ferguson arguing that Section 5 of the FTC Act can preempt state AI laws requiring "alterations to truthful outputs of AI models" — is now the most consequential single document in U.S. AI governance. It is also, in critical ways, legally hollow.

Within days, [36 state attorneys general had moved to block the executive order's preemption strategy](https://www.usresistnews.org/2026/03/16/36-states-move-to-block-federal-preemption-of-ai-laws-setting-up-major-court-fight-technology-brief-164/), setting up a federal-state court battle that could drag on for years. Senator Edward Markey had already introduced the ["States' Right to Regulate AI Act"](https://www.axios.com/2025/12/19/trump-democrats-fight-back-ai-order) in December — a bill that would bar the administration from using federal funds to implement the EO's preemption push — with ten Democratic co-sponsors and bipartisan sympathy on the question of legislative authority. And the Supreme Court's 2024 decision in [*Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*](https://www.scotusblog.com/cases/case-files/loper-bright-enterprises-v-raimondo/), which eliminated judicial deference to agency statutory interpretations, has handed every state AG a potent litigation weapon against any preemption theory the FTC advances.

The honest assessment: the FTC's March 11 statement is not a deregulatory victory. It is the opening move in a multi-year legal war that will produce neither the clarity nor the stability that AI innovators actually need. The path out runs through Congress — not through a non-binding agency document of uncertain legal force.

## What the FTC Actually Said

The March 11 policy statement applies Section 5 of the FTC Act — the prohibition on "unfair or deceptive acts or practices" — to AI systems. Its core argument is that certain state laws mandating bias mitigation, or requiring AI models to alter their outputs, could compel "deceptive" behavior under Section 5, triggering a conflict preemption theory: federal law wins when state law makes compliance with both impossible, or when state law obstructs federal objectives.

The statement is technically an interpretive document. It is not a rulemaking. It does not void any state law on its own authority. As [legal experts and TechFreedom have noted](https://techfreedom.org/ftcs-ai-policy-statement-no-substitute-for-rulemaking/), the FTC issued the statement without notice-and-comment — an omission that weakens its standing and invites procedural challenges. The statement acknowledges, if obliquely, that actual preemption would require formal rulemaking or successful litigation to vindicate; it cannot simply declare state laws void.

That is a significant concession buried in the document's architecture. What the administration has produced is a statement of legal theory, not a binding legal outcome. Whether that theory prevails depends on federal courts that, post-*Loper Bright*, are no longer in the habit of deferring to agency statutory interpretations.

## The Preemption Paradox

Federal preemption of a 50-state AI patchwork sounds pro-innovation. For companies operating national AI deployments, startups trying to scale, and developers who cannot afford a different compliance attorney in each state, the appeal is obvious. Uniform rules beat fragmented ones.

But the mechanism matters enormously — for reasons that should unsettle anyone thinking about what regulatory authority will look like in 2029 or 2033.

An FTC policy statement is not a statute. It carries [only Skidmore deference](https://www.clearygottlieb.com/news-and-insights/publication-listing/after-chevron-what-the-supreme-courts-loper-bright-decision-changed-and-what-it-didnt) post-*Loper Bright*, meaning courts will give it persuasive weight if they find the reasoning credible and discard it if they do not. There is no guarantee they will find it credible. The FTC is arguing that Section 5's prohibition on deception implicitly preempts state transparency and bias-mitigation requirements. That is not an obvious reading of the statute — it is a contested one. Courts exercising independent judgment, as *Loper Bright* now requires, may well reject it.

More fundamentally: if an executive-directed FTC statement can preempt state AI law in a direction the current administration favors, the next administration can deploy the same mechanism in a direction it favors. The precedent is not "federal uniformity." The precedent is "whoever controls the FTC controls AI governance by interpretive fiat." That is a precedent the technology industry should not want, regardless of who currently runs the agency.

The [IAPP has questioned whether the FTC has preemption authority at all](https://iapp.org/news/a/a-view-from-dc-can-the-ftc-preempt-state-ai-laws-) under the current statutory framework. [Ropes & Gray's March 2026 analysis](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/03/examining-the-landscape-and-limitations-of-the-federal-push-to-override-state-ai-regulation) characterizes the federal preemption push as carrying significant landscape-level limitations. The administration has a theory. It does not yet have a court win.

## The Patchwork Problem Is Real

None of this should be read as a defense of the status quo. The compliance fragmentation tax on AI developers is real, documented, and growing.

[New state AI laws took effect in California, Texas, Illinois, and Indiana on January 1, 2026](https://www.kslaw.com/news-and-insights/new-state-ai-laws-are-effective-on-january-1-2026-but-a-new-executive-order-signals-disruption). Colorado's comprehensive AI Act — the first U.S. state statute systematically targeting "high-risk" AI — takes effect June 30, 2026, with penalties up to $20,000 per violation. Each of these laws differs in scope, definitions, covered entities, and enforcement mechanisms. California's [SB 53 (Frontier AI Transparency Act)](https://www.kslaw.com/news-and-insights/new-state-ai-laws-are-effective-on-january-1-2026-but-a-new-executive-order-signals-disruption) applies only to frontier models trained on more than 10²⁶ floating-point operations. Texas's [Responsible AI Governance Act](https://www.bakerbotts.com/thought-leadership/publications/2026/january/us-ai-law-update) imposes documentation and red-teaming requirements on a broader class of developers and deployers. Illinois amends the Human Rights Act to prohibit discriminatory AI in employment — a separate framework entirely.

A developer building an AI hiring tool faces three different anti-discrimination regimes, three different transparency disclosure requirements, and three different enforcement agencies — none of which share definitions of core concepts. That is before Colorado's law activates in June.

The economic literature on regulatory fragmentation is clear: compliance costs are not linear. They fall disproportionately on startups and mid-sized companies that cannot field the legal armies of Google or Microsoft. The firms best equipped to navigate a 50-state patchwork are precisely the large incumbents that consumer advocates claim they want to constrain. Compliance fragmentation functions, in practice, as an incumbent protection regime.

The Trump administration was right to identify this problem. It was wrong to reach for an interpretive policy statement as the solution.

## Why the Legal Battle Ahead Produces No Winners

The administration has built multiple mechanisms into the December EO beyond the FTC statement. The [DOJ AI Litigation Task Force](https://www.paulhastings.com/insights/client-alerts/president-trump-signs-executive-order-challenging-state-ai-laws), created January 9, 2026, is actively developing Commerce Clause and First Amendment challenges to state AI laws. The EO conditions [$42 billion in BEAD broadband funding](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2025/12/what-to-watch-as-white-house-moves-to-federalize-ai-regulation) on states not having "onerous" AI laws — a coercive federalism maneuver that will itself attract constitutional scrutiny. Thirty-six state attorneys general are already organized in opposition.

Consider the litigation timeline realistically. The DOJ Task Force files challenges. State AGs file defensive motions. Courts apply *Loper Bright* standard review to the FTC's Section 5 preemption theory and reach different conclusions in different circuits. A Supreme Court resolution may not arrive for two to three years. In the meantime, every company deploying AI at national scale operates under a cloud of legal uncertainty that is arguably worse than the original patchwork — because now there is genuine doubt about which laws even apply.

A legally contested, easily reversed agency statement does not improve that uncertainty. It worsens it — by triggering years of litigation while providing no enforceable federal floor. The only certainty it delivers is guaranteed work for appellate litigators.

## What Congress Proved Last July

Opponents of federal action often argue Congress cannot move on AI. The evidence does not support this.

On July 1, 2025, the Senate voted [99-1 to strip a proposed 10-year moratorium on state AI laws](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2025/7/senate-strikes-ai-moratorium-from-budget-reconciliation-bill-in-overwhelming-99-1-vote/8415a728-fd1d-4269-98ac-101d1d0c71e0) from the "One Big Beautiful Bill" budget reconciliation package. The bipartisan amendment — led by Senators Blackburn, Cantwell, Markey, and Collins — demonstrated overwhelming congressional consensus on one point: federal displacement of state AI law requires a deliberate legislative act, not an executive shortcut.

That 99-1 vote is not a signal that Congress opposes federal uniformity. It is a signal that Congress insists on owning the question.

Senator Ted Cruz has pledged to reintroduce the moratorium as standalone legislation. That is the right instinct applied to the wrong vehicle — a blunt 10-year freeze would trade one problem for another. What Congress should produce instead is a narrow, principles-based federal floor for AI: uniform definitions of key terms (AI system, high-risk application, covered developer); a federal transparency baseline that preempts duplicative state disclosure mandates; a single national anti-discrimination framework for consequential AI decisions; and a clear statement of preemptive effect in covered areas. The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) provides ready-made vocabulary and conceptual structure. Existing sectoral agencies — FDA, CFPB, EEOC, FTC — retain enforcement authority within their lanes.

This is not a U.S. version of the EU AI Act. It is a constitutional settlement: one that gives industry the regulatory certainty it needs and gives states the consumer protections that motivated their action in the first place.

## The Path Forward

The FTC's March 11 policy statement should be read as what it is: a marker, not a resolution. It signals the administration's intent and legal theory. It does not deliver federal uniformity. It will not survive unchallenged.

The administration deserves credit for identifying compliance fragmentation as a genuine policy problem and for acting with urgency. It does not deserve credit for choosing an instrument — an interpretive agency statement issued without notice-and-comment rulemaking — that is legally fragile, democratically suspect, and likely to produce years of court battles rather than the regulatory certainty industry needs.

Regulatory certainty is not the same as deregulation. An AI developer making a five-year infrastructure investment needs to know what rules will govern that deployment in 2030. A non-binding FTC statement currently being litigated by 36 state AGs does not provide that answer. An Act of Congress does.

The 99-1 Senate vote demonstrated that legislators understand the stakes. The March 11 statement demonstrates what happens when the executive branch substitutes maneuver for legislation. It is time for Congress to provide what only Congress can: durable federal authority that settles the federal-state AI governance question for a generation, rather than kicking it to the courts for a decade.
