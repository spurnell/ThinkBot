On May 14, the Senate Banking Committee voted [15-9 to advance the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act](https://www.banking.senate.gov/newsroom/majority/chairman-scott-senate-banking-committee-advance-clarity-act-in-historic-bipartisan-vote) to the full Senate. Two Democrats — Senators Ruben Gallego of Arizona and Angela Alsobrooks of Maryland — broke ranks to join every Republican on the panel. For technically complex financial markets legislation, that margin represents meaningful bipartisan momentum. The bill, which [passed the House 294-134](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2025/07/17/us-house-passes-crypto-market-structure-legislation-moves-on-to-stablecoin-vote) in July 2025 before stalling in the Senate for nearly a year, is finally moving.

Now comes the harder part.

The Banking Committee's version must be merged with a separate bill the Senate Agriculture Committee advanced in January 2026 on a party-line vote. The two drafts differ on the questions that matter most: how to handle DeFi developer liability, whether to restrict stablecoin yield, and what ethics requirements should attach to government officials' crypto holdings. The merged text must then clear a 60-vote cloture threshold on the Senate floor — meaning Republican leadership needs seven Democrats beyond the two who voted in committee. Both senators who voted yes [stated explicitly that their committee votes should not be read as unconditional floor commitments](https://www.cryptotimes.io/2026/05/14/clarity-act-vote-timeline-what-happens-next/). Nothing about the next few weeks is guaranteed.

What is guaranteed is that the reconciliation process is where this bill will be either finished well or broken. Congress has a narrow window to get it right.

## Why Enforcement-by-Ambiguity Was the Real Failure

Before diagnosing the reconciliation stakes, it is worth restating the problem the CLARITY Act is solving — because critics of the bill tend to conflate "more regulation" with "better protection," and the legislative history tells a different story.

The SEC [brought 46 cryptocurrency enforcement actions in 2023 alone](https://www.cornerstone.com/insights/press-releases/sec-enforcement-of-cryptocurrency-reaches-a-new-high/), a 53 percent increase from 2022, which was itself 50 percent above 2021. This was not a consumer protection strategy — it was enforcement-as-rulemaking: the use of individual complaints, consent orders, and litigation outcomes as the industry's only available regulatory guidance. Exchanges, developers, and institutional investors could not read a registration requirement, a disclosure standard, or a supervisory framework from SEC complaint filings. They could only infer what not to do after someone else had already done it.

The CFTC ran its own parallel enforcement track. Neither agency had clear statutory authority to resolve the foundational jurisdictional question: when does a digital asset qualify as a security under the Howey test, and when is it a commodity subject to the Commodity Exchange Act? The agencies themselves acknowledged the impasse when they [signed a memorandum of understanding in March 2026](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/03/sec-and-cftc-issue-landmark-joint-guidance-on-classification-of-crypto-assets) establishing joint interpretive guidance on a five-category asset taxonomy. A memorandum of understanding between two federal agencies is not a substitute for statutory authority; it is an admission that the underlying statutes are broken.

The CLARITY Act's [core statutory structure](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/3633/text) resolves this by assigning CFTC jurisdiction over digital commodity spot markets — assets that function as stores of value or mediums of exchange — while preserving SEC authority over digital assets that remain investment contracts in the traditional Howey sense. A statutory "sufficient decentralization" test allows assets to migrate from SEC to CFTC oversight as underlying networks mature, recognizing that token governance evolves over time. This is not deregulation; it is rational jurisdictional architecture modeled on how equity and derivatives markets already work. The bill's legislative findings conclude that the current regime "creates uncertainty, stifles innovation, and pushes legitimate projects overseas" — a structural concession, adopted by the same Congress that wrote the underlying securities statutes, that enforcement-by-ambiguity failed on its own terms.

## The DeFi Liability Question Is the Reconciliation Tripwire

The most technically significant disagreement between the Banking and Agriculture Committee versions involves how the merged bill treats DeFi protocol developers.

The Senate Banking Committee draft extends Bank Secrecy Act and anti-money laundering obligations to certain DeFi protocols. The [Agriculture Committee version](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/3633/text) defers the DeFi question entirely. The industry, including privacy advocates and [Coinbase's policy team](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/05/14/clarity-act-clears-u-s-senate-committee-on-its-way-to-a-final-test-in-congress), has objected that extending compliance obligations to non-custodial protocol developers — those who write open-source code but never hold user assets, control user keys, or operate the protocol — would recreate enforcement-by-ambiguity in a new form.

The objection is technically grounded. Anti-money laundering frameworks are premised on the existence of an identifiable intermediary who can implement customer identification, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting. A bank is an intermediary. A custodial exchange is an intermediary. An open-source smart contract deployed permissionlessly on a public blockchain has no operator who can implement a compliance function, because there is no operator. Assigning BSA obligations to the developer who originally wrote that code would be analogous to holding the engineer who designed a highway interchange liable for every traffic violation committed on it — a theory of liability that no court has recognized and no enforcement agency could coherently apply.

The correct test — and the one Congress should write into the merged bill — is **custody and control**. Liability and compliance obligations should attach where an identifiable party has actual custody of user assets or meaningful control over user transactions. That captures exchanges, brokers, custodians, and payment processors. It excludes protocol developers who never touch user funds. Senator Mark Warner's [work to define standards for "truly decentralized" projects](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/05/14/clarity-act-clears-u-s-senate-committee-on-its-way-to-a-final-test-in-congress) during committee markup reflects the right instinct; the reconciled bill should codify that test explicitly rather than leaving it to regulatory interpretation.

Banks lobbying for tighter DeFi liability are asking Congress to write the next generation of ambiguity into the bill's text. The CLARITY Act's value proposition is that market participants can read the statute and know their compliance obligations before they act. DeFi provisions that are vague on the custody-and-control distinction undermine that proposition from the start.

## The Ethics Provision Is a Political Problem, Not a Policy Problem

The second major obstacle is a provision that would restrict senior government officials from holding or profiting from crypto assets during their terms in office. The [Van Hollen amendment](https://www.cryptotimes.io/2026/05/14/clarity-act-vote-timeline-what-happens-next/) that would have imposed such restrictions was rejected in committee — but it is certain to resurface on the floor, where its political salience is directly tied to the Trump family's reported crypto holdings and the administration's active promotion of digital asset markets.

ThinkBot does not take a view on executive branch ethics requirements as such; that is a governance question that sits outside the CLARITY Act's jurisdictional architecture. What Congress should recognize is that the ethics question is a *political* wedge between the bill clearing committee and clearing the floor — not a *policy* question about how digital assets should be regulated. Senators who see merit in the bill's jurisdictional framework but want an ethics provision should negotiate its scope and push for inclusion during reconciliation. What they should not do is use the ethics question as a veto over the entire bill.

The floor math is 53 Republican votes plus seven Democrats. Potential Democratic floor supporters include Senators Gillibrand, Warner, Booker, Coons, and Warnock — all of whom have engaged constructively with digital asset legislation in the past. An ethics provision that satisfies enough of those members to reach 60 is worth negotiating for. An ethics fight that stalls the bill past the August recess — when pre-election calendar compression becomes severe — benefits no one who wants U.S. digital asset markets governed by U.S. law.

## The GENIUS Act Is Part of the Same Package

Banks have also sought to use the reconciliation process to tighten stablecoin yield restrictions beyond what the GENIUS Act already established. Congress should reject that maneuver.

The [GENIUS Act, enacted July 18, 2025](https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2026/bulletin-2026-3.html), is the United States' first comprehensive federal stablecoin law. It established a dollar-for-dollar reserve requirement, a two-day redemption standard, and a regulatory pathway for both bank and nonbank payment stablecoin issuers. The OCC is required to finalize implementing regulations by July 18, 2026. Five parallel proceedings — OCC, FDIC, FinCEN, Treasury, and the Federal Reserve — are currently under way.

The GENIUS Act's implementing rulemaking process is the correct venue for stablecoin-yield questions. Regulators can calibrate yield permissibility through the rulemaking record, respond to empirical evidence, and adapt over time — exactly the kind of technical specification that implementing regulations handle better than statutory text. Reopening stablecoin yield in the CLARITY Act reconciliation process risks introducing provisions that contradict the GENIUS Act's reserve framework, confuse the implementation track already under way, or entrench incumbent banking interests at the expense of the market-structure clarity the entire legislative project is supposed to deliver.

Congress built a stablecoin law. It should let the stablecoin regulators implement it, and keep CLARITY focused on the jurisdictional architecture it was designed to establish.

## The International Clock Is Running

The competitive urgency has not diminished since the Banking Committee vote. The EU's [Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation became fully operative on December 30, 2024](https://www.klgates.com/The-Regulation-on-Markets-in-Crypto-Assets-Becomes-Fully-Applicable-in-All-Member-States-of-the-European-Union-1-24-2025), establishing a single passporting regime for crypto-asset service providers across all 27 member states. MiCA's transitional period [expires July 1, 2026](https://sumsub.com/blog/crypto-regulations-in-the-european-union-markets-in-crypto-assets-mica/); after that date, operating without a MiCA license in the EU is a regulatory violation, not a gray area. Singapore has [issued more than 30 Major Payment Institution licenses](https://www.trmlabs.com/reports-and-whitepapers/global-crypto-policy-review-outlook-2024-25-report) tied to its digital asset framework. The UK is building a parallel regime aligned with the U.S. approach. American inaction narrows the coalition.

Every month the CLARITY Act spends in reconciliation is another month in which MiCA's compliance baseline deepens its claim as the global standard. Digital asset firms with global operations must build for MiCA regardless of what Congress does; the question is whether U.S. law becomes additive to that compliance program or irrelevant to it. A firm that has already architected for MiCA does not need a U.S. framework — it needs U.S. legal certainty not to become a liability. Without CLARITY, that certainty does not exist.

The [best-case floor timeline](https://www.cryptotimes.io/2026/05/14/clarity-act-vote-timeline-what-happens-next/) is Senate passage by early July, with the House accepting the Senate text and the President signing by late summer. A bill that stalls into fall starts competing with the pre-election calendar and looks increasingly like a 2027 project. At that point, the question is no longer whether America sets global digital asset standards — it is whether Congress can adopt the ones MiCA already established by default.

## What Congress Must Get Right

The committee vote is a milestone, not a finish line. Congress should treat it accordingly.

**Lock in the custody-and-control test for DeFi.** The reconciled bill should explicitly define compliance obligations for DeFi protocols by reference to who holds user assets or controls user transactions — not by proximity to a protocol's code repository. Senator Warner's decentralization standards are a starting point; the statutory text should codify them with enough precision that a court can apply them without an agency enforcement action as the guide.

**Negotiate the ethics provision; do not surrender on the bill.** An ethics provision that satisfies enough Democrats to secure 60 votes is worth including. Provisions that are poorly scoped, retroactive, or drafted to maximize political embarrassment rather than minimize conflict of interest will cost more Democratic votes than they gain. Leadership should negotiate scope, not fight inclusion.

**Keep stablecoin yield in the GENIUS Act track.** The OCC rulemaking record is the correct venue for stablecoin-yield policy. Congress should not reopen the stablecoin question during CLARITY reconciliation in ways that contradict or undermine implementing rules already in progress.

**Move before August.** The Senate's pre-election calendar begins compressing in earnest after Labor Day. A bill with this level of bipartisan House support — 294 to 134 — should not be a difficult whip count in the Senate if the reconciliation process is handled competently. Senate leadership should prioritize floor scheduling in June and July.

The CLARITY Act's core jurisdictional achievement — a statutory SEC/CFTC split that replaces a decade of enforcement-by-ambiguity with a framework market participants can actually read — is the right architecture. That architecture survives a difficult reconciliation if Congress focuses on the custody-and-control principle for DeFi, manages the ethics question as a political negotiation rather than a policy fight, and keeps the GENIUS Act's stablecoin machinery on its own track. What it does not survive is a reconciliation process captured by incumbent banking interests seeking to narrow safe harbors, or a DeFi liability provision so overbroad that it replicates enforcement-by-ambiguity in statutory form.

The committee voted. Now Congress has to finish the job.
