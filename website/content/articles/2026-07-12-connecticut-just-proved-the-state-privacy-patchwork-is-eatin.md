On July 1, 2026, Connecticut's amended data privacy law took effect and quietly redefined who counts as a "covered business." The [Connecticut Data Privacy Act](https://www.wiley.law/alert-Major-Changes-to-Connecticut-Consumer-Privacy-Law-Will-Take-Effect-July-1-2026) originally applied to entities that processed the personal data of at least 100,000 state residents. As of this month, that threshold drops to 35,000 — and two new "no-threshold" triggers mean any business that processes *any* sensitive data or sells personal data to a third party is covered regardless of size. The same amendment adds [neural data](https://www.beneschlaw.com/insight/connecticut-broadens-data-privacy-act-requirements-effective-july-1-2026/) — signals from EEG headsets and brain-computer interfaces — to the statute's sensitive-data category, and imposes a new requirement that covered businesses disclose in their privacy notices whether personal data is used to [train large language models](https://www.bclplaw.com/en-US/events-insights-news/connecticut-quietly-adds-ai-disclosure-mandate-to-consumer-privacy-law.html).

Connecticut wasn't alone. The same week, Arkansas's [Children and Teens' Online Privacy Protection Act](https://www.alstonprivacy.com/arkansas-enacts-children-and-teens-online-privacy-protection-act/) took effect, flatly banning targeted-advertising data collection from anyone 16 or younger — with no consent exception carved out for the advertising use itself, unlike most state laws that let a parent opt a teen back in. Utah's [Digital Choice Act](https://ash.harvard.edu/resources/utah-digital-choice-act-reshaping-social-media/) went live the same day, requiring social platforms to build data portability and interoperability infrastructure so users can migrate their data to competitors. Add Alabama, Louisiana, Oklahoma and Vermont's new laws earlier this year, and the count of [comprehensive state privacy regimes reaches 24](https://www.bytebacklaw.com/2026/06/u-s-state-privacy-law-landscape-expands-to-24-states-what-the-latest-legislative-wave-means-for-businesses/) — up from 20 at the start of the year.

This is the clearest evidence yet that the "patchwork" isn't settling into a de facto national standard. It's actively getting worse, and the businesses least able to absorb the cost are the ones getting hit hardest.

**Scope creep, not scope by design.** A 100,000-resident threshold plausibly targets platforms with meaningful market reach. A 35,000-resident threshold, combined with a no-floor trigger for any sensitive-data processing, sweeps in a regional insurance broker, a mid-size health app, or a New England retailer running a loyalty program — businesses that were never Hartford's intended target when the CTDPA was drafted in 2022. Connecticut's own statutory cure period — the grace window that let businesses fix violations before facing penalties — [expired at the end of 2024](https://www.workforcebulletin.com/connecticut-overhauls-its-data-privacy-act), so these newly captured firms face enforcement exposure from day one, with no runway to build compliance programs before the state can act.

**The goalposts move after the fact.** The neural-data and LLM-disclosure provisions aren't part of a new law — they're retroactive additions to a statute businesses already built compliance programs against. A company that stood up a CTDPA-compliant privacy program in 2023 now has to re-audit its data-training pipelines and sensitive-data classifications in 2026, three years later, because the legislature decided the original sensitive-data taxonomy was incomplete. That's a fundamentally different compliance posture than "meet this standard once." It's "meet whatever standard the legislature decides on next, indefinitely" — a cost structure no fixed compliance budget can plan around.

**Divergence, not convergence, on exactly the provisions that matter.** Defenders of the state-by-state model point out, correctly, that baseline consumer rights — access, deletion, correction, opt-out — have converged across most of the 24 states into something close to a common template, as the [IAPP's own analysis of 2026 amendments](https://iapp.org/news/a/new-threads-in-the-patchwork-key-trends-in-us-comprehensive-state-privacy-law-amendments) and industry trackers note. But that convergence exists precisely in the parts of these laws that were already easy to standardize. The *new* provisions — the ones state legislatures are actively adding in 2026 — are diverging hard. Connecticut's neural-data and LLM-training-disclosure mandates, Arkansas's flat 16-and-under ad-targeting prohibition, and Utah's social-media portability infrastructure requirement are not three variations on a shared model law. They are three structurally distinct regulatory instruments — a sensitive-data classification, a categorical prohibition, and a technical build mandate — layered onto three different baseline statutes with three different thresholds. A company operating in all three states doesn't comply with "state privacy law." It complies with three separate regulatory regimes that happen to share a name.

**Who can actually absorb this.** [ITIF's modeling](https://itif.org/publications/2022/01/24/looming-cost-patchwork-state-privacy-laws/) put the out-of-state compliance cost of the fragmented patchwork at $98–112 billion annually, exceeding $1 trillion over a decade — with small businesses alone bearing $20–23 billion of that burden every year, a share disproportionate to their revenue or risk profile. A national platform with in-house privacy counsel amortizes the marginal cost of a 24th state's idiosyncratic rule across hundreds of millions of users. A regional business crossing Connecticut's new 35,000-resident line for the first time has to build a sensitive-data classification system, an LLM-disclosure workflow, and a state-specific notice regime from scratch, for a customer base a fraction of that size. The fixed costs of tracking, interpreting, and implementing 24 divergent and moving statutory targets don't scale down with company size — they function as a tax on market entry that incumbents, by definition, have already paid.

That inverts the usual "laboratories of democracy" defense of state experimentation. Six years into meaningful state privacy legislation since the CCPA, the laboratory isn't converging on portable best practices for the provisions that are still being invented — it's producing 24 non-interchangeable compliance obligations that reward firms with the scale to hire specialized counsel and punish those without it. That's not federalism serving competition; it's patchwork protectionism serving incumbency.

None of this argues against data protection. Neural data probably *should* be treated as sensitive; disclosure of AI training uses is a reasonable ask. The problem is architectural: a system where 24 legislatures can each independently define sensitive-data categories, applicability thresholds, and novel mandate types — and can revise them retroactively against companies that already built to the prior standard — cannot produce a stable compliance target no matter how good any individual state's substantive judgment is.

Congress has a live vehicle to fix this. The [SECURE Data Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/8413), still pending after a [split June 3 subcommittee hearing](https://energycommerce.house.gov/events/cmt-hearing-examining-legislation-to-establish-a-federal-comprehensive-privacy-and-data-security-law), would establish a single federal floor — access, correction, deletion, and opt-out rights enforced by the FTC and state AGs, without displacing states' ability to pursue additional enforcement of that shared standard. What Connecticut's July 1 amendments demonstrate is the cost of further delay: every month Congress doesn't act, more states have both the incentive and the template to follow Connecticut's threshold-lowering example, and the businesses paying for that delay increasingly aren't the platforms the laws were written to reach.
