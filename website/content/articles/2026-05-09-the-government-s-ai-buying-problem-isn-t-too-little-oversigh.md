On April 13, 2026, the Government Accountability Office published [GAO-26-107859](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107859), a systematic audit of how federal agencies — including the Department of Defense, Department of Homeland Security, the General Services Administration, and the Department of Veterans Affairs — are acquiring artificial intelligence systems. The findings are uncomfortable: agencies are moving fast, spending real money, and systematically failing to document what works, what doesn't, and why. There is no shared institutional memory on AI acquisitions.

Contracting officers report difficulty accessing technical experts capable of evaluating vendor proposals. Pricing is opaque. Acquisition timelines are mismatched with how AI systems actually develop and iterate. GAO issued four recommendations; all agencies concurred.

The report has received almost no sustained policy commentary. Four weeks after publication, [FedScoop](https://fedscoop.com/agency-ai-procurement-gao-report/) was among the few outlets to cover it substantively; no major think tank has published a response. That silence is a problem, because the reform debate is quietly being framed in the wrong direction.

The instinct in Washington — in Congress, in OMB, and in the oversight community — is to respond to a procurement failure by adding a compliance layer. Documentation requirements, vendor certification regimes, pre-award attestation mandates. The logic is intuitive: if agencies are making bad decisions, require them to produce more paper before making those decisions. The logic is also wrong.

Federal AI procurement dysfunction is a *competition deficit*, not an oversight gap. The institutions that are supposed to discipline procurement — price competition, performance feedback, credible alternatives — are structurally absent from most federal AI buying. The fix is to restore competitive pressure and hold agencies accountable for outcomes. Adding bureaucratic friction will entrench the incumbents who created the problem and price out the challengers who could solve it.

## What the GAO Actually Found

The GAO report is worth reading carefully, because its findings are more specific — and more diagnostic — than the headline suggests.

Agencies are not failing because vendors are hiding information. They are failing because nothing in the federal acquisition system *requires* them to capture and share what they learn. There is no mandated lessons-learned process for AI acquisitions. Each agency — sometimes each program office within an agency — is effectively starting from scratch. When a contract underperforms, that information stays local. When a contracting approach works, it isn't systematized. The result is an institutional amnesia that compounds with every new AI purchase.

This is an organizational failure. It is not a market failure, and it is not evidence that vendors are behaving deceptively. The absence of shared institutional memory reflects the absence of institutional requirements, not the presence of vendor misconduct. That distinction matters enormously for designing the right response.

The report also flags that agencies struggle to access technical experts capable of evaluating AI proposals. This is a genuine workforce capacity problem — and it has a direct effect on procurement quality. When contracting officers cannot independently assess whether a proposed AI system will perform as advertised, they become dependent on vendor representations. That dependence is exploitable. It is also a structural invitation for incumbents with established relationships and pre-cleared proposal templates to win awards that more capable challengers might otherwise contest.

The pricing opacity the GAO identifies is the downstream consequence of this dynamic. When competition is thin — when there are one or two viable bidders rather than five or ten — vendors have pricing power. Opacity is not the cause of that power; it is the symptom. Mandatory disclosure requirements don't generate competition. Only actual competitors do.

## The Wrong Response: More Compliance Overhead

The emerging policy response to these findings reflects a reflex that predates the AI era: when a procurement fails, layer on more documentation requirements.

OMB's December 2025 memorandum [M-26-04](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/M-26-04-Increasing-Public-Trust-in-Artificial-Intelligence-Through-Unbiased-AI-Principles-1.pdf) — nominally titled "Increasing Public Trust in AI Through Unbiased AI Principles" — imposes new contractual documentation requirements on AI vendors doing business with the federal government, including attestations that their systems are free from "ideological bias." The memo is responsive to a real concern about AI systems producing skewed outputs, but the mechanism it prescribes is nearly meaningless as a performance standard and actively harmful as a procurement policy.

"Ideological neutrality" is not a measurable attribute of an AI system. It cannot be verified by an inspector, audited by a contracting officer, or tested by an independent evaluation. What the attestation requirement does is create legal exposure for contractors — who now face potential liability for a compliance standard they cannot operationally define — and divert compliance resources from genuine performance evaluation. As [Lawfare has documented](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/omb-releases-guidance-on-trump-s--woke-ai--executive-order), the memo's operative requirements were drafted primarily in response to the executive order on "woke AI," not in response to the specific acquisition failures GAO identified. The two documents exist in largely parallel universes.

The deeper problem with the compliance-layer approach is structural: large incumbent primes can absorb new documentation requirements. They have compliance departments. They have legal teams. They have existing relationships with contracting officers who will help them navigate novel attestation demands. A nimble AI-native startup does not have these resources. Every new pre-award requirement — however well-intentioned — is a moat that benefits the firms already inside the perimeter.

This is not theoretical. The [ITIF](https://itif.org) and others have documented how the federal acquisition system's compliance overhead progressively narrows the vendor pool. The Pentagon has [documented the result in its own industrial base analysis](https://media.defense.gov/2022/Feb/15/2002939087/-1/-1/1/STATE-OF-COMPETITION-WITHIN-THE-DEFENSE-INDUSTRIAL-BASE.PDF): the number of prime contractors competing for major defense awards fell from 51 to 5 over three decades of consolidation. Adding documentation requirements calibrated to large-firm compliance capacity only accelerates that trend. The agencies that are already struggling to evaluate proposals from diverse vendors will find themselves, after the next round of compliance layering, evaluating proposals from even fewer vendors at even higher prices.

## The Right Diagnosis: A Procurement Monoculture

The structural driver of federal AI procurement failure is incumbent insulation. The Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) system, designed for a Cold War defense procurement environment, systematically advantages firms with established procurement relationships, pre-cleared facilities, existing security clearances, and the institutional patience to navigate multi-year acquisition cycles. These are not irrelevant criteria — security clearances matter for classified work, and past performance is a legitimate evaluation factor. But the system has calcified in ways that go well beyond legitimate security requirements.

AI development cycles operate on timescales of months, not years. A model that is state-of-the-art when a contract is solicited may be obsolete before the option years are exercised. The incumbents who win multi-year contracts are not necessarily building the best systems; they are the firms that are best at winning multi-year contracts. These are different competencies, and the gap between them is widening as AI capabilities advance.

The pricing opacity the GAO documents is a direct consequence of competitive thinness. When a contract goes to a sole-source incumbent or a small number of pre-qualified primes, those firms set prices without market discipline. Disclosure requirements don't fix this; only actual competition does. The question for reform is: how do you inject competition into a procurement system that has been structurally engineered to suppress it?

## What Works: Outcome-Based Contracting

Before turning to reform proposals, it is worth noting what the evidence shows about federal AI acquisitions that *have* worked. The GAO report, for all its criticism, identifies a consistent pattern among better-performing programs: agencies that tied AI contracts to measurable performance thresholds report better vendor accountability than those that specified inputs and processes.

This finding aligns with broader evidence from the defense acquisition literature. Agencies that defined AI contracts in terms of output requirements — accuracy rates on specified task classes, false-positive ceilings for automated screening systems, latency benchmarks for real-time decision support — were able to evaluate vendor performance against objective criteria. When systems underperformed against those criteria, agencies had contractual levers to demand remediation or exit. When systems performed, agencies could justify option-year extensions with confidence.

Agencies that specified *how* AI systems should be built — what training data should be used, what model architectures were permissible, what documentation had to accompany the deliverable — often found themselves locked into vendor approaches that became outdated before deployment. Process specifications protect vendors from accountability; outcome specifications protect agencies from underperformance.

The [CSET](https://cset.georgetown.edu) and the [Mercatus Center](https://www.mercatus.org) have both published work on performance-based contracting approaches that apply cleanly to AI acquisitions. The model is not new. What is new is the urgency of applying it to a technology category where capabilities evolve faster than any procurement cycle can track.

## The Reform Agenda: Three Levers

The right response to GAO-26-107859 is not a new compliance mandate. It is a targeted set of structural reforms that inject competition, reward performance, and build the institutional knowledge base that agencies currently lack. Three reforms deserve immediate priority.

### 1. Expand Other Transaction Authority and Commercial Solutions Openings

[Other Transaction Authority (OTA)](https://www.diu.mil/work-with-us) allows DoD and certain other agencies to enter into agreements for research and prototype projects that are not subject to the standard FAR requirements. This flexibility has successfully on-boarded AI-native vendors — firms that would never survive a traditional FAR procurement cycle — into the federal market. The Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA) and the [Defense Innovation Unit (DIU)](https://www.diu.mil/) have used OTA extensively to access cutting-edge capabilities that established primes were not providing.

Commercial Solutions Openings (CSOs) are the civilian-agency analog: a streamlined solicitation mechanism that allows agencies to procure innovative commercial capabilities without full FAR compliance overhead. Both mechanisms have proven records. Both are systematically underused because procurement cultures favor familiar processes, and OTA awards require more upfront technical engagement than contracting officers are routinely staffed to provide.

The reform priority is not to invent new pathways but to widen the existing ones. Congress should extend OTA authority to all major civilian agencies with significant AI procurement budgets. OMB should issue guidance directing agencies to default to CSO pathways for AI acquisitions below a dollar threshold — say, $50 million — before resorting to traditional FAR procurements. The goal is to make competitive, flexible acquisition the path of least resistance rather than the exception requiring special justification.

Critically: the security clearance requirements that genuinely apply to classified programs are fully compatible with OTA and CSO pathways. The argument that competitive acquisition compromises security is not supported by the track record of these mechanisms. DARPA and DIU have awarded OTA contracts to firms that subsequently obtained the necessary clearances; the sequencing is manageable.

### 2. Require Outcome-Based Performance Standards in FAR Updates

The Federal Acquisition Regulation Council should update the FAR to require outcome-based performance specifications for AI acquisitions above a materiality threshold. Specifically: any AI contract exceeding $10 million should specify measurable performance benchmarks as a contractual condition — not as an aspirational goal but as a basis for option-year decisions and contract remediation.

The mechanics of this are not trivial. Defining meaningful performance benchmarks for AI systems requires technical expertise that many contracting officers currently lack — which returns us to the workforce capacity problem the GAO identified. The solution is not to defer the reform but to pair it with investment in the technical acquisition workforce. The Pentagon's [Defense Acquisition University](https://www.dau.edu) and GSA's training programs should develop AI acquisition curriculum that equips contracting officers to write and evaluate outcome-based specifications. This is a multi-year investment, but it is the kind of institutional capacity building that compounds.

The alternative — continuing to allow process-based specifications that immunize vendors from performance accountability — is demonstrably more expensive. The GAO report documents this cost at the agency level. The aggregate cost across government is almost certainly larger.

### 3. Convert the GSA AI Repository into Competitive Intelligence

OMB's guidance directs GSA to develop an AI acquisition repository — a centralized resource for capturing lessons learned from federal AI procurements. This is the right idea, implemented in the wrong mode.

Compliance archives are read by auditors. Competitive intelligence tools are used by acquisition officers making live decisions. The difference is not cosmetic. A repository structured as a compliance artifact — filed away after award, reviewed during IG audits — will not change acquisition behavior. A repository structured as a living, searchable performance database — updated quarterly, populated with vendor performance data, accessible to contracting officers preparing solicitations — will.

The GSA repository should surface what vendors won which contracts, what performance benchmarks were specified, how systems performed against those benchmarks, what pricing was achieved, and what acquisition pathway was used. This is market intelligence. It allows a contracting officer at the VA preparing an AI procurement to ask: what did DoD pay for comparable capabilities? What vendors delivered against their benchmarks? What acquisition structures correlated with better outcomes?

Privacy and source-selection sensitivity concerns are real but manageable. Aggregate performance data can be shared without revealing proprietary pricing to competitors. The [FAR's existing framework for contractor performance assessments](https://www.acquisition.gov/far/subpart-42.15) provides a model; the AI repository should extend and modernize that framework rather than duplicate it. The goal is market discipline through information transparency — not a compliance archive that satisfies an OMB checkbox.

## The Stakes

The federal government is one of the largest AI buyers in the world. How it buys AI matters — not just for taxpayers but for the shape of the AI market. When federal procurement concentrates around a small number of incumbent primes, it channels R&D investment toward firms optimized for compliance rather than capability. When it rewards vendors who perform against measurable benchmarks, it sends market signals that drive genuine innovation.

The GAO report is a moment of institutional honesty about a real problem. The question is whether the policy response will address the structural root cause — incumbent insulation and absent competition — or layer on documentation requirements that make the problem worse while making it harder to see.

Performance accountability is not the enemy of responsible AI acquisition. It is its precondition. Agencies that know what they are paying for, that can verify what they received, and that can compare their results against other agencies' experiences are agencies that can be held accountable. The compliance instinct produces paperwork. The competition instinct produces results.

The reformers reaching for new certification regimes and attestation mandates are solving the wrong problem. The right problem — the one the GAO identified and the one the procurement monoculture sustains — is the absence of competitive pressure that disciplines every other market where AI is being deployed. Fix that, and the documentation will follow. Paper the symptoms without fixing the structure, and the expensive mistakes will continue, duly certified and thoroughly unaddressed.
