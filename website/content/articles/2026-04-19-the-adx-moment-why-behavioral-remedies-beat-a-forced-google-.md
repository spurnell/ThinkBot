Judge Leonie Brinkema's self-imposed March 31 deadline for her remedies ruling in *United States v. Google* has passed without a decision. The courthouse in Alexandria is quiet, but the ruling is expected at any moment — and when it arrives, it will set the terms for how the most consequential antitrust case in digital advertising resolves.

The framing war is already well underway. The DOJ's proposed remedy — forced divestiture of Google's ad exchange, AdX, potentially followed by divestiture of DoubleClick for Publishers (DFP) — has attracted support from structural breakup advocates who see it as the only way to genuinely open the digital advertising stack to competition. They are wrong. The proportionate and economically sound remedy is targeted behavioral relief: mandated open-auction access, non-discriminatory real-time bidding parity for rival ad servers, and interoperability requirements that sever the tying mechanism Brinkema actually adjudicated. A forced structural breakup would deliver disruption, not competition.

## What Brinkema Must Actually Decide

This framing point matters enormously: Brinkema found Google liable for *tying* AdX and DFP to foreclose rival ad exchanges and ad servers. That is the adjudicated harm. It is a specific, mechanism-based violation — one that can be remedied by severing the tie through interoperability requirements, without severing the companies entirely.

[During closing arguments, Brinkema herself signaled deep skepticism about the DOJ's structural proposal](https://www.adexchanger.com/online-advertising/doj-v-google-judge-brinkema-calls-for-less-ad-tech-window-dressing-in-the-adx-and-dfp-divestiture-debate/), pointedly noting that DOJ had identified no buyer for AdX and that any acquisition would itself require additional antitrust review. That skepticism is well-founded. A remedy is only as sound as its causal connection to the underlying harm. [Google's behavioral proposal](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/cb2c3ded/what-you-need-to-know-from-closing-arguments-in-us-v-google) — making AdX's real-time bids available to competing ad servers, ending self-preferencing mechanisms like First Look and Last Look, and enabling server-to-server integration with Prebid — directly addresses the foreclosure mechanism without requiring the court to restructure the entire programmatic supply chain. The remedy must match the harm. Here, that means severing the tie, not the company.

## The Mehta Precedent: DOJ Signals That Behavioral Is the Right Tool

The remedies phase in the parallel search monopoly case, *United States v. Google LLC* before Judge Amit Mehta, offers instructive precedent. After [Mehta issued a September 2025 remedies order imposing behavioral relief](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11362) — principally, a ban on exclusive default search distribution contracts, with no order to divest Chrome or Android — the DOJ ultimately backed away from its structural appeal in favor of a choice-screen approach. [Alphabet stock rallied 4% on April 8](https://markets.financialcontent.com/stocks/article/marketminute-2026-4-8-alphabet-dodges-structural-breakup-doj-choice-screen-mandate-triggers-relief-rally-for-google-parent) when the structural threat formally receded.

The market read that correctly. DOJ's pivot was not capitulation — it was a recognition that forced spinoffs of deeply interdependent platform assets introduce transition chaos, destabilize adjacent markets, and tend to benefit rivals far more than consumers. That reasoning transfers directly to AdX. If DOJ's own enforcement apparatus has concluded that behavioral remedies are the appropriate tool for a search monopoly built on exclusive distribution deals, the analytical case for structural divestiture of an auction-based ad exchange is considerably weaker.

## Why Divestiture Would Harm the Publishers It Means to Protect

The structural breakup argument treats AdX as a static monopoly fortress. It is not. AdX is an auction market — and in auction markets, liquidity *is* the product.

[Current market data shows AdX holds roughly 34% of the header-bidding market](https://databeat.io/blog/us-programmatic-trends-march-2025/), operating alongside Prebid at 39% and Amazon's TAM at 26%. High-volume DSPs like The Trade Desk and Google's DV360 route a substantial share of their spend through AdX. That concentration reflects bid density — the aggregated demand that makes AdX valuable to publishers. Force a divestiture of AdX from Google's publisher and advertiser relationships, and you do not get a competitive open-access exchange. You get a fragmented auction with thinner demand pools, reduced bid density, and lower floor CPMs for the small publishers and independent news organizations the DOJ's case was nominally designed to protect.

That is a genuine consumer welfare loss, not a rounding error. The breakup advocates owe a serious answer to this question and have not provided one.

## What Structural Proponents Get Wrong

The most persistent argument for divestiture is that behavioral remedies are toothless — that Google will game any compliance requirement. This is a real concern, but it is an argument for rigorous remedy design, not blunt structural surgery.

A properly specified behavioral remedy is not weak. It includes: real-time bidding parity for third-party ad servers with independent technical verification; structural separation of AdX's auction logic from Google's proprietary first-party data inputs; a neutral technical compliance committee with subpoena power; and retained court jurisdiction with contempt as a genuine deterrent. [Behavioral remedies fail when they are vague](https://digiday.com/marketing/googles-ad-tech-antitrust-remedy-phase-explained-doj-wants-googles-offerings-and-where-the-judge-might-land/). They succeed when they are specific, monitored, and enforced.

Structural proponents also make a static market assumption: they treat today's programmatic landscape as permanent. It is not. [Amazon's DSP has grown to reach 46% of digital ad buyers](https://adage.com/article/digital-marketing-ad-tech-news/inside-google-amazon-and-trade-desks-dsp-battle/2572876/), displacing Google's own DV360 into third place. [The Trade Desk continues to compete aggressively across agency and direct-client channels](https://www.marketingbrew.com/stories/2026/04/09/the-trade-desk-strategy-exec-changes-agency-scrutiny). Retail media networks represent a structurally distinct supply pathway that barely existed five years ago. Dynamic market entry is already happening. The behavioral remedy creates space for this evolution to continue; the divestiture remedy assumes markets are permanently frozen and only judicial restructuring can move them.

## The Congressional Risk

There is a second-order risk worth flagging. Legislators on the Senate and House Judiciary Committees are watching Brinkema's ruling closely, and some will use whatever she decides as a template for legislative structural breakup mandates targeting ad tech — or tech platforms generally.

That would be a category error. Judicial remedies in *United States v. Google* are tailored to a specific adjudicated harm under Section 2 of the Sherman Act. They emerge from a trial record, particularized findings, and court-supervised relief. Congress does not have Brinkema's liability findings, her evidentiary record, or her institutional authority to impose proportionate, targeted relief. Translating a specific antitrust remedy into sweeping industrial policy by statute is not antitrust enforcement — it is industrial reorganization wearing antitrust clothing. Staff on the congressional antitrust committees should resist that inference when the ruling drops.

## The Right Call

Brinkema found a real violation. It deserves a real remedy. A robust behavioral package — mandated open-auction access, real-time bidding parity enforced by independent technical verification, genuine interoperability requirements between DFP and rival ad servers, and serious contempt-backed compliance mechanisms — can sever the foreclosure mechanism, restore competitive conditions in publisher ad serving, and open AdX's auction to the broader market.

Forced divestiture of AdX would be a disproportionate instrument aimed at a structural outcome the adjudicated violation does not require and the market does not need. The AdX moment calls for proportionate, targeted relief. Judge Brinkema should deliver it.
