# The Incumbents' Playbook: Why Banks Want to Slow-Walk the GENIUS Act

The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency's comment period on its proposed stablecoin rules under the [GENIUS Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/1582) closed on May 1, 2026. If you were expecting the loudest objections to come from cryptocurrency skeptics or consumer protection advocates, you were wrong. The most strategically crafted requests for delay came from the American Bankers Association, the Bank Policy Institute, the Consumer Bankers Association, and the Independent Community Bankers of America — the four major trade groups representing the institutions that stablecoins most directly threaten.

This is the incumbents' playbook, and it has been run before. When a new market threatens established players, those players don't always argue against the new market on the merits. Instead, they weaponize the regulatory process itself — demanding extensions, questioning sequencing, citing complexity — until the regulatory runway for nonbank competitors is so compressed that incumbents can establish a structural head start in the "compliant" version of the market they once tried to stop. The GENIUS Act is the most significant pro-innovation financial legislation in a generation. Its implementation timeline must be defended.

## What the GENIUS Act Actually Created

Signed into law on July 18, 2025, the GENIUS Act is the first comprehensive federal regulatory framework for payment stablecoins in American history. Before its passage, stablecoins operated in a fragmented legal void: technically unregulated at the federal level, subject to inconsistent state money-transmitter licensing, and periodically targeted by SEC enforcement theories that treated certain digital tokens as unregistered securities. The [Cleary Gottlieb 2026 digital assets review](https://www.clearygottlieb.com/news-and-insights/publication-listing/2026-digital-assets-regulatory-update-a-landmark-2025-but-more-developments-on-the-horizon) described the Act as setting forth "a comprehensive federal regulatory framework for payment stablecoins" and clarifying that "permitted payment stablecoins are not securities, commodities or deposits, but instead part of a separate regulatory regime."

That clarity matters enormously. A global stablecoin market now exceeding [$320 billion in total supply](https://www.cryptotimes.io/2026/04/05/stablecoin-market-hits-317b-amid-1-36b-weekly-inflows/) — with Tether's USDT commanding approximately $185 billion and Circle's USDC approaching $78 billion — has been operating without the federal legal foundation that institutional adoption at scale requires. The GENIUS Act provides that foundation. It authorizes national banks, federal savings associations, and — crucially — nonbank entities to issue payment stablecoins under federal supervision, establishing a new category of Permitted Payment Stablecoin Issuers (PPSIs) subject to OCC oversight.

The Act's effective date is the earlier of 120 days after primary federal regulators finalize their implementing rules, or January 18, 2027 — eighteen months after enactment. That timeline is not bureaucratic boilerplate. It is a structural feature of the law: a deadline designed to prevent agencies from sitting on their hands while foreign jurisdictions cement competitive advantages in a market the United States created.

## Four Agencies, One Market, and a Scheduling Problem

Implementing the GENIUS Act requires simultaneous rulemaking across four federal agencies — each proceeding on its own track, each with its own comment period, each with its own institutional incentives. Understanding this fragmentation is essential to understanding why the banks' delay request is so strategically clever.

- **The OCC** published its proposed rule on February 25, 2026, covering reserve requirements, redemption rights, licensing pathways for nonbank PPSIs, and risk management standards. The [376-page NPRM](https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2026/bulletin-2026-3.html) posed more than 200 questions and over 1,000 sub-questions. Comment period closed May 1, 2026. Final rules due July 18, 2026.

- **The FDIC** published its own NPRM on April 10, 2026, covering requirements for FDIC-supervised stablecoin issuers and insured depository institutions. [Comment period deadline: June 9, 2026.](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/04/10/2026-06974/genius-act-requirements-and-standards-for-fdic-supervised-permitted-payment-stablecoin-issuers-and)

- **FinCEN and OFAC** jointly published AML/CFT and sanctions compliance program requirements on April 10, 2026. Comment deadline: June 9, 2026.

- **The Treasury Department** separately published an advanced notice of proposed rulemaking on state-level regulatory equivalence — determining which state frameworks will qualify as equivalents to the federal regime. Comment deadline: June 2, 2026.

The Federal Reserve is also expected to issue related guidance on bank holding company activities and capital treatment for stablecoin operations.

This is a genuine governance design problem. Five separate proceedings, imperfectly coordinated, with different timelines and different lead agencies — each producing rules that interact with the others. But "this is complicated" has never been a sound justification for missing a statutory deadline. It is, however, an excellent foundation for a delay request.

## The Delay Request, Dissected

On April 21, 2026, the American Bankers Association, Bank Policy Institute, Consumer Bankers Association, and Independent Community Bankers of America sent a [joint letter](https://www.aba.com/advocacy/policy-analysis/extension-request-genius-act-nprms) to Treasury, the FDIC, FinCEN, and OFAC requesting that the comment deadlines for all three non-OCC NPRMs be extended to 60 days *after* the OCC issues its final rule. Their argument: the other rules are "directly contingent on the OCC's final framework," and comprehensive comments on FDIC and FinCEN rules cannot be prepared until the OCC framework is settled.

The same groups also requested that the OCC extend its own comment period by 60 days — from May 1 to approximately July 1, 2026 — citing the volume and complexity of the proposed rule.

Evaluate both requests honestly. The OCC's NPRM is genuinely complex: 376 pages, 200-plus questions, technical reserve composition requirements, two alternative diversification frameworks, automatic approval provisions for nonbank applicants, and novel guidance on co-branded stablecoins and yield restrictions. The substance is demanding. The banks are not wrong that understanding it takes time.

But here is the question the OCC must ask: demanding time is not the same as deserving it. The GENIUS Act was [enacted on July 18, 2025](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/1582). The banking industry has had the better part of a year to staff up, retain outside counsel, review the legislative text, and prepare substantive analysis. The OCC issued a [pre-NPRM bulletin](https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2026/bulletin-2026-3.html) flagging the regulatory agenda before the formal NPRM. These are not institutions caught flat-footed by a surprise rulemaking. These are institutions that employ entire teams of regulatory specialists.

The "interdependency" argument is more sophisticated but equally circular. Yes, the FDIC and FinCEN rules interact with the OCC framework. But regulatory sequencing is standard practice in Washington. Rules routinely finalize before related proceedings close, incorporating cross-references, conditional provisions, and commitments to issue conforming guidance as parallel proceedings conclude. That is not a bug in the regulatory process; it is a feature. An FDA drug approval does not wait for FTC advertising standards to finalize before it takes effect.

What the delay request would actually accomplish is more important than what it claims to accomplish. If the OCC misses its July 18 deadline, the GENIUS Act cannot take effect until January 18, 2027 — regardless of when other agencies finalize. Every month of slippage compresses the window for nonbank PPSIs to build operational infrastructure, attract institutional clients, and establish market presence before incumbent banks leverage their existing relationships, balance sheets, and regulatory capital into the compliant stablecoin market. Procedural delay is competitive strategy dressed up as administrative conscientiousness.

The ABA's OCC comment, [as reported by the ABA Banking Journal](http://bankingjournal.aba.com/2026/05/aba-state-bankers-associations-urge-occ-to-close-yield-loopholes-in-stablecoin-rule/), actually reveals the banks' underlying competitive concern: they want the OCC to close "yield loopholes" that would allow nonbank stablecoin issuers to effectively compete with bank deposit products. In other words, the banks want the regulatory framework — not just the timeline — shaped to protect their deposit base. They are using the comment period to pursue two objectives simultaneously: delay that limits nonbank runway, and substantive rule changes that disadvantage nonbank business models.

## The OCC's Framework Is Broadly Sound

The banks are right that the OCC's NPRM is complex. They are wrong that it is deficient. The proposed framework, as [analyzed by Sullivan & Cromwell](https://www.sullcrom.com/insights/memo/2026/March/OCC-Proposes-Regulations-Implement-GENIUS-Act) and [Nixon Peabody](https://www.nixonpeabody.com/insights/alerts/2026/04/02/proposed-occ-regulations-for-payment-stablecoins-under-the-genius-act), is broadly consistent with the statute and represents a reasonable implementation of the GENIUS Act's core requirements.

On **reserve composition**, the OCC requires dollar-for-dollar backing in segregated reserves — cash, insured deposits, short-term Treasury notes, and government money market funds. Two diversification options give issuers flexibility: Option A provides a voluntary safe harbor requiring 10% of reserves convertible to cash daily and 30% weekly; Option B makes those minimums mandatory. For large issuers with $25 billion or more outstanding, a minimum 0.5% allocation to insured deposits (capped at $500 million) is required.

On **redemption rights**, holders can redeem within two business days. Circuit breakers activate if redemptions exceed 10% of outstanding issuance within 24 hours — a prudent operational backstop that does not meaningfully impair the stablecoin's utility as a payment instrument.

On **nonbank licensing**, the framework creates a genuine pathway for PPSIs that is distinct from bank chartering. Applications are automatically approved after 120 days if the OCC takes no action — a provision designed to prevent regulatory inertia from functioning as a de facto licensing denial.

Better Markets, which submitted [comments opposing the framework as inadequate on systemic risk grounds](https://bettermarkets.org/newsroom/occ-genius-act-proposal-fails-to-address-stablecoin-risks-raising-the-chance-turmoil-spreads-into-the-broader-economy/), represents the opposite error: demanding heavier capital buffers and run-risk requirements that would effectively make stablecoin issuance non-viable for any non-bank entity. Progressive advocacy organizations seeking to layer bank-equivalent prudential regulation onto stablecoin issuers are not the constituency the OCC should optimize for. The GENIUS Act created a new regulatory category precisely because stablecoins are not deposits and should not be regulated as such.

## Consensys's Concerns: Legitimate Edge Cases, Not a Rationale for Delay

Not all critical comments deserve to be dismissed as incumbency protection. Consensys submitted [substantive objections](https://www.thecoinrepublic.com/2026/05/02/stablecoin-news-consensys-pushes-back-on-occ-rules-under-genius-act/) raising legitimate edge-case concerns about the OCC's yield prohibition and co-branded stablecoin rules.

On yield, Consensys argues that Congress deliberately limited the prohibition to stablecoin *issuers* — rejecting amendments that would have extended it to third parties. Under the OCC's current reading, third parties who receive commercial fees and independently allocate portions to user incentives could be classified as issuer-funded yield. For DeFi lending protocols like Aave, where users actively transfer stablecoins and accept protocol risk independently, this reading creates real ambiguity.

On co-branded stablecoins, Consensys argues for transparency requirements — mandatory reserve backing disclosures and segregated asset pools for different branded tokens — rather than outright restrictions on single issuers producing multiple tokens.

These are genuine interpretive questions. They are also precisely the kinds of edge-case concerns that supplemental guidance documents are designed to resolve. The OCC can finalize its core framework on schedule and commit to issuing interpretive guidance on yield treatment and co-branding within a defined period — 90 to 120 days after the final rule. That approach addresses Consensys's concerns without handing the banks a delay-based competitive advantage. Reopening or extending the full comment period on the NPRM is not proportionate to the issues Consensys has raised.

## The Geopolitical Stakes No One Is Saying Loudly Enough

There is a dimension to this debate that deserves more attention than it gets in Washington comment letters. The United States currently dominates global stablecoin issuance. [Tether's USDT commands roughly 59% of the $320 billion market](https://www.cryptotimes.io/2026/04/05/stablecoin-market-hits-317b-amid-1-36b-weekly-inflows/); dollar-denominated stablecoins collectively account for more than 99% of global supply. That is a form of dollar hegemony operating at internet speed.

But that dominance is not permanent and not guaranteed. The EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) framework, fully in effect since December 30, 2024, has already [reshaped the European stablecoin market](https://blogs.law.ox.ac.uk/oblb/blog-post/2025/11/europes-mica-moment-racing-against-time-stablecoin-wars) — forcing the delisting of over $140 billion in non-compliant stablecoins from European exchanges. European institutions are not waiting for American rulemaking to decide what compliant dollar-denominated stablecoins look like for their markets. Singapore's Payment Services Act, the UAE's VARA framework, and Hong Kong's regulatory regime are each offering their own pathways for compliant stablecoin issuance under non-American jurisdiction.

Every month the United States delays finalizing its GENIUS Act implementation is a month offshore issuers can operate in regulatory jurisdictions with settled frameworks while American nonbank entrants remain in legal limbo. Implementation delay is de facto market concession. The compliant dollar-denominated stablecoin market will be built by someone. The GENIUS Act was designed to ensure it is built here, under American law, with American oversight. The banks' delay requests, if granted, would undermine that objective — even if that is not their stated intention.

## A Better Solution: Coordinate, Don't Delay

There is a genuine governance problem embedded in the multi-agency GENIUS Act rulemaking that deserves a real solution. Five agencies issuing five separate NPRMs on interrelated subjects, with imperfectly coordinated timelines, creates legitimate risks of inconsistency, duplicative compliance obligations, and interpretive gaps that nonbank issuers will face in trying to build compliant operations.

The solution is not delay. It is coordination.

Congress gave the OCC the lead role in implementing the GENIUS Act's federal PPSI framework. The FDIC, FinCEN, OFAC, and Federal Reserve are issuing supplementary rules within that framework. What is missing is a formal interagency coordination mechanism — something analogous to the [SEC-CFTC Memorandum of Understanding](https://www.cftc.gov/sites/default/files/idc/groups/public/@lrenforcementactions/documents/legalpleading/enfmouSECCFTC.pdf) that governs jurisdictional handoffs between those two agencies — that specifies how agencies will cross-reference each other's rules, resolve interpretive conflicts, and maintain a unified compliance interface for stablecoin issuers operating across multiple regulatory perimeters.

Treasury should convene an interagency working group — OCC, FDIC, FinCEN, OFAC, and the Fed — to produce a joint implementation guidance document within 60 days of the OCC's final rule. That document should: (1) map which provisions of each agency's rule govern which issuer activities; (2) identify any cross-rule conflicts and resolve them through agreed interpretations; (3) establish a single point of contact for nonbank PPSI applicants navigating the multi-agency framework. This is not bureaucratic wishful thinking — it is standard interagency practice for complex regulatory regimes.

What it is emphatically not is a reason to miss the July 18 deadline.

## Policy Recommendations

**For the OCC:** Finalize implementing rules on schedule by July 18, 2026. Reject blanket comment period extension requests. Commit to issuing supplemental guidance on yield treatment and co-branded stablecoins within 90 days of the final rule's effective date.

**For Treasury:** Convene an interagency coordination working group immediately. Require joint implementation guidance within 60 days of the OCC final rule. Reject the banking groups' request to tie FDIC and FinCEN comment periods to OCC finalization.

**For Congress:** If the multi-agency rulemaking architecture proves unworkable in practice, the appropriate fix is statutory clarification of agency roles — not a comment period extension that primarily benefits incumbents. A legislative amendment designating the OCC as the single point of regulatory coordination for PPSI applicants would be a proportionate response.

**For Fintech and Crypto Industry Stakeholders:** Submit substantive comments that help the OCC improve edge-case guidance without undermining the July deadline. The GENIUS Act is the framework you have been waiting for. Do not allow procedural maneuvering by incumbents to delay its implementation.

---

The GENIUS Act represents a genuine inflection point: the United States is, for the first time, building a comprehensive federal legal structure for the payment stablecoin market it created. Getting the framework right matters. But getting it on time matters just as much. The banks understand this — which is precisely why they are asking for more of it.

The OCC should not oblige.
