On January 1, 2026, a new kind of gatekeeper went live in six American states. Under the [CMS WISeR (Wasteful and Inappropriate Service Reduction) Model](https://www.cms.gov/priorities/innovation/innovation-models/wiser), machine learning algorithms now screen prior authorization requests for 15 Medicare Part B services — epidural steroid injections, cervical fusions, vagus nerve stimulators, bioengineered skin substitutes for lower extremity wounds, and others — before a single human clinician reviews the case. Six technology companies, each covering a specific state or region across New Jersey, Ohio, Oklahoma, Texas, Arizona, and Washington, are responsible for issuing determinations within 72 hours. The program runs through December 31, 2031.

This is not a pilot program in the conventional sense. WISeR is the federal government deploying AI at operational scale to make consequential gatekeeping decisions affecting real Medicare beneficiaries — today, right now, not in a theoretical future. The policy debate is no longer about whether AI belongs in prior authorization. It is already there. The question is whether the governance architecture surrounding it is adequate to the task.

The answer, at present, is no. And the legislative response forming in state capitals — [more than 35 bills in 2026 alone](https://www.manatt.com/insights/newsletters/health-highlights/manatt-health-health-ai-policy-tracker) proposing to prohibit "algorithmic denials" or mandate physician review — while well-intentioned, cannot fix the problem. Most of those bills cannot legally reach the populations at greatest risk, and together they create a compliance patchwork that burdens commercial insurers while leaving Medicare largely untouched.

The right answer is a federal framework: mandatory human clinician review for all AI-assisted denials, algorithmic transparency obligations, and robust CMS audit rights — enforceable through the Administrative Procedure Act's notice-and-comment rulemaking process using authority CMS already possesses. Not 35 state bans. Not a new congressional AI law. A coherent federal floor, built on the accountability infrastructure that already exists.

## What WISeR Actually Does

WISeR is architecturally novel in federal healthcare policy. It is [the first CMS Innovation Center model](https://www.dinsmore.com/publications/is-ai-wiser-cms-models-ai-based-prior-authorization-process-in-six-states/) in which technology companies — not healthcare providers or insurers — serve as the primary participants. Each vendor is responsible for prior authorization determinations under Original Medicare (fee-for-service) for a defined list of high-risk Part B services that CMS has identified as having elevated potential for waste, fraud, and abuse.

The incentive structure is where governance complexity immediately emerges. Participating vendors are compensated by [receiving 10–20% of the Medicare savings associated with averted care](https://www.beneschlaw.com/resources/cms-launches-wiser-model-a-technology-enabled-effort-to-reduce-wasteful-medicare-spending.html) — in direct terms, the more procedures the algorithm declines to approve, the more money the vendor earns. CMS argues that quality-score adjustments and audit exposure will prevent perverse incentives from distorting outcomes. Critics — including the American Hospital Association, which filed [detailed comments in October 2025](https://www.aha.org/lettercomment/2025-10-23-aha-comments-cms-wiser-model) — argue the payment model structurally incentivizes denials regardless of medical necessity, creating financial pressure that operates against the clinical judgment the system is supposed to support.

WISeR's own design includes one critical safeguard that deserves to anchor the governance debate: when coverage is denied, the determination cannot be issued on the basis of algorithmic screening alone. A human clinician must review the case before the denial is finalized. That design choice is not incidental — it is the governance principle ThinkBot argues should be codified as a federal floor for all AI-assisted prior authorization, in WISeR and beyond.

## The Status Quo Was Already Broken

Before engaging any critique of AI in prior authorization, it is necessary to confront an uncomfortable empirical baseline: the pre-AI prior authorization system was causing demonstrable harm to patients and providers on a massive scale.

According to the [AMA's 2024 prior authorization physician survey](https://www.ama-assn.org/system/files/prior-authorization-survey.pdf), the average medical practice completed 39 prior authorization requests per physician per week, with physicians and staff spending approximately 13 hours weekly on associated administrative tasks. Ninety-three percent of physicians report that prior authorization delays access to necessary care; 94% report a negative impact on clinical outcomes; and 89% say the burden significantly increases physician burnout. More than a third of surveyed practices employ staff dedicated exclusively to prior authorization work.

The numbers at the payer level are equally troubling. In Medicare Advantage alone, insurers processed [nearly 53 million prior authorization requests in 2024](https://www.kff.org/medicare/medicare-advantage-insurers-made-nearly-53-million-prior-authorization-determinations-in-2024/), denying approximately 4.1 million — a 7.7% denial rate. Only 11.5% of those denials were appealed. Yet 80.7% of the appeals that were filed overturned the initial denial. The implication is significant: the majority of appealed denials were initially wrong, and the vast majority of denied patients never appealed at all.

This is the baseline against which AI-assisted prior authorization should be measured: a system characterized by chronic delay, wide inter-reviewer inconsistency, massive administrative burden, near-total opacity about how decisions are made, and an overturn rate on appeal that strongly suggests systematic over-denial with no meaningful accountability mechanism to correct it. The political framing that casts AI as the threat to patients inverts the actual problem. The threat is the status quo. A properly governed AI system is, at minimum, an improvement.

That said, proper governance is not optional. WISeR's incentive structure — vendors paid on savings from denials — makes governance mechanisms urgent precisely because the financial pressure runs in the wrong direction. An algorithm that earns its operator more money each time it recommends denial requires accountability mechanisms proportionate to that risk. The question is whether we build those mechanisms intelligently or whether we panic and prohibit.

## The State Legislative Response: Well-Intentioned, Structurally Defective

The legislative energy in the states has been real and rapid. Arizona's governor signed [legislation in 2026](https://www.beckerspayer.com/policy-updates/new-arizona-law-bars-ai-from-final-say-in-denials/) requiring a licensed medical professional to have final authority over claims denials and prior authorization decisions, prohibiting AI as the sole decision-maker. Washington's law specifies that AI algorithms may approve, but not deny, prior authorization requests without professional review. Minnesota's HF2500 would prohibit AI-issued adverse prior authorization determinations outright. Texas passed legislation in 2025 requiring human oversight before any automated denial can be issued. Similar bills have been introduced across California, Connecticut, Illinois, Maryland, Missouri, and Oregon.

These bills share a legitimate underlying concern: algorithmic systems making consequential coverage decisions without meaningful human oversight pose unacceptable risks to patients. ThinkBot shares that concern. The problem is structural, not motivational.

The structural failure flows from two intersecting federal preemption doctrines that severely constrain what states can actually accomplish.

**ERISA preemption.** Under the Employee Retirement Income Security Act, [self-funded employer health plans are broadly preempted from state insurance mandates](https://www.commonwealthfund.org/publications/issue-briefs/2022/may/state-cost-control-reforms-erisa-preemption). If a state law directly affects a self-insured plan's administration, structure, or design — which prior authorization requirements plainly do — ERISA preempts it. Self-funded plans cover the large majority of Americans with employer-sponsored coverage. State prior authorization bills, regardless of how carefully drafted, cannot legally reach them.

**Medicare Act preemption.** Medicare Advantage (Part C) plans operate under federal contract and are comprehensively regulated by the Medicare Act and CMS, not by state insurance commissioners. The Medicare Act's regulatory scheme preempts state insurance mandates as applied to Medicare Advantage plans. This is not a peripheral carve-out: Medicare Advantage now covers more than half of all Medicare beneficiaries. WISeR itself operates in Original Medicare — outside state insurance oversight entirely.

The combined effect is stark. The largest and most vulnerable patient populations — Medicare Advantage beneficiaries and Americans in self-funded employer plans — are structurally beyond the reach of state prior authorization legislation. The bills being enacted largely apply to commercial fully-insured plans: a meaningful market segment, but not the one the legislation nominally protects. Meanwhile, each new state law creates a separate compliance architecture, a separate disclosure regime, and a separate legal risk profile — costs borne by health systems, insurers, and ultimately patients through higher premiums.

This is regulatory fragmentation without commensurate protection. It is not a substitute for federal governance; it is a delay of it.

## The Right Framework: A Federal Accountability Floor

ThinkBot does not argue that AI-assisted prior authorization should be unregulated. We argue it should be regulated correctly — and that the correct vehicle is federal, outcomes-focused, and grounded in existing statutory authority.

Three elements constitute the minimum accountability floor.

**First: Mandatory human clinician review for all AI-assisted denials.** This is WISeR's own design principle, and it should be codified as a condition of participation for any AI-assisted prior authorization program operating under federal healthcare programs. The rule is simple: when an AI-assisted review recommends denial of a prior authorization request, a licensed clinician must independently review and sign off before that determination is finalized. This does not restrict AI-assisted approval, which represents the vast majority of prior authorization volume. It applies targeted human accountability at the decisional moment that most affects beneficiaries — the denial margin.

The logic parallels Miranda's prophylactic rule in criminal procedure: warnings are not required for every police encounter, only at the specific point of maximum jeopardy — custodial interrogation. A human-in-the-loop mandate for AI denials follows the same principle: not an innovation-blocking requirement across all AI-assisted review, but a minimum legitimacy standard at the one point where the stakes are highest. For programs like WISeR — where the vendor's financial incentive runs toward denial — that floor is not merely good practice; it is a structural necessity.

**Second: Algorithmic transparency obligations.** Vendors operating AI prior authorization systems under federal programs should be required to disclose, at minimum: the training data used to develop the algorithm, the criteria governing denial recommendations, denial rates by service type and beneficiary demographic category, and the rate at which AI denial recommendations are overturned by human reviewers. These are not proprietary trade secrets — they are the inputs necessary for CMS, providers, and beneficiaries to evaluate whether the system is performing as designed.

CMS required Medicare Advantage plans to publish [aggregate prior authorization metrics](https://medicare.chir.georgetown.edu/cms-suspends-new-medicare-advantage-prior-authorization-transparency-rules-amid-public-concerns-about-care-denials/) beginning in 2026 — approval rates, denial rates, and turnaround times — but subsequently suspended more granular service-level disclosure requirements, citing regulatory burden. Contract-level aggregate figures are inadequate for meaningful oversight of a system like WISeR, where the financial incentives of individual vendors are the primary risk factor. WISeR participants should face a higher standard: algorithmic-level disclosure by service type and beneficiary demographic, with overturn rates accessible to CMS auditors and, ultimately, the public.

**Third: Robust CMS audit rights with public reporting.** WISeR's program design already authorizes CMS to audit participant determinations and adjust vendor quality scores — and compensation — based on accuracy. That mechanism is the right architecture, but it needs to be operationalized with genuine teeth. CMS should publish audit results at regular intervals, establish clear accuracy thresholds for continued program participation, and maintain the affirmative authority to terminate vendors whose denial patterns are statistically inconsistent with Medicare coverage criteria. Given the incentive structure of WISeR — vendors whose revenue depends on denials — independent audit is not a background assurance. It is the primary enforcement mechanism in the program.

## The APA Path: CMS Already Has the Authority

The most critical point for health policymakers is this: **Congress does not need to pass new AI legislation to achieve any of the above.** CMS already possesses the statutory authority to mandate each of these three elements.

[Section 1115A of the Social Security Act](https://www.ssa.gov/OP_Home/ssact/title11/1115A.htm) — the statute establishing the CMS Innovation Center — grants the Secretary broad authority to test innovative payment models and establish conditions of participation, payment conditions, and program requirements for those models. The HHS Office of the National Coordinator for Health IT published a [Request for Information in December 2025](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/12/23/2025-23641/request-for-information-accelerating-the-adoption-and-use-of-artificial-intelligence-as-part-of-clinical-care) seeking broad public input on how HHS should structure AI governance in clinical care. The comment period closed February 23, 2026, and the [AHA's formal response](https://www.aha.org/lettercomment/2026-02-23-aha-response-hhs-rfi-ai-health-care) — representing the healthcare establishment — called for robust oversight standards, algorithmic transparency, and strong audit mechanisms. Not prohibition. Governance.

The RFI record now exists. The stakeholder input has been received. The appropriate next step is a notice-and-comment rulemaking under the APA — technically informed, deliberate, and legally binding in a way that neither state legislation nor voluntary standards can achieve. The APA rulemaking process is the right vehicle: it is the mechanism Congress designed for agencies to exercise statutory authority through a transparent, deliberative, and legally binding process. CMS setting enforceable standards for AI in prior authorization through rulemaking is not regulatory overreach; it is administrative law functioning as intended.

Pending legislation like the [Ban AI Denials in Medicare Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/6361/text) (H.R. 6361), which would prohibit WISeR and any comparable CMS Innovation Center model outright, is the wrong answer. It would eliminate a program that, under proper governance, addresses a genuine Medicare fraud and waste problem — and it would leave the status quo intact, which is precisely the outcome it purports to prevent. Prohibition is not accountability; it is abdication.

## The Case for Federal Coherence

A uniform federal standard for AI-assisted prior authorization serves three constituencies simultaneously.

It serves **patients** by guaranteeing that when an algorithm recommends denying their care under any federally administered or contracted program, a licensed clinician will review that recommendation before it becomes final — a protection that 35 divergent state bills cannot reliably deliver to the populations who need it most.

It serves **providers** by establishing a single national compliance architecture rather than 50 state-by-state regulatory regimes with inconsistent disclosure obligations, conflicting procedural timelines, and different liability exposure. [AJMC has documented](https://www.ajmc.com/view/contributor-prior-authorization-in-2026-cms-is-rebuilding-the-operating-model) that CMS is already restructuring the prior authorization operating model; a federal standard complements that restructuring rather than fragmenting it.

It serves **innovators** by providing the regulatory clarity that allows health systems, insurers, and AI developers to build scalable solutions once — rather than engineering 35 separate compliance architectures for a system that is inherently national in structure. A coherent federal standard is not an innovation brake; it is the predictable legal environment that responsible innovation requires.

## Conclusion

WISeR's operational launch is an inflection point in American healthcare governance. For the first time, the federal government is not merely regulating how private actors use AI in healthcare — it is operating an AI gatekeeping system itself. That distinction matters. When the government is the actor deploying AI to make coverage decisions, the due process obligations of administrative law apply with particular force. The APA was built for precisely this kind of moment.

The governance framework should be constructed now, while WISeR is in its first year of operation, while the HHS RFI record is fresh, and while the policy window is open. Waiting for the first high-profile denial to generate congressional panic — followed by a blunt prohibition bill that installs the status quo as the permanent default — would be the worst outcome for every stakeholder in this debate, including the patients whose care hangs in the balance.

Three elements. Existing authority. An open rulemaking docket. CMS knows what to do. The question is whether it will move before the political window closes.
