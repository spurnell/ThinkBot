The Department of Labor has a knack for solving yesterday's problem in ways that damage tomorrow's economy. Its [March 27 proposed rule](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/03/27/2026-06017/improving-wage-protections-for-the-temporary-and-permanent-employment-of-certain-foreign-nationals) overhauling H-1B prevailing wage methodology is a textbook example. Dressed in the language of worker protection, it would price out the AI researchers, machine learning engineers, and data scientists that American competitiveness most urgently requires — and hand every rival AI nation a structural recruiting advantage at the worst possible moment. The comment window closes **May 26**. That gives stakeholders sixteen days to put a better idea on the record.

## What the Rule Actually Does

The DOL proposes to shift the wage percentile anchors substantially upward across the board: Level I wages would move from the 17th to the 34th percentile of the occupational wage distribution; Level II from the 34th to the 52nd; Level III from the 50th to the 70th; and Level IV from the 67th to the 88th. These are not modest calibrations. By DOL's own estimates — summarized in [Holland & Knight's analysis of the proposed rule](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/03/dol-targets-prevailing-wages-sweeping-increases-proposed-for-h1b-h1b1) — more than **75 percent** of Labor Condition Applications certified between FY2020 and FY2024 would fall below the proposed new floors. In practical terms, three-quarters of all employer-worker arrangements certified over the past five years would fail the new floors — a measure of how fundamentally the methodology would shift.

The wage floor methodology relies on Bureau of Labor Statistics [Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS)](https://www.bls.gov/oes/) data drawn from a three-year rolling survey — a collection approach that introduces a meaningful lag between the reference period and actual market conditions. In AI and machine learning, where compensation benchmarks at frontier labs have been rewritten annually by demand the survey was never designed to track, this lag is not a technical footnote. It is a structural defect. The floors being proposed are calibrated to a market that no longer exists.

## Who Bears the Cost

Here is the distributional reality the rule's proponents skip past: **the employers least able to pay are the ones most exposed.**

Google, Meta, and OpenAI can absorb $30,000–$45,000 per-engineer annual cost increases without restructuring their hiring plans. Their compensation bands already land at Level III and Level IV — the [Penn Wharton Budget Model](https://budgetmodel.wharton.upenn.edu/p/2026-04-08-the-impact-of-higher-prevailing-wages-on-the-h-1b-visa-lottery/) confirms that under the new floors, only 7 percent of registrations would reach Level IV, while 21 percent fall entirely below the new Level I floor and receive no lottery entries whatsoever. The senior engineers at established tech giants are largely insulated. The people who lose access to the H-1B system are the entry-to-mid level researchers — the PhD graduates joining university spinouts, pre-revenue AI startups, national laboratory teams, and defense contractors whose compensation structures were never built to compete with Big Tech salary bands and were never expected to.

This is not worker protection. It is market incumbency protection. The DOL will entrench Big Tech's existing talent monopoly while describing the mechanism as a labor standard.

## The Pipeline Problem Is Long-Horizon

The talent damage from this rule is deferred but compounding. Entry-level researchers priced out of the H-1B system in 2026 do not simply vanish. They relocate, they build careers elsewhere, and they become the frontier model architects, quantum computing researchers, and biotech innovators of 2033 — working for someone else's national AI program. The [National Security Commission on Artificial Intelligence](https://reports.nscai.gov/final-report/chapter-10) warned in its 2021 final report, with bipartisan sign-off, that the H-1B system's structural barriers to early-career STEM immigration were compressing the long-term talent pipeline that feeds U.S. technological leadership. The DOL rule does not fix that problem. It makes it worse by a dimension.

## The Alternatives Are Not Hypothetical

The standard rebuttal to concerns about restrictive immigration policy is that foreign talent will simply wait America out. That is no longer credible. The alternatives are operational, fast, and actively marketed.

[Canada's Global Talent Stream](https://www.cicnews.com/2025/12/skilled-workers-in-these-occupations-can-get-a-work-permit-within-two-weeks-1263290.html) processes work permits for high-skill tech roles in approximately two weeks. The [UK's High Potential Individual visa](https://www.gov.uk/high-potential-individual-visa) requires no job offer and admits graduates of top global universities for up to three years of open work authorization. Singapore is launching a dedicated [ONE Pass AI and Tech track](https://www.envoyglobal.com/news-alert/singapore-announced-one-pass-track-for-ai-and-tech-talent/) in January 2027 explicitly designed to attract the world's leading AI researchers. Meanwhile, China introduced its own [K visa](https://itif.org/publications/2025/11/13/china-welcomes-stem-talent-while-the-united-states-pushes-it-away/) in late 2025, targeting young STEM professionals without requiring employer sponsorship. As ITIF has documented, China is simultaneously producing STEM PhDs at nearly double the U.S. rate — and is now building immigration infrastructure designed to capture the researchers America is pushing away.

The engineers the DOL is about to price out of U.S. institutions have options. They know it.

## The Lottery Interaction Doubles the Damage

The wage-floor increase doesn't operate in isolation — it compounds an H-1B lottery already structured to disadvantage early-career researchers. The FY2027 lottery operates on a wage-weighted basis: Level IV positions receive four lottery entries, Level III positions three, Level II positions two, and Level I positions one. The system is specifically engineered to favor senior engineers at established firms over early-career researchers at smaller organizations. Raising the wage floors reinforces this bias at every tier, filtering the talent pool toward exactly the employers who need the program least. The [Penn Wharton analysis](https://budgetmodel.wharton.upenn.edu/p/2026-04-08-the-impact-of-higher-prevailing-wages-on-the-h-1b-visa-lottery/) finds the combined effect of the wage-weighted lottery and the proposed prevailing wage increases would raise average selected-registrant compensation by $20,611 — an 18 percent jump over the prior random lottery baseline. That number sounds like a win for American workers. What it actually represents is a structural filter that removes AI researchers who cannot command Big Tech salaries from access to the program entirely.

## What Should Replace It

The argument here is narrow. H-1B reform is legitimate; the wage floor *methodology* is analytically wrong. Documented wage suppression by specific employers should be addressed through targeted enforcement — ex-post, employer-specific auditing — not through ex-ante price floors calibrated to BLS data that systematically undercount AI/ML compensation.

Three targeted fixes would address the real concerns without the collateral damage:

**First**, create AI and STEM occupational carve-outs that use competitive market data rather than OEWS survey averages. Specialized compensation benchmarks already exist in the private market; the DOL should be required to use them for high-velocity technical occupations where the survey lag is demonstrably distortive.

**Second**, establish a startup exemption — tiered by firm age and revenue — consistent with the International Entrepreneur Rule framework the NSCAI recommended. Pre-revenue AI research organizations, including federally funded national labs, cannot be held to private-sector compensation bands that the federal government is simultaneously competing against through CHIPS Act, NSF, and DARPA investment priorities.

**Third**, codify academic and national lab exemptions aligned with existing Optional Practical Training and Curricular Practical Training frameworks. The institutions that generate the foundational research on which commercial AI development depends should not be priced out of the talent market by a rule ostensibly designed to protect workers in the commercial sector.
