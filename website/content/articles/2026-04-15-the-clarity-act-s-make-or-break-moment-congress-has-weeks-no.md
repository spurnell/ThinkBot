The Senate Banking Committee is staring down one of the most consequential markup windows in recent financial regulation history — and the clock is running out. [White House crypto adviser Patrick Witt signaled on April 13](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/04/13/white-house-s-top-crypto-adviser-witt-says-talks-clearing-other-points-on-clarity-act/) that remaining hurdles on the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act are being cleared. Yet Chairman Tim Scott has still not announced a markup date, and industry observers and administration officials alike are warning that [missing the pre-May window likely kills the bill](https://www.cryptotimes.io/2026/04/14/the-clarity-act-countdown-will-the-senate-deliver-a-crypto-win-or-run-out-the-clock/) before November midterms reset the legislative clock entirely.

This is not a normal legislative delay. It is a structural deadline. Congress should act this week.

## The Problem Isn't Regulatory Ambiguity — It's Enforcement by Litigation

For the better part of a decade, U.S. digital asset markets have operated without a statutory market structure framework. In the absence of congressional action, the SEC filled the vacuum not through rulemaking — with its notice-and-comment procedures, public participation, and substantive review — but through complaint filings. The Coinbase case, the Ripple suit, the Binance enforcement action: these were not aberrations. They were the policy. The agency made law through litigation, deciding case-by-case which assets constituted securities and which trading platforms required registration, without ever providing a clear prospective standard that firms could plan around.

This enforcement-by-litigation regime is not a neutral regulatory default. It is, in practice, regulation without the procedural safeguards that administrative law requires. It punishes compliance-seeking firms that tried to engage with regulators, rewards incumbents who can afford years of litigation, and provides no intelligible standard for new market entrants. Legal limbo is itself a regulatory choice — and it is among the most costly and least accountable choices a government can make.

The Digital Asset Market Clarity Act ends this. It establishes a clear statutory framework: which digital assets are securities subject to SEC jurisdiction, which are commodities governed by the CFTC, and how that determination tracks through an asset's lifecycle from initial issuance to secondary market trading. Jurisdictional clarity of this kind is not a concession to the industry — it is the restoration of rule-of-law principles that should have governed this market from the beginning.

## The Last Major Obstacle Has Been Cleared

For months, the primary substantive obstacle to Senate floor consideration was a dispute over stablecoin yield — specifically, whether stablecoin issuers could pay interest to holders and how that would interact with banking and securities law. [Senators Thom Tillis and Angela Alsobrooks reached an agreement in late March](https://www.disruptionbanking.com/2026/03/22/clarity-act-unblocked-stablecoin-yield-compromise-reached/) that prohibits passive holding rewards while permitting activity-based rewards tied to transactions and platform usage. The deal removed the provision that had been the primary Democratic holdout and cleared a path to a committee vote.

What remains on the table — DeFi developer liability definitions and ethics language prohibiting senior government officials from personally profiting from crypto holdings during their tenure — does not warrant further delay. [The White House is "cautiously optimistic"](https://coinpedia.org/news/clarity-act-senate-vote-2026-why-the-white-house-is-now-cautiously-optimistic-about-passage/) precisely because these are the kinds of targeted drafting questions that get resolved in markup, not before it. DeFi developer liability concerns are addressable through safe harbor carve-outs that distinguish passive protocol developers from active market operators — a distinction [the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32023R1114) already draws. The ethics provisions, meanwhile, are broadly popular on both sides of the aisle: barring senior officials from personally profiting from digital asset holdings while in office is more protective than the status quo, not less.

Letting definitional perfectionism block a decade-overdue market structure bill would be a significant policy failure. Statutory frameworks are always refined through subsequent agency rulemaking and judicial interpretation — that is how all financial regulation works. The Commodity Exchange Act did not arrive fully formed. Neither did Dodd-Frank. The goal is to establish a workable prospective standard; the details get adjudicated from there.

## The Midterm Clock Is a Structural Fact

[Key legislative calendar dates for U.S. crypto regulation in 2026](https://www.dlnews.com/articles/regulation/key-dates-for-us-crypto-regulation-in-2026/) make the constraint clear: a Senate Banking Committee markup must happen before May for the bill to retain a credible path to the floor before the November elections. This is not political conjecture — it is the arithmetic of a congressional schedule compressed by recess periods, appropriations deadlines, and the traditional slowdown as campaigns intensify.

A new Congress in the 120th means starting from scratch. Every negotiated compromise — the Tillis-Alsobrooks stablecoin yield deal, the SEC/CFTC jurisdictional line-drawing, the treatment of decentralized protocols — would need to be renegotiated with a new committee composition and a new set of member priorities. The Tillis-Alsobrooks compromise alone took the better part of a year to reach. [Senate Banking Committee debate on the CLARITY Act has been ongoing](https://nationaltoday.com/us/dc/washington/news/2026/04/11/senate-banking-committee-debates-digital-asset-market-clarity-act/) — the groundwork is laid. Losing it to a calendar failure would not be a delay; it would be a reset.

## The Global Competitiveness Cost Is Compounding

While Congress has deliberated, the rest of the world has not waited. The EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation became fully applicable in late 2024, giving European digital asset firms a clear statutory framework for issuance, trading platform licensing, and stablecoin operations. Singapore's Monetary Authority, Dubai's Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority, and Hong Kong's licensing framework are not hypothetical futures — they are operational regimes actively attracting capital, engineering talent, and corporate domicile decisions from U.S.-trained founders who concluded that operating in American legal limbo was simply not worth the risk.

This is a structural competitive disadvantage, and it is compounding monthly. The firms that have relocated to Singapore or Dubai to operate under a clear regulatory framework are not coming back once the CLARITY Act passes. First-mover advantage in financial market infrastructure accrues over years, not quarters. The longer American digital asset markets operate under an enforcement-by-litigation regime while competitors operate under prospective statutory standards, the more durable that structural gap becomes.

## What Needs to Happen This Week

Chairman Scott should announce a Senate Banking Committee markup date for the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act before the end of April. The substantive objections have been resolved or are resolvable in markup. The White House has signaled its support. The competitive case for action is concrete and documented. The only input still missing is scheduling.

American digital asset markets have waited long enough for a rule-of-law framework to replace an enforcement-by-litigation regime. The pre-May window is not a lobby's deadline — it is a structural feature of the congressional calendar. Congress should use it.
