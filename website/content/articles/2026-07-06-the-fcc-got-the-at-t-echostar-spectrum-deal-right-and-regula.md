On May 12, 2026, the Federal Communications Commission's Wireless Telecommunications Bureau [approved AT&T's $23 billion purchase](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-26-470A1.pdf) of EchoStar's nationwide 3.45 GHz and 600 MHz spectrum licenses — the largest secondary-market spectrum transfer in years, and a companion to EchoStar's roughly $17 billion sale of AWS-4/H-Block spectrum to SpaceX that the Commission [conditionally approved the same day](https://www.fierce-network.com/wireless/fcc-gives-conditional-ok-echostars-spectrum-sales-att-spacex). The order came after months of public sparring: T-Mobile had asked the FCC to condition or outright deny the AT&T transfer, demanding aggressive geographic buildout benchmarks, drive-test verification, and — most tellingly — the cancellation of unconstructed 600 MHz licenses for re-auction if AT&T didn't move fast enough. AT&T and EchoStar [fired back](https://broadbandbreakfast.com/at-t-echostar-fire-back-at-spectrum-deal-opponents/), arguing that a rival's preferred timeline was not a lawful basis for blocking or reshaping a license transfer.

The Commission split the difference in exactly the right way: it approved the deal, attached its own — not T-Mobile's — buildout conditions, and declined to touch the underlying license grants. That outcome, and the process that produced it, deserves to become the template for how spectrum-transfer review works going forward, because the alternative — letting a well-resourced competitor dictate the terms of a rival's regulatory approval — is not a competition safeguard. It's a competitive weapon, and regulators should be increasingly wary of it as more of these transactions come before the FCC and DOJ.

## The Question Is Highest-Value Use, Not a Rival's Comfort

Spectrum is a scarce, government-allocated input, and the antitrust and telecom-policy question worth asking about any transfer is narrow: does this move spectrum to a higher-value use than its current owner is putting it to? On that question, the AT&T-EchoStar deal is not a close call. EchoStar told the FCC that [regulatory and financial uncertainty](https://www.lightreading.com/5g/echostar-to-fcc-severe-uncertainty-led-to-spectrum-sales) — not a strategic decision to cash out at peak value — drove the sale. That is a distressed-asset reallocation, the exact kind of transaction merger and license-transfer review exists to facilitate rather than obstruct. AT&T, for its part, is a network operator with the capital and an operational incentive to deploy the spectrum it doesn't yet use.

T-Mobile's framing — that this is a rival "hoarding" spectrum until it can extract more value — inverts the actual dynamic. The seller here is not hoarding; it is exiting. The question isn't whether EchoStar should be allowed to sell distressed spectrum, but whether the buyer can be trusted to build it out on a credible timeline. That is a real and answerable engineering question, not a proxy for market-share anxiety, and the FCC has existing tools to answer it.

## Existing Rules Already Police Warehousing

This is the part of the debate that gets lost in the "dueling filings" framing: the FCC does not need a rival's bespoke conditions to prevent spectrum warehousing. [47 CFR Part 27.14](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/47/27.14) already imposes interim and final population-coverage benchmarks on wireless licensees, with automatic, non-reinstatable forfeiture for licensees who miss them. That ex-post framework — generally applicable, not drafted by a competing bidder for a specific transaction — is precisely the right level of intervention. Layering rival-authored, transaction-specific buildout mandates and reversion threats on top of it doesn't close a regulatory gap; it substitutes a competitor's commercial preferences for a generally applicable rule the Commission already enforces.

AT&T's argument that it needs meaningful lead time to deploy 600 MHz — new multiband radios and antennas, handset-vendor coordination, tower leases and permitting, since AT&T doesn't currently operate in that band — is a genuine engineering constraint that any acquirer of unfamiliar spectrum would face, not a company-specific excuse. Any credible transaction-review framework has to leave room for integration timelines that reflect physical and contractual reality, or it will systematically punish exactly the buyers with the resources to build out neglected spectrum.

## The FCC Threaded the Needle — That's the Model

To its credit, the Commission's May 12 order didn't simply wave the deal through. It attached buildout deadlines of its own, [tighter in some respects than AT&T had proposed](https://www.fierce-network.com/wireless/fcc-gives-conditional-ok-echostars-spectrum-sales-att-spacex), and required EchoStar to escrow $2.4 billion against pending tower-litigation liabilities. What it did *not* do is grant T-Mobile's request to treat unbuilt licenses as forfeit before the buyer had a chance to build, or to make the transfer's approval contingent on a rival's preferred coverage math. That is the distinction that matters: evidence-based, generally applicable conditions tied to the Commission's own buildout framework are legitimate; competitor-drafted, transaction-specific mandates functioning as a brake on a rival's spectrum access are not. Notably, two days after approval, AT&T, T-Mobile, and Verizon [jointly announced a dead-zone coverage and spectrum-pooling arrangement](https://about.att.com/story/2026/new-joint-venture.html) — a reminder that adversarial regulatory filings and ordinary competitive cooperation are not mutually exclusive, and that dynamic markets tend to resolve tension faster than static "market structure" narratives suggest.

## Precedent Risk Is the Real Stake

The reason this case matters beyond its own facts is precedent. If regulators start treating a rival's petition as license to impose bespoke buildout conditions or dangle license revocation mid-transaction, every future spectrum sale inherits a new risk premium: sellers discount distressed assets further, buyers price in the possibility that a competitor's objection could unwind a closed deal, and the secondary spectrum market — already burdened by exactly the transaction costs [Thomas Hazlett has spent a career cataloguing](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2440003) — gets less liquid, not more. That is the opposite of what critics of "spectrum hoarding" claim to want. Friction that deters transfers doesn't get underused spectrum built out faster; it keeps it fallow longer.

This is also consistent with the FCC's own stated direction under Chairman Brendan Carr, whose deregulatory agenda — from mid-band auction planning to rule-elimination initiatives — treats faster, more liquid spectrum reallocation as a wireless-leadership priority, not an antitrust risk to be managed. A Commission serious about that priority should continue resisting the temptation to let competitor petitions rewrite the terms of license transfers on a case-by-case basis.

None of this means buildout obligations don't matter, or that the FCC should rubber-stamp every spectrum transfer a well-capitalized bidder proposes. It means the burden of proof belongs on demonstrated harm to consumers — delayed deployment, foreclosed entry, degraded service — not on a rival's preference for a faster clock or a bigger stick. The 2020 T-Mobile-Sprint merger, which itself only proceeded after the [DOJ required divestiture of spectrum and prepaid assets to what became EchoStar's wireless business](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-requires-t-mobile-and-sprint-divest-prepaid-business-dish-and-enter), is proof that conditions calibrated to actual competitive harm work. Conditions calibrated to a rival's competitive interest do not. The FCC got that distinction right in May. It should keep getting it right the next time a distressed spectrum holder finds a buyer, and a competitor finds a microphone.
