On Thursday, April 30, the Federal Communications Commission will convene its [open meeting](https://spacepolicyonline.com/events/fcc-open-meeting-incl-spectrum-sharing-for-satellite-broadband-apr-30-2026-dc-online-1030-am-1230-pm-et/) to vote on a Report & Order that has attracted relatively little attention outside the satellite industry — and deserves far more. At stake is a fundamental overhaul of how geostationary (GSO) and non-geostationary (NGSO) satellite systems share spectrum, rules that have remained largely unchanged since the 1990s. If adopted, the order would replace a rigid, prescriptive technical regime with a performance-based framework the FCC projects could unlock [over $2 billion in economic benefits](https://www.fcc.gov/document/modernizing-spectrum-sharing-satellite-broadband) and expand space-based broadband capacity by up to sevenfold.

In a policy environment where Washington's instincts have trended heavily toward ex ante mandates, litigation-friendly private rights of action, and blunt-force structural interventions across nearly every tech domain, this order is an exception worth documenting. It's what adaptive, technology-neutral regulation actually looks like.

## A Framework Built for a World That No Longer Exists

The Commission's current satellite interference rules are built around [Equivalent Power Flux Density (EPFD) limits](https://www.fcc.gov/document/modernizing-spectrum-sharing-satellite-broadband) — engineering constraints developed in the late 1990s to protect geostationary satellites from interference by the handful of NGSO systems then in existence. The underlying assumption was a sparsely populated orbital environment with static, predictable interference patterns. That assumption no longer holds.

The satellite industry has been transformed since those limits were written. Dense low-earth-orbit constellations operating hundreds or thousands of satellites have become the defining architecture of modern satellite broadband. These systems use adaptive beamforming, dynamic power control, and software-managed interference mitigation that simply did not exist when the EPFD framework was designed. Applying 1990s worst-case static limits to adaptive systems isn't neutral protection — it's regulatory archaeology that actively caps what modern networks can deliver.

The Commission had already acknowledged this implicitly: the FCC's Space Bureau [granted EPFD waivers to NGSO constellation operators](https://www.satellitetoday.com/connectivity/2026/01/12/fcc-gives-spacex-approval-for-7500-more-starlink-gen2-satellites/) to allow their systems to function at all. A regulatory framework that survives only through a patchwork of individual waivers is a framework that has already failed. The April 30 order clears the table and replaces it with something calibrated to 2026.

## Performance-Based Standards Are the Right Tool

The core of the reform is a shift from rigid EPFD caps to outcome-oriented protection criteria. The new framework establishes a [3% time-weighted average throughput degradation threshold](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DOC-420708A1.txt) as the long-term protection criterion for GSO satellite links using adaptive coding and modulation, paired with a short-term limit capping absolute link unavailability increases at 0.1 percentage points. A minimum 3-degree avoidance angle from the GSO arc provides an additional geometric safeguard.

These are not looser rules — they are more precisely calibrated ones. The EPFD regime protected against a theoretical interference scenario derived from worst-case assumptions. The new thresholds define the harm directly — actual throughput degradation and link availability — and leave engineers free to choose the means. Technology-neutral, outcome-oriented standards don't freeze the technological frontier at the date the rule was written; they let innovation run forward while the protection floor holds steady.

The Commission estimates capacity gains of between [100% and 700%](https://www.fcc.gov/document/modernizing-spectrum-sharing-satellite-broadband) in affected areas, with some analyses showing NGSO systems could serve areas using up to eight satellites simultaneously rather than one under current constraints. A sevenfold capacity expansion in satellite broadband is not incremental progress.

## The Rural Broadband Case Is Concrete

The downstream consequences in this case are anything but abstract. As of the FCC's most recent [broadband data collection](https://www.fcc.gov/BroadbandData), approximately 24 million Americans lack access to fixed broadband service, with rural and Tribal communities carrying the largest share of that gap. For millions of households beyond the economic reach of fiber or cable deployment, satellite broadband is not a backup option — it is the option.

The best rural broadband policy maximizes what private capital can build without subsidy. Programs like BEAD are valuable but carry real constraints: competitive bidding cycles, cost-share requirements, slow deployment timelines, and political durability risk. Regulatory modernization that unlocks sevenfold capacity growth in the satellite layer doesn't require an appropriations rider or a state broadband office. It requires one Commission vote.

FCC Chairman Brendan Carr put it plainly: ["By discarding last century's satellite regulations, we could see billions of dollars in benefits for the American economy and broadband speeds many times faster than what is available today."](https://www.satellitetoday.com/government-military/2026/04/08/fcc-to-vote-on-order-to-update-epfd-framework/) The Commission is right. And that case should be made loudly.

## US Spectrum Leadership Has Geopolitical Consequences

Satellite spectrum governance is not purely a domestic affair. How the FCC resolves GSO/NGSO coexistence will shape the United States' posture heading into the [2027 World Radiocommunication Conference (WRC-27)](https://www.itu.int/wrc-27/) — the ITU process where binding international spectrum allocations are negotiated. WRC-23 already took steps toward NGSO accommodation, and WRC-27 preparatory studies are [actively examining whether existing EPFD limits remain adequate](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/communications-and-networks/articles/10.3389/frcmn.2026.1730921/full) for high-density orbital environments.

US adoption of a modern, performance-based framework positions American negotiators to advocate for similar updates at the international level from a position of demonstrated domestic practice. The alternative — maintaining a waiver-dependent 1990s regime — cedes the standard-setting initiative to regulatory models that may be more conservative, more protectionist, or simply less adapted to the realities of dense constellation operations. American satellite leadership is a strategic asset; the spectrum rules that govern it should reflect that.

## A Template Worth Replicating

The value of this order extends beyond the satellite industry. It is worth noting what the FCC is doing correctly, especially at a moment when Washington's impulse — across agencies and across parties — has been to reach for broader mandates, harder enforcement, and less deference toward regulated industries.

The satellite spectrum order does none of that. It identifies a rule calibrated to obsolete technological assumptions. It replaces that rule with an outcome-oriented standard that defines the harm to be prevented without prescribing the engineering means. It runs the process through a public proceeding with technical comment, and it votes.

That is not deregulation. It is regulatory modernization — the kind that produces net consumer gains because it is responsive to actual technological change rather than political pressure. Contrast it with sector-wide AI mandates drafted in advance of demonstrated harms, or divestiture remedies imposed without consumer welfare analysis. The difference in regulatory quality is stark.

Washington would do well to notice.
