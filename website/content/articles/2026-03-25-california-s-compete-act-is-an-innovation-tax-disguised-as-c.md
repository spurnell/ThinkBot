On March 23, 2026, Assembly Majority Leader Cecilia Aguiar-Curry introduced [California AB 1776](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260AB1776) — the Competition and Opportunity in Markets for a Prosperous, Equitable and Transparent Economy Act, or COMPETE Act. The bill's name is a triumph of branding. Its substance is something else entirely.

AB 1776 would rewrite California's Cartwright Act to impose single-firm antitrust liability without a market share threshold, allow plaintiffs to forgo proof of consumer harm in many cases, and explicitly instruct courts to disregard federal antitrust precedents that California's ideological vanguard finds inconvenient. It codifies the [California Law Revision Commission's unanimous recommendations](https://www.gibsondunn.com/california-law-revision-commission-proposes-far-reaching-reform-for-single-firm-conduct-law/) — proposals that attracted a unified wall of opposition from businesses representing nearly every sector of the California economy, and that the [Information Technology and Innovation Foundation warned](https://itif.org/publications/2026/01/13/comments-to-the-california-law-revision-commission-regarding-tentative-recommendation-antitrust-law-single-firm-conduct/) would "harm and upend the status quo" in ways that "chill consumer welfare and innovation."

The [California Chamber of Commerce called it an "extreme proposal."](https://advocacy.calchamber.com/2026/02/17/business-groups-oppose-undermining-of-californias-antitrust-law-in-ab-1776/) That characterization is, if anything, understated.

## The Consumer Welfare Standard Is a Feature, Not a Loophole

The core of AB 1776 is its departure from the consumer welfare standard — the organizing principle of American antitrust law since *Reiter v. Sonotone Corp.* (1979) and the framework the Supreme Court reaffirmed in [*Ohio v. American Express Co.*, 585 U.S. ___ (2018)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/585/16-1454/). Under that standard, the relevant question is whether challenged conduct harms consumers — through higher prices, reduced output, diminished quality, or suppressed innovation. Harm to a competitor alone is not actionable.

AB 1776 severs that connection. By eliminating the requirement that plaintiffs demonstrate the defendant possesses market power above the threshold recognized under Section 2 of the Sherman Act, and by permitting courts to disregard federal doctrines deemed unhelpful, the bill invites condemnation of competitive conduct — aggressive pricing, exclusive dealing, vertical integration — without ever asking whether consumers were worse off.

This is not modernizing antitrust law. It is politicizing it. As Justice Scalia wrote in [*Verizon Communications Inc. v. Law Offices of Curtis V. Trinko*, 540 U.S. 398 (2004)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/540/398/), the antitrust laws were designed to protect competition, "not to prevent businesses from gaining the upper hand by superior skill, foresight, and industry." Remove the consumer welfare anchor, and vague references to "exclusionary conduct" and harm to "the competitive process" become a blank enforcement check — available to challenge any business practice someone finds politically objectionable.

## California Is Building the EU's DMA on American Soil

AB 1776's intellectual lineage is unmistakable. The bill follows the same ex-ante, firm-size-agnostic logic as the [EU's Digital Markets Act](https://digital-markets-act.ec.europa.eu/about-dma_en), which designated gatekeepers and imposed conduct obligations without requiring proof of anticompetitive harm in any specific market. California's COMPETE Act takes the same posture: if a firm is large enough to be sued, it is large enough to be liable, regardless of consumer harm.

The DMA's early enforcement record should give California's legislature pause. The Commission's [investigations into Apple, Google, and Meta](https://www.winston.com/en/blogs-and-podcasts/competition-corner/early-assessment-of-the-digital-markets-act1) have generated compliance complexity and legal uncertainty without demonstrable consumer benefits. What they have done is create an expensive regulatory apparatus that smaller competitors — who lack dedicated compliance teams — cannot easily replicate. Regulation that purports to help competitors often cements incumbents who can afford the overhead.

The story also does not end at the California border. [As legal scholars have documented](https://harvardlawreview.org/print/vol-133/antitrust-federalism-preemption-and-judge-made-law/), when a dominant-economy state adopts expansive liability rules, national companies face a choice: comply with the most restrictive state's standards everywhere, or absorb the cost of state-by-state legal fragmentation. New York and Connecticut followed California's lead on algorithmic pricing legislation after AB 325. What California enacts, others emulate — producing a patchwork of incompatible antitrust regimes that harms scaling startups far more than it inconveniences the large platforms it targets.

## Expanding Liability Without Evidence of Harm

Proponents argue that federal antitrust enforcement has "failed" consumers, justifying state-level intervention. The claim does not survive scrutiny.

Federal enforcement has been robust. The DOJ and FTC [took 32 merger enforcement actions in FY2024 alone](https://www.arnoldporter.com/en/perspectives/publications/2026/01/antitrust-agency-insights-fourth-quarter-2025). The DOJ [prevailed against Google in two separate antitrust trials](https://globalcompetitionreview.com/review/the-antitrust-review-of-the-americas/2026/article/big-tech-remains-top-priority-doj-and-ftc-in-us-antitrust-litigation) — search and advertising technology — with remedies proceedings underway. The contention that federal law has left consumers unprotected is simply not borne out by the enforcement record.

More fundamentally, AB 1776's proponents have offered no empirical evidence that California consumers have been harmed by the absence of stronger single-firm liability under the Cartwright Act. No consumer price data. No market concentration studies. No output analysis. The California Chamber [pointedly noted](https://advocacy.calchamber.com/2026/02/17/business-groups-oppose-undermining-of-californias-antitrust-law-in-ab-1776/) that the Law Revision Commission conducted no economic analysis to validate its recommendations. Three years of expert study produced a legislative proposal without a single market-level demonstration of the consumer harm it purports to cure.

Antitrust litigation imposed without proof of harm is not consumer protection. It is, as the [U.S. Chamber assessed the broader neo-Brandeisian program](https://www.uschamber.com/assets/documents/White-Paper-Assessing-the-Impact-of-the-Neo-Brandeisian-Movement_2025-03-31-151816_odwz.pdf), an exercise in using competition law as an instrument of economic redistribution.

## An Innovation Tax on the Startups California Claims to Protect

AB 1776's most corrosive feature is what it does to legal predictability. The bill removes that predictability in three ways.

First, by eliminating market share thresholds, it makes virtually any successful firm a potential defendant. A startup capturing 15% of a regional market through better technology and lower prices could face exclusionary conduct claims — because the bill does not require the plaintiff to show market power.

Second, by authorizing courts to discard federal precedents — including the recoupment requirement from [*Brooke Group Ltd. v. Brown & Williamson Tobacco Corp.*, 509 U.S. 209 (1993)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/509/209/) and the voluntary dealing requirement from *Trinko* — it creates doctrinal uncertainty with no floor. What must a California antitrust defendant prove? Courts will take years to answer.

Third, by inviting treble-damages litigation under more plaintiff-friendly pleading standards, the bill will generate substantial rent-seeking — claims filed not because consumers were harmed, but because the potential damages award makes litigation economically rational for plaintiffs' counsel. The [Duane Morris analysis of AB 1776](https://www.duanemorris.com/alerts/californias_ab_1776_would_significantly_expand_state_antitrust_law_0326.html) specifically warns of "an increased likelihood of investigation or enforcement action by state authorities, even where federal enforcement has been limited or declined."

That litigation tax falls hardest on growth-stage companies. Established platforms with antitrust compliance departments can absorb it. Venture-backed startups cannot.

## What Congress Should Do

The durable answer is federal action to clarify antitrust preemption — statutory language making clear that state laws governing single-firm conduct in interstate commerce must remain tethered to consumer harm standards. That would preserve legitimate state enforcement authority while preventing any single state from imposing its ideological preferences on the national economy.

In the meantime, the California legislature should answer a simple question before advancing AB 1776: where is the evidence that California consumers are being harmed? If the answer is "we'll find it after we change the law," that is not competition policy. It is enforcement looking for a theory. The businesses — large and small — that make California the world's fifth-largest economy deserve better than that.
