When a bill attracts the co-sponsorship of Josh Hawley *and* Amy Klobuchar, Lindsey Graham *and* Dick Durbin, Chuck Grassley *and* Sheldon Whitehouse, the instinct in Washington is to call it a bipartisan achievement. On Section 230, that instinct is exactly backwards. The breadth of the coalition behind [S.3546, the Sunset Section 230 Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/3546/text), isn't evidence that Congress has found the right answer. It's evidence that the coalition is held together by incompatible grievances that no single liability regime can satisfy — and that the resulting law, if it passes, will produce the worst possible outcome for online speech.

Introduced in December 2025 by [Senators Graham and Durbin](https://www.durbin.senate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/durbin-graham-introduce-bill-to-sunset-section-230-immunity-for-tech-companies-protect-americans-online) and quickly joined by [Grassley and a roster of ideological adversaries](https://www.grassley.senate.gov/news/news-releases/grassley-graham-bipartisan-colleagues-introduce-legislation-to-sunset-section-230-protect-americans-online), the bill would repeal 47 U.S.C. § 230 two years after enactment — with no replacement framework in place. The [March 18, 2026 Senate Commerce Committee hearing](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2026/3/liability-or-deniability-platform-power-as-section-230-turns-30), titled "Liability or Deniability? Platform Power as Section 230 Turns 30," injected fresh momentum into the effort. With the 2026 midterm clock ticking, a sunset deadline is now a live possibility.

The stakes demand clarity: Section 230 repeal without a workable replacement is not accountability. It is legislative malpractice.

## The Coalition's Fatal Internal Contradiction

To understand why the Sunset Section 230 Act is bad policy regardless of its bipartisan pedigree, start with what its co-sponsors actually want — and notice that they want mutually exclusive things.

Democrats supporting the sunset want platforms held *liable for failing to remove* harmful content. The core grievance on the left is that platforms allow too much to remain online — harassment, health misinformation, radicalization content, election interference. Their theory of liability is one of omission: platforms knew about the harm and did nothing.

Republicans supporting the sunset want platforms held *liable for removing too much*. The core grievance on the right is that platforms suppress conservative speech — that content moderation is discriminatory editorial policy dressed up as neutral enforcement. Their theory of liability is one of commission: platforms acted, and acted selectively.

These are not reconcilable positions. No legal regime can simultaneously punish platforms for *over-removal* and for *under-removal* without creating paralyzing legal uncertainty. Any bill that clears a Senate cloakroom will impose liability standards so ambiguous, so untethered to a coherent theory of platform behavior, that no general counsel at any platform will permit anything contested to stay up. The "compromise" between these factions doesn't split the difference — it combines both incentives into a liability fog so thick that blanket removal becomes the only rational response.

Senator Cruz — who chairs the Commerce Committee and convened the March hearing — has himself acknowledged the paradox. [His framing at the hearing](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2026/3/cruz-is-it-time-to-reform-section-230) reflects a senator who understands that removing liability immunity drives over-removal, which is precisely what the Republican case against Section 230 claims to oppose. The right that has spent years arguing platforms censor too much is now advancing legislation whose most predictable effect is to make platforms censor dramatically more. That is not reform. It is a legislative strategy that directly contradicts the coalition's own stated purpose.

## The Liability Paradox: Tort Exposure Produces More Censorship, Not Less

The mechanism is not complicated, and it explains why repeal produces the opposite of what its supporters want.

Under current law, platforms that host user-generated content are not treated as publishers of that content. Section 230's core immunity provision means that a platform is not civilly liable for what its users post. This does *not* mean platforms are blameless — it means tort law is not the instrument that governs their editorial choices. Platforms can and do face liability for their *own* conduct: under the FTC Act, under state consumer protection law, under FOSTA-SESTA for sex trafficking content, under NCMEC reporting requirements for CSAM.

Remove the immunity, and the calculus for every legal team in Silicon Valley shifts dramatically. The question changes from "does this content violate our policies?" to "does hosting this content expose us to a lawsuit?" Those are different questions with different answers. [Legal scholars and platform policy experts consistently find](https://theconversation.com/law-that-built-the-internet-turns-30-a-legal-expert-explains-what-would-happen-if-efforts-to-repeal-section-230-succeed-253326) that platforms facing tort exposure for third-party content will over-moderate aggressively to minimize legal risk — removing anything that could conceivably form the basis of a claim, regardless of its speech value. MIT management professor Sinan Aral has put it plainly: if Section 230 is repealed, "either platforms will decide they don't want to moderate anything, or platforms will moderate everything." Neither outcome is what reformers claim to want.

[NetChoice's analysis of the first thirty years of Section 230](https://netchoice.org/30-years-of-230-censorship-by-lawsuit-threatens-the-internet-section-230-is-the-firewall/) describes the mechanism as "censorship by lawsuit" — and that framing is exactly right. Weaponized liability doesn't target bad actors. It targets contested speech, because contested speech is the speech most likely to attract litigation. The marginal voices, the political dissent, the communities already vulnerable to removal — legal risk falls hardest on the content that established interests most want gone. Republican critics of content moderation who vote for S.3546 will have handed Democrats a legal instrument to amplify the very censorship they claim to oppose.

The [*Reno v. ACLU* (1997)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/521/844/) Court understood this dynamic in a different context: it struck down the CDA's indecency provisions precisely because attempting to protect minors by regulating internet speech "effectively suppresses a large amount of speech that adults have a constitutional right to receive." The lesson — that overbroad legal instruments chill protected speech well beyond their stated targets — applies with equal force to broad platform liability.

## Thirty Years of Infrastructure, Not Immunity

Section 230's detractors treat it as a loophole — a corporate subsidy that insulates platforms from accountability. This framing is historically backwards and analytically incoherent.

[The statute was enacted in 1996](https://law.stanford.edu/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/A-Juridical-History-of-Section-230.pdf) by Representatives Cox and Wyden as an explicit response to *Stratton Oakmont v. Prodigy*, a case in which a platform faced defamation liability for attempting to moderate its forums — the theory being that moderation implied editorial control, which implied publisher liability. Section 230 corrected this perverse incentive: it ensured that platforms that *tried* to moderate content couldn't be punished for moderating imperfectly. The immunity wasn't designed to protect bad actors from accountability. It was designed to make moderation itself viable.

The results speak for themselves. The United States hosts virtually all of the world's dominant internet platforms — search, social media, messaging, e-commerce, video, cloud infrastructure. Section 230's permissionless liability rules did not guarantee this outcome, but they were part of the legal architecture that made it possible. The EU's comparable liability framework — which never included anything like Section 230's breadth — produced no equivalent ecosystem. That is not a coincidence.

[The University of Chicago Law Review's analysis of Section 230's first thirty years](https://lawreview.uchicago.edu/online-archive/uproot-or-upgrade-revisiting-section-230-immunity-digital-age) is careful to note the genuine policy tensions the law creates. Those tensions are real: the status quo is not beyond criticism, and Section 230's interaction with algorithmic amplification raises legitimate questions that deserve serious analysis. But the appropriate response to genuine policy tensions is targeted legislation — not demolition of the entire foundation.

## The EU Digital Services Act Is Not a Model. It's a Warning.

Proponents of Section 230 repeal sometimes point to the EU's Digital Services Act as evidence that robust platform regulation can coexist with a functioning internet ecosystem. This reading of the DSA is dangerously optimistic.

The DSA imposes tiered compliance obligations on platforms, with the heaviest requirements falling on "very large online platforms" and "very large online search engines." On paper, the framework is carefully calibrated. In practice, [ITIF's February 2026 analysis](https://itif.org/publications/2026/02/06/dsa-puts-unfair-target-american-tech/) documents how the DSA has placed an asymmetric regulatory burden on American technology companies, creating compliance costs that function as a competitive tax. The European Commission had launched 14 investigations into DSA compliance by November 2025 — a regulatory posture more suggestive of adversarial enforcement than calibrated accountability.

[ADF International's assessment of the DSA's speech effects](https://adfinternational.org/commentary/eu-digital-services-act-one-year) documents real chilling effects on legal expression. Platforms facing enforcement risk in European markets have taken down content they would have left up under a purely First Amendment-governed regime. That is not a feature of the DSA. It is a documented cost.

Most critically, the DSA hasn't produced greater accountability — it has produced compliance bureaucracies. Large incumbents with extensive legal teams can absorb DSA overhead; smaller competitors cannot. Congress should consider the DSA for what it actually is: a cautionary tale about what complex platform liability frameworks generate in practice. The answer is not accountability. The answer is compliance departments, market exit, and entrenched incumbents.

## Targeted Fixes Already Exist. Use Them.

The repeal advocates' core assertion is that the specific harms they care about — CSAM, algorithmic amplification of harmful content, child exploitation, government-compelled speech suppression — cannot be addressed without gutting Section 230's immunity architecture. The legislative record says otherwise.

Congress has already modified Section 230 for targeted purposes. FOSTA-SESTA created express carve-outs from immunity for sex trafficking content, with criminal penalties and civil liability for knowing facilitation. The EFF has documented [FOSTA-SESTA's substantial collateral harms to legal speech](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2022/09/fight-overturn-fosta-unconstitutional-internet-censorship-law-continues) — the law's overbreadth drove platforms to remove legal content involving sexuality, LGBTQ communities, and harm-reduction resources that had nothing to do with trafficking. FOSTA-SESTA is not a model to replicate wholesale. But it demonstrates the principle: targeted carve-outs for specific, demonstrable harms can modify Section 230 without repealing the foundational architecture.

The actual free-expression problem facing the internet in 2026 is not insufficient platform liability. It is government jawboning — the use of executive branch pressure campaigns to coerce platforms into suppressing protected speech. The Supreme Court's [*Murthy v. Missouri* decision (2024)](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/23pdf/23-411_3dq3.pdf) dismissed the Biden-era jawboning case on standing grounds, [leaving unresolved the critical First Amendment question](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/07/supreme-court-dodges-key-question-murthy-v-missouri-and-dismisses-case-failing) of when government communications with platforms cross the line into unconstitutional coercion. Congress has been slow to fill the gap.

Senator Cruz's forthcoming [JAWBONE Act](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/press/rep/release/sen-cruz-we-must-better-protect-americans-from-government-jawboning/) — targeting government pressure campaigns on platforms — addresses the actual mechanism of state-compelled speech suppression. [Punchbowl News has reported](https://punchbowl.news/article/tech/cruz-jawboning-bill/) Cruz's intent to give Americans a right of action when government coercion results in the removal of their speech. That is the right instrument for the right problem. A legislative framework that creates transparency around government-platform communications and provides individual standing when coercive contact causes identifiable speech harm does something specific and constitutionally coherent. It is vastly more useful than a sunset provision attached to nothing.

## Who Actually Gets Hurt

The framing of Section 230 repeal as an accountability measure targeting large, powerful platforms is politically effective and analytically misleading.

Incumbent platforms — Meta, Google, Amazon, X — have legal teams capable of navigating regulatory uncertainty. They have the compliance infrastructure to manage an ambiguous liability environment. They can survive a world without Section 230, though at significant cost to their users and to the diversity of content they host. Who cannot survive that world is everyone else.

The startups that depend on user-generated content without the resources to litigate every third-party post. The open-source platforms and forums that operate on minimal budgets. The smaller publishers and community spaces that would face ruinous exposure for content they lack the capacity to comprehensively review. The two-year sunset window of S.3546 does not create policy space for a thoughtful replacement framework to emerge. It creates a litigation bonanza. The incumbents will navigate it; the next generation of internet platforms will not form.

The [*Packingham v. North Carolina* (2017)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/15-1194) Court described social media as "the modern public square" — a space essential to "the legitimate exercise of political speech." Policies that eliminate the legal conditions for that square to exist do not produce accountability. They produce concentration, incumbency, and impoverishment of public discourse.

## Conclusion

Bipartisan consensus, when it rests on irreconcilable foundations, is not a reason to move legislation forward. It is a reason to ask harder questions about what the legislation actually does. The Sunset Section 230 Act cannot simultaneously satisfy the Democratic demand for liability over failing to remove and the Republican demand for liability over removing too much. The "compromise" will be legal fog — and legal fog produces over-removal, not accountability.

Thirty years of internet architecture rest on the foundational immunity of [47 U.S.C. § 230](https://www.eff.org/issues/cda230). The EU's DSA offers a live experiment in what platform regulation looks like without it: compliance bureaucracies, incumbent entrenchment, and documented chilling effects on legal speech. The specific harms that motivate this coalition — child exploitation, government-compelled censorship, algorithmic amplification — are addressable through targeted, constitutionally coherent legislation that does not require dismantling the open internet to achieve results.

Congress should pass the JAWBONE Act. Congress should enforce existing CSAM reporting obligations with teeth. Congress should fund the FTC's capacity to pursue Section 5 deception claims against platforms that misrepresent their moderation practices. Congress should, if it chooses, mandate transparency about algorithmic amplification without dictating its outcomes.

What Congress should not do is set a two-year clock on the legal architecture of the American internet with no replacement framework, no constitutional theory, and no policy precision — on the theory that because the coalition is wide, the policy must be right.

The coalition's width tells you how many people are angry. It tells you nothing about whether their anger points in the same direction. On Section 230, it doesn't. And the open internet will pay the price if Congress mistakes political momentum for policy analysis.
