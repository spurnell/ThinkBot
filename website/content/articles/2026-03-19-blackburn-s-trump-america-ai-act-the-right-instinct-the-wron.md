# Blackburn's TRUMP AMERICA AI Act: The Right Instinct, the Wrong Bill

Senator Marsha Blackburn released a nearly 300-page discussion draft this week for the [TRUMP AMERICA AI Act](https://www.blackburn.senate.gov/2026/3/technology/blackburn-releases-discussion-draft-of-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence/3b3b6458-b6c7-478b-9859-374949586765) — formally, the "Republic Unifying Meritocratic Performance Advancing Machine Intelligence by Eliminating Regulatory Interstate Chaos Across American Industry Act" — making it both the most ambitious AI governance proposal yet to emerge from Congress and, unfortunately, one of the most legislatively precarious.

The draft's core ambition is correct. A statutory federal AI framework that displaces the metastasizing patchwork of state laws is the right vehicle for AI governance in 2026. The instinct behind the bill — that 50 conflicting regulatory regimes are worse than one clear federal standard — is sound policy and good economics. But buried in Blackburn's framework is a Section 230 sunset provision that transforms a focused AI bill into a platform liability grenade, threatening to either sink the effort entirely or, worse, produce a hybrid monstrosity that gets both AI governance and internet liability law badly wrong.

## The Case for Federal Preemption Is Overwhelming

Start with what Blackburn gets right, because critics will not give it fair credit.

The [99-1 Senate vote last July](https://time.com/7299044/senators-reject-10-year-ban-on-state-level-ai-regulation-in-blow-to-big-tech/) stripping a 10-year moratorium on state AI laws from the reconciliation package was a political moment, not a policy statement. It said Congress won't hand AI companies a decade of insulation from accountability — it did not say fragmented state regulation is sound. Those are different propositions, and conflating them has produced exactly the muddled situation we now face.

[Trump's December 2025 executive order](https://www.mondaq.com/unitedstates/new-technology/1755166/march-2026-federal-deadlines-that-will-reshape-the-ai-regulatory-landscape) directing agencies to challenge state AI laws and conditioning $21 billion in BEAD broadband funding on state compliance is legally fragile. Executive orders don't preempt state law — statutes do. Blackburn's fundamental insight is constitutionally correct: proper preemption requires legislation.

The economic stakes of uniformity are real and routinely understated. California's SB 1047 debate, Colorado's algorithmic accountability framework, New York's automated employment decision tool law, Virginia's proposed AI standards — each imposes a distinct compliance architecture. For a frontier AI lab with hundreds of lawyers, navigating this patchwork is expensive but manageable. For a Series B startup deploying a workflow automation tool, it is an existential compliance burden. Federal preemption's primary beneficiaries are smaller companies and open-source developers who lack the legal departments to track 50 moving targets.

The [FTC's March 11 policy statement](https://regulations.ai/regulations/RAI-US-NA-ENFORCE-2026) — issued under the executive order deadline — signaled a preference for existing consumer protection authority over new AI-specific mandates. That is the right baseline. The TRUMP AMERICA AI Act's core preemption architecture moves in the same direction.

## The Section 230 Poison Pill

Blackburn's draft sunsets Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act two years after enactment, folding in Sen. Lindsey Graham's longstanding reform legislation. This is not a modest tweak. Section 230 is the liability architecture that enables essentially all user-generated content platforms to operate at scale. Sunsetting it without a replacement framework creates a liability cliff affecting every social media platform, every review site, every forum, every comment section on the internet — and every AI system that processes user-generated content.

Bundling this into an AI bill is a strategic and substantive error on multiple dimensions.

**Strategically**, it is a poison pill. Section 230 reform has been debated in Congress for nearly a decade without resolution because it scrambles normal partisan coalitions. Conservatives want platforms penalized for alleged anti-conservative bias; progressives want liability for harms to children; libertarians want to preserve existing immunity. There is no stable majority for any specific reform. Attaching a sunset to an AI bill means the framework inherits all of that unresolved toxicity.

**Substantively**, the combination is incoherent. AI governance questions — foundation model liability, disclosure requirements for high-risk deployments, treatment of AI-generated content — are genuinely distinct from the platform hosting questions Section 230 addresses. Conflating them produces bad law for both. As the [Center for Data Innovation has observed](https://datainnovation.org/2026/03/blackburns-discussion-draft-is-not-a-serious-starting-point-for-a-federal-ai-framework/), the draft "forces together weak ideas to advance a political agenda rather than meaningfully solving a policy problem."

The constructive position is simple: Section 230 reform deserves dedicated legislation, full committee deliberation, and its own political reckoning — not a footnote in an AI bill.

## The Enforcement Architecture Needs Work

The draft grants the FTC and state attorneys general enforcement authority over the bill's copyright and creator protection provisions. This deserves scrutiny.

The FTC is administratively capable, but its ideological character swings sharply across administrations. Biden's FTC was aggressively expansionist; the current commission is more restrained. Granting durable statutory enforcement authority to an agency whose posture can flip every four years creates regulatory uncertainty that is itself an innovation tax.

State AG enforcement compounds the problem. Fifty state attorneys general with concurrent jurisdiction over a federal AI framework is not uniformity — it is preemption in name only. A cleaner model would vest primary enforcement in a single federal agency with a clear statutory mandate and meaningful judicial review. The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence), codified through the draft's Center for AI Standards and Innovation, provides the right voluntary baseline to build from.

## The Training Data Question Should Travel Separately

The draft's declaration that AI training on copyrighted works is categorically *not* fair use would be, if enacted, the most significant change to copyright law in decades — and it arrives as a subsection of an AI bill. The [ongoing litigation](https://rollcall.com/2026/03/19/ai-draft-bill-would-revamp-online-landscape/) in cases like *NYT v. OpenAI* should inform this question. Congress legislating categorical rules before those cases are resolved risks codifying the wrong answer. The fair use question for AI training is genuinely hard and deserves its own process through the Judiciary Committee.

## What Congress Should Do

The path forward is not complicated, even if it is politically difficult.

**Preserve the preemption architecture.** A clean federal AI framework displacing the state patchwork is the right vehicle. That core must survive.

**Strip Section 230.** Remove the sunset provision entirely. If Blackburn believes Section 230 reform is urgent — and there are legitimate arguments that it is — introduce it separately and let it stand or fall on its own merits.

**Narrow enforcement.** Designate a single federal agency with clear statutory authority and constrain concurrent state enforcement.

**Separate the copyright question.** Training data fair use should run through the Judiciary Committee, not be resolved in a technology bill.

**Codify NIST, not mandates.** The NIST and NAIRR provisions are the bill's strongest elements — voluntary standards, research infrastructure, international coordination. Build the framework around those.

Blackburn described the draft as a "guideline" and a starting point. [The White House signaled](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/18/blackburn-updated-ai-plan-trump) it is watching with interest. That means there is still time to shape this into something workable — but discussion drafts move fast, commentary periods close, and bad provisions have a way of surviving into final text once a bill acquires momentum.

The federal uniformity instinct embedded in the TRUMP AMERICA AI Act is correct. The execution, in its current form, is not. Congress should fix the bill — not abandon it.
