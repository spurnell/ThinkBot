Two fact patterns, seven months apart, involving officials from opposite ends of the political spectrum, illustrate the same underlying problem. In September 2025, FCC Chairman Brendan Carr told broadcasters that Disney and ABC affiliates could "do this the easy way or the hard way" over Jimmy Kimmel's on-air comments about the killing of Charlie Kirk — and warned there would be "additional work for the FCC ahead" if networks didn't act. [Nexstar, which had a merger pending before the FCC, pulled Kimmel off the air within hours](https://www.cnn.com/2025/09/18/media/brendan-carr-jimmy-kimmel-fcc-first-amendment); Disney suspended the show soon after. Two weeks later, on a different subject, the Department of Justice under Attorney General Pam Bondi began [pressuring Apple and Meta to remove apps and groups that tracked ICE enforcement activity](https://www.fire.org/news/fire-sues-bondi-noem-censoring-facebook-group-and-app-reporting-ice-activity) — starting with the ICEBlock app, which had grown to [more than 1.14 million users](https://slate.com/technology/2025/12/iceblock-app-lawsuit-pam-bondi-trump-joshua-aaron-ice.html) before Apple pulled it in early October, and continuing weeks later with a nearly 100,000-member Facebook group that Meta disabled after the same DOJ outreach. In April 2026, a federal judge in *Rosado v. Bondi* found the plaintiffs' free-speech injuries were ["likely traceable to government-coerced enforcement"](https://reason.com/2026/04/20/judge-says-doj-and-dhs-likely-coerced-tech-firms-to-censor-ice-tracking-platforms/) — a companion case, *Aaron v. Bondi*, is proceeding on the same theory.

Different targets, different administrations' officials, same mechanism: a government official with regulatory leverage tells a speech intermediary — a broadcaster, a platform, an app store — what to do about a category of speech, and the intermediary complies not because it agrees but because it fears the consequences of saying no. That mechanism has a name in First Amendment law — jawboning — and as of last year, it has a unanimous Supreme Court holding condemning it. What it has never had is a workable remedy. The bipartisan [JAWBONE Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/4749), introduced by Senate Commerce Chairman Ted Cruz and Senator Ron Wyden in June 2026, is the first serious legislative attempt to fix that gap. It deserves ThinkBot's support, and it deserves a markup.

## Vullo settled the constitutional question — not the practical one

The Supreme Court's decision in *[NRA v. Vullo](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/23pdf/22-842_6kg7.pdf)* should have ended the debate over whether informal government pressure on intermediaries can violate the First Amendment. Writing for a unanimous Court, Justice Sotomayor held that New York's financial regulator crossed a constitutional line when it leaned on banks and insurers to cut ties with the NRA after Parkland — not by passing a rule, but by conveying, in the Court's words, threats of adverse regulatory action "in order to punish or suppress" disfavored speech. The Court wasn't announcing new doctrine; it was reaffirming a line of cases going back to *[Bantam Books v. Sullivan](https://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/372/58)* (1963). Coercion by insinuation is still coercion.

What *Vullo* did not do is give the next victim a fast or reliable way to vindicate that right. The NRA's case took the better part of a decade and produced only the prospect of injunctive relief and a remand — cold comfort to a broadcaster whose affiliate deal is unwinding in real time, or an app developer whose product is delisted before a court can even hear the motion. Government officials who understand this asymmetry have every incentive to keep jawboning: the speech gets suppressed on the timeline that matters (now), and the legal consequences, if any, arrive on a timeline that doesn't (years later, against an office the offending official may no longer hold). *Vullo* fixed the doctrine. It left the deterrence gap wide open.

## What JAWBONE actually does

The JAWBONE Act — formally the Justice Against Weaponized Bureaucratic Overreach to Networked Expression Act — closes that gap with two mechanisms, and it's worth being precise about both because they do different work.

**A private right of action with money damages.** The bill creates a federal cause of action against government agencies and employees who coerce, or attempt to coerce, social media platforms, broadcast licensees, and AI systems into adding, altering, or removing lawful speech. Critically, according to the [bill text and section-by-section summary](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/wp-content/uploads/2026/06/JAWBONE-Act-FINAL.pdf), the cause of action attaches whether or not the coercion succeeds — the wrong is the threat, not just its result — and plaintiffs can recover compensatory damages, injunctive relief, and attorney's fees. That last piece matters more than it sounds: injunctive-relief-only regimes are effectively unavailable to individual posters, small broadcasters, and app developers who can't front years of litigation costs against the federal government for a non-monetary win. Damages change the incentive calculus on both sides of the transaction — for the official deciding whether to make the call, and for the lawyer deciding whether to take the case.

**A disclosure and logging requirement.** Agencies and employees have to log summaries of covered communications with the platforms, broadcasters, and AI companies the bill covers, in a public-facing portal. This is, in our view, the more consequential half of the bill, and the one that deserves the most attention as it moves. Damages suits compensate victims after the fact; disclosure changes officials' behavior before the fact, because coercion depends on its own invisibility. A phone call leaves no paper trail the way a subpoena does, and it is precisely that informality that lets it evade the procedural and public accountability formal government action carries.

A mandatory log doesn't just create evidence for future lawsuits; it makes officials do the coercion, if they're going to do it, on the record. That is a much cheaper and faster deterrent than litigation, and it's the provision Senate Commerce staff, First Amendment litigators, and reporters covering FCC and DOJ conduct should watch first once the bill's disclosure portal — if enacted — starts populating. The first several months of logged entries will tell us more about whether jawboning is actually declining than any early damages verdict will.

The bill has drawn support across an unusually wide ideological range for a speech bill in this Congress — [FIRE, the ACLU, and the Knight First Amendment Institute](https://knightcolumbia.org/blog/knight-institute-endorses-bipartisan-bill-to-protect-against-government-jawboning) on one side, [Cato and AEI](https://www.aei.org/technology-and-innovation/the-jawbone-act-a-sensible-start-for-addressing-coercive-government-censorship/) on the other, with the [Center for Democracy and Technology](https://cdt.org/insights/cdt-endorses-the-cruz-wyden-jawbone-act/) and [R Street](https://www.rstreet.org/commentary/common-ground-on-censorship-two-bills-to-check-government-coercion-of-speech/) also on record in favor. That range is itself informative. Bills that regulate what platforms must publish or remove reliably split along predictable ideological lines, because they're really fights about whose speech wins. A bill that instead constrains what *government* may do to *any* intermediary — left-coded platforms pressured over misinformation, right-coded broadcasters pressured over on-air commentary, immigrant-rights apps pressured over ICE-sighting content — doesn't have a natural ideological camp, because the tool being restricted has been used by officials of both parties and will be again.

## Why the conduct/content distinction is the whole ballgame

It is worth dwelling on what JAWBONE does *not* do, because the distinction is what makes this bill compatible with a strong free-expression baseline in a way that most content-moderation legislation isn't. [KOSA](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/1748) imposes a duty of care on platforms' design choices. The [NO FAKES Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/1367) creates a new liability regime around what platforms must take down. Both of those bills — whatever their individual merits — regulate the *platform's* speech and editorial output.

JAWBONE regulates the *government's* conduct toward the platform. It doesn't tell Meta, Apple, or a local ABC affiliate what content policy to run; it tells the FCC chairman and the Attorney General what pressure tactics they may not use to get platforms to run one. That is a deregulatory bill wearing a regulatory bill's clothing: its entire effect is to strip government of an informal, extralegal lever over private editorial judgment, restoring rather than displacing the platform's own First Amendment-protected discretion recognized in the Supreme Court's *[Moody v. NetChoice](https://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/22-277)* framework. A world where JAWBONE works as intended is a world with *less* government influence over what stays up and what comes down — precisely the deregulatory direction ThinkBot wants speech policy to move in, not a new front in the same fight.

## The drafting risk Senate Commerce needs to close before markup

None of that means the bill is finished. The hardest problem in any jawboning statute is drawing a workable line between illegitimate coercion and legitimate government speech, and it is a genuinely hard problem, not a rhetorical one. Government agencies have entirely legitimate reasons to talk to platforms: warning about a coordinated fraud campaign, transmitting a foreign-influence takedown request backed by actual legal process, flagging a pattern of disinformation-for-hire the platform's own trust-and-safety team hasn't caught. If the coercion trigger is drawn too loosely — if any regulatory inquiry, public criticism, or unwelcome warning can now support a damages suit — agencies have an obvious and rational response: stop talking to platforms altogether.

That has costs of its own. Some of the pressure campaigns platforms most want early warning about (foreign election interference, CSAM networks, scam operations) depend on exactly the kind of informal, fast government-to-platform communication a badly drawn coercion standard would freeze.

The bill's current text tries to manage this with a multi-factor coercion test and carve-outs for law-enforcement process and government-as-speaker activity, an approach several reviewers have credited as [a reasonable starting balance](https://www.cato.org/blog/jawbone-act-right-track-stop-government-coercion) that puts the burden on the government to show its conduct was lawful rather than on the speaker to prove it wasn't. But the underlying tension the Cato analysis flags is real and won't fully resolve on the page: an agency with licensing authority or investigative power can make what reads as a polite suggestion carry an implicit threat simply because of who is speaking, and a test built around explicit threats risks giving that agency an easy way to keep every future Carr-style phone call just vague enough to stay outside the statute. Senate Commerce staff should use markup to pressure-test the coercion definition against both failure modes explicitly — walk through a Carr-to-broadcaster call and a DOJ-to-Apple call under the proposed language, and separately walk through a garden-variety CISA fraud advisory or an NCMEC-referral communication, and confirm the text reliably sorts the first pair as actionable and the second pair as protected. If it doesn't yet, that's a fixable drafting problem, not a reason to shelve the bill.

## Move it

The JAWBONE Act sits in the rare category of proposed speech legislation that ThinkBot can endorse without qualification on structure, because it regulates the coercer rather than the platform. Its two-part remedy — damages for victims, disclosure for prevention — corrects exactly the gap *Vullo* left open: a constitutional violation everyone now agrees exists, with no realistic way for most victims to vindicate it. The Carr/Kimmel and Bondi/ICE episodes are seven months apart, involve different agencies and different targets, and prove the point better than either alone could: this is not one party's tool, which is exactly why a durable statutory remedy — one that outlasts whoever holds the coercive levers in any given administration — is the right institutional response rather than a partisan one. With no Senate Commerce markup yet scheduled as of this writing, the bill risks the fate of most well-intentioned, no-natural-lobby legislation: quiet death by inattention. Committee staff should tighten the coercion standard, and then move it.
