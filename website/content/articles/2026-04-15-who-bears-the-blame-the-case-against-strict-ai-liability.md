Two pieces of legislation now circulating on Capitol Hill share a common premise: that the right way to hold AI accountable is to treat it like a toaster. The [AI LEAD Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2937/text) (S.2937), introduced by Senators Dick Durbin and Josh Hawley in September 2025, creates a federal cause of action allowing victims to sue AI developers under a strict products liability standard — meaning liability attaches "notwithstanding that the developer exercised all possible care." Sen. Marsha Blackburn's [Trump America AI Act discussion draft](https://www.blackburn.senate.gov/2026/3/technology/blackburn-releases-discussion-draft-of-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence/3b3b6458-b6c7-478b-9859-374949586765), released March 18, 2026, incorporates that same framework into a nearly 300-page federal AI architecture that also repeals Section 230 and imposes new deployer obligations.

With a Senate Judiciary markup window now open and the Blackburn draft actively circulating for comment, this is no longer a theoretical debate. It is a live legislative decision that will set the liability architecture for the entire AI industry. And both bills are building that architecture on a foundation that is doctrinally incoherent, practically unworkable, and structurally designed — however unintentionally — to protect the largest incumbents in the market.

Congress should replace the strict liability theory with a federal duty-of-care negligence standard tied to published safety benchmarks, paired with a safe harbor for compliant developers and a liability shield for open-weight model releases. That framework holds negligent actors accountable, rewards genuine safety investment, and avoids handing the most powerful AI companies the moat they never could have built on their own.

## What "Strict Liability" Actually Means — and Why It Fails for AI

Products liability law, as codified in the [Restatement (Third) of Torts § 2](https://opencasebook.org/casebooks/9486-torts-basic-fluency-in-a-fundamental-legal-language-revised/resources/6.2.1-restatement-third-of-products-liability-section-1-2-on-classes-of-product-defects/), is built on the concept of a *defect*: a physical departure from the product's intended design, or a foreseeable design risk that reasonable alternative precautions could have reduced. Strict liability for manufacturing defects was developed precisely because contaminated drugs, faulty brake assemblies, and structurally compromised ladders all share a critical characteristic — they fail in a way that departs from what the manufacturer intended. The defect is discrete. It is identifiable. It is distinguishable from the product functioning correctly.

AI systems do not work this way. Large language models are probabilistic inference engines. They generate outputs by predicting statistically likely token sequences given input prompts, training data, and context. A hallucination — a confidently stated but factually wrong response — is not a departure from the intended design. It is the intended design operating under conditions where the model's statistical priors lead it astray. There is no blueprint the AI "departed from." The output space of a frontier model contains billions of possible responses, not all of which are accurate, and managing the boundary between accurate and inaccurate probabilistic outputs is an unsolved research problem, not a manufacturing quality control issue.

As a [RAND analysis of AI tort liability](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA3243-4.html) found, applying traditional tort doctrine to AI creates "considerable uncertainty" around causation precisely because the probabilistic nature of AI outputs makes it difficult to isolate any single failure point. Courts confronting strict liability claims will not be asking "did this product depart from its intended design?" They will be asking "is this probabilistic output bad enough to count as a defect?" — a question with no principled answer in existing doctrine.

The litigation the AI LEAD Act would generate will not resemble product liability in pharmaceuticals, where defect theory is scientifically grounded. It will resemble the worst outcomes-based tort litigation: plaintiffs winning on theories disconnected from any developer misconduct, with causation proxies substituting for genuine causal analysis. That is not an accountability regime. It is an innovation tax imposed retroactively, without regard to fault.

## The Deployer Modification Problem Is Not a Drafting Error

Both the AI LEAD Act and the Blackburn draft acknowledge, in passing, that "substantial modifications" by downstream deployers may break the liability chain. That concession is legally necessary — established product liability doctrine, reflected in cases like *Webb v. Special Electric Co.* and the Restatement's component parts provisions, holds that a component manufacturer is not liable when an integrator's modifications proximately cause the harm. But neither bill defines what constitutes a "substantial modification" in the context of AI deployment. That is not a drafting error that markup can fix. It reflects an unresolved conceptual problem at the heart of the strict liability theory.

Consider the full stack of what happens between a foundation model release and a user interaction that causes harm:

- The deployer fine-tunes the model on proprietary or domain-specific data, altering its behavioral priors
- The deployer inserts a system prompt that shapes, restricts, or redirects model outputs
- The deployer applies output classifiers and content filters that intercept certain model responses
- The deployer constructs a retrieval-augmented generation pipeline that determines what information the model can access
- The deployer builds a user interface that shapes how responses are presented and interpreted

Each of these interventions materially changes what the model says. [As practitioners analyzing AI product liability exposure have noted](https://www.leakeandersson.com/product-liability-claims-against-ai-developers-an-uncertain-future/), the causal chain between a foundation model's training decisions and a specific harmful downstream output runs through layers of deployer customization that the original developer neither controls nor observes. Treating the developer as strictly liable for the output at the end of that chain — without requiring plaintiffs to establish that the developer's choices, rather than the deployer's modifications, proximately caused the harm — is doctrinally incoherent.

The practical effect would be a perverse set of incentives. Strict liability would push developers to be less transparent about model capabilities (transparency creates discoverable evidence of known risks), less willing to provide detailed technical documentation to deployers (documentation establishes duty), and more aggressive in using contractual indemnification to shift exposure downstream. None of these behaviors improve safety. All of them degrade the information ecosystem that genuine accountability requires.

## Liability-Driven Consolidation Is the Real Harm

Proponents of strict AI liability argue that the asymmetry of power between AI developers and harmed individuals justifies holding developers to a strict standard. That argument misreads the actual distribution of market power in AI. Strict liability doesn't redistribute power from Big Tech to injured consumers. It redistributes power from AI startups and researchers to Big Tech incumbents — and does so structurally, not incidentally.

Strict liability requires the capacity to absorb open-ended claims. [The economic literature on liability regimes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/article/pii/S0267364923000055) is unambiguous: liability costs that are large relative to firm size disproportionately burden new entrants, raising the effective cost of market participation and cementing incumbency among firms large enough to self-insure. OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and Amazon Web Services can build reserve funds, purchase specialized coverage, and spread liability exposure across diversified revenue bases. A Series A AI startup with 40 employees cannot.

A federal strict liability floor enacted today would be the most effective market barrier Congress has ever handed the largest technology companies — not through regulatory capture or industrial policy, but through tort law that only hyperscalers can operationally absorb. As the [Foundation for American Innovation concluded](https://www.thefai.org/posts/the-trump-america-ai-act-is-a-disaster) in its analysis of the Blackburn draft, the bill's liability framework "would be a disaster" for competition, functionally insulating incumbents from meaningful challengers. Anyone genuinely concerned about concentrated AI market power should oppose strict liability at least as strenuously as they oppose anticompetitive mergers.

State-level trends compound the problem. [Wiley's 2026 survey of state AI bills](https://www.wiley.law/article-2026-State-AI-Bills-That-Could-Expand-Liability-Insurance-Risk) documents a wave of liability-expanding measures advancing across multiple state legislatures — each creating additive private rights of action for AI-related harms. A federal strict liability floor layered under a fragmented state patchwork of private rights of action would not just burden small players; it would make nationwide AI product deployment financially viable only for organizations with the legal infrastructure to manage dozens of conflicting exposure regimes simultaneously. That is a description of Microsoft. It is not a description of a two-person research lab in Pittsburgh.

## The Open-Source Casualty

The downstream casualty least visible in the Congressional debate is the open-source AI research ecosystem — and it is the one ThinkBot is most concerned about.

As of spring 2026, [Hugging Face hosts over two million public models](https://huggingface.co/blog/huggingface/state-of-os-hf-spring-2026), with more than 39 percent of model downloads attributable to independent and unaffiliated developers — not corporate labs. EleutherAI, university research groups, and thousands of individual contributors release model weights publicly, without compensation and without the institutional capacity to indemnify every downstream deployer who builds on their work.

Strict liability for foundation model outputs would transform every open-weight release into an uninsurable liability event. A graduate student at Carnegie Mellon who releases a model that is subsequently fine-tuned, deployed in an enterprise application, and involved in a harm-causing interaction could face personal exposure for an output they never saw, in an application they never designed, through a causal chain running through actors they never contracted with. The rational response is to stop releasing models publicly — or never build them at all. The result would be the effective end of meaningful open-source AI development in the United States, ceding that ecosystem to researchers in jurisdictions with no equivalent liability exposure.

The Section 230 parallel is instructive. When Congress enacted the Communications Decency Act in 1996, it extended intermediary immunity to online platforms for third-party content precisely because holding platforms strictly liable for user speech would have made the hosting of user content legally untenable. The structural problem is similar here: if liability follows the model rather than the deployment context, the act of releasing weights publicly becomes legally indistinguishable from operating a harmful system. An open-weight model liability shield — analogous to Section 230's protection for the act of hosting, not for specific harmful content — would preserve the research ecosystem while preserving full accountability for deployers who actually control the harm-generating context.

## What a Workable Framework Looks Like

A federal AI liability regime that actually improves safety, rather than just generating litigation, has three components.

**A duty-of-care negligence standard.** Federal legislation should establish that AI developers owe a duty of reasonable care to foreseeable users, with liability attaching when a developer breaches that duty and the breach proximately causes harm. The [Lawfare analysis of negligence liability for AI developers](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/negligence-liability-for-ai-developers) has it right: negligence already contains the doctrinal flexibility to address AI-related harms without the category-error problems of strict products liability. The standard should be calibrated to the [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) — a rigorously developed, interoperable benchmark for governing, mapping, measuring, and managing AI risk that already represents the closest thing to industry consensus on responsible development practice.

**A safe harbor for benchmark compliance.** Developers who demonstrate adherence to published safety benchmarks — the NIST AI RMF, relevant OECD guidelines, and sector-specific standards as they mature — and who provide adequate, documented disclosure to deployers about system capabilities and known limitations should receive a qualified safe harbor from negligence liability. Safe harbors create positive investment incentives: rather than minimizing paper trails to limit litigation exposure, developers are motivated to actually invest in measurable safety. This is the model that has worked in pharmaceuticals, aviation, and cybersecurity, and it can work in AI.

**An open-weight model liability shield.** Congress should establish that releasing model weights publicly, without more, does not constitute the act of deploying an AI system or proximately causing downstream harms. Liability should attach to those who deploy systems in contexts they control — who make the product, distribution, and use-case decisions that create the risk — not to those who release weights into a research ecosystem they do not govern. This is not immunity for AI companies; it is a structural protection for the open-source research ecosystem that produces the scientific substrate on which the entire industry depends.

## The Markup Window Is Open

The Senate Judiciary Committee has an active markup window. The Blackburn discussion draft is circulating for comment. This is the moment when the liability architecture for AI gets set — and once set, it will be extraordinarily difficult to revise.

The case against strict liability is not a case against accountability. Developers who act negligently — who ignore known safety risks, who misrepresent system capabilities to deployers, who deploy systems without adequate testing in high-stakes contexts — should face legal consequences. Negligence law provides exactly that recourse, with the causal rigor that strict liability abandons.

What strict liability provides instead is an innovation tax on every developer regardless of fault, a liability-driven consolidation gift to the incumbents least in need of competitive protection, and an existential threat to open-source AI research that no amount of carve-out language will fully repair. The seductive simplicity of "if AI hurts someone, the developer pays" is precisely the kind of policy design that looks like accountability from the outside and functions as a market moat from the inside.

Congress can do better. The negligence standard, the safe harbor, and the open-weight shield together constitute a framework that channels genuine safety investment, protects the research ecosystem, and ensures that developers who cut corners face the legal consequences of their choices. That is what holding AI accountable actually looks like.
