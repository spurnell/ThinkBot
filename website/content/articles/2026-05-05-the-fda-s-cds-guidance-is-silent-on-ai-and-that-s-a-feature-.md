On January 6, 2026, the Food and Drug Administration issued its [final Clinical Decision Support Software guidance](https://www.fda.gov/media/191560/download), the culmination of a regulatory process dating back to the 21st Century Cures Act of 2016. FDA Commissioner Marty Makary framed the release as a deregulatory triumph — a red-tape cut that would accelerate innovation in digital health.

By March 11, the agency convened a [public town hall](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/medical-devices-news-and-events/town-hall-clinical-decision-support-software-final-guidance-03112026) to field questions about the guidance. The questions, it turns out, were pointed.

Legal analysts at [Covington & Burling](https://www.cov.com/en/news-and-insights/insights/2026/01/5-key-takeaways-from-fdas-revised-clinical-decision-support-cds-software-guidance), [Arnold & Porter](https://www.arnoldporter.com/en/perspectives/advisories/2026/01/fda-cuts-red-tape-on-clinical-decision-support-software), and the [ABA's health law section](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/health_law/news/2026/fda-guidance-clinical-decision-support-software/) converged on a striking observation: despite being publicly characterized as an AI-forward modernization, the final guidance is entirely silent on artificial intelligence. The four-criteria test that determines whether clinical decision support software qualifies as a regulated medical device — unchanged from the 2022 draft — makes no mention of machine learning, foundation models, probabilistic reasoning, or emergent behavior. The same framework that governed deterministic, rule-based 1990s logic trees now nominally governs large language models and neural network-based prediction engines deployed inside hospital electronic health record systems.

That gap has provoked calls for the FDA to assert broader jurisdiction over clinical AI. Those calls are understandable — but they point the policy arrow in the wrong direction. The FDA's light-touch approach is analytically defensible on structural grounds: the agency's pre-market product review framework was never designed to govern continuously-updated, hospital-deployed software embedded inside clinical workflows. The governance gap is real. But the right answer is not more FDA. It is investing authority in the institutions that are structurally better positioned to govern clinical AI: the Joint Commission, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services, and the institutional review mechanisms that academic medical centers are already voluntarily building.

## What the Four Criteria Actually Say

To understand why the guidance's AI silence matters, it helps to understand what the guidance actually does. Under the 21st Century Cures Act's statutory framework, CDS software avoids regulation as a medical device if it meets all four criteria: it must not analyze medical images or physiological signals; it must display information normally communicated between clinicians; it must offer recommendations rather than directives. The fourth criterion — the critical one — requires that the software [provide the basis for its recommendations](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/software-medical-device-samd/clinical-decision-support-software-frequently-asked-questions-faqs) so that a healthcare professional can independently review them and does not rely primarily on the software's output.

The 2026 final guidance layers onto this framework a transparency push: developers of non-device CDS must provide plain-language descriptions of their algorithms' logic, input data, validation methods, and known limitations. [Faegre Drinker's analysis](https://www.faegredrinker.com/en/insights/publications/2026/1/key-updates-in-fdas-2026-general-wellness-and-clinical-decision-support-software-guidance) notes that this transparency requirement makes it harder for "black box" ML models to qualify for the non-device exemption — in theory.

But that "in theory" carries enormous weight. A transparency disclosure requirement attached to a non-device exemption is not the same as regulatory review. It is a labeling standard. There is no FDA pre-submission process, no clinical validation requirement, no adverse event reporting pathway. If a hospital-integrated readmission prediction model drifts on a new patient population, there is no federal mechanism to detect it. The guidance's transparency provisions are better understood as a market-conditioning norm than as a governance backstop.

The conceptual tension runs deeper. [Nixon Law Group's review](https://www.nixonlawgroup.com/resources/fda-relaxes-clinical-decision-support-and-general-wellness-guidance-what-it-means-for-generative-ai-and-consumer-wearables) of the guidance's treatment of generative AI reveals the problem at the core of Criterion Four: the "independent review" standard was designed for rule-based software whose logic could be inspected. Applied to a GPT-class model producing clinical summaries or a deep learning system generating differential diagnoses, the requirement to provide a "basis for recommendations" that enables independent clinician review runs headlong into the opacity that makes foundation models simultaneously powerful and difficult to audit. The guidance gestures at this problem — it notes that proprietary algorithms must still disclose their logic — but does not resolve it.

## The Structural Mismatch — and the Gap It Creates

The more consequential gap is not about commercial AI products that navigate the four-criteria test. It is about the enormous category of clinical AI that sits entirely outside the SaMD regulatory pathway.

Hospitals and health systems that develop and deploy their own AI tools — a sepsis prediction model built on internal EHR data, a readmission risk scorer embedded into clinical workflows, a length-of-stay model used for discharge planning — are not selling software at a point of commercial entry. They are not subject to FDA's SaMD framework, which governs *products* entering the market, not internal operational tools. As the [Bipartisan Policy Center's analysis of FDA health AI oversight](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/issue-brief/fda-oversight-understanding-the-regulation-of-health-ai-tools/) makes clear, no single federal agency is responsible for overseeing the ethical development and deployment of medical AI at the institutional level. The regulatory gap is structural, not incidental.

This matters because internally deployed hospital algorithms have already produced documented patient safety failures. The [Epic Sepsis Model (ESM)](https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jamainternalmedicine/fullarticle/2781307), deployed at hundreds of US hospitals and validated only by its developer, was the subject of a landmark JAMA Internal Medicine study showing an area under the curve of just 0.63 — substantially below the developer's reported performance — while generating alerts for 18 percent of all hospitalized patients, the vast majority of them false positives. [Fierce Healthcare's coverage](https://www.fiercehealthcare.com/tech/epic-s-widely-used-sepsis-prediction-model-falls-short-among-michigan-medicine-patients) documented that the model's sensitivity was lower in Black patients, a pattern researchers attributed to the model's training on data shaped by unequal healthcare access. The model entered clinical deployment without FDA review, without mandatory external validation, and without a regulatory mechanism to capture post-deployment performance drift.

The ESM is not a fringe case. It is representative of how clinical AI actually enters hospitals: through EHR vendor integrations, institutional data science teams, or third-party analytics contracts — none of which pass through FDA's regulatory gate. Calling for FDA to fill this gap misunderstands the structural cause of it. FDA's SaMD pathway reaches products at the moment of sale. The hospital-deployed algorithm problem is, by definition, past that moment.

## The Right Locus: The Joint Commission, CMS, and Institutional Accountability

The appropriate answer is not to stretch FDA's product-at-market-entry framework into a deployment-phase oversight mechanism it was not designed to be. It is to invest regulatory energy in the institutions that already have continuous reach into hospitals: the Joint Commission and the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services.

The Joint Commission accredits roughly 22,000 healthcare organizations in the United States. Accreditation is not voluntary in any meaningful sense — it is the functional prerequisite for Medicare and Medicaid participation. In September 2025, the Joint Commission [partnered with the Coalition for Health AI (CHAI)](https://www.jointcommission.org/en-us/knowledge-library/news/2025-09-jc-and-chai-release-initial-guidance-to-support-responsible-ai-adoption) to release the first comprehensive guidance on responsible AI adoption for U.S. health systems — laying groundwork for a forthcoming AI certification standard. That is the right direction. The next step is embedding concrete requirements into accreditation standards: hospitals seeking or maintaining Joint Commission accreditation should be required to maintain an AI algorithm registry, conduct prospective validation before deploying novel ML tools in high-stakes clinical pathways, and report post-deployment monitoring results.

[CHAI's Blueprint for Trustworthy AI](https://www.chai.org/workgroup/responsible-ai/blueprint-for-trustworthy-ai), developed with input from nearly 3,000 member organizations including health systems, patient advocacy groups, and technology companies, provides a substantive governance framework that could be operationalized into accreditation requirements without congressional action. This is precisely how accreditation should work: the profession and the accreditor co-develop standards, and the accreditor enforces them continuously.

CMS offers an even more powerful lever. Conditions of Participation — the statutory requirements hospitals must meet to participate in Medicare and Medicaid — currently govern clinical quality, patient safety, and nursing standards. CMS has already demonstrated willingness to use this authority to address algorithmic tools in adjacent contexts: the agency's [WISeR model](https://www.cms.gov/priorities/innovation/innovation-models/wiser), launching across six states in 2026, requires that AI-assisted prior authorization determinations follow standardized, transparent, evidence-based clinical criteria with human clinician review at each step. Extending Conditions of Participation to require algorithmic transparency disclosures for EHR-integrated clinical AI — mandating that hospitals disclose which high-stakes algorithmic tools they have deployed, their validation methods, and their post-deployment performance — would reach every hospital in America without creating a new pre-market review bureaucracy.

## IRB-Adjacent Review: A Proof of Concept

The most promising institutional model emerging from practice is the hospital AI committee — a prospective review body that evaluates novel algorithmic deployments before clinical integration. Academic medical centers have been the early adopters. Stanford Health Care's governance framework applies its ["FURM" evaluation standard](https://stanfordhealthcare.org/campaigns/ai-education/responsible-use.html) — assessing whether AI tools are Fair, Useful, and Reliable — before any new clinical AI tool enters institutional use. [A peer-reviewed study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9652023/) documenting the establishment of a hospital AI committee at James A. Haley Veterans' Hospital in Tampa offers a replicable institutional model: a standing committee with clinician, bioethicist, legal, and technical expertise reviews AI products against criteria covering autonomy protection, safety, transparency, accountability, equity, and sustainability.

The governance challenge is scaling this beyond well-resourced academic medical centers. A [PubMed analysis of IRBs and clinical AI governance](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33683015/) concludes that traditional ethics committees, designed for discrete research protocols, are ill-suited to the continuous, evolving nature of deployed ML systems — and that these bodies must expand to include AI technical expertise alongside clinicians and bioethicists. The solution is not to force clinical AI through a biomedical research IRB. It is to establish hospital AI committees as a condition of CMS participation for any institution deploying high-stakes algorithmic tools in clinical decision pathways. Make the requirement involuntary where it currently is not, and provide smaller community hospitals with a CMS-supported pathway to participate in regional review consortia. That extends accountability without requiring every rural critical-access hospital to build independent AI governance infrastructure from scratch.

## The Broader Governance Lesson

The FDA's clinical decision support guidance illustrates a principle that AI governance debates consistently rediscover: centralized federal pre-market review is a poor fit for embedded software with continuously-evolving behavior. FDA's product-review model was built for a world of discrete medical devices — hardware that could be tested, approved, and monitored at the point of sale. Clinical AI is something fundamentally different: it operates in a clinical context, is shaped by local patient populations, drifts over time as case mix changes, and produces risks that emerge through deployment, not through product defects identifiable at market entry.

The governance architecture that matches those realities is continuous, institution-level, and clinically grounded — which is exactly what Joint Commission accreditation and CMS conditions of participation provide. This is not a regulatory gap waiting to be filled by a new federal bureau; it is an institutional division of labor that needs to be activated. The clinical profession, the accreditor, and the payer operate continuously inside the hospital in ways the FDA cannot replicate through point-in-time pre-market review. Leveraging those institutions is not a second-best alternative to FDA expansion — it is the structurally superior answer.

The precedent matters beyond healthcare. The same logic applies to AI embedded in financial advice platforms, educational assessment tools, and criminal justice risk instruments — domains where existing sector-specific institutions have continuous operational reach that a centralized AI regulatory regime would struggle to replicate. The healthcare case, with its mature accreditation and conditions-of-participation frameworks, is the strongest available proof of concept for sector-specific AI governance done right.

## Policy Recommendations

**For the Joint Commission and CHAI**: Accelerate the development of mandatory accreditation standards for clinical AI governance. The September 2025 guidance is a starting point. Within 18 months, codify requirements for an algorithmic registry, prospective validation documentation for high-stakes clinical AI deployments, and post-deployment monitoring reporting as formal elements of hospital accreditation.

**For CMS**: Expand Conditions of Participation to require algorithmic transparency disclosures for EHR-integrated clinical AI tools used in high-stakes decision pathways. Model the requirement on existing CMS quality reporting frameworks — not FDA pre-market review. Create a regional consortium pathway so smaller hospitals can meet the requirement without building independent governance infrastructure.

**For Congress**: No new legislation is required for either the Joint Commission or CMS to act. If Congress wishes to move, the most targeted intervention is directing HHS to issue an NPRM updating CMS Conditions of Participation to address clinical AI transparency — not creating a new FDA software division or a horizontal AI liability statute that would preempt the institutional accountability mechanisms now forming.

**For the FDA**: Continue the current approach. The January 2026 guidance's transparency-forward, enforcement-discretion framework is appropriate for commercially sold CDS products. Resist pressure to extend SaMD jurisdiction to hospital-deployed algorithms. The agency lacks the infrastructure, clinical expertise, and operational continuity to govern post-deployment clinical AI — and attempting to acquire them would divert resources from the pre-market review functions it already performs well.

The FDA's silence on AI in its January 2026 CDS guidance is not a regulatory failure. It reflects a structural reality about where the agency's authority begins and ends. Naming that reality honestly, and investing in the institutions that can fill the accountability gap, is the more productive policy response — and the model the broader AI governance debate should be watching closely.
