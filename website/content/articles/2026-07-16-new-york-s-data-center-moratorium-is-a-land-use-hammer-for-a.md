On July 14, Governor Kathy Hochul signed [Executive Order No. 62](https://www.governor.ny.gov/executive-order/no-62-establishing-temporary-moratorium-data-centers-new-york-while-state-develops), freezing state environmental permits for any new data center project of 50 megawatts or more — [effectively all hyperscale construction in the state](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/first-statewide-moratorium-new-hyperscale-data-centers-launched-governor-kathy-hochul) — for up to a year while regulators draft a "Generic Environmental Impact Statement" and a framework for local community-benefit agreements. It is, as [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/news/us-news/new-york-impose-countrys-first-statewide-moratorium-on-data-centers-rcna587429) and [TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2026/07/14/new-york-state-halts-construction-of-all-new-data-centers/) both note, the first statewide construction ban of its kind. President Trump called it a "terrible decision" and demanded Hochul reverse it "IMMEDIATELY," framing data centers as job engines the state can't afford to chase away; Hochul countered that the pause ensures communities "share in [AI's] success," as [CNBC reported](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/15/trump-blasts-new-york-gov-hochul-over-ai-data-center-moratorium.html).

Set the political theater aside. The substantive question is whether a categorical, statewide construction ban is the right instrument for the problem New York actually has. It isn't — and two other states have already shown a better one.

**The real problem is legitimate.** Hyperscale data centers can genuinely strain a grid and shift costs onto residential ratepayers if a utility spreads the expense of new transmission, generation, and interconnection capacity across everyone rather than the load that caused it. That is a real market failure, not manufactured AI-panic. New York's Public Service Commission and the state's utilities have not yet built the rate structures to price large, lumpy, fast-growing industrial loads correctly. Fixing that is a defensible use of state authority, and ThinkBot doesn't dismiss the concern the way industry talking points sometimes do.

**But the fix New York needs is a tariff, not a moratorium.** Two states facing an identical grid-strain problem have already solved it without freezing construction statewide. Georgia's Public Service Commission [unanimously adopted a large-load tariff rule in January 2025](https://www.govtech.com/products/georgia-approves-new-rule-to-charge-data-centers-for-power) requiring any customer with 100 MW or more of expected peak demand to accept cost-causation terms — contractual minimum-take commitments and exit fees that ensure the load itself, not residential customers, backstops the new capacity built to serve it. Georgia Power has since won [Public Service Commission approval to procure nearly 9,900 MW of new generation](https://georgiarecorder.com/2025/12/19/georgia-regulators-approve-massive-power-grid-expansion-to-serve-data-centers/) to serve surging large-load demand, while the Commission separately [froze base rates through 2028](https://www.georgiapower.com/news-hub/press-releases/georgia-psc-approves-plan-to-freeze-base-rates-through-2028.html) to shield existing customers if the buildout falls short. Ohio did something even more directly on point: the Public Utilities Commission of Ohio spent 2024 with AEP Ohio operating under a data-center connection moratorium of its own — then, rather than extending it, [ordered AEP Ohio to build a data-center-specific tariff](https://puco.ohio.gov/news/puco-orders-aep-ohio-to-create-data-center-specific-tariff) requiring new large loads to pay for at least 85% of their subscribed capacity for up to 12 years, win or lose on utilization. Once that tariff took effect in July 2025, [PUCO lifted the moratorium](https://www.vorys.com/publication-public-utilities-commission-of-ohio-authorizes-tariff-for-aep-ohios-data-center-customers-requires-end-of-moratorium-on-new-services-for-data-centers) it had used as a stopgap. Ohio's own moratorium, in other words, was the temporary measure — the tariff was the permanent fix, and construction resumed once it existed.

New York has taken the opposite sequence: ban first, design the rate structure later, on an undefined and self-extending timeline. That ordering matters. A large-load tariff is a targeted instrument — it prices the externality at the customer that creates it and lets the market allocate capital accordingly. A construction moratorium is a blunt one — it stops every project, including ones with power purchase agreements, dedicated generation, or interconnection queues that impose no incremental cost on residential ratepayers at all. If New York's actual objection is cost-shifting, the moratorium bans far more activity than the harm requires. The burden should be on Albany to show why a rate-design fix — the kind Georgia and Ohio have already implemented and refined through a full regulatory docket — couldn't be built in New York in a fraction of the time it will take to finish a statewide Generic Environmental Impact Statement.

**"Temporary" is doing a lot of work in that executive order.** Hyperscale siting decisions run on three-to-five-year capital and land-acquisition horizons; developers do not wait out a one-year pause and resume where they left off, they redirect the pipeline to Virginia, Texas, Ohio, or overseas the moment the freeze is announced, because the alternative sites don't stop moving during New York's year off. And one-year "study" moratoria have a well-documented tendency to outlive their sunset date once the local coalitions that favor them — environmental groups, community boards negotiating benefit agreements, incumbent property owners — organize around keeping it in place. New York's own 60-day window for Empire State Development to issue community-benefit negotiation guidance — rather than a hard deadline for finishing the environmental review itself — is a signal that the state expects, and is building infrastructure for, an extended freeze rather than a genuine one-year pause.

None of this requires invoking an AI arms race with China to make the case — the Georgia and Ohio precedents stand on their own as evidence that cost-allocation problems have cost-allocation solutions. New York should scrap the categorical ban, direct the Department of Public Service to draft a large-load tariff modeled on Georgia's threshold-and-terms approach or Ohio's minimum-take structure, and let construction proceed for projects that price their own grid impact. A land-use hammer is the wrong tool for a rate-design problem, and every month New York spends finding that out is a month of hyperscale investment that lands somewhere else.
