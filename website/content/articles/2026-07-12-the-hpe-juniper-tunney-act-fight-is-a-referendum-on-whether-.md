On March 23, 2026, in a courtroom that normally hosts one of the more procedurally sleepy exercises in federal antitrust law, Judge P. Casey Pitts of the Northern District of California pressed Justice Department lawyers on a question that goes to the heart of how merger enforcement actually works: is a negotiated settlement acceptable merely because it's "better than nothing"? According to reporting on the hearing, Pitts said he was "struggling" with exactly that question, and pushed both DOJ and HPE's counsel on whether merger remedies of this kind have ever meaningfully protected competition or whether they tend to accomplish little ([SDxCentral](https://www.sdxcentral.com/news/hpe-juniper-judge-questions-whether-a-weak-remedy-is-better-than-nothing/)).

That question hangs over the consent decree that resolved the [DOJ's complaint](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-requires-divestitures-and-licensing-commitments-hpes-acquisition-juniper) seeking to block HPE's $14 billion acquisition of Juniper Networks — a deal DOJ itself had sued to stop in January 2025 on the theory that it would leave the enterprise wireless LAN market with only two credible competitors behind Cisco. The settlement, struck roughly two weeks before trial, requires HPE to divest its global "Instant On" campus and branch WLAN business within 180 days and to auction a perpetual, non-exclusive license to Juniper's Mist AIOps source code, with an additional licensing obligation if auction bids exceed $8 million ([Network World](https://www.networkworld.com/article/4014283/hpe-juniper-deal-clears-doj-hurdle-but-settlement-requires-divestitures.html)). Under the Tunney Act, that settlement is now before Judge Pitts for public-interest review — except this time, a coalition of twelve states and the District of Columbia, led by Colorado Attorney General Phil Weiser, has intervened to argue the settlement should be rejected outright ([Colorado AG](https://coag.gov/2025/intervene-in-alleged-corrupt-hpe-juniper-networks-merger-settlement/)).

A ruling is expected soon. It should not be read narrowly as a decision about routers and Wi-Fi controllers. It is a decision about whether the Tunney Act — a 1974 statute written to catch corrupt settlements, not to relitigate merger economics DOJ already traded away from trial risk — is about to become a second full-blown merger trial available to any coalition of states unhappy with the deal DOJ struck. Get that wrong, and the damage extends to every consent decree DOJ negotiates going forward.

## What the Tunney Act actually requires

The Tunney Act was a post-Watergate reform, enacted in 1974 specifically to prevent antitrust settlements from being the product of "improper influence" by "powerful corporations and their well-connected lobbyists" — not to convert every consent decree into a merits trial ([reporting on the Act's history](https://www.pymnts.com/cpi-posts/federal-judge-lays-out-rules-for-states-challenging-hpe-juniper-deal/)). Courts applying it have consistently described the resulting review as narrow and deferential. As one summary of the governing case law puts it, a Tunney Act proceeding is not an open forum for the court — or for commenters — to "second-guess" DOJ's exercise of its "broad discretion" over enforcement decisions; "the court is only authorized to review the decree itself" and has no authority to "effectively redraft the complaint" or assume the role of Attorney General ([Antitrust Source, Hogan Lovells](https://www.hlc.com/~/media/hogan-lovells/pdf/publication/tunneyact_pdf.pdf)).

That deference has limits, but the case law shows those limits run to process, not to result — and the leading precedent actually cuts against the states' position, not toward it. In [*United States v. Microsoft*](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/F3/56/1448/623900/), 56 F.3d 1448 (D.C. Cir. 1995), the D.C. Circuit reversed a district judge who had, on his own initiative, refused to enter DOJ's original consent decree with Microsoft on the ground that the remedy failed to address conduct described in the government's complaint. The appeals court held that a district judge exceeds his Tunney Act authority when he demands information beyond what the statute requires and substitutes his own judgment about remedy adequacy for the government's — the same maneuver the Colorado coalition is now asking Judge Pitts to perform. Congress later responded to broader concerns that Tunney Act review had become a rubber stamp by amending the statute in 2004 to add disclosure and explicit multi-factor review requirements, but even that reform stopped short of authorizing courts to redesign remedies. In [*United States v. SBC Communications*](https://www.justice.gov/atr/case-document/file/509841/dl), 489 F. Supp. 2d 1 (D.D.C. 2007) — the first opinion to substantially interpret those amendments — Judge Emmet Sullivan held that "a close reading of the law demonstrates that the 2004 amendments effected minimal changes," and that the court's "scope of review remains sharply proscribed by precedent and the nature of Tunney Act proceedings." Both cases, in other words, stand for procedural adequacy and informational sufficiency as the outer limits of judicial intervention — not for the proposition that a court can independently decide a remedy wasn't tough enough and reject the deal on that basis alone.

The Colorado coalition is asking Judge Pitts to go further than that. Their intervention motion argues the settlement is "facially inadequate" because it fails to address "the harm pled in the complaint," and separately that "the settlement process lacked transparency" and may have been "tainted" by lobbying and political influence ([Duane Morris](https://www.duanemorris.com/alerts/13_state_attorneys_general_file_motion_intervene_federal_review_doj_settlement_allowing_1025.html)). Those are two different claims, and the difference matters enormously for what Judge Pitts should do with each.

## Two allegations, two very different Tunney Act bases

**The adequacy claim** — that the Instant On divestiture and Mist licensing structure don't fully restore the competition lost when the DOJ's own third-largest and second-largest WLAN vendors combined — is not, standing alone, a valid basis for a court to reject a consent decree. DOJ negotiated this remedy against the backdrop of a trial it might have lost; a settlement, almost definitionally, will typically be less aggressive than the theoretical best-case litigated outcome. If "I would have gotten a better deal" were sufficient grounds for judicial rejection, no negotiated resolution could ever survive contact with a disappointed party, because there is always a hypothetically stronger remedy on the table. That is precisely the "mockery of judicial power" standard Congress and subsequent courts moved away from — but moving away from a rubber stamp is not the same as installing courts as merger-remedy designers. As one antitrust commentator has argued of this very case, the Tunney Act's public-interest inquiry is supposed to remain "limited, procedural, and deferential," and reopening a settled matter merely because state litigants preferred a different bargain would create "incentives against settlement" across the board ([Bloomberg Law](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/legal-exchange-insights-and-commentary/relitigating-the-hpe-juniper-merger-risks-judicial-overreach)).

**The corruption claim** is different, and it deserves to be taken seriously rather than waved off as sour grapes. The states and outside advocacy groups have made specific, falsifiable allegations: that two senior Antitrust Division officials, Roger Alford and Bill Rinner, were pushed out after objecting to the settlement terms; that then-DOJ Chief of Staff Chad Mizelle coordinated with outside lobbyists before his own departure; and that Stanley Woodward faced Senate scrutiny over reported "backroom deals with politically-connected lobbyists" ([American Economic Liberties Project](https://www.economicliberties.us/press-release/court-lets-states-join-hpe-juniper-case-as-allegations-of-corrupt-doj-process-and-meddling-mount/)). If those allegations hold up under scrutiny, they describe exactly the kind of "improper influence" the Tunney Act's 1974 drafters were trying to prevent, and a court would be right to probe them. Judge Pitts should — and by all accounts intends to — examine whether the *process* was corrupted.

But that is a factual inquiry with a limited scope: was the decision-making process compromised, yes or no. It is not a license to then re-litigate whether the resulting remedy was strong enough, because a corrupted process and an inadequate remedy are logically independent claims that happen to point toward the same outcome the states want. A settlement negotiated through a clean process can still turn out, in hindsight, to be weaker than a losing litigant would have preferred — that disappointment is not evidence of corruption. Conversely, if the process is found tainted, the correct remedy is to unwind the process (a new negotiation, disclosure of the contacts at issue, potentially DOJ leadership accountability) — not for a district court to freehand its own preferred divestiture package from the bench. The risk in this case is that the adequacy argument rides in on the coattails of the corruption argument, using serious and specific personnel allegations to smuggle in a standard of review — "the remedy wasn't strong enough, full stop" — that the statute doesn't support even where corruption is proven.

## What the Instant On fire sale actually tells us

The states, and some press coverage, have pointed to HPE's struggle to find a buyer for Instant On as after-the-fact proof the remedy was inadequate from the start. The facts here are genuinely bad optics: reported bids for the divested business ranged as low as $1 — from investors proposing to spin Instant On into a standalone company while candidly noting it is unprofitable with "flat revenue and gross profit margins that are well below industry average" — up to roughly $15 million from other bidders including CXApp and Fatpipe ([Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/hpe-assets-struggled-with-buyer-interest-in-antitrust-divestment); [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/cpi-posts/low-offers-for-instant-on-undercut-hpes-juniper-merger-remedy/)).

That is a real problem, but it is a *remedy-design* problem, not a legal-standard problem, and conflating the two would be a mistake with consequences well beyond this deal. Divestiture buyer troubles are a well-understood failure mode in merger-remedy practice: when a decree doesn't require an identified, pre-vetted "upfront buyer" before the deal closes, or when the carved-out asset lacks standalone R&D, customer contracts, and personnel sufficient to compete independently, sellers can end up structurally motivated to find any qualified taker rather than a genuinely viable competitor — because the alternative under some decrees is a court-appointed trustee selling the assets at whatever price clears. The FTC and DOJ have both moved toward upfront-buyer requirements in more recent merger consent decrees precisely because after-the-fact "any willing buyer within N days" structures have produced exactly this kind of fire sale in prior cases. If Instant On's carve-out was scoped too thin to be independently viable, or the settlement should have required an identified buyer before HPE and Juniper were allowed to close, that's a legitimate critique of DOJ's remedy design — the fix is procedural (better decree drafting, a Bureau of Competition-style structural review of asset viability) and belongs to DOJ, the FTC's merger remedies guidance process, and Congress if they want to require upfront buyers by statute. It is not evidence that the settlement was the product of a corrupted negotiation, and it is not a basis for a court to declare, months after signing, that a decree it hasn't yet approved has already failed and must therefore be rejected. A weak execution of a design choice is a reason to design better decrees next time — not a reason to hand district courts standing authority to override negotiated relief whenever a divestiture proves hard to consummate.

## Why this matters far beyond Santa Clara county

Twelve states plus D.C. effectively asking a federal court to override the U.S. Department of Justice's prosecutorial and settlement judgment is itself a governance event worth naming plainly, separate from whether the underlying corruption allegations prove out. State attorneys general unquestionably have real, independent *parens patriae* enforcement authority — they can and do bring their own antitrust suits, and nothing here questions that. But intervening to override *DOJ's* negotiated resolution of *DOJ's own* federal case is a different posture: it is asking a court to let the losing side of a federal enforcement decision — states that wanted a bigger remedy than the elected administration's Antitrust Division chose to extract — re-run that judgment call through Tunney Act litigation instead of through their own independent Section 1/2 suits or state-law claims, which remain available to them regardless of what happens here.

If Judge Pitts treats "twelve states think the remedy is too weak" as sufficient grounds to force an evidentiary hearing on remedy adequacy (as opposed to narrowly on the corruption allegations), the precedent metastasizes immediately. Every future consent decree — in tech, in pharma, in any sector where a coalition of state AGs disagrees with DOJ's judgment call — becomes contestable by whichever states want a bigger structural fix than DOJ negotiated. DOJ's rational response to that risk is not to negotiate more carefully; it's to negotiate less, because a settlement that isn't reliably final isn't worth the trade-offs of settling in the first place. That pushes the Antitrust Division toward all-or-nothing litigation on every contested merger — a shift that raises deal costs and timelines across the board, discourages efficiency-justified vertical and horizontal combinations that would have cleared with a tailored fix, and does nothing to improve the actual quality of merger review. Complex technology mergers in particular — where remedies often require licensing, firewalls, or partial divestitures rather than clean asset sales — are the deals most dependent on DOJ's ability to negotiate targeted relief rather than litigate to verdict. Making consent decrees hostage to state-intervention risk doesn't produce tougher enforcement; it produces fewer negotiated resolutions and more abandoned or fully litigated deals, which is a worse outcome for competition policy than either alternative on its own.

## What Judge Pitts should do

The correct path threads a needle the parties on both sides have incentives to blur. Judge Pitts should take the corruption allegations seriously on their own terms: if Alford, Rinner, Mizelle, and Woodward's conduct amounts to the kind of improper influence the Tunney Act was written to catch, an evidentiary hearing focused on *that* question — process, contacts, decision-making integrity — is squarely within the statute's post-Watergate purpose, and DOJ should not get a pass because the underlying merger might otherwise be procompetitive on net. But he should decline the states' invitation to treat "the remedy could have been stronger" as an independent, freestanding basis for rejection under a heightened-scrutiny standard that neither the 1974 Congress nor the 2004 amendments wrote into the statute. Those are different questions, and only one of them belongs to the judiciary. The Instant On fire sale is a lesson for DOJ's next decree-drafting exercise, not a mandate for this one, or the next, or any future settlement to be second-guessed by whichever coalition of states didn't get the deal they wanted.
