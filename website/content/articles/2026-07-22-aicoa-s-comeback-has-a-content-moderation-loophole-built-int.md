Senators Chuck Grassley and Amy Klobuchar [reintroduced the American Innovation and Choice Online Act](https://www.judiciary.senate.gov/press/rep/releases/grassley-klobuchar-introduce-bipartisan-legislation-to-lower-prices-expand-consumer-choice-and-restore-online-competition-in-the-digital-marketplace) on June 11, 2026, with a bipartisan cosponsor list running from Josh Hawley to Cory Booker. The bill's core pitch is unchanged from its 2021–2024 iterations: bar the largest platforms from favoring their own products in rankings, tying access to unrelated services, and using their gatekeeper position to disadvantage rivals. Enforcement runs through the Department of Justice, the Federal Trade Commission, and state attorneys general — no new agency, no private right of action, just expanded tools for existing enforcers.

Part of that toolkit is well-designed. AICOA's ranking provision bars covered platforms from favoring their "products, services, or lines of business" in "any user interface, including search or ranking functionality," except pursuant to standards that are "neutral, nondiscriminatory, and fairly applied to all similarly situated business users," per [the bill text at Congress.gov](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/4746/text). That is a defensible, narrowly targeted response to a demonstrated harm — the same self-preferencing theory that anchored [the European Commission's 2017 Google Shopping decision](https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/detail/en/IP_17_1784) and [the DOJ's search case against Google](https://www.justice.gov/atr/case/us-and-plaintiff-states-v-google-llc). Ranking manipulation is measurable, product-market conduct with an identifiable competitive victim: a rival whose listing gets buried beneath the platform's own.

But AICOA doesn't stop there. A separate provision, Section 3(a)(1)(C), makes it unlawful for a covered platform to "apply or enforce the terms of service" among "similarly situated business users in a discriminatory manner that would materially harm competition" — and the bill defines "materially harms competition" to sweep in "any actual or reasonable risk of lessening of competition or impairing the competitive process that is more than a de minimis amount." Unlike the ranking provision, this clause isn't tethered to search results or product placement. It reaches every act of terms-of-service enforcement a platform takes against a business user — which, for any platform hosting third-party publishers, advertisers, or sellers, includes demonetization, demotion, labeling, and removal decisions made in the ordinary course of content moderation.

Nothing in the statutory text tells a court, an FTC investigator, or a state AG how to treat a moderation call under this test. Is a publisher "similarly situated" to a competitor it accuses of receiving preferential treatment because both cover the same beat, or only if both violated the same policy in the same way? Does a platform's decision to demote misinformation "materially harm competition" if the demoted publisher can plausibly claim the policy was applied more strictly to it than to a competitor? The bill offers no test, and given the "more than de minimis" competitive-harm threshold, the pleading bar is not high.

This is a different animal from the FTC's [February 2025 request for comment on "technology platform censorship,"](https://www.ftc.gov/policy/public-comments/request-public-comments-regarding-technology-platform-censorship) which was an information-gathering exercise under existing authority, subject to ordinary administrative scrutiny and, ultimately, judicial review if the agency ever acted on it. AICOA would convert that inquiry's premise — that content decisions might be actionable competitive discrimination — into an enforceable statutory cause of action available to DOJ, FTC, and up to fifty state attorneys general, each free to reach its own reading of "similarly situated" and "materially harms." That doesn't eliminate the speech-code risk regulators have already signaled interest in; it distributes it across dozens of jurisdictions with no common standard and no comment-and-review process to discipline it.

Critics have noticed. [Reporting by MLex](https://www.mlex.com/mlex/technology/articles/2488565) captured the Chamber of Progress's assessment that the bill's "vague ban on treating 'similarly situated business users' differently could allow enforcers to recast editorial judgment and content moderation as illegal discrimination" — handing the current DOJ and FTC leadership exactly the antitrust-flavored lever over platform speech decisions that the Section 230 debate has spent a decade trying to keep out of competition law. That critique is correct, but it's also where most of AICOA's opponents stop. The better response isn't to abandon the self-preferencing ban — it's to fix the clause that invites the abuse.

Two amendments would do it. First, Congress could cabin the nondiscrimination trigger the way it cabined the ranking provision: expressly limit Section 3(a)(1)(C)'s terms-of-service discrimination clause to conduct affecting a business user's access to a defined commercial function — placement, pricing, payment processing, data access — while excluding content-moderation actions (removal, demotion, labeling, demonetization) taken pursuant to a platform's disclosed, generally applicable policy. Second, and less bluntly, Congress could tighten "materially harms competition" itself, requiring plaintiffs to show the differential treatment affects a rival's ability to compete in a relevant product market — not merely that a disfavored publisher was treated worse than a favored one under a moderation policy.

The EU's Digital Markets Act offers an imperfect but instructive comparison. Article 6(5)'s self-preferencing duty is anchored specifically to "search or ranking functionality" — reproducing, almost verbatim, the discipline AICOA's own ranking clause already has. But the DMA's broader gatekeeper obligations lack an equivalent carve-out for content policy, and [its permitted exemptions](https://law.stanford.edu/publications/no-103-an-analysis-of-article-65-dma-as-the-core-of-self-preferencing-in-digital-markets/) are limited to public morality, health, and security — leaving Brussels to litigate the moderation-versus-competition line case by case rather than settle it by statute. That is the outcome AICOA should avoid, not imitate.

Antitrust law has historically been skeptical of forcing firms to deal with rivals on terms they didn't choose — the *Trinko* line of cases exists precisely because open-ended nondiscrimination mandates invite courts and agencies into managing day-to-day business decisions they are poorly equipped to police. A platform's content policy is not a neutral input like shelf space; treating an editorial judgment call as presumptively suspect competitive conduct risks exactly the kind of false positive — chilled moderation investment, litigation-driven over- or under-enforcement of platform rules — that a consumer-welfare framework is supposed to guard against. AICOA's ranking provision passes that test. Its nondiscrimination clause, as written, does not. Committee staff have a narrow window to fix that before markup — and the fix is a matter of statutory drafting, not a referendum on whether antitrust belongs in this fight at all.
