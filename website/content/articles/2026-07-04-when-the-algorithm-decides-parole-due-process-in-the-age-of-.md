In August 2024, Calvin Alexander — more than a decade into a 20-year sentence, nearly blind, with a parole hearing two months away — received a letter informing him he was no longer eligible for parole. Nothing about his case had changed. What had changed was a number: TIGER, Louisiana's risk-assessment algorithm, had classified him as a "moderate risk," and a new state law made that classification dispositive. He would never get to make his case to a parole board [(ProPublica)](https://www.propublica.org/article/tiger-algorithm-louisiana-parole-calvin-alexander).

Alexander is not alone. TIGER — "Targeted Interventions to Greater Enhance Re-entry" — was built around 2014 with a $1.75 million federal grant through Louisiana State University and the Department of Public Safety and Corrections, originally as a rehabilitative tool to help caseworkers identify which programming an inmate needed before release [(ProPublica)](https://www.propublica.org/article/tiger-algorithm-louisiana-parole-calvin-alexander). In 2024, the Louisiana legislature repurposed it: a law authored by state Sen. Patrick McMath converted TIGER's score from one input among many into the sole gate for parole eligibility. Only prisoners rated "low risk" may even petition the board. Roughly 13,000 prisoners — nearly half the state's incarcerated population — carry moderate or high scores and are now categorically barred [(ProPublica)](https://www.propublica.org/article/tiger-algorithm-louisiana-parole-calvin-alexander). Monthly parole grants fell from 32 in 2023 to six after the law took effect; at least 70 hearings were canceled in the law's first four months [(Louisiana Illuminator)](https://lailluminator.com/2025/04/10/parole-algorithm/).

Legal scholars have been sounding the alarm since the law's implementation, and the argument has only sharpened through 2026 as more prisoners exhaust administrative appeals and turn to the courts. The core claim, as University of Chicago law professor Sonja Starr has put it, is that "the opportunity for parole has traditionally been considered part of a sentence" — meaning a law that retroactively strips previously-eligible prisoners of any path to parole, based on a score built from facts fixed before they were incarcerated, functions as a retroactive sentence enhancement barred by the Constitution's ex post facto clause [(ProPublica)](https://www.propublica.org/article/tiger-algorithm-louisiana-parole-calvin-alexander). Louisiana is the only state in the country that uses a risk score this way — as an automatic disqualifier rather than one factor a parole board weighs [(Verite News)](https://veritenews.org/2025/04/10/tiger-algorithm-louisiana-parole-calvin-alexander/).

Algorithmic risk assessment in criminal justice has drawn sustained policy debate for years, and that debate is usually framed as a referendum on whether algorithms belong in courtrooms at all. That framing is wrong, and Louisiana's TIGER law is the clearest illustration of why. The problem isn't that a statistical model is involved in a liberty-affecting decision. The problem is what Louisiana did with the model: it took a decision-support tool and converted it into an unreviewable, un-improvable, binding gate — with no human discretion, no contestability, and no way for a prisoner to ever move the number that controls his fate.

## Decision-support versus decision-replacement

The relevant precedent is not a debate about whether courts can use algorithms — it's settled that they can, within limits. In *State v. Loomis*, the Wisconsin Supreme Court upheld a trial court's use of the COMPAS recidivism score at sentencing against a due-process challenge, but only on narrow terms: the score could not determine whether a defendant was incarcerated or set the length of the sentence, had to be accompanied by an independently reasoned sentencing rationale, and had to come with a written warning about the tool's limitations attached to the presentence report [(Wisconsin Supreme Court, 2016 WI 68)](https://www.wicourts.gov/sc/opinion/DisplayDocument.pdf?content=pdf&seqNo=171690); [(Harvard Law Review)](https://harvardlawreview.org/print/vol-130/state-v-loomis/). The U.S. Supreme Court declined to review the case, leaving *Loomis*'s framework as the operative constitutional floor: an algorithm can inform a human decision-maker's judgment, but it cannot replace that judgment.

TIGER as implemented in 2024 is a different animal. It is not one factor a parole board weighs alongside institutional conduct, victim input, and a hearing. It is the entire determination. A moderate-or-high score doesn't make parole less likely — it makes the hearing itself impossible. Keith Jones, a former Louisiana parole board member, described the mismatch directly: "It doesn't make much sense to me that a score generated by a process that the inmate has no control over takes away the authority and the power of the parole board" [(ProPublica)](https://www.propublica.org/article/tiger-algorithm-louisiana-parole-calvin-alexander). That is the fault line that matters — not "AI in the justice system" as a category, but decision-support versus decision-replacement. *Loomis* tolerated the former because a human remained accountable for the outcome and a defendant retained an avenue to contest the score's application. Louisiana's statute eliminates both safeguards.

## The defect is architectural, not algorithmic

The second problem compounds the first. TIGER's inputs — prior criminal history, work history, age at first arrest, marijuana-related convictions, prior parole revocations — are drawn entirely from a prisoner's life before incarceration [(ProPublica)](https://www.propublica.org/article/tiger-algorithm-louisiana-parole-calvin-alexander). None of them can be changed by anything a person does while serving their sentence. This is precisely backwards from how parole has functioned for a century: parole boards have always weighed institutional conduct, program completion, and evidence of rehabilitation because that is the entire theoretical justification for parole as an institution — the belief that people can change and that the system should be able to detect it. A scoring system that is frozen at intake cannot, by construction, ever register that change. A prisoner who completes every available program, works every available job, and accumulates a decade of clean conduct will carry the identical TIGER score he had on his first day. That is not a tuning problem or a bias problem in the ordinary machine-learning sense — it is a design choice to make the score static where the underlying decision has always assumed dynamism.

This also sharpens the ex post facto exposure, distinct from due process. Courts assessing whether a change to parole rules is punitive typically look at the practical, retroactive effect on eligibility for those already sentenced, not merely the state's stated intent — a line of reasoning that traces to *Garner v. Jones*, 529 U.S. 244 (2000), and *Weaver v. Graham*, 450 U.S. 24 (1981), on retroactive changes to parole and gain-time schemes. A law that takes an already-incarcerated population and locks a majority of it out of any possibility of parole, using variables that predate their sentence and cannot be revised, looks far more like an after-the-fact enhancement of punishment than a neutral administrative reform — regardless of whether it was framed, as bill sponsor Sen. McMath framed it, as an efficiency measure. That efficiency rationale is also empirically weak: parole board caseloads had already fallen 40 percent between 2016 and 2023, undercutting the claim that a docket-clearing tool was needed [(ProPublica)](https://www.propublica.org/article/tiger-algorithm-louisiana-parole-calvin-alexander). And because Black Louisianans make up nearly two-thirds of the state's prison population — more than double their share of the general population — and the frozen inputs (employment history, drug convictions) correlate with well-documented enforcement disparities, the practical effect falls disproportionately along racial lines even though race is not itself a variable [(Louisiana Illuminator)](https://lailluminator.com/2025/04/10/parole-algorithm/).

## A due-process baseline, not a ban

None of this is an argument against risk-assessment tools. Structured, validated risk instruments are more consistent than unaided judicial or parole-board discretion, which research has long shown to vary by time of day, caseload, and individual decision-maker in ways no one would defend as fair. The alternative to TIGER is not "no algorithm" — it is a properly bounded algorithm. Consistent with a broader skepticism of premature, one-size-fits-all AI mandates, the fix here is procedural and targeted at the specific failure mode Louisiana exhibited, not a categorical prohibition on automated risk tools in corrections.

Any jurisdiction using automated risk scoring in a liberty-affecting decision — parole, bail, sentencing — should be required to build in four things:

**1. Contestability and explanation rights.** An individual subject to an adverse score must be able to see the factors that produced it and challenge their accuracy or relevance before a human decision-maker, not merely receive a notice of ineligibility.

**2. A dynamic scoring path.** Any tool used as a gate, rather than as advisory input, must include a mechanism by which post-conviction conduct — program completion, disciplinary record, work assignments — can move the score. A risk tool that cannot register a decade of good conduct is not measuring risk; it is measuring the past.

**3. Mandatory human override authority.** A parole board, judge, or comparable decision-maker must retain the legal authority to overrule the tool's output in an individual case, with a written rationale — precisely the safeguard *Loomis* required and Louisiana's 2024 law eliminated.

**4. Periodic third-party validation.** The underlying model should undergo regular, independent review for predictive accuracy and disparate impact by evaluators without a stake in the tool's continued procurement, with results made public to the extent consistent with legitimate security concerns.

This baseline would leave TIGER's core function — flagging risk for caseworker attention — intact while eliminating exactly the feature that makes the 2024 law constitutionally fragile: its use as an automatic, static, unappealable bar.

## The federal hook worth engaging

Congress's Artificial Intelligence Civil Rights Act (S. 3308 / H.R. 6356), introduced by Sen. Ed Markey and Rep. Yvette Clarke and now before committee, explicitly designates criminal-justice risk and threat assessments — including bail determinations, sentencing, and "administration of parole" — as "consequential actions" subject to the bill's transparency and accountability provisions [(H.R. 6356, 119th Cong.)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/6356); [(Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights Under Law)](https://www.lawyerscommittee.org/ai-civil-rights-act/). Among its mechanisms is a requirement that the FTC issue regulations letting individuals contest a covered algorithmic decision and elect to have it redone by a human without the algorithm [(Sen. Markey and Rep. Clarke's office, Dec. 2, 2025)](https://www.markey.senate.gov/news/press-releases/sen-markey-rep-clarke-reintroduce-ai-civil-rights-act-to-eliminate-ai-discrimination-and-enact-guardrails-on-use-of-algorithms-in-decisions-impacting-peoples-rights-civil-liberties-livelihoods).

The AI Civil Rights Act's broader architecture warrants skepticism on its own terms — its pre-deployment audit mandates and private right of action for treble damages sweep across employment, housing, credit, and insurance decisions with a one-size-fits-all compliance regime better suited to sector-specific regulators than a horizontal FTC rulemaking. That critique stands on its own, and nothing here should be read as a blanket endorsement of the bill. But the contestability-and-human-alternative mechanism, applied narrowly to the criminal-justice "consequential actions" the bill already enumerates, is worth taking seriously on its own terms — it is close to a codified version of the *Loomis* safeguards, and it directly addresses the specific defect Louisiana's law exhibits. Congress does not need to pass a comprehensive AI civil-rights statute to fix the TIGER problem; a narrow amendment to Louisiana's own 2024 parole statute restoring board discretion and a contestability process would do the job in a single legislative session. But if federal lawmakers want a template for what a properly scoped contestability mandate looks like in this specific domain, this is closer to it than most of what has moved through Congress so far.

## The narrow lesson

Louisiana did not prove that algorithms don't belong in parole decisions. It proved that a state legislature can take a reasonably designed decision-support tool and, through a single statutory change, strip out every safeguard that made it defensible — discretion, contestability, and the possibility of change. The remedy is to restore those three things, not to discard a technology that, properly bounded, produces more consistent outcomes than the unaided discretion it replaced. States drafting their own risk-assessment statutes, and the corrections departments procuring these tools, should treat *Loomis*'s limits and the four-part baseline above as the floor — and treat Louisiana's 2024 law as the case study in what happens when a legislature ignores it.
