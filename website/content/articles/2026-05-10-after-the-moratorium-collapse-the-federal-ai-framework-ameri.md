# After the Moratorium Collapse: The Federal AI Framework America Actually Needs

When the Senate voted [99-1 last July](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/big-beautiful-bill-ai-moratorium-ted-cruz-pass-vote-rcna215111) to strip the AI moratorium from the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, the conventional read was that federal preemption of state AI law was politically dead. That read is wrong — and acting on it would be one of the costlier mistakes Congress could make on technology policy.

The moratorium failed not because the Senate rejected federal supremacy over AI regulation, but because it offered federal supremacy over nothing. A ten-year freeze on state AI laws, with consumer protections governed by an ambiguously worded "undue or disproportionate burden" standard, was not a coherent regulatory architecture. It was a void with a deadline. Senators Marsha Blackburn (R-TN) and Maria Cantwell (D-WA) — who ultimately co-sponsored the amendment to strip the provision entirely — were not rejecting the premise that federal AI policy should take precedence over state experimentation. They were objecting, correctly, to replacing state protections with nothing.

That political distinction matters enormously for what comes next. The 99-1 vote did not foreclose positive federal preemption. It foreclosed *negative* federal preemption — the freeze-and-void approach — and in doing so pointed directly toward the kind of federal AI framework that could actually survive a floor vote. Congress should take the hint.

## What the Moratorium's Failure Actually Tells Us

The politics of the moratorium's collapse are worth reconstructing carefully, because they contain the blueprint for a viable path forward.

The original House language imposed a sweeping ten-year ban on new state AI regulation tied to BEAD broadband funding — a provision that would have put $42.45 billion in federal infrastructure money at risk for any state that passed an AI consumer protection law. After [Senate Commerce Chairman Ted Cruz revised the provision](https://www.govtech.com/artificial-intelligence/u-s-senate-votes-to-strike-moratorium-on-ai-regulation) to address Byrd Rule concerns, the resulting language conditioned funding on states refraining from AI regulation, with narrow exemptions for child safety, deepfakes, and consumer protection — but only where those laws didn't impose what the bill defined as an "undue or disproportionate burden" on AI systems. That qualifier was the provision's fatal flaw. It was a carve-out whose catch swallowed the exception.

Forty state attorneys general and seventeen Republican governors [lined up against the measure](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2025/7/senate-strikes-ai-moratorium-from-budget-reconciliation-bill-in-overwhelming-99-1-vote/8415a728-fd1d-4269-98ac-101d1d0c71e0), not because they are AI enthusiasts, but because their constituents have already been harmed by AI systems — in hiring decisions, credit determinations, medical triage, and law enforcement applications — and the moratorium offered them no substitute remedy. The objection was not philosophical; it was practical. Governors said as much in [their joint statement](https://governor.arkansas.gov/news_post/following-calls-from-sanders-ai-regulatory-moratorium-removed-from-one-big-beautiful-bill/): "We support President Trump's vision of American AI dominance, but we cannot support a provision that takes away states' powers to protect our citizens."

That framing is the political key. The coalition that sank the moratorium was not anti-preemption; it was anti-void. Solve the void problem and the political coalition largely dissolves.

## The Cost of the Resulting Fragmentation

Unfortunately, solving the void problem is not what Congress has done since July. What has filled the space instead is the most expansive state AI legislative surge in American history.

As of March 2026, state lawmakers have introduced [1,561 AI-related bills across 45 states](https://www.multistate.ai/artificial-intelligence-ai-legislation) — already exceeding the full-year total from 2024. The growth trajectory is steep: fewer than 200 AI bills were introduced nationwide in 2023; that climbed to over 600 in 2024 and 1,200 in 2025. This year's pace suggests the total will exceed 2,000 by year's end. These bills cover algorithmic accountability, automated hiring decisions, AI transparency requirements, and high-risk AI deployment — with conflicting definitions, incompatible compliance timelines, and mutually inconsistent enforcement mechanisms.

The compliance burden this creates is not distributed evenly. For the legal, regulatory, and lobbying departments of large technology companies, a 50-state patchwork is a cost center. For a Series A AI startup, it is an existential problem. When autonomous vehicle startup PerceptIn tried to navigate the existing regulatory landscape, it budgeted [$10,000 for AI compliance — and received a bill for $344,000](https://fortune.com/2026/01/30/americas-ai-regulatory-patchwork-is-crushing-startups-and-helping-china/), more than double its R&D costs for the same period. An [analysis in Harvard Kennedy School's Student Policy Review](https://studentreview.hks.harvard.edu/why-compliance-costs-of-ai-commercialization-maybe-holding-start-ups-back/) has quantified the structural dynamic: a 200 percent increase in fixed compliance costs converts a startup's operating margin from positive 13 percent to negative 7 percent — the arithmetic difference between a viable company and a dead one.

The macroeconomic structure of compliance fragmentation is, counterintuitively, a gift to the companies state legislators are most concerned about. Regulatory frameworks that impose substantial fixed costs are easiest to absorb at scale. A Fortune 50 technology company running a compliance budget that dwarfs entire startups can navigate 50 divergent state regimes; the startup that might eventually challenge that company cannot. Compliance fragmentation is not consumer protection. It is an incumbency moat that Congress is currently building on Big Tech's behalf.

That is the cost of congressional inaction since the moratorium's failure. It is a cost that compounds monthly as more states pass conflicting laws.

## H.R. 5388 and the Constructive Path

There is a live legislative vehicle that threads the needle the moratorium missed. [H.R. 5388](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/5388), the American Artificial Intelligence Leadership and Uniformity Act, introduced by Representative Michael Baumgartner (WA-05) in September 2025, establishes a framework that should serve as the basis for congressional action.

The bill does what the moratorium failed to do: it pairs preemption with affirmative content. Rather than freezing state regulation and leaving a void, H.R. 5388 creates a five-year moratorium on new state and local AI mandates that fragment interstate commerce while simultaneously mandating a National AI Action Plan and preserving existing state enforcement of consumer protection and civil rights law. The preemption is positive, not negative — states cannot enact new conflicting AI mandates, but they retain full authority to enforce the legal protections their citizens already have.

That design directly addresses the coalition concern that sank the moratorium. Republican governors and state AGs who objected to the OBBBA provision can read H.R. 5388 and find that state consumer protection enforcement is explicitly preserved. The bill is not asking states to trust that federal law will protect their citizens eventually; it writes that protection into the federal baseline and leaves state attorneys general with authority to enforce it.

The preemption instrument itself is not a new regulatory bureaucracy. H.R. 5388 does not create a new federal AI agency, establish a licensing regime for AI systems, or impose mandatory pre-deployment assessments on low-risk applications. It consolidates the compliance landscape — replacing 50 incoherent state ceilings with a single federal floor — which reduces total regulatory burden for any company operating nationally. This is regulatory consolidation, not more regulation. The compliance overhead of 50-state legal operations is far larger than any federal filing requirement the bill contemplates.

## What Good Federal AI Governance Actually Looks Like

Framing H.R. 5388 accurately requires distinguishing what a well-designed federal AI framework is from what it is not. A recurring risk in AI governance debates is conflating any federal standard with the sweeping horizontal regulatory layer Congress has rightly been skeptical of. The EU AI Act — with its risk-tiered licensing requirements, mandatory conformity assessments, and expansive obligations for "general-purpose AI" providers — represents that model. The United States should not replicate it, for reasons that have nothing to do with the EU's good intentions.

A sound federal framework has five defining characteristics:

**Risk-tiered compliance requirements.** Not all AI systems carry equal risk. A spam filter and a bail recommendation algorithm are both "AI systems" — they should not face the same compliance burden. A coherent federal framework calibrates requirements to actual risk of harm, imposing near-zero overhead on low-risk applications while focusing scrutiny on high-risk deployments in credit, employment, healthcare, and criminal justice contexts. Horizontal frameworks that impose uniform obligations regardless of application are effectively taxes on innovation, not protections against harm.

**Sector-specific enforcement through existing authorities.** The appropriate regulator for AI in healthcare is not a new AI agency — it is the FDA and HHS, exercising existing statutory authority with AI-specific guidance. The appropriate regulator for AI in financial services is the SEC and CFPB. Building AI governance on top of existing sectoral expertise rather than creating a parallel bureaucracy both reduces institutional risk and concentrates regulatory attention where it is most warranted.

**The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) as the compliance backbone.** The NIST AI RMF is already widely adopted by federal contractors and referenced by multiple agency enforcement programs; its 2025 updates addressed generative AI risks and supply chain vulnerabilities. Using the NIST RMF as the compliance reference for federal AI governance creates continuity with what industry has already invested in, rather than requiring companies to rebuild compliance programs from scratch. Voluntary standards that achieve effective adoption are preferable to mandatory regimes that generate paper compliance.

**Preservation of state enforcement of existing law.** The political sustainability of federal preemption depends on states retaining authority over what their citizens already have. State AGs enforcing consumer protection and civil rights law against AI-enabled discrimination or deception are not a patchwork — they are a feature of federalism that has operated in other technology contexts without generating compliance chaos. The preemption target is new, conflicting AI-specific mandates, not existing legal authorities.

**A clear preemption standard.** The moratorium failed in part because its carve-out language — the "undue or disproportionate burden" qualifier — introduced so much interpretive ambiguity that no one could reliably predict what it covered. Good preemption language is precise: federal baseline requirements preempt state-specific AI mandates that go beyond or conflict with the federal standard; state laws of general applicability are unaffected. That line is administrable, which is why it works in other federal preemption contexts.

## The EU Benchmark and the Cost of Further Delay

Congress does not have the luxury of indefinite deliberation on this question. The EU AI Act becomes [fully applicable on August 2, 2026](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/implementation-timeline/) — roughly twelve weeks from now. When that date arrives, American companies deploying AI into EU markets will face a comprehensive, mandatory regulatory framework developed in Brussels, applied by the EU AI Office, and increasingly treated by multinational enterprises as the de facto global standard.

The competitive dynamic this creates is not hypothetical. Regulatory regimes that achieve first-mover status in setting global standards tend to persist. American companies that build compliance programs around the EU AI Act's architecture — its risk classifications, its documentation requirements, its third-party assessment obligations — are implicitly calibrating their compliance infrastructure to European priorities. If Congress eventually acts to create a federal framework, it will face pressure to harmonize with EU rules rather than diverge from them, particularly from companies that have already invested in EU-compatible compliance systems.

The question for American policymakers is not whether AI will be governed by a comprehensive regulatory framework. That question was answered when the EU AI Act entered into force. The question is whether American companies will be governed by a framework designed in Washington or one designed in Brussels. Congressional inaction does not preserve optionality — it forfeits it.

[Stanford's 2026 AI Index](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2026-ai-index-report) found that China has nearly closed the US lead on key performance benchmarks, with the frontier gap narrowing to less than three percentage points. Regulatory fragmentation that raises compliance costs for American AI startups while doing nothing to slow Chinese AI development is not a neutral policy — it is a subsidy to the strategic objective of AI leadership by a country that spends 23 times less than the United States on private AI investment yet is nearly competitive at the frontier.

## The Window Is Not Closed — But It Is Narrowing

The moratorium's failure was a political lesson, not a policy verdict. The Senate did not tell Congress that federal AI governance is impossible; it told Congress what a viable federal AI framework must include. Affirmative protections. Preserved state enforcement authority. Preemption that consolidates rather than voids.

H.R. 5388 incorporates those lessons. Congress should move it — and in doing so should resist two complementary temptations: the temptation to do nothing because the moratorium failed, and the temptation to overreach by constructing the kind of comprehensive regulatory bureaucracy that the EU has built and that would validate critics who warned that preemption was cover for a new federal licensing regime.

The goal is not more federal regulation of AI. It is one coherent federal framework replacing 50 incoherent state ones. That distinction is the difference between an outcome favorable to innovation and a regulatory burden that compounds for years regardless of which government designed it.

The 99-1 vote cleared the decks. The question is whether Congress uses that clearance to build something durable — or leaves the field to Brussels and Sacramento by default.
