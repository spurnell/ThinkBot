On July 16, 2026, the European Commission issued two binding specification decisions ordering Google to restructure core Android and Search functionality. The first, under Article 6(7) of the Digital Markets Act, requires Google to open [11 Android system features](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/320760/20260716/eu-gives-rival-ai-assistants-system-level-android-access-google-reserved-gemini.htm) — voice invocation, contextual awareness, task delegation, and device-resource access — to rival AI assistants, with most obligations due by the Android 18 release and a hard compliance date of August 1, 2027 (concurrent wake-word support follows under Android 19, by August 1, 2028). Users will be able to summon a third-party assistant the way they now say "Hey Google." The second, under Article 6(11), [orders Google to share anonymized Search data](https://digital-markets-act.ec.europa.eu/commission-provides-guidance-google-ai-interoperability-android-and-sharing-google-search-data-under-2026-07-16_en) with competing search engines and AI chatbots, with a pricing offer due by January 2027 under a Commission-defined anonymization protocol and a "fair" pricing formula.

Google's Kent Walker — President of Global Affairs and Chief Legal Officer — called the rulings a threat to "vital privacy and security guardrails for millions of Europeans," adding that the Commission's decisions "discount extensive evidence of user harm." Coverage across [Semafor](https://www.semafor.com/article/07/17/2026/eu-orders-google-to-open-android-ai-system-to-rivals), [Winbuzzer](https://winbuzzer.com/2026/07/17/eu-orders-google-to-open-android-and-search-to-rivals-xcxwbn/), and [MediaNama](https://www.medianama.com/2026/07/223-eu-orders-google-open-android-search-data-ai-rivals/) converges on the same framing: this is the DMA's most aggressive AI-specific intervention to date, and all three name the same beneficiaries — Anthropic, OpenAI, and especially French startup Mistral — as the parties positioned to gain from compelled access.

That naming is the tell. This is not competition policy remedying a proven exclusionary act. It is industrial policy dressed in competition-policy language — and the distinction matters more than the Commission's press materials suggest.

## An ex ante mandate, not an adjudicated remedy

Process is where the DMA departs most sharply from anything a U.S. court would sanction. When Judge Amit Mehta imposed behavioral remedies on Google in the search monopolization case, he did so after a liability trial establishing exclusionary conduct, a remedies trial weighing competing proposals, and an appellate process now underway at the D.C. Circuit. The remedy was calibrated to a specific, litigated theory of harm: exclusive default-placement agreements that foreclosed rivals from the distribution channels that matter. Whatever one thinks of the outcome, it followed evidence tested through adversarial process — the model ThinkBot has long endorsed over both structural overreach and regulatory improvisation.

The DMA's Article 6 gatekeeper regime works backward from that model. A firm is designated a "gatekeeper" based on size thresholds — users, market capitalization, "durable position" — not adjudicated conduct. Once designated, the Commission can issue "specification decisions" — like the July 16 orders — that convert open-ended statutory obligations into bespoke engineering mandates for a named firm, without the evidentiary process a liability finding would require in U.S. antitrust law. There was no finding that Google's assistant integration foreclosed rivals from a relevant market, no *Brooke Group*-style showing of harm to competition rather than to competitors, no counterfactual analysis of what the market would look like absent the challenged design. The obligation exists because Google is designated, not because a specific act was found unlawful — regulation by classification, carrying a far lower burden of proof than the consumer welfare framework demands before the state reengineers a firm's product architecture.

## The security and privacy costs Brussels waves past

The Commission's own materials describe the Search data-sharing order as requiring "multi-layered anonymization" aligned with GDPR principles. That language obscures a genuine problem: query-level search data is notoriously hard to anonymize without destroying its competitive value, and easy to re-identify when combined with other signals rivals already hold. This isn't novel. AOL's 2006 release of "anonymized" search logs was [reverse-engineered within days](https://www.techcrunch.com/2006/08/09/first-person-identified-from-aol-data-thelma-arnold) to identify individual users from nothing but their query history, and a [landmark 2008 study](https://www.cs.cornell.edu/~shmat/shmat_oak08netflix.pdf) on re-identifying Netflix's "anonymized" ratings data has only reinforced the point since. Mandating Google share Search data at scale while treating the privacy problem as solved by a pricing formula turns a hard technical tradeoff into a compliance checkbox.

The Android side raises a parallel problem. Granting third-party assistants system-level access to invocation, context, and device resources — the tier Google currently reserves for its own integration — expands the attack surface of every Android device in the EU. Voice-trigger activation of an arbitrary third-party assistant, task delegation to book taxis or query location data, and on-device context access are precisely the permissions security researchers flag as highest-risk when granted broadly. None of this makes interoperability impossible to do safely; it means these are engineering tradeoffs best resolved by the firms building and securing the product, in dialogue with regulators addressing demonstrated harms — not dictated wholesale by a fixed-date specification decision.

## Vertical integration is a quality axis, not just a moat

The Commission's working theory treats Google's integration of assistant, OS, and search as presumptively suspect — something to pry open rather than a legitimate design choice. But tight integration is not merely a barrier to entry; it is how latency, context-awareness, and personalization — the quality dimensions consumers actually value in an AI assistant — get built. *Ohio v. American Express* (2018) established that two-sided platforms must be evaluated for net effects across the whole platform, not whether one side could theoretically be more "open." The DMA's ex ante model skips that analysis: any firm meeting the gatekeeper thresholds has its integration treated as suspect by default, regardless of whether it is actually harming rivals or is instead the source of the product's value.

The chilling effect cuts both ways. If firms conclude that a well-integrated AI stack invites a mandatory-access order past a size threshold, the rational response is to under-invest in the integration that improves the product — or to architect defensively around compliance rather than around users. That is a bad incentive to institutionalize precisely when assistant-layer competition between Gemini, ChatGPT, Claude, and Mistral's Le Chat is arguably tech's most dynamic contestability story right now.

## Winners named in advance

That every account of the decision names the same three beneficiaries — Anthropic, OpenAI, and Mistral — is not incidental color; it is the clearest signal that this is not neutral market-structure repair. A genuine contestability remedy defines an access right generally and lets entry and competition determine which firms use it. A remedy whose stated purpose is to rebalance the playing field for a shortlist of named firms — one of them a European AI startup the Commission has separate incentive to see succeed under its broader AI sovereignty agenda — is allocation, not market design: the Commission choosing which challengers get subsidized system access, a decision competition authorities are not well-positioned to make. Markets, not commissions, decide which challengers earn distribution.

To be clear, this isn't a defense of Google's product choices, nor an argument against interoperability as such — ThinkBot has consistently backed voluntary interoperability and open standards, from data portability to open APIs to negotiated industry standards, as pro-competitive tools that expand contestability without a regulator dictating terms to one named firm. The objection is narrower: compelled, asymmetric access engineered around specific beneficiaries, absent an adjudicated finding of exclusionary conduct, is a fundamentally different instrument than a voluntary standard or a harm-tethered court remedy.

## Don't import the template

This decision deserves attention beyond Brussels because its architecture — ex ante gatekeeper designation, specification decisions as bespoke design mandates, enforcement timelines set independent of any litigated harm finding — is exactly the model recurring U.S. legislative proposals have sought to import. The American Innovation and Choice Online Act's self-preferencing and interoperability provisions drew nearly identical criticism when first introduced: R Street's analysis of [the revised AICOA](https://www.rstreet.org/commentary/revised-antitrust-bill-remains-fundamentally-flawed/) warned its open-ended data-access mandates would force covered platforms to grant broad data access to "similarly situated" rivals under only vague limiting principles — the same privacy and security objections now surfacing in Brussels. State-level DMA-copycat bills follow the same logic: designate by size, mandate by fiat, skip the adjudicative step.

The consumer welfare standard exists precisely to prevent both failure modes the DMA now illustrates in one decision: under-enforcement against genuinely exclusionary conduct, and over-enforcement that substitutes a regulator's prediction about market structure for evidence of actual harm. U.S. courts adjudicating *United States v. Google* took nearly two years, a full liability trial, and a contested remedies proceeding to reach a narrower, conduct-specific set of behavioral remedies — still working through D.C. Circuit review. Brussels reached a comparably sweeping outcome, covering an installed base that [reaches roughly 60% of EU adults](https://www.semafor.com/article/07/17/2026/eu-orders-google-to-open-android-ai-system-to-rivals), through a specification proceeding with no equivalent adjudicative record. If the Android AI mandate becomes the template American reformers point to next, the burden falls on them to explain why the DMA's process gap, security tradeoffs, and champion-picking are features rather than bugs. They are not. Congress and the FTC should watch this decision closely — as a cautionary tale, not a blueprint.
