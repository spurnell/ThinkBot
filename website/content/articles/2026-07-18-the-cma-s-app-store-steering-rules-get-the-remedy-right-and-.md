On June 30, 2026, the UK Competition and Markets Authority published its first conduct requirements for Apple's and Google's mobile platform Strategic Market Status designations under the [Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/cma-consults-on-new-requirements-for-apple-and-googles-mobile-platforms) — the proposed "steering" rules governing how the two companies must treat developers who point users to cheaper payment options off-platform. Both firms' mobile ecosystems had previously operated under voluntary [Strategic Market Status commitments](https://www.gov.uk/cma-cases/apples-mobile-platform), a weaker mechanism than a binding conduct requirement; this is the first time the CMA has moved either one to the harder instrument. It is the first real test of whether Britain's answer to the EU's Digital Markets Act can distinguish a narrow structural fix from an open-ended regulatory program. On the central question, the CMA gets it right: it would strike down Apple's outright ban and Google's restrictions on developers telling users about cheaper ways to pay off-platform. On the follow-on question — how much platforms may charge for the privilege of being steered around — it gets it wrong, and the July 28 comment window is the moment to say so.

**The permission is the correct remedy.** Anti-steering provisions bar developers from even mentioning, inside their own apps, that a cheaper subscription exists on the open web. That is not a quality-control rule or a security safeguard; it is a restriction on commercial speech that exists solely to prevent users from discovering an alternative price. There is no efficiency rationale on offer — Apple and Google are not protecting users from fraud by hiding a hyperlink, they are protecting a take rate from discipline. The U.S. experience under the *Epic v. Apple* injunction confirms the diagnosis: once Apple was ordered to permit external links, and after the Ninth Circuit and Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers [found the company had tried to functionally recreate the ban through fee-laden compliance](https://www.fenwick.com/insights/publications/ninth-circuit-largely-upholds-ruling-in-epic-v-apple), the practical fight shifted immediately to what platforms could charge for the link, not whether developers could have one. Permitting steering, full stop, is the textbook pro-competitive move: it removes a naked restraint and lets alternative payment rails — card processors, direct billing, wallet providers — compete for the transaction the app store used to capture by default. That is deregulation in the classical sense, and ThinkBot has no quarrel with the CMA acting here; the ban was the harm.

**The fee framework is where the CMA overreaches.** Rather than stop at permission, the draft conduct requirement pairs it with a mandate that any steering fee be "fair and reasonable," assessed against the cost of providing the service and the value it delivers. On paper this sounds like a modest anti-circumvention backstop. In practice it commits the CMA to an ongoing, deal-by-deal rate-setting exercise: auditing platform cost allocations, estimating "value delivered" to millions of distinct developer relationships, and adjudicating disputes over what counts as a legitimate premium versus a punitive surcharge designed to nullify the permission. The CMA's own supporting analysis leans heavily on the Competition Appeal Tribunal's [*Kent v Apple* [2025] CAT 67](https://www.catribunal.org.uk/sites/cat/files/2025-12/14037721%20Dr.%20Rachael%20Kent%20v%20Apple%20Inc.%20and%20Apple%20Distribution%20International%20Ltd%20-%20%20Judgment%20%5B2025%5D%20CAT%2067%2023%20Oct%202025.pdf) ruling, which found "a significant and persistent difference between the price of the services in the App Store and the costs of those services" and rejected Apple's argument that cost-allocation complexity should shield it from scrutiny — a [holding the CMA is now importing wholesale into its framework for both Apple and Google](https://legalblogs.wolterskluwer.com/competition-blog/the-cmas-steering-fee-framework-for-apple-and-google-cost-value-and-a-nod-to-the-cat/), even though *Kent* applied a different legal test (Chapter II/Article 102 abuse doctrine) to a single defendant and remains under appeal. Grafting a live-appeal, single-firm abuse finding onto a permanent, two-firm regulatory standard is exactly the kind of institutional shortcut that produces bad law later.

This is not a hypothetical risk. Regulators who take on open-ended "reasonableness" adjudication tend to get stuck there. FRAND licensing disputes over standard-essential patents — where courts and regulators have spent the better part of two decades litigating what counts as "fair, reasonable and non-discriminatory" — are the cautionary template, not a model to emulate. The UK Supreme Court's own 2020 ruling in [*Unwired Planet v Huawei*](https://www.supremecourt.uk/cases/uksc-2018-0214), which found UK courts competent to set global FRAND rates, did not resolve FRAND disputes; it entrenched English courts as a permanent forum for relitigating them, case by case, patent portfolio by patent portfolio, for years. A "cost of providing the service and the value it delivers" test for steering fees invites the same dynamic at platform scale: thousands of developer relationships, each a candidate for a bespoke cost-plus adjudication, with every marginal dollar of fee difference litigable as either legitimate value-capture or disguised circumvention. The CMA would be appointing itself price-setter for a dynamic, two-sided market it has no comparative advantage in pricing — and inviting exactly the kind of platform gaming (creative cost allocation, manufactured "value" justifications) that *Kent* itself shows firms are already skilled at.

**The regime doesn't need to work this way.** The CMA's own parallel proposal — opening Apple's NFC chip to third-party wallets, with [comments due July 21](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/cma-consults-on-new-requirements-for-apple-and-googles-mobile-platforms) — is closer to the right template: a structural access mandate aimed at a discrete technical interface, not a standing bureaucracy assessing the reasonableness of every transaction that flows through it. It still raises a pricing question for that one interface, but that is a bounded, one-time design choice, not a recurring cost-and-value audit replicated across an entire developer ecosystem indefinitely. Once steering is legal, the disciplining mechanism the CMA is looking for already exists: developers who can point users to a cheaper checkout will do so, and platforms that price steering fees to defeat that option will lose the fee revenue from transactions users route around them entirely. The CMA does not need to referee that outcome in advance.

**The practical ask for the July 28 window**: finalize the steering permission now — it is sound, minimal, and consistent with where U.S. courts have already landed. Drop, or at minimum radically narrow, the fair-and-reasonable cost/value test, replacing it with a simple anti-circumvention floor (no outright fee bans on steering, no discriminatory friction against off-platform links). Commit explicitly to revisiting fee levels only if post-permission data shows steering fees are functionally recreating the ban the CMA just correctly abolished. That is an ex-post correction mechanism triggered by evidence, not an ex-ante rate bureaucracy triggered by the CMA's own discretion. The DMCCA's Strategic Market Status regime is still defining what kind of regulator the CMA intends to be in digital markets. It should choose the version that removes restraints and lets competition do the rest — not the version that quietly becomes a standing price commission for the mobile economy.
