# Preempt the Patchwork: Why Federal AI Law Must Override the States

The failure of the federal AI moratorium was supposed to settle the debate. When the Senate voted [99-to-1 to strip the 10-year state AI moratorium from the One Big Beautiful Bill](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2025/07/alerts-practices-aiml-federal-ai-moratorium-dies-on-the-vine) last July, opponents declared victory: states would keep their authority to regulate artificial intelligence, and federalism would prevail. The celebration was premature.

The moratorium's defeat didn't resolve the underlying problem — it intensified it. On December 11, 2025, President Trump signed an executive order titled ["Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence"](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/), directing the Attorney General to establish an AI Litigation Task Force charged with challenging state AI laws on constitutional and statutory grounds, and directing the FCC and FTC to develop federal standards with preemptive effect. Within weeks, Senate Commerce Committee Chair Ted Cruz [reintroduced his preemption framework](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2025/9/sen-cruz-unveils-ai-policy-framework-to-strengthen-american-ai-leadership) as part of a broader 2026 legislative agenda. Meanwhile, California, Colorado, Texas, Virginia, Utah, and Illinois are all pressing forward with divergent — and in critical respects incompatible — AI governance regimes.

The patchwork isn't a hypothetical risk. It is already here. And without congressional action, it will calcify into a permanent structural drag on America's AI competitiveness.

This is the moment for Congress to act — not with a blunt moratorium, but with a well-designed federal floor that protects consumers, sets coherent national standards, and expressly preempts the structural incoherence now building at the state level. The window is open. It will not stay open.

## The Compliance Tax Is Real, and It Falls Hardest on Challengers

Critics of federal preemption invariably invoke Big Tech as the silent beneficiary: surely Google and Microsoft prefer federal regulation to a patchwork of state rules they can't fully control. The argument has intuitive appeal, but it gets the incentive structure exactly backwards.

Large incumbents can absorb compliance costs across 50 jurisdictions. They have the general counsels, the government affairs shops, and the compliance infrastructure. For a well-capitalized platform already generating tens of billions in revenue, multi-state legal complexity is a cost of doing business — and one that raises barriers to entry for every challenger.

It is the startups, mid-market AI vendors, and enterprise software companies deploying AI in consequential domains that face genuine existential pressure from regulatory fragmentation. [According to ITIF, multi-state regulatory compliance adds approximately 17% overhead to AI system development and deployment costs](https://itif.org/publications/2025/05/30/fragmented-ai-laws-will-slow-federal-it-modernization-in-the-us/) — overhead that a seed-stage company or a Series A startup building on foundation models simply cannot absorb.

Consider what "compliance" actually requires under the current patchwork. [Colorado's SB 24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205), the Colorado AI Act, requires developers and deployers of "high-risk" AI systems — those making or substantially influencing consequential decisions in employment, housing, credit, education, and healthcare — to conduct annual impact assessments, disclose system risks and limitations up the supply chain, and provide consumers with notice and opt-out rights. The law incorporates both disparate treatment *and* disparate impact theories of algorithmic discrimination, meaning an AI system can be found in violation even absent discriminatory intent, if it produces outcomes that disproportionately affect protected groups.

[California's AB 2013](https://kslaw.com/news-and-insights/new-state-ai-laws-are-effective-on-january-1-2026-but-a-new-executive-order-signals-disruption), which took effect January 1, 2026, imposes a fundamentally different obligation: mandatory audit and disclosure of training datasets for all generative AI systems developed or substantially modified since January 1, 2022, covering data sources, ownership, categories of data used, and whether the dataset includes protected intellectual property or personal information. [Texas's Responsible AI Governance Act (TRAIGA)](https://www.bakerdonelson.com/2026-ai-legal-forecast-from-innovation-to-compliance), also effective January 1, 2026, adds yet another layer with its own definitions, applicability thresholds, and enforcement mechanisms.

These aren't parallel implementations of a common framework. They reflect fundamentally different regulatory philosophies — Colorado focused on algorithmic discrimination and outputs, California on training data transparency and provenance, Texas on responsible governance standards with distinct scope definitions. A company building an AI system for employment screening must satisfy potentially conflicting obligations across all three regimes, plus whatever frameworks Virginia, Illinois, and Utah finalize in the coming months.

As [ITIF has documented](https://itif.org/publications/2025/05/30/fragmented-ai-laws-will-slow-federal-it-modernization-in-the-us/), this fragmentation doesn't just drive up compliance costs — it distorts the market, reduces scale, and degrades the quality and availability of AI tools for American businesses and government agencies. The compliance tax is an innovation tax, and it compounds with every new state law.

Federal preemption of inconsistent state rules isn't deregulation. It is rationalization. And rationalization helps challengers more than incumbents.

## Network-Scale Technology Doesn't Respect State Lines

The "laboratory of democracy" argument has genuine force in many policy domains. States experimenting with different approaches to land use, criminal justice reform, or insurance regulation can yield valuable information, and the costs of bad experiments are locally contained.

That logic fails when the regulated object is a global inference call.

AI models are not manufactured in one state and shipped to another. A foundation model trained in a data center in Oregon is queried simultaneously by users in all 50 states, across dozens of countries, in milliseconds. The model doesn't know — and under most architectures, can't know — which state's law governs any given inference. Its outputs are not jurisdictionally differentiated. Its training data was not assembled according to state boundaries. Its risk profile doesn't bifurcate at the California-Nevada border.

When a company deploys an AI hiring tool, it doesn't run separate models for Colorado applicants and Texas applicants — it runs one system. When Colorado requires disparate impact assessment and Texas requires a different set of governance disclosures, the developer must either build a compliance layer satisfying the most restrictive interpretation of both (adding cost and complexity), avoid deploying in one of the jurisdictions (exiting the market), or run afoul of one regime or the other (accepting legal exposure). None of these outcomes serves workers, consumers, or innovation.

For genuinely local harms — a chatbot deployed by a Colorado state agency, an AI system used exclusively within a single hospital system — state experimentation makes sense, and states retain appropriate authority. But for AI models that operate at national and global scale, the laboratory-of-democracy rationale doesn't just strain — it breaks. You cannot run 50 regulatory experiments on the same inference engine.

This is why federal preemption precedents in other network-scale domains are instructive. The [Fair Credit Reporting Act](https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/statutes/fair-credit-reporting-act) established a federal floor for consumer credit reporting while preempting inconsistent state laws — precisely because credit markets operate nationally and require uniform rules to function. [CAN-SPAM](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/can-spam-act-compliance-guide-business) preempted state anti-spam laws to enable a coherent national commercial email ecosystem. [COPPA](https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/rules/childrens-online-privacy-protection-rule-coppa) set uniform federal standards for children's online privacy, heading off 50 conflicting children's privacy regimes before they could emerge. In each case, federal floors preserved consumer protections while preventing the regulatory balkanization that would have made national-scale services legally impossible or economically unviable.

None of these statutes prevented states from acting in adjacent domains. None eliminated consumer protection. All of them enabled national markets to function. That is the model Congress should follow for AI.

## Beijing Is Not Running 50 Regulatory Experiments

The competitive context for this debate cannot be overstated.

China is executing a coordinated, unified national AI industrial policy. The [State Council's New Generation AI Development Plan](https://www.rand.org/pubs/perspectives/PEA4012-1.html) established a strategic roadmap to make China the world's primary AI innovation center by 2030 — a goal embedded in the 14th Five-Year Plan and reiterated at the highest levels of party leadership. In April 2025, President Xi Jinping's Politburo study session [designated AI a "strategic technology"](https://www.ginc.org/chinas-national-ai-strategy/) requiring accelerated national investment.

The Ministry of Industry and Information Technology is executing that strategy across hardware, software, and applications simultaneously. The ["AI+ Manufacturing" initiative](https://www.china-briefing.com/news/understanding-chinas-ai-manufacturing-roadmap-implications-on-fies/) aims to integrate AI across the industrial base, targeting 70% penetration for AI agents and intelligent terminals by 2027 and 90% by 2030. [China's core AI industry is already valued at approximately $97.5 billion](https://ashleydudarenok.com/china-ai-strategy/) — roughly 70% of its own 2030 target — and China now accounts for [nearly 36% of global AI publications and approximately half of the world's AI researchers](https://www.rand.org/pubs/perspectives/PEA4012-1.html).

Chinese AI companies face a single national regulatory framework, coordinated industrial subsidies, and unified government procurement. American AI companies, if the current trajectory continues, face 50 state regulatory frameworks, no federal procurement standard, and a liability landscape that varies by jurisdiction.

The asymmetry is not an abstraction. It shows up in deployment timelines, capital allocation decisions, and the willingness of investors to fund infrastructure-scale AI projects that must navigate multiyear regulatory uncertainty across dozens of jurisdictions. A startup deciding whether to build an AI-powered medical records system in the United States is making a different calculation than it was five years ago — one that now includes the cost of 50-state legal compliance alongside the cost of GPUs and talent.

This is the structural advantage that regulatory fragmentation hands Beijing. Not immediately. Not dramatically. But compoundingly, year by year, as the compliance overhead grows and the gap between coordinated Chinese deployment and fractured American governance widens.

American AI leadership is not guaranteed. It must be chosen — and sustained through policy that enables the national-scale deployment America's global competitive position requires.

## The Litigation Task Force Is a Bridge, Not a Destination

The Trump administration deserves credit for identifying the problem clearly and moving faster than any prior administration to address it. The [December 2025 executive order](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/) is a serious policy document, not a symbolic gesture. The AI Litigation Task Force has real teeth: it can challenge state laws on dormant Commerce Clause grounds, argue conflict preemption under existing federal authority, and condition discretionary federal funding on state compliance. The FCC and FTC directives to develop federal standards with preemptive effect represent genuine regulatory muscle.

But executive action has structural limitations that no amount of administrative energy can overcome.

First, legal viability. The dormant Commerce Clause arguments contemplated in the order face a genuinely uncertain judicial path. Courts have not established a clear doctrinal framework for when AI regulation constitutes an undue burden on interstate commerce, and challenges will generate years of appellate litigation before any decisive resolution. As [NPR's reporting on the order noted](https://www.npr.org/2025/12/11/nx-s1-5638562/trump-ai-david-sacks-executive-order), the administration's legal theories — however creative — do not provide an obvious path to appellate victory.

Second, and more fundamentally: executive action is revocable. The Litigation Task Force exists by executive order. The next administration can dissolve it on day one. The FCC and FTC policy statements contemplated in the order can be reversed through notice-and-comment rulemaking. Companies making ten-year capital allocation decisions — building data centers, signing compute contracts, developing enterprise AI platforms — cannot plan around regulatory certainty that evaporates with each election cycle.

Durable legal certainty that drives long-horizon investment requires statutory authority. Only Congress can deliver the framework that industry needs to invest with confidence. Senator Cruz's [SANDBOX Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2750/text) and his broader [legislative framework for American AI leadership](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2025/9/sen-cruz-unveils-ai-policy-framework-to-strengthen-american-ai-leadership) — with its five pillars including explicit state preemption — represent the right legislative architecture for Congress to build from. The House Energy and Commerce Committee and Senate Commerce Committee have the jurisdiction, the political context, and now the executive branch alignment to move this year.

The Litigation Task Force has bought time. Congress should use it.

## What a Well-Designed Federal Floor Looks Like

Advocates of federal preemption must be precise about what they are — and are not — proposing. A blanket ten-year moratorium on all state AI legislation, as the House originally passed, was the wrong instrument: too blunt, constitutionally dubious, and politically untenable, as the 99-to-1 Senate vote demonstrated. The goal is not regulatory silence. It is regulatory coherence.

A well-designed federal AI framework should have four core features.

**A clear preemption clause targeting inconsistent state laws.** Federal standards should expressly preempt state laws that conflict with, or impose requirements incompatible with, the federal framework — without barring states from acting in genuinely local domains. Child safety protections, state government procurement standards, and AI applications genuinely confined to a single jurisdiction can and should remain subject to state authority, consistent with the carve-outs the December 2025 executive order already recognizes.

**A tiered risk framework grounded in existing sector authority.** High-risk AI applications in healthcare should be regulated primarily through FDA and HHS authority. Financial AI should fall under SEC and CFPB jurisdiction. This is not a gap in the framework — it is the feature. Sector-specific expertise, established liability frameworks, and existing enforcement mechanisms are the right tools for regulating AI in consequential domains. A horizontal federal AI law layered on top of sector regulation creates duplication; a framework that rationalizes sector authority while establishing a preemptive floor for general-purpose AI deployment is more coherent and more durable.

**Minimum national standards for transparency and consumer notice.** A federal floor doesn't mean a consumer-protection vacuum. At minimum, federal law should require meaningful disclosure when AI systems make or substantially influence consequential decisions affecting individuals, establish a right to human review for high-stakes automated decisions, and create clear liability rules for AI-generated harms. These concepts are not novel — they draw directly from the FCRA, which has required adverse action notices and consumer dispute rights for decades. The goal is not to abandon consumer protection; it is to establish it uniformly rather than through 50 inconsistent state regimes.

**A voluntary standards track anchored to NIST, with statutory safe harbors.** The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) represents the right model: an evidence-based, living framework developed through industry and civil society collaboration. Statutory safe harbors tied to NIST RMF compliance would give companies a credible, demonstrable path to responsible AI development without mandating a one-size-fits-all approach. Built-in review cycles — ideally every three years — would ensure the framework keeps pace with capabilities rather than calcifying into obsolete rules applied to a technology it no longer describes.

## The Window Is Now

The political calendar is unusually favorable. The Q2 2026 legislative session is the most hospitable environment for federal AI standards legislation in the current cycle. The executive branch has provided political cover through the December order. Senate Commerce has a framework bill on the table. The House Energy and Commerce Committee has jurisdiction and a favorable majority. And the states — with their divergent, compounding, and increasingly incompatible AI regimes — are making the case for federal action more forcefully with every new law they pass.

The objections to preemption deserve direct engagement rather than dismissal. Preemption of *conflicting* state rules is not preemption of *all* state consumer protection — the federal floor preserves and nationalizes core consumer rights. The case against regulatory speed does not apply here: the problem is not that states are moving fast, but that they are moving in irreconcilable directions. And the compliance burden that regulatory fragmentation creates falls hardest on the startups and challengers that opponents of preemption claim to champion; incumbents absorb multi-jurisdictional overhead, while entrants are deterred by it.

The laboratory-of-democracy argument is compelling for genuinely local problems. AI is not a local problem. It is a global computational infrastructure that American companies built, American researchers advanced, and American capital funded — and that American policy now threatens to fracture through regulatory entropy.

Fifty incompatible state AI frameworks are not federalism. They are a structural drag on the nation's most strategically important technology sector. Congress has the tools, the political alignment, and the moment to fix this. The question is whether it will act before the patchwork becomes permanent.
