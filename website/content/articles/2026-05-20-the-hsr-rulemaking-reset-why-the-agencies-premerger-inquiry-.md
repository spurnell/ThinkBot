Six days from now, on May 26, 2026, the public comment window closes on one of the most consequential regulatory redesign moments in antitrust history. The FTC and DOJ are rebuilding the Hart-Scott-Rodino premerger notification process from scratch — not by choice, but because a federal district court told them they had to.

In February 2026, [Judge Jeremy D. Kernodle of the U.S. District Court for the Eastern District of Texas vacated](https://www.wilmerhale.com/en/insights/client-alerts/20260213-a-very-merry-unbirthday-to-the-new-hsr-form-texas-judge-vacates-final-ftc-rule) the expanded HSR form that had taken effect just one year earlier. The U.S. Chamber of Commerce, Business Roundtable, American Investment Council, and Longview Chamber of Commerce had challenged the rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act. The court agreed: the FTC had exceeded its statutory authority and its cost-benefit analysis was, in the court's own words, "illusory or, at least, unsubstantiated." On March 19, 2026, [the Fifth Circuit denied the Commission's full motion for a stay](https://www.dechert.com/knowledge/onpoint/2026/3/breaking--fifth-circuit-denies-ftc-stay--pre-2025-hsr-rules-rein.html), reinstating the pre-2025 filing regime. The agencies did not seek emergency Supreme Court relief to restore the 2025 form.

While the merits appeal continues, the agencies [launched a parallel fresh public inquiry on March 25, 2026](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2026/03/federal-trade-commission-department-justice-seek-public-comment-premerger-notification-report-form) — a Request for Information inviting public comment on what a new HSR form should look like. The comment window closes May 26.

This is a once-in-a-generation opportunity. The Hart-Scott-Rodino Act was enacted in 1976. The premerger notification process it created has not seen a comprehensive, court-supervised redesign in the five decades since. The RFI is a blank slate. And the question before the agencies — what information does a premerger notification actually need to contain? — is the right question. The answer, grounded in the statute and in the economics of merger review, should be: far less than the agencies tried to demand last time.

---

## I. The Court Got It Right

The 2025 HSR form overhaul was not a modest update. It was a structural expansion of the front-end merger review burden, dressed in the language of efficiency. The agencies required, for the first time, detailed narrative descriptions of competitive overlaps, expanded early document production obligations, disclosure of supply chain relationships, information on products still in development, and data on officer and director interlocks. The FTC's own rulemaking document estimated that [average filing preparation time would increase from 37 hours to 105 hours per filing](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/11/12/2024-25024/premerger-notification-reporting-and-waiting-period-requirements) — nearly tripling the compliance burden — with some practitioners noting the real-world figure would be substantially higher for transactions with complex competitive overlaps.

The legal problem was equally fundamental. The HSR Act grants the FTC authority to require information "necessary or appropriate" for determining whether a pre-merger notification is required and whether the waiting period should be extended. That is a narrow grant. It authorizes the agencies to ask: *should we look at this deal more closely?* It does not authorize a front-loaded discovery exercise designed to substitute for — or predetermine — the second-request investigation.

That is exactly what the Eastern District found. The court concluded that [the FTC's rulemaking exceeded its statutory authority](https://www.aoshearman.com/en/insights/federal-district-court-vacates-expanded-hsr-premerger-notification-rule-key-takeaways) and was arbitrary and capricious because the agency failed to demonstrate that the benefits of the expanded disclosures would outweigh their significant and widespread costs. Judge Kernodle observed that, despite repeated requests, counsel for the FTC could not identify a single illegal merger in the 46-year history of the prior form that the new form would have prevented. The Fifth Circuit's refusal to stay the ruling was a signal that the statutory and APA objections had real force.

The vacatur was not anomalous. It was the administrative law system working as designed. When agencies exceed their statutory mandates and fail to rigorously justify their cost-benefit calculus, courts push back. *Chamber of Commerce v. FTC* is the correct outcome.

## II. The Deal-Timeline Innovation Tax

The procedural stakes of this debate are real and have a direct impact on innovation-driven markets. A merger review process that functions primarily as a burden-maximizing engine — rather than a competitive harm detection tool — distorts transaction decisions upstream, long before filings are ever submitted.

Consider the pipeline problem. For significant deals, parties should plan on [up to 12 months for agency investigation, with an additional 6 to 12 months if they want to preserve litigation rights](https://www.clearygottlieb.com/news-and-insights/publication-listing/ma-2025-in-review-and-a-look-ahead-to-2026) — a total runway of 18 to 24 months in contested cases. For acqui-hires, talent acquisitions, and sub-threshold technology deals, the shadow of the second request creates a rational deterrence effect even when no filing is required: acquirers price in the possibility that HSR compliance will be triggered, drawn-out, and costly.

The 2025 form made this worse by front-loading what had previously been second-request-level inquiries. Narrative competitive overlap assessments, document collections keyed to pre-submission timelines, and supply relationship disclosures are classic second-request demands. Requiring them at the initial notification stage did not eliminate second requests — it added a new layer of pre-filing burden that increased deal uncertainty without meaningfully expediting agency review.

This has consequences for the startup ecosystem that are often underappreciated in antitrust discourse. Venture-backed companies design their financing around expected exit timelines. When merger review uncertainty extends exit windows by 12 to 18 months, it compresses returns to early investors, raises the cost of capital at the Series B and C stages, and distorts founder incentives toward organic growth paths that may not be optimal for the companies or for consumers. A regulatory process that functionally taxes M&A exits is not neutral — it shapes which companies get built and how they are funded.

The agencies themselves acknowledged in [the 2026 RFI](https://www.ftc.gov/system/files/ftc_gov/pdf/2026.03.25-HSR-RFI.pdf) that a core objective of any reformed form should be "avoiding compliance burdens that outweigh the value of the information provided, particularly for transactions unlikely to raise competitive concerns." That is the right principle. The challenge is building it into the form's structure, not merely its preamble.

## III. What a Reformed Form Should Look Like

The agencies' RFI asks the right questions. Which requirements of the 2025 form genuinely improved competitive risk assessment? Which imposed disproportionate costs relative to probative value? Are there transaction categories that could be subject to streamlined reporting, safe harbors, or materiality thresholds? These are good-faith design questions, and the public comment process is the mechanism for answering them rigorously.

The answer, informed by the economics of merger screening and the statutory limits of the HSR Act, turns on three principles.

**First, scope the form to competitive harm signals.** The initial premerger notification exists to answer one question: does this transaction warrant further investigation? The information required should be calibrated to that question. Market definition inputs — overlapping revenue segments, customer categories, and geographic scope — are legitimately relevant. Vertical relationship flags, where they bear on potential foreclosure concerns, have a defensible place. Supply chain specifics, labor market data categorized by BLS occupational codes, and detailed descriptions of products still in development are not screening tools. They are fishing expeditions. A reformed form should draw a bright line between information that informs the screening decision and information that advances a second-request investigation that has not yet been triggered.

**Second, build in proportionality mechanisms.** Not every reportable transaction presents the same competitive risk profile. A transaction between two firms with minimal revenue overlap in uncorrelated markets is categorically different from a horizontal merger of the second and third largest competitors in a concentrated sector. The agencies have signaled they may consider safe harbors or streamlined reporting for categories of transactions less likely to raise antitrust concerns. This is sound policy. A tiered notification system — full disclosure for transactions meeting defined overlap thresholds, abbreviated notification for transactions below them — would concentrate agency resources where competitive risk is highest while reducing burden on the bulk of reportable deals that present no competitive concern at all.

**Third, resist the temptation to expand into adjacent surveillance functions.** The RFI signals the agencies may consider requiring CFIUS-related disclosures, information on contracts with the U.S. government, and data on sovereign wealth fund involvement. Some of these have legitimate national security rationales that belong in CFIUS review — not in the HSR process. The HSR Act is a competition statute. Its premerger notification process is designed to detect competitive harm, not to replicate the functions of CFIUS, OFAC, or federal procurement oversight. Importing national security and government-contractor disclosures into the HSR form would dilute its core competitive harm detection function while adding cost and complexity for filers whose transactions raise no competition concerns.

## IV. Process Reform Is Not Enforcement Softness

A word on framing that matters for this debate. The case for a streamlined, well-scoped HSR form is sometimes caricatured as a brief for deal impunity — a demand from M&A practitioners to weaken merger enforcement. That characterization is wrong, and it should be rejected clearly.

The consumer welfare standard requires that antitrust enforcement be calibrated to actual competitive harm. A merger review process that treats every large transaction as presumptively suspect — loading it with information burdens designed to produce leverage for agency objections — is not rigorous enforcement. It is a caricature of enforcement that substitutes process maximalism for substantive analysis.

The relevant standard, rooted in the economics of antitrust from *United States v. Philadelphia National Bank* through *Ohio v. American Express*, is whether the transaction is likely to harm competition in a defined market. The HSR form exists to screen transactions for that question. A form that does its job well — rapidly identifying transactions that warrant second-request investigation and clearing those that don't — is more enforcement, not less. Agency staff time is finite. Second-request resources devoted to processing irrelevant supply-chain disclosures from benign transactions are resources not spent on investigating deals that actually threaten market competition.

There is also an error-cost argument that practitioners sometimes under-emphasize. False positives in merger review — blocking or deterring pro-competitive transactions — are not costless. When a data-driven acquisition that would produce genuine efficiencies is abandoned because the regulatory timeline makes it economically nonviable, consumers lose those efficiencies. When an acqui-hire that would have accelerated product development is structured differently to avoid HSR thresholds, the outcome is not neutral — it may be worse for competition than a straightforward transaction would have been. A well-functioning merger review process minimizes both false positives and false negatives. The 2025 form, in maximizing upfront burden, raised the risk of false positives without demonstrably improving detection of false negatives.

## V. The Comment Window Is the Mechanism — Use It

The May 26 deadline is not administrative paperwork. It is the statutory mechanism through which the rulemaking record is built. The APA requires agencies to consider comments submitted in response to an RFI when crafting a subsequent rule. A rulemaking record populated primarily by comments from parties favoring expansive process burden will produce a form that reflects those comments. A rulemaking record that includes detailed, economically grounded arguments for a streamlined, competitive-harm-focused form will constrain the agencies' discretion to repeat the errors of 2024.

The [FTC and DOJ's RFI](https://www.ftc.gov/system/files/ftc_gov/pdf/2026.03.25-HSR-RFI.pdf) poses specific, answerable questions: Which requirements of the 2025 form had probative value in proportion to their cost? Which transaction categories are appropriate candidates for safe harbors or streamlined treatment? What is the right scope of narrative overlap descriptions? These are questions that M&A practitioners, in-house antitrust counsel, competition economists, and startup ecosystem participants are uniquely positioned to answer with specificity and evidence.

[Norton Rose Fulbright](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en-us/knowledge/publications/7266a0fe/ftc-and-doj-launch-public-inquiry-into-future-of-hsr-premerger-reporting), [Gibson Dunn](https://www.gibsondunn.com/ftc-and-doj-request-public-comment-on-potential-modifications-to-premerger-notification/), [Sullivan & Cromwell](https://www.sullcrom.com/insights/memo/2026/March/FTC-DOJ-Seek-Public-Comments-Potential-HSR-Changes), and [Davis Polk](https://www.davispolk.com/insights/client-update/antitrust-agencies-request-public-comment-new-changes-hsr-form-and-reporting) have each published detailed client alerts analyzing the RFI's scope. The tools exist. The window is open for six more days.

Venture capital firms and startup founders should be particularly active in this comment process. The RFI's question about whether "certain categories of transactions are less likely to raise antitrust concerns and could be subject to streamlined reporting, safe harbors, or materiality thresholds" is directly relevant to the acqui-hire and early-stage exit market. Comments that provide data on how filing burden affects deal structuring at the sub-$500 million range — the sweet spot for startup acquisition activity — would add evidence to a rulemaking record that too often reflects only the perspective of large-cap strategic transactions.

Trade associations representing the technology sector should be filing detailed comments. The Senate Judiciary Antitrust Subcommittee and the House Judiciary Subcommittee on Courts, Intellectual Property, and the Internet should be monitoring the rulemaking record and, if the agencies' next iteration again exceeds statutory bounds, should consider whether statutory clarification of the HSR Act's information-gathering authority is warranted.

---

## Conclusion

The Eastern District of Texas did not damage merger enforcement when it vacated the 2025 HSR form. It enforced the limits of agency authority under the APA and returned the question of premerger notification design to a process where it belongs: public notice, comment, and a demonstrated justification for every burden the form imposes.

The agencies now have a clean slate and a sophisticated set of questions to answer. The public comment process — closing May 26 — is the moment to fill that record with economically grounded, empirically specific arguments for a form that does its statutory job well: screen transactions for competitive harm, flag those that warrant deeper review, and clear those that don't without consuming the deal certainty and compliance resources that could be spent on genuine competitive analysis.

The HSR process has operated under essentially the same architecture for nearly 50 years. The opportunity to modernize it — not to expand it — comes around once in a generation. [Comments can be submitted through Regulations.gov](https://www.regulations.gov) before May 26, 2026.
