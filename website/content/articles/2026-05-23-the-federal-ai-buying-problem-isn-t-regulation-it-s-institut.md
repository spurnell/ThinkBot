# The Federal AI Buying Problem Isn't Regulation — It's Institutional Memory

The federal government's AI adoption trajectory is, on balance, a success story. Federal agencies documented [710 AI use cases in 2024](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107859) — more than double the 370 catalogued in 2023. The Pentagon is deploying AI-assisted logistics and predictive maintenance. The VA is using machine learning to improve benefits processing times. GSA is piloting AI-driven contract analysis tools. These are wins. They represent genuine operational progress, and they will compound.

But a [Government Accountability Office report published April 13, 2026](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107859) landed with a sobering finding: the four agencies audited — DoD, DHS, GSA, and the VA — are all systematically failing to capture and share what they have learned from AI acquisitions. Despite an explicit mandate in [OMB Memorandum M-25-22](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-22-Driving-Efficient-Acquisition-of-Artificial-Intelligence-in-Government.pdf) directing agencies to document and disseminate AI procurement lessons, the knowledge infrastructure to do so has not materialized. Agencies are spending real money, deploying real systems, making avoidable mistakes — and then losing those lessons to institutional amnesia.

The oversight community will be tempted to read this finding as evidence that agencies are moving recklessly fast with AI adoption and that Congress should pump the brakes. That would be exactly the wrong lesson to draw. The problem GAO identified is not that agencies are buying AI too quickly. It is that the government's knowledge-sharing architecture is too weak to keep pace with the adoption curve it is already on. The fix is not a procurement slowdown — it is better institutional plumbing.

## What GAO Actually Found

[GAO-26-107859](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107859) is a targeted audit, not a sweeping indictment. The report focused specifically on whether agencies are implementing the knowledge-sharing provisions of OMB's AI acquisition guidance — the requirement to document lessons learned, share contract performance data, and contribute to a government-wide AI repository that GSA was directed to build.

On each of these dimensions, the audited agencies fell short. As [Nextgov/FCW reported](https://www.nextgov.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/04/agencies-are-missing-step-share-information-better/412801/), the agencies acknowledged the gap and agreed with GAO's recommendations. That concurrence is telling: these are not agencies that dispute the value of lessons-sharing. They simply have not built the systems or habits to do it, because nothing in the current incentive structure compels them to.

That distinction matters enormously for how policymakers respond. An agency that disputes the value of oversight is a governance problem. An agency that agrees lessons-sharing is valuable but hasn't prioritized it in the absence of enforcement architecture is a coordination problem. Coordination problems have different and more tractable solutions.

The doubling of AI use cases — from 370 to 710 in a single year — is not itself a red flag. It reflects agencies responding to genuine operational needs and to executive-branch AI adoption directives. As [Biometric Update noted](https://www.biometricupdate.com/202604/gao-warns-federal-ai-procurement-is-repeating-the-same-mistakes/), the pattern GAO identified is agencies "repeating the same mistakes" — not novel catastrophic failures, but familiar, preventable ones: inadequate requirements definition, insufficient vendor evaluation capacity, poor contract performance monitoring. These are not uniquely AI problems. They are the predictable consequences of scaling up any new technology category faster than institutional knowledge accumulates.

The solution is to accelerate institutional knowledge accumulation — not to decelerate technology adoption.

## Why OMB's Architecture Misfired

The Trump administration's April 2025 AI policy framework — [M-25-21 on federal AI use](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-21-Accelerating-Federal-Use-of-AI-through-Innovation-Governance-and-Public-Trust.pdf) and [M-25-22 on AI acquisition](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-22-Driving-Efficient-Acquisition-of-Artificial-Intelligence-in-Government.pdf), both of which updated and replaced the Biden-era M-24-10 — is directionally sound. The memos push agencies toward practical risk management, interoperability requirements, and performance-based contracting. M-25-22 specifically directed GSA to build, within 200 days of issuance, an online knowledge repository for AI acquisition lessons. [CDT's one-year retrospective on federal AI guidance implementation](https://cdt.org/insights/one-year-retrospective-on-the-federal-governments-implementation-of-updated-ai-guidance-accelerating-usage-with-incomplete-safeguards/) found that while agencies have accelerated usage, safeguard implementation remains uneven — consistent with GAO's findings.

The fundamental problem is that M-25-22 created the repository without creating the incentives to populate it. GSA was directed to build the infrastructure. Agencies were directed to contribute. But there is no budget signal attached to contributions. There is no audit exposure for non-compliance. There is no consequence — in the form of constrained contracting authority or budget justification friction — for agencies that simply do not feed the system.

This is a well-documented failure mode in government information-sharing initiatives. The [Federal IT Acquisition Reform Act (FITARA)](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/it-modernization/2020/06/10-years-later-why-fitara-never-fully-delivered/), passed in 2014, required CIOs to have meaningful authority over IT investment decisions and established a scoring mechanism to track compliance. The scorecard produced real behavioral change — not because agencies feared the scorecard per se, but because it created congressional attention that translated into budget scrutiny. The AI lessons-sharing mandate has no equivalent accountability mechanism. Predictably, it has produced the same outcome as every other voluntary government data-sharing initiative without teeth: under-contribution and institutional drift.

The lesson is not that OMB's guidance is wrong. It is that guidance without enforcement architecture is a suggestion, and suggestions do not reliably change procurement behavior in large bureaucracies with competing priorities.

## The Real Binding Constraint: Technical Expertise

GAO's institutional knowledge finding points to a deeper problem that no repository, however well-designed, can solve on its own: federal agencies lack the embedded technical capacity to evaluate AI contractor proposals in the first place.

Procurement failures often begin not at the contract performance phase but at the requirements definition and proposal evaluation phases. Agencies that cannot clearly specify what they need from an AI system will get contracts that technically comply with the statement of work while delivering systems that don't perform as intended. Agencies that cannot critically evaluate vendor claims about model accuracy, reliability, and operational constraints will select the wrong vendors. Documentation of lessons learned from a poorly-scoped AI acquisition is useful; not making the mistake in the first place is better.

The [Defense Innovation Unit (DIU)](https://www.diu.mil/) offers a model worth studying. DIU deploys small teams of technical experts — commercial engineers and scientists who understand how AI systems actually work — to help DoD components navigate acquisition decisions for advanced technology. The model is not to centralize AI procurement in DIU, but to provide surge technical capacity to program offices that lack it. The result is better requirements documents, better source selection, and better contract performance management.

This model needs scaling. Not every agency has a DIU equivalent. Civilian agencies facing AI procurement decisions — at DHS, VA, SSA, IRS, and elsewhere — often have neither internal AI expertise nor access to a shared pool of technical advisors who can help them evaluate proposals credibly. The consequence is over-reliance on contractors to define their own requirements, a dynamic that predictably produces expensive, underperforming systems.

A shared federal AI acquisition support capacity — modeled on DIU's technical-expert deployment approach, housed perhaps at GSA's [Technology Transformation Services](https://www.gsa.gov/about-us/organization/federal-acquisition-service/technology-transformation-services) or a joint OMB-GSA technical office — would do more to prevent avoidable AI procurement mistakes than any documentation mandate. Building it would require a modest and justified appropriation, not new legislation.

## Accountability Without Bureaucracy

The GAO findings will prompt proposals for new oversight mechanisms. Some will be constructive. Others will mistake process requirements for results. The distinction matters.

**What constructive accountability looks like:**

*Tie AI acquisition reviews to annual IT budget justifications.* Agencies submit IT investment justifications annually through OMB's Capital Planning and Investment Control process. Requiring agencies to include a summary of AI acquisition performance data — including lessons learned and contract outcomes — as part of CPIC submissions creates a direct budget incentive to maintain the knowledge infrastructure. OMB can implement this under existing authority. No new legislation required.

*Condition GWAC access on repository contributions.* GSA is in the process of establishing a government-wide AI contract vehicle — an AI GWAC — that agencies will want to use for streamlined access to pre-vetted AI vendors at pre-negotiated prices. Conditioning agency participation in this vehicle on meeting AI lessons-sharing requirements is a powerful incentive. Agencies that want the procurement efficiency of the GWAC must contribute to the shared knowledge base that makes the GWAC trustworthy. This is a carrots-not-mandates architecture: the clearinghouse becomes a condition of access to a valuable tool, not a compliance burden layered on top of existing work.

*Make technical review a GWAC prerequisite for vendors, not agencies.* Rather than requiring agencies to undergo pre-approval review before buying AI, require AI vendors seeking inclusion on the GWAC to submit technical documentation — model cards, performance benchmarks, audit trail specifications — as part of their application. This front-loads the technical evaluation at the market-entry stage, where GSA's central capacity can conduct it once, rather than requiring each agency to reinvent the evaluation wheel independently.

**What destructive accountability looks like:**

Pre-approval requirements for individual AI acquisitions would create procurement backlogs without improving outcomes. Mandatory AI impact assessments before every federal AI contract — the kind of proposal that inevitably circulates in post-GAO-report Congressional hearings — would produce compliance theater: lengthy documents that check boxes, consume staff time, and provide little actual protection against poor system performance. The [FITARA experience is instructive](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/it-modernization/2020/06/10-years-later-why-fitara-never-fully-delivered/). The law passed with bipartisan support and high ambitions. A decade later, FITARA scorecards have become annual rituals of marginal relevance, CIO authority over IT investment remains contested in many agencies, and IT modernization has advanced primarily through other mechanisms — largely through GSA vehicles and agency-specific initiatives.

The risk is that Congress, presented with GAO's findings, reaches for a FITARA 2.0 for AI: a prescriptive reform statute with reporting requirements, scoring mechanisms, and oversight mandates that produce paper compliance without functional improvement. The agencies GAO audited have self-imposed policy update deadlines running through mid-2026 — DHS by July 31 and VA by August 1. If they miss those deadlines, the Congressional response will define the policy environment for federal AI procurement for years.

## A Constructive Path Forward

The right response is a three-part package that Congress and OMB can implement through existing authority and modest appropriations:

**1. Establish a GSA AI Acquisition Clearinghouse with real incentive architecture.** GSA should build out the M-25-22 repository as a mandatory condition of AI GWAC participation — for agencies on the buyer side and vendors on the supplier side. The clearinghouse should aggregate contract performance data, lessons learned, model performance benchmarks, and reusable requirements documents. Agencies that contribute get faster access to the most current vendor performance data; agencies that don't contribute can still buy AI but outside the GWAC efficiency. The incentive architecture matters: knowledge-sharing tied to procurement efficiency converts a compliance burden into a competitive advantage for participating agencies.

**2. Fund a shared federal AI acquisition support capacity.** A $50–75 million annual appropriation — modest by federal IT standards — could fund a pool of 200–300 technical experts available on a reimbursable basis to civilian agencies facing significant AI procurement decisions. This is the DIU model extended to civilian government. [CSET has documented the federal government's AI talent gap](https://cset.georgetown.edu/wp-content/uploads/CSET-HUB-Roundtable-Series_Report-2_Government-as-an-Employer-of-AI-Talent.pdf) extensively; a shared acquisition support capacity is one of the most targeted ways to address it without requiring every agency to independently build deep AI engineering bench strength.

**3. Integrate AI acquisition performance into CPIC.** OMB should revise its CPIC guidance to require AI-specific acquisition performance reporting as part of annual IT investment justifications. This creates a recurring budget incentive for lessons documentation without new legislation, while giving OMB's Office of the Federal CIO visibility into agency AI acquisition quality across the enterprise.

## The Strategic Stakes

Federal agencies are going to keep buying AI. The question is not whether but how well. Adversaries and competitors are investing heavily in AI-enabled government operations — [China's military-civil fusion strategy](https://cset.georgetown.edu/publication/pulling-back-the-curtain-on-chinas-military-civil-fusion/) explicitly integrates AI development across government, military, and commercial sectors. An American government that buys AI slowly, badly, or both is not a safer government. It is a less capable one.

The GAO report is a useful diagnostic. It identified a real institutional knowledge failure in federal AI procurement. But the correct response to a scaling challenge is not to slow down the scaling. It is to build the institutional infrastructure that allows scaling to proceed intelligently. A GSA-anchored clearinghouse with real incentive teeth, combined with shared technical acquisition capacity and CPIC integration, addresses the actual problem GAO found without creating new procurement friction that would set back the operational AI adoption the federal government urgently needs.

Congress is watching. The mid-2026 agency deadlines are the near-term test. If agencies demonstrate credible progress on the lessons-sharing architecture before those deadlines arrive, the Congressional response can be constructive: appropriations for the clearinghouse and shared technical capacity. If agencies miss the deadlines, the response will be legislative — and history suggests it will be prescriptive, compliance-focused, and counterproductive.

The better outcome is achievable. It requires OMB and GSA to move faster on incentive architecture than they have so far, and it requires Congress to direct resources at the actual constraint — technical expertise and knowledge infrastructure — rather than mandating another round of paperwork requirements that agencies will dutifully file and then largely ignore.

The federal government doubled its AI footprint in a year. The goal now should be to make that growth smarter, not smaller.
