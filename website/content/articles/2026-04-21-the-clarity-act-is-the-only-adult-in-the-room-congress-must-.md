Nine months after the House of Representatives [passed it 294 to 134](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2025/07/17/us-house-passes-crypto-market-structure-legislation-moves-on-to-stablecoin-vote), the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act sits in Senate limbo — a hostage to stablecoin-yield politics and a vacant CFTC chair. The vote margin should have sent a signal: in an era when Congress struggles to pass resolutions naming post offices, a bipartisan 160-vote margin on a technically complex financial markets bill is extraordinary. The Senate should treat it accordingly.

Instead, Senate Banking and Agriculture committee staff are negotiating stablecoin-yield carve-outs while the rest of the digital asset world moves on. The EU's [Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation](https://www.esma.europa.eu/esmas-activities/digital-finance-and-innovation/markets-crypto-assets-regulation-mica) is fully operational and embedding itself as the de facto global compliance baseline. California's Digital Financial Assets Law [takes effect July 1](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2026/02/registration-under-california-digital-financial-assets-law-begins-march-9-law-takes-effect-july-1), beginning the state-patchwork balkanization that federal legislation was supposed to prevent. And the SEC and CFTC, unable to resolve their jurisdictional turf war through nearly a decade of enforcement action, finally [signed a memorandum of understanding in March 2026](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/03/sec-and-cftc-issue-landmark-joint-guidance-on-classification-of-crypto-assets) acknowledging the confusion — an admission that only Congress can clean up the mess the agencies made.

The CLARITY Act is not a perfect bill. No market structure legislation ever is. But it is the most coherent framework the United States has produced for resolving the SEC-CFTC jurisdictional tangle that has been the single greatest drag on domestic digital asset competitiveness. The Senate should pass it, address the stablecoin-yield disagreement in GENIUS Act implementing regulations — due by [July 18, 2026](https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2026/bulletin-2026-3.html) — and stop letting a narrow banking-industry objection sink a generation-defining piece of market structure legislation.

## The Jurisdictional Vacuum Is the Regulation

The SEC and CFTC have spent nearly a decade using enforcement actions as a substitute for the rulemaking and legislation they were never authorized to produce on their own. The SEC [brought 46 crypto enforcement actions in 2023 alone](https://www.cornerstone.com/insights/press-releases/sec-enforcement-of-cryptocurrency-reaches-a-new-high/) — a 53 percent increase from the 30 actions brought in 2022, which was itself up 50 percent from 2021. The CFTC pursued its own parallel track. Neither agency had clear statutory authority to resolve the foundational question: when is a digital asset a security under the Howey test, and when is it a commodity under the Commodity Exchange Act?

The result is what the Georgetown Law Center on Transnational Business aptly described as [the SEC's enforcement-as-rulemaking playbook](https://www.law.georgetown.edu/ctbl/blog/beyond-enforcement-the-secs-shifting-playbook-on-crypto-regulation/): an approach in which individual enforcement settlements and litigation outcomes serve as the industry's regulatory guidance. As Goodwin Procter [observed in 2023](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2023/05/insights-otherindustries-dcb-regulation-by-enforcement), enforcement actions "are inherently backward-looking in nature" — they tell market participants what was wrong after the fact, not what compliance looks like going forward. Congress reached the same verdict: the CLARITY Act's [legislative findings](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/3633/text) conclude that the current uncoordinated regulatory framework "creates uncertainty, stifles innovation, and pushes legitimate projects overseas" — a damning assessment from the very branch of government that created the underlying statutes.

The March 2026 MOU and joint interpretive guidance — which [classified 16 crypto assets as digital commodities](https://www.jenner.com/en/news-insights/client-alerts/sec-and-cftc-issue-landmark-joint-interpretation-on-crypto-asset-classification) and established a five-category token taxonomy — is being celebrated in some quarters as a breakthrough. It is, at best, a workaround. The joint guidance is an admission that the agencies have been operating in overlapping jurisdictional chaos; that they needed a memorandum of understanding to coordinate their own activities is not a success story, it is an institutional failure that only statutory clarification can remedy. Agency guidance, no matter how thoughtfully drafted, can be revised, withdrawn, or litigated into irrelevance. Legal certainty requires law.

## What the CLARITY Act Actually Does

The [Digital Asset Market Clarity Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/3633/text) is, at its core, a jurisdictional statute — it defines *who* regulates which digital assets and under what conditions, not *how little* regulation applies. Critics who frame it as a deregulatory measure misread the bill. CLARITY preserves robust federal oversight; it simply ends the jurisdictional ambiguity that has made compliance structurally impossible.

The architecture rests on three pillars. First, the CFTC receives exclusive jurisdiction over digital commodity spot markets — assets that, like Bitcoin, function as stores of value or mediums of exchange rather than investment contracts. Second, the SEC retains authority over digital assets that remain investment contract assets — tokens where investors reasonably expect returns derived from the efforts of others, the classic Howey analysis. Third, the bill establishes a "sufficient decentralization" test: assets that begin their lives under SEC jurisdiction can migrate to CFTC oversight once the underlying network achieves sufficient decentralization and the token no longer functions primarily as a claim on issuer effort.

This is not a loophole — it is a recognition that blockchain protocols evolve. Bitcoin and Ethereum are mature, decentralized networks; a newly issued token whose value depends entirely on the promises of its founding team is something else entirely. CLARITY draws that line. Market participants — developers, exchanges, institutional investors, retail users — would at last be able to structure their activities around a knowable regulatory regime, rather than guessing which agency will file the next enforcement action.

The 294–134 House margin reflects genuine bipartisan recognition that this framework is sound. [Jones Day noted](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2025/07/us-house-passes-genius-and-clarity-acts-signaling-bipartisan-support-for-digital-assets) that the vote "signal[ed] bipartisan support for digital assets" at a moment when such signals are rare in financial markets legislation. That consensus deserves respect in the Senate — not indefinite procedural delay.

## MiCA Is Not Waiting for American Comity

While Congress debates, Europe has moved. The EU's [Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation became fully applicable on December 30, 2024](https://www.klgates.com/The-Regulation-on-Markets-in-Crypto-Assets-Becomes-Fully-Applicable-in-All-Member-States-of-the-European-Union-1-24-2025), completing a two-stage rollout that now covers crypto-asset service providers across all 27 member states. MiCA establishes a single licensing passporting regime — get authorized in one EU jurisdiction, operate across all of them — with standardized disclosure, reserve, and conduct requirements. [Skadden's assessment](https://www.skadden.com/insights/publications/2025/07/mica-update-six-months-in-application) after six months of application: the framework is functioning, firms are adapting, and the EU has established the first truly comprehensive statutory baseline for digital asset markets among major economies.

The competitive implication is direct. Any digital asset firm with global ambitions must now understand MiCA compliance. When there is no comparable U.S. framework, European standards become the baseline by default — not because they are superior, but because they exist. This is regulatory arbitrage in reverse: American firms must comply with MiCA to access European markets while facing legal uncertainty at home. The rational compliance response is to build for MiCA first and treat the U.S. market as a secondary consideration, contingent on enforcement risk tolerance.

The World Economic Forum has [noted convergences between the GENIUS Act and MiCA](https://www.weforum.org/stories/2025/09/us-genius-act-eu-mica-convergence-crypto-rules/) on stablecoin standards, which is encouraging on a narrow track. But stablecoins are only one segment of digital asset markets. Without CLARITY, the broader market structure — who can operate spot exchanges, what registration is required, how digital commodity brokers are supervised — remains a U.S. regulatory void that MiCA continues to fill by default.

Congress should want American standards to shape global norms, not inherit European ones. That outcome is still available — but not if the Senate takes another year.

## California Fills the Vacuum on July 1

If MiCA represents the international competitive threat, California's [Digital Financial Assets Law](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2026/02/registration-under-california-digital-financial-assets-law-begins-march-9-law-takes-effect-july-1) represents the domestic policy failure. The DFAL takes effect July 1, 2026. Registration opened March 9. Any entity engaging in "digital financial asset business activity" with California residents must hold a DFAL license by July 1 or have a completed application pending — or cease operating with the state's 39 million residents.

California's framework is not unreasonable on its own terms. The [California Department of Financial Protection and Innovation](https://dfpi.ca.gov/regulated-industries/digital-financial-assets/) has moved carefully, and the DFAL includes sensible consumer protection provisions. The problem is not what the DFAL does — it is that it exists at all as a state-level substitute for a nonexistent federal standard. Other states will follow. New York's BitLicense regime already operates in parallel. Without federal preemption, digital asset firms will face a patchwork of 50 divergent licensing regimes, disclosure requirements, reserve standards, and conduct rules — precisely the fragmented regulatory environment that makes U.S. domicile a competitive disadvantage.

The CLARITY Act was designed to resolve this by establishing a federal framework that preempts state-by-state improvisation. Congressional inaction is not neutral: it actively cedes market structure governance to Sacramento, Albany, and whatever state attorney general decides to make digital assets their next enforcement priority.

## The Stablecoin Fight Is Solvable — Separately

The Senate's impasse centers on a discrete question: whether yield-bearing stablecoins — instruments that pay holders a return on their stablecoin balances — should be permitted under a federal framework. The banking industry, concerned about deposit substitution and uneven competitive footing, has [lobbied aggressively](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/03/10/senators-try-to-unlock-stalled-crypto-clarity-act-with-compromise-on-stablecoin-yield) to restrict or prohibit this feature.

This is a legitimate policy debate. It is also a well-defined one that has its own legislative home: the GENIUS Act, signed into law on July 18, 2025, which established the federal payment stablecoin framework. Federal financial regulators — the OCC, FDIC, Federal Reserve, and Treasury — are required to issue implementing regulations [by July 18, 2026](https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2026/bulletin-2026-3.html). [Treasury has already issued a proposed rulemaking](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0428) on state-level regulatory pathways, and the OCC has issued its own proposed rule. The GENIUS Act rulemaking process is the right venue for resolving stablecoin-yield questions — it is specifically designed for exactly this kind of technical specification.

Allowing the stablecoin-yield dispute to block the CLARITY Act is a category error. CLARITY is a market structure bill addressing jurisdictional authority over thousands of digital assets across spot exchanges, brokers, and dealers. The stablecoin-yield question is a narrow product design question about one class of instruments. These belong on different legislative tracks, and the Senate knows it: by [mid-March 2026, negotiators reported the stablecoin yield question was 99 percent resolved](https://www.fintechweekly.com/news/clarity-act-stablecoin-yield-resolved-community-bank-deregulation-senate-march-2026). Remaining friction is political, not substantive. White House crypto adviser Bo Hines [said as recently as April 13](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/04/13/white-house-s-top-crypto-adviser-witt-says-talks-clearing-other-points-on-clarity-act) that talks are clearing other obstacles, and Coinbase's chief policy officer [predicted a Senate floor vote in May](https://www.disruptionbanking.com/2026/04/17/clarity-act-deadlock-coinbase-chief-policy-officer-predicts-senate-floor-vote-in-may/).

If the May window closes without action, the next realistic floor opportunity likely falls after August — when the Senate's pre-election calendar begins compressing controversial votes. That delay carries a compounding cost: California's July 1 deadline passes without federal preemption, GENIUS implementing regulations lock in stablecoin structures without CLARITY's overarching market framework in place, and MiCA's position as the global baseline deepens with another six months of American regulatory vacuum.

## The Stakes Are High Enough to Act Imperfectly

It is worth noting what the current legislative environment has already done for digital asset markets. The sector raised [more than $16 billion in venture investment in 2025](https://www.houlihancapital.com/the-state-of-crypto-venture-capital-in-2025/) — driven significantly by the regulatory clarity signals of the GENIUS Act's passage and a more cooperative posture from both the SEC and CFTC under new leadership. The March 2026 joint guidance, limited as it is, [contributed to renewed institutional engagement](https://www.alston.com/en/insights/publications/2026/04/sec-cftc-coordinated-crypto-classification) by giving sophisticated market participants a clearer read on the taxonomy of their holdings. These are the fruits of regulatory clarity — partial, imperfect, but real.

What CLARITY offers is the statutory foundation that turns agency guidance into durable law. A joint SEC-CFTC interpretive document can be revised by the next commission. A five-category taxonomy issued in 68 pages of interpretive guidance carries no weight against a subsequent enforcement action under a different legal theory. Legislation does. The digital asset market's 2025 investment surge demonstrates the demand for legal certainty; the CLARITY Act is the supply.

## What Congress Must Do

The legislative moment is as favorable as it has ever been for U.S. digital asset reform — [the Latham & Watkins US Crypto Policy Tracker](https://www.lw.com/en/us-crypto-policy-tracker/legislative-developments), which has monitored federal developments across three administrations, has not logged conditions like these before. The window is open. Congress should use it.

**Pass the CLARITY Act before Memorial Day.** The May floor vote window is real and the White House is engaged. Senate Banking and Agriculture committee leadership should finalize the package and bring it to the floor. A bill this bipartisan should not be a hard whip count.

**Separate the stablecoin-yield question explicitly.** The conference report or accompanying legislative history should direct GENIUS Act implementing regulators to address yield-bearing stablecoin permissibility in the rulemaking process due July 18. This gives the banking industry its fight on a dedicated track without allowing it to veto the broader statute.

**Include explicit federal preemption of state digital asset licensing regimes.** CLARITY's federal framework should preempt state-by-state licensing patchworks — including California's DFAL — for the categories of activity CLARITY covers. Without preemption language, the jurisdictional clarity Congress establishes at the federal level remains subject to erosion by 50 state regulators operating on independent timelines.

The SEC and CFTC have done what they can within their statutory authority — the March 2026 joint guidance represents genuine institutional progress. But the agencies themselves have acknowledged what nearly a decade of enforcement-as-regulation could not accomplish: the jurisdictional question can only be resolved by Congress. The CLARITY Act resolves it. The Senate should act like it.
