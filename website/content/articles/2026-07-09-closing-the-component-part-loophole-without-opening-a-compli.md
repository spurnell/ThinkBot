On July 22, the Federal Communications Commission will vote on a Third Report and Order that closes a real hole in how the United States screens telecommunications hardware for national-security risk. Buried inside a device that has never appeared on any restricted-vendor list, a chipset from a Covered List entity — Huawei, ZTE, or a similar designated supplier — can currently reach the U.S. market because the FCC's equipment-authorization rules were written to evaluate finished devices, not the logic-bearing silicon inside them. The [FCC's tentative agenda for its July 2026 open meeting](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DOC-422735A1.pdf), released July 2, and the accompanying [fact sheet](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DOC-422746A1.pdf) confirm the Commission intends to close that gap, alongside a set of companion measures that reach considerably further: online marketplace liability, mandatory FCC ID display at point of sale, full recertification for Covered List-linked modifications, a redefined "critical infrastructure" standard, and a Further Notice teeing up hardware and software bills-of-materials mandates.

This is not a comment-period trial balloon. The vote is [scheduled for 10:30 a.m. on July 22](https://www.tlp.law/2026/07/02/fcc-releases-july-tentative-open-meeting-agenda/), roughly two weeks from publication of this brief, and it follows a [December 4, 2025 Notice of Proposed Rulemaking](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/12/04/2025-21928/protecting-against-national-security-threats-to-the-communications-supply-chain-through-the) that closed for comment in early February. The record is built; the only question left is what the Commission actually adopts on the 22nd. ThinkBot's view is that the core component-part ban deserves unqualified support — it is the rare instance of a regulator meeting its burden of proof before acting. The ancillary provisions deserve a much harder look, because they convert a targeted fix aimed at a demonstrated security vector into an undifferentiated compliance obligation that will fall hardest on the market participants least equipped to bear it.

## The component-part ban is the fix to endorse without hedging

The rule at the center of the item is straightforward: the FCC would [prohibit authorization of any device that incorporates logic-bearing hardware components produced by a Covered List entity](https://techinformed.com/fcc-supply-chain-plan-could-raise-costs-for-device-makers-and-online-marketplaces/), even where the finished device itself carries no restricted-vendor branding. The draft [defines the covered category narrowly](https://www.lightreading.com/regulatory-politics/carr-aims-to-close-loopholes-on-covered-equipment) — digital components operating above 9 kilohertz (9,000 pulses per second) that process, store, retrieve, transfer, or control data — explicitly excluding passive, "dumb" parts like screws, connectors, and plastic housings. New and pending applications would be covered; previously authorized equipment already on the market would not be reopened.

This is what targeted, evidence-grounded supply-chain regulation looks like, and ThinkBot does not need to hedge in saying so. Three things distinguish it from the kind of speculative, ex ante regulation this fellow ordinarily argues against. First, the predicate risk is not hypothetical: the [Covered List](https://www.fcc.gov/supplychain/coveredlist) is a statutory creation under Section 2 of the [Secure and Trusted Communications Networks Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/116th-congress/house-bill/4998), built on a documented record of espionage and network-integrity risk from designated vendors, not a novel agency theory of harm. Second, the rule is scoped to function, not to guilt by association with an entire product category — it targets the specific hardware layer (logic-bearing components) where the security risk actually lives, rather than sweeping in every part with any connection to a restricted vendor. Third, the record has already survived judicial scrutiny once, and the Commission is responding to a court remand rather than acting on a blank slate (more on that below). A device that embeds a Huawei baseband chip is a genuine security-relevant device regardless of what logo is printed on the case, and closing the pathway that let that chip reach U.S. networks through a compliant-looking third-party product is exactly the kind of narrowly tailored intervention that earns the benefit of the doubt.

## Where the costs actually land: marketplace liability

The item does not stop at manufacturers. It also [clarifies that the FCC's marketing rules reach online marketplaces](https://techinformed.com/fcc-supply-chain-plan-could-raise-costs-for-device-makers-and-online-marketplaces/) — platforms that list, distribute, or offer unauthorized equipment for sale — and requires those platforms to display the FCC ID at the point of online sale and to identify and remove unauthorized listings. The [FCC's own fact sheet argues](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DOC-422746A1.pdf) this should impose only "minimal compliance costs," reasoning that marketplaces already publish product specifications and regulatory marks.

That reasoning holds for the FCC ID display requirement in isolation — that is a metadata field, not an audit. It does not hold for the broader marketing-liability sweep. Displaying an ID that a seller supplies is trivial; verifying that the ID is accurate, current, and attached to the actual unit being sold — across a catalog that for a mid-sized marketplace can run into the hundreds of thousands of third-party SKUs — is a different order of task entirely. CTIA's comments in the docket warned that component-level verification obligations would create ["a bevy of new practical hurdles,"](https://techinformed.com/fcc-supply-chain-plan-could-raise-costs-for-device-makers-and-online-marketplaces/) and the Commercial Drone Alliance noted that a single device can contain hundreds of individual components, each requiring source tracing that manufacturers cannot reasonably certify when supplier disclosures are "beyond their control." A marketplace sits even further downstream from the component supply chain than the manufacturer does — it has no visibility at all into a third-party seller's upstream bill of materials, let alone the logic-bearing status of a chip buried inside a device four supplier tiers removed.

The predictable effect is that large platforms — Amazon, Walmart, Best Buy's marketplace arm — will absorb this as a cost of doing business, building compliance infrastructure they can amortize across enormous transaction volume. Smaller marketplaces, vertical resellers, and independent electronics retailers will face the same undifferentiated obligation without the scale to spread it, and many will respond by narrowing catalogs or exiting device categories altogether rather than accepting open-ended liability for facts they cannot verify. That is not a hypothetical externality; it is the standard mechanism by which broad ex ante compliance mandates entrench incumbents, and it is one the Commission should recognize here. The fix is not to abandon marketplace accountability, but to pair it with a **safe harbor for good-faith reliance on supplier attestations**: a marketplace that requires and reasonably relies on a seller's certification of FCC authorization status should not face the same liability exposure as one that knowingly lists Covered List-linked equipment. That preserves the deterrent against bad actors while not converting every online reseller into a customs inspector for the entire mobile supply chain.

## Recertification should track function, not sourcing history

The draft would also require full recertification for any modification or permissive change to previously authorized equipment made by a Covered List entity. As written, the trigger is sourcing history — any touch by a designated entity — rather than function. That framing risks sweeping in incidental or legacy component relationships that carry no genuine security relevance: a firmware patch delivered through a contract manufacturer that happens to have an immaterial, non-logic-bearing relationship with a Covered List supplier should not trigger the same full-certification burden as a modification that alters or reintroduces restricted logic-bearing hardware.

The Commission should adopt a **materiality threshold tied to function** — does the modification touch RF or logic-bearing components with a Covered List nexus — rather than a threshold tied to whether a Covered List entity appears anywhere in the modification's provenance. This is a small drafting change with a large practical effect: it keeps the recertification requirement pointed at the actual security question (does this modification reintroduce restricted logic into an authorized device) instead of at a sourcing-history checkbox that will catch immaterial relationships alongside real ones.

## The critical infrastructure redefinition is a test of restraint

The Commission's proposed narrowed definition of "critical infrastructure" is not optional housekeeping — it is a direct response to the D.C. Circuit's 2024 decision in *Hikvision USA, Inc. v. FCC*, 97 F.4th 938 (D.C. Cir. 2024), which held that the Commission's prior definition — reaching any system or asset "connected to" a broad set of government functions — was ["arbitrarily broad"](https://www.cooley.com/news/insight/2024/2024-04-09-court-instructs-fcc-to-amend-definition-of-critical-infrastructure-in-equipment-authorization-order) and [remanded the matter](https://www.fcc.gov/document/dc-circuit-denies-pets-review-part-hikvision-usa-v-fcc) with instructions to align the definition and its justification with the statutory text of the underlying National Defense Authorization Act provisions. The Commission's new proposed language — tying critical infrastructure to systems and assets "so vital to the United States that the incapacity or destruction of such systems would have a debilitating impact on" national security, national economic security, or public health and safety — tracks the statutory formulation the court demanded.

That is the right instinct, and Congressional Commerce Committee staff and FCC's Office of Engineering and Technology should hold the Commission to it on the 22nd. A court-ordered narrowing is exactly the moment an agency's discretion is most likely to creep back in through interpretive footnotes and case-by-case application, even while the headline definition looks appropriately cabined. The test is not whether the new definition reads well on paper, but whether it forecloses future expansion into device categories that have no plausible nexus to the infrastructure functions the statute actually describes. A definition anchored to statutory text, applied without a residual "or other purposes the Commission deems relevant" catch-all, is the version that survives the next remand.

## The bills-of-materials proposal deserves the most skepticism

The Third Further Notice of Proposed Rulemaking accompanying the order goes considerably further than the order itself, seeking comment on bifurcating the Covered List into producer/provider-based and production-location-based categories, restricting import pathways for covered equipment, strengthening post-market enforcement — and mandating hardware and software bills of materials (HBOMs and SBOMs) as a standing supply-chain transparency requirement.

This is the provision that most resembles the kind of prescriptive, ex ante paperwork regime ThinkBot generally opposes, and it is worth being precise about why. The FCC's own fact sheet estimates SBOM compliance could cost [under $5,000 per software program and HBOM compliance up to $10,000 per piece of hardware equipment](https://techinformed.com/fcc-supply-chain-plan-could-raise-costs-for-device-makers-and-online-marketplaces/) — the Commission's own floor, not an industry worst case, and one that applies per program and per equipment line regardless of whether that specific product carries any Covered List exposure. The Consumer Technology Association's comments in the docket warned that extending third-party certification-style documentation requirements to equipment currently cleared through the FCC's lower-tier Supplier's Declaration of Conformity process — a self-certification track available for lower-risk devices — "would drastically increase costs and delays without commensurate security benefit." That is precisely the failure mode of a universal BOM mandate: it imposes a fixed compliance cost on every entrant and every product line, scaled to catalog size rather than to actual Covered List risk, at exactly the moment the U.S. is trying to lower barriers for smaller domestic device makers to compete against vertically integrated incumbents.

A universal BOM mandate is not the only way to get supply-chain visibility, and the Further Notice format — seeking comment rather than adopting a rule — gives the Commission room to get this right before it hardens into a Fourth Report and Order. ThinkBot recommends the FCC use this comment cycle to develop a **risk-tiered BOM framework**: full HBOM/SBOM disclosure for equipment categories and manufacturers with documented Covered List exposure (component sourcing history, prior recertification triggers, or import origin flags), and no standing disclosure obligation for equipment with no such nexus. That approach delivers the transparency the Commission is actually after — visibility into the supply chains that pose the risk the rule exists to address — without taxing every device on the market for a risk profile that applies to a small subset of it.

## What Congress and OET should look for on July 22

Commerce Committee staff and the Office of Engineering and Technology have a narrow window to shape how this item lands. Three asks are actionable before the vote: build a good-faith reliance safe harbor into the marketplace liability language rather than leaving platforms exposed for facts they cannot verify; convert the recertification trigger from sourcing history to functional materiality; and direct the Further Notice's BOM inquiry explicitly toward risk-tiering rather than treating a universal mandate as the working default going into the next rulemaking round. None of these changes touch the component-part ban itself, which should be adopted as drafted. The Commission has done the hard part — building a record that ties a specific, statutorily grounded risk to a specific hardware layer. The remaining work is making sure the enforcement architecture around that fix stays proportionate to the risk it targets, rather than becoming a general-purpose compliance tax on everyone who touches a device between the factory and the consumer.
