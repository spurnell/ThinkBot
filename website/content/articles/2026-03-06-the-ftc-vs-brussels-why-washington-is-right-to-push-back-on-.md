# The FTC vs. Brussels: Why Washington Is Right to Push Back on EU Tech Overreach

The Federal Trade Commission has fired the opening shot in what may become the defining transatlantic regulatory confrontation of the decade. [FTC Chairman Andrew Ferguson sent formal letters to 13 major tech companies](https://www.techbuzz.ai/articles/ftc-orders-big-tech-to-reject-eu-digital-services-act) — including Apple, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft — ordering them to reject compliance with provisions of the EU's Digital Services Act that, the agency argues, undermine American free speech rights and consumer privacy protections. Companies were given a deadline to report how they intend to handle the conflict.

The predictable reaction from Brussels has been indignation. The EU's competition chief, Teresa Ribera, has urged the bloc to stand firm. Commentators on both sides of the Atlantic have reached for the "trade war" framing. Some have declared the companies caught in an "impossible choice."

The FTC's principle is sound and overdue. The execution warrants scrutiny. And the companies crying "impossible choice" should know better — they've been navigating jurisdictional fragmentation for decades.

## The DSA Is Not Neutral Technical Regulation

Start with what the Digital Services Act actually requires. Platforms designated as Very Large Online Platforms — those exceeding 45 million monthly active users in the EU — face [mandatory annual independent audits under Article 37](https://www.holisticai.com/blog/rules-for-independent-audits-digital-services-act), compulsory data sharing with government-vetted researchers under [Article 40](https://algorithmic-transparency.ec.europa.eu/news/faqs-dsa-data-access-researchers-2025-07-03_en), obligations to offer non-personalized recommendation feeds, and content removal timelines enforced under threat of fines reaching 6% of global revenue.

Defenders of the DSA frame it as consumer protection — transparency, safety, accountability. That framing obscures the mechanism. When Brussels mandates that platforms conduct risk assessments for content it deems to carry "systemic risks," and when those risk assessments are evaluated by European regulators applying European norms, the result is not neutral oversight. It is the [export of European speech law](https://builtin.com/articles/trump-vs-eu-digital-services-act) to platforms that serve global audiences.

The critical distinction is between the EU regulating conduct *within its borders* and the EU dictating how American platforms govern themselves *globally*. The former is legitimate. The latter is extraterritorial overreach.

## The FTC's Theory Is Coherent

Critics have dismissed the FTC's action as legally strained. It isn't. Chairman Ferguson's letters anchor the directive in Section 5 of the FTC Act — the agency's core authority to prohibit unfair or deceptive acts or practices. When a company represents to American consumers that it maintains strong privacy protections, then weakens those protections to satisfy a foreign regulator's demands, the gap between promise and reality constitutes a deceptive practice under U.S. law.

The [encryption concern is not hypothetical](https://www.techradar.com/computing/cyber-security/ftc-calls-on-big-tech-to-resist-uk-and-eu-demands-to-weaken-encryption-and-censor-content). Both the DSA and the UK's Online Safety Act create regulatory pressure toward content-scanning architectures that are structurally incompatible with end-to-end encryption. If Apple tells American users their iMessage conversations are private, then modifies the system to satisfy a Brussels audit requirement, that is a material misrepresentation. The FTC Act has reached less obvious deceptions.

## The "Impossible Choice" Is a Myth

The loudest objection from industry is that the FTC is forcing companies into an impossible regulatory dilemma: comply with Brussels and face Washington's enforcement; resist Brussels and face EU fines.

This framing is operationally lazy. [Jurisdictional segmentation is standard practice](https://cadeproject.org/updates/us-warns-tech-companies-against-following-eu-and-uk-online-content-rules/) in regulated industries. Financial services firms maintain separate compliance architectures for MiFID II and SEC obligations simultaneously. Pharmaceutical companies run bifurcated clinical disclosure regimes across the Atlantic. Tech companies already apply jurisdiction-specific rules for CSAM detection, sanctions compliance, and local legal takedowns. The argument that content moderation policies cannot be segmented by geography is technically dubious and strategically convenient — it serves companies that would prefer a single global governance architecture centered, by default, on the most demanding regulatory regime.

The EU is entitled to regulate what happens on its platforms, within its territory, for its users. It is not entitled to determine how American companies structure their global operations. That is the line the FTC is drawing.

## What the GDPR Taught Us

This is not the first time Brussels has used compliance costs as a structural weapon. [Research on the GDPR's economic impact](https://cepr.org/voxeu/columns/gdpr-effect-how-data-privacy-regulation-shaped-firm-performance-globally) consistently shows that U.S. Fortune 500 companies spent approximately [$7.8 billion on compliance](https://datainnovation.org/2019/06/what-the-evidence-shows-about-the-impact-of-the-gdpr-after-one-year/) in the run-up to enforcement, while [venture capital investment in EU tech startups fell by more than 26%](https://www.americanactionforum.org/insight/the-price-of-privacy-the-impact-of-strict-data-regulations-on-innovation-and-more/) in the year following enactment. The DSA's audit and transparency mandates extend this compliance architecture to the governance layer of the platform itself.

The December 2025 [Executive Order on AI explicitly framed U.S. innovation primacy as a national security matter](https://datamatters.sidley.com/2025/12/23/unpacking-the-december-11-2025-executive-order-ensuring-a-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence/). The FTC's DSA directive is best understood in that context: not as a one-off trade skirmish, but as part of a deliberate assertion that the United States will not outsource its digital governance to precautionary European regulators. [EU enforcement is only accelerating heading into 2026](https://europeanbusinessmagazine.com/european-news/eu-prepares-tougher-tech-enforcement-in-2026-as-trump-warns-of-retaliation/), and the [transatlantic divergence on digital governance is now structural, not cyclical](https://www.quinnemanuel.com/the-firm/publications/client-alert-key-eu-competition-law-developments-2025-overview-and-2026-predictions/).

## A Note of Caution on Institutional Role

The jurisdictional principle here is correct. But the vehicle matters.

The FTC is a competition and consumer protection agency. Its authority derives from a statutory mandate to protect American consumers — not from trade law, national security prerogatives, or foreign policy. When the agency's directives begin functioning as instruments of trade retaliation, the institutional architecture strains. The FTC's credibility as an independent regulator depends on remaining anchored in its consumer protection mandate — credibility that future administrations of both parties will need.

The right vehicle for asserting U.S. regulatory sovereignty is a combination of USTR trade diplomacy, bilateral negotiations, and — if necessary — WTO dispute mechanisms. Secretary of State Rubio's [reported effort to lobby for DSA amendment](https://www.legal.io/articles/5721294/FTC-Chair-s-DSA-Warning-Draws-Pushback-from-Brussels-and-London-Partners) is more institutionally appropriate than FTC enforcement letters, even if those letters are legally defensible.

The principle Washington is defending is strong enough to win on its merits — in trade negotiations, in courts, and in the court of public opinion. It does not require the FTC to become a foreign policy instrument.

Draw the jurisdictional line. Use the right tools to draw it.
