# The AI Preemption Vacuum: What Happens After the Moratorium Dies

On March 11, 2026, a deadline passed quietly in Washington that most of the AI policy world wasn't watching. Under President Trump's [December executive order on AI preemption](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/), the Secretary of Commerce was required to publish an evaluation identifying state AI laws that conflict with federal policy — and the FTC was required to issue a statement describing how federal deceptive practices law preempts those laws. Whether either report [landed on schedule](https://rcrtech.com/ai-infrastructure/march-11-deadline-hits-for-dept-of-commerce/) remains unclear. What is clear is this: the moment the Trump administration's preemption machinery was supposed to produce results, it arrived in a landscape fundamentally shaped by a political defeat from which Washington has yet to recover.

Last summer, the [Senate voted 99-1](https://www.citizen.org/news/senate-votes-99-1-to-remove-ai-moratorium-from-budget-in-major-blow-to-big-tech-and-sen-cruz/) to strip from the Republican reconciliation bill a provision that would have blocked state and local governments from enforcing most AI regulations for a decade. The amendment — championed by Senator Ted Cruz as a centerpiece of federal AI policy — was not simply defeated. It was annihilated by a coalition of [40 state attorneys general](https://coag.gov/press-releases/attorney-general-phil-weiser-bipartisan-ag-letter-congress-artificial-intelligence-regulations-5-16-25/), [260 state lawmakers](https://ari.us/state-lawmakers-urge-congress-to-drop-ai-law-preemption/), over 130 advocacy groups, and senators from across the ideological spectrum. The political post-mortems [celebrated this](https://epic.org/senate-overwhelmingly-votes-to-strike-ai-state-law-moratorium/) as a blow to Big Tech and a win for state sovereignty.

They're missing the point. The moratorium was the wrong tool — but the problem it was trying to solve is entirely real, is getting worse, and now has no solution in sight.

## Right Diagnosis, Catastrophically Wrong Prescription

The Cruz provision was a blunt, decade-long regulatory freeze covering essentially all AI regulation at the state and local level. That's not a federal framework — it's a veto. It would have preempted not just duplicative or contradictory AI mandates but also targeted, narrowly drawn protections that states have every legitimate interest in enacting. The coalition that formed against it — stretching from the ACLU to conservative state legislators to child safety advocates — was not wrong to oppose it. A 10-year moratorium on all state AI regulation is neither politically sustainable nor substantively defensible.

But the victory laps are premature: killing the moratorium did nothing to address the underlying regulatory fragmentation that made the moratorium politically conceivable in the first place. The problem Congress chose not to solve didn't disappear. It hardened.

## What the Patchwork Actually Looks Like

As of January 1, 2026, [a new cohort of state AI laws took effect](https://www.kslaw.com/news-and-insights/new-state-ai-laws-are-effective-on-january-1-2026-but-a-new-executive-order-signals-disruption) that gives a clear picture of where this is heading. California alone contributed multiple sweeping mandates: the Transparency for Frontier AI Act (TFAIA), requiring large AI developers to publish catastrophic-risk frameworks and manage safety incidents; AB 2013, requiring training data disclosures for public-use generative AI; AB 489, prohibiting AI from impersonating healthcare professionals; and SB 243, mandating chatbot disclosures with specific protections for minors. Texas's Responsible AI Governance Act (RAIGA) came online, prohibiting AI use in a range of "restricted purposes" including algorithmic discrimination and illegal deepfakes, applying broadly to any company conducting business in the state. Illinois continued expanding its AI hiring discrimination law.

And this is only the beginning of the year. Colorado, New York, and a growing list of states are advancing additional AI transparency, algorithmic accountability, and automated decision-making bills. [Experts tracking state AI legislation](https://www.techpolicy.press/expert-predictions-on-whats-at-stake-in-ai-policy-in-2026/) expect 2026 to produce a record number of state AI enactments — accelerated partly by the perception, now confirmed, that Congress will not act.

The result is not merely complex. For a startup building an AI hiring tool, a healthcare AI system, or a generative content platform, the compliance picture is functionally incoherent. California defines a "high-risk AI system" differently than Colorado. Texas's prohibited-use categories don't map cleanly onto Illinois's employment discrimination framework. Training data disclosure obligations vary by jurisdiction. A company operating nationally must parse 50 different — and in some cases actively contradictory — regulatory regimes, with civil liability exposure in each.

Large incumbents can absorb this cost. They have the compliance infrastructure, the legal teams, the regulatory affairs departments. Startups and mid-sized AI developers cannot — not without diverting resources from the actual work of building. That is precisely the outcome good AI governance should avoid.

## The Executive Order's Moment of Truth

The Trump administration came into office with what looked like an executive-branch substitute for congressional action. The January executive order directing agencies to remove barriers to AI leadership was followed in December 2025 by a more targeted order containing an explicit preemption provision: it directed the Attorney General to establish a litigation task force to challenge state AI laws deemed inconsistent with federal policy, and tasked the Commerce Department and FTC with identifying and acting against conflicting state requirements by March 11.

This was always a shaky substitute for legislation, and the deadline's arrival illustrates why. Executive preemption litigation is slow, expensive, and constitutionally uncertain. Courts have consistently held that federal preemption of state law requires clear statutory authority — and without an underlying federal AI statute, the DOJ litigation task force is operating on a thin constitutional premise. The Supremacy Clause doesn't automatically void state laws in the absence of federal law occupying the field; it requires either express preemption language in a federal statute or a clear conflict with federal regulatory objectives. An executive order expressing a preference for federal AI policy is not the same thing as federal law.

The FTC's preemption statement faces similar limits. The Commission can argue that certain state AI mandates conflict with its Section 5 authority over unfair and deceptive acts — but this works only for a narrow slice of AI regulation. State laws governing algorithmic hiring discrimination, training data transparency, or AI safety incident reporting don't obviously conflict with the FTC's existing statutory toolkit. They exist in a regulatory vacuum that the executive branch cannot fill unilaterally.

The Trump administration's instincts here are not wrong. Regulatory fragmentation is a real problem, and executive agencies should use every available tool to push back against particularly burdensome or preemptable state mandates. But instincts are not a substitute for statute. The March 11 deadline has passed. The patchwork remains.

## What a Real Federal AI Framework Should Look Like

The HIPAA analogy is instructive. When Congress passed the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act in 1996, it established a federal floor for health data privacy — a baseline set of patient rights and covered-entity obligations — and expressly preempted conflicting state law while permitting states to enact more protective rules within defined lanes. The result was not uniform silence but structured coexistence: federal law set the rules of the road, and states could go further in designated areas without creating incompatible multi-jurisdictional obligations for covered entities operating nationally.

A federal AI framework built on this model would rest on three pillars.

**Risk-tiered baseline obligations.** Federal law should establish minimum requirements for high-risk AI applications — automated employment decisions, healthcare diagnostics, financial underwriting, critical infrastructure management — calibrated to risk level rather than technology type. The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) provides a ready conceptual foundation for this tiering. Low-risk AI applications should face minimal federal obligations, not a one-size-fits-all compliance regime that treats a consumer chatbot the same as an algorithm making parole recommendations.

**Express preemption of conflicting state requirements.** Federal law should preempt state AI mandates that impose conflicting or duplicative obligations in covered high-risk categories. States would retain authority in areas the federal framework doesn't reach and could enact additional protections that complement — rather than contradict — the federal baseline. This is the HIPAA model: a federal floor, not a federal ceiling.

**Sector-specific delegation to expert agencies.** Rather than creating a single horizontal AI regulator, Congress should delegate AI oversight within each sector to existing agencies with domain expertise — FDA for healthcare AI, CFPB for consumer financial AI, EEOC for hiring AI — with a coordinating role for NIST and OSTP. This avoids the regulatory sprawl and inevitable turf wars of a new standalone AI agency, and leverages the institutional knowledge that sector regulators have already built.

This is not a radical proposal. It is the basic architecture of how American regulatory federalism handles preemption in pharmaceuticals, financial services, and telecommunications. What is radical is the current situation: no federal framework, no preemption, and 50 state legislatures filling the void.

## The Competitiveness Cost of Inaction

The geopolitical stakes deserve more than a footnote. The EU's AI Act — once the nightmare scenario for American AI developers — is [struggling with its own implementation timeline](https://www.onetrust.com/blog/eu-digital-omnibus-proposes-delay-of-ai-compliance-deadlines/), with the European Commission's Digital Omnibus package proposing to push back high-risk enforcement deadlines by up to 16 months and harmonized technical standards still unfinished. That creates a brief window: American AI developers, freed from the EU's compliance burden for the near term, should be positioned to accelerate their lead.

A 50-state patchwork squanders that advantage. The compliance overhead of navigating divergent state mandates falls disproportionately on the mid-sized companies and well-funded startups that represent America's most dynamic AI development capacity. Large incumbents like Google, Microsoft, and Amazon can manage it. The companies that might otherwise challenge them — or that might build the next generation of AI applications — cannot. Regulatory fragmentation functions as a moat for established players, not a check on their power.

Meanwhile, China's AI development faces no equivalent friction. The Cyberspace Administration of China and the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology [issue unified national standards](https://securiti.ai/china-ai-regulatory-landscape/); Chinese AI developers operate under one regulatory regime, however imperfect. American AI policy is handing our primary strategic competitor an organizational advantage that no amount of compute export controls can offset.

## The Way Forward

The Senate's near-unanimous vote to kill the Cruz moratorium carried an implicit message from 99 senators: this is not how you solve the state preemption problem. Fair enough. But the vote came with no alternative — no federal AI framework, no preemption authority, no substitute mechanism. The message to state legislatures was unambiguous: Washington is not coming. Build what you want.

Congress should treat this as the warning it is. The moratorium debate, dysfunctional as it was, revealed something important: there is genuine bipartisan recognition that regulatory fragmentation is a problem. That recognition should be the foundation for a real federal AI baseline — not a moratorium, not executive action dressed up as preemption, but a statute that establishes federal standards, delegates sector-specific oversight to expert agencies, and provides clear preemptive authority over conflicting state mandates.

The window for getting this right is not unlimited. Every month without a federal framework is another month of state legislatures acting — another layer of compliance obligations, another jurisdictional conflict, another reason for an AI startup to think twice about building in a country that can't make up its mind. The moratorium is dead. The problem it was trying to solve is not. Washington needs to try again, with better tools.
