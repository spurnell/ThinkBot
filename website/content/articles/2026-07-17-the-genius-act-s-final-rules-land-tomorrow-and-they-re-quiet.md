Tomorrow, five federal banking regulators — the OCC, the Federal Reserve, the FDIC, the NCUA, and FinCEN — must finalize the implementing rules for the GENIUS Act, the stablecoin framework President Trump [signed into law](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/1582/text) one year ago tomorrow. It is the moment the statute's abstract promise of a "federal framework" for dollar-backed stablecoins becomes binding compliance text, and the terms that have emerged from the rulemaking record are notable less for what they regulate than for whom they will actually bind.

The [OCC's proposed rule](https://occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/bulletins/2026/bulletin-2026-3.html) sets a $5 million minimum capital floor for newly chartered payment stablecoin issuers during an initial three-year de novo period, plus a requirement to hold twelve months of operating expenses in liquid assets — a floor the agency's own recent approvals for national trust banks pursuing stablecoin issuance have already cleared by a wide margin. The OCC's own rulemaking materials note that its experience chartering those trust banks points to minimum capital in the [$6.05 million to $25 million range](https://www.gibsondunn.com/occ-proposes-comprehensive-stablecoin-regulatory-framework-to-implement-the-genius-act/) as what's actually needed to run a viable stablecoin business — well above the floor a first-time applicant would face. Layered on top is a bank-grade Customer Identification Program mandate — the same 31 CFR 1020.220 architecture used for banks and broker-dealers, requiring name, date of birth or formation date, address, and taxpayer ID verification before any mint or redemption, plus five-year recordkeeping — [proposed jointly by the five agencies](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/06/22/2026-12460/permitted-payment-stablecoin-issuer-customer-identification-program) on June 18 and still open for comment through August 21. And the FDIC has confirmed, sensibly, that [reserve deposits backing stablecoins will not carry pass-through deposit insurance to token holders](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/03/11/stablecoins-won-t-get-any-kind-of-deposit-insurance-under-genius-rules-says-fdic-chief) — a disclosure clarification that belongs in a different category from the other two provisions entirely, and one this piece is not contesting.

ThinkBot has flagged the capital floor and the KYC mandate separately as this rulemaking cycle progressed — [the KYC rule's uniform verification standard on July 8](/articles/2026-07-08-genius-act-kyc-rulemaking-stablecoin-oligopoly), [the capital floor's flat-dollar structure on July 14](/articles/2026-07-14-genius-act-capital-floor-stablecoin-concentration). Tomorrow both lock into place at once, which is the point of returning to this: the two provisions are not separate mistakes. They are the same mistake, made twice, converging on the same deadline.

Both are uniform, flat requirements applied identically to a firm processing its first transaction and to the incumbents already processing hundreds of billions of dollars in stablecoin volume. As of mid-2026, two issuers — Tether and Circle — account for roughly 83 percent of the approximately [$315 billion stablecoin market](https://defillama.com/stablecoins), per DefiLlama tracking data. Both already run compliance and capital infrastructure that clears the proposed thresholds without modification, because both have spent years building institutional-grade audit, reserve-attestation, and sanctions-screening operations to satisfy exchange listing requirements, banking partners, and — for Circle — public-company disclosure obligations following its 2025 IPO. A rule calibrated to what the largest incumbents already do is not neutral toward a prospective competitor building that infrastructure for the first time. It is a fixed cost that bites hardest exactly where Congress said it wanted more entrants, not fewer.

That's worth tracing back to the statute itself. The GENIUS Act [passed the Senate 68–30](https://www.sullcrom.com/insights/memo/2025/June/Stablecoin-Legislation-Senate-Passes-GENIUS-Act) and the House with bipartisan support specifically to create — in the words of the [Congressional Research Service's summary of S. 1582](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IN12553) — "a path for nonbank financial firms to compete with banks for payment services" by letting them obtain a limited federal charter. A statute written to expand competitive entry is now being implemented, provision by provision, as a compliance moat that only the entities Congress meant to create competition against can clear without friction. That is not a hypothetical concern; it is a documented pattern in U.S. bank chartering. Federal banking agencies approved an average of 144 new bank charters annually between 2000 and 2007. Between 2010 and 2023, that fell to 71 total approvals — an average of five per year, with four years registering zero — and, as of a January 2025 analysis, the OCC had gone [nearly 1,500 days without approving a single de novo fintech bank charter](https://clsbluesky.law.columbia.edu/2025/01/28/how-to-improve-the-bank-charter-application-process/), according to Columbia Law School's Blue Sky Blog. The mechanism in both cases is the same: capital and compliance requirements calibrated to what a mature, risk-averse regulator finds comfortable rather than to what a genuine startup needs to prove it can operate safely. Regulators writing rules that entrench the firms already inside the perimeter is not a novel failure mode. It is the default outcome of flat thresholds, and it was foreseeable before the ink on these NPRMs was dry.

None of this is an argument against oversight of stablecoin issuance. Sanctions screening, illicit-finance controls, and capital adequate to cover startup losses are legitimate guardrails for an instrument that functions as programmable dollar cash. The argument is narrower: a $5 million flat floor and an undifferentiated CIP mandate are blunt instruments where risk-scaled ones would serve the same safety goals without the same entry cost. Precedent for the alternative already exists inside the same regulatory apparatus — FinCEN's own [2011 Prepaid Access Rule](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-31/subtitle-B/chapter-X/part-1010) sets tiered, transaction-volume-based verification thresholds ($1,000 for reloadable, $2,000 for closed-loop instruments) that have survived judicial scrutiny for over a decade. Congress's own statutory text for GENIUS already directs a "risk-based" AML program tied to transaction volume and customer risk, not a uniform bank-grade floor. The agencies' proposals cite proportionality as an aspiration but do not build it into the actual thresholds.

Two tracks remain open before this locks in fully. The joint KYC/CIP rule's comment period runs through August 21 — the last formal notice-and-comment window on any piece of this framework, and the place to press for a transaction-volume tier or a de minimis threshold modeled on the Prepaid Access Rule rather than a flat, no-exceptions bank CIP mandate. The capital floor finalizes tomorrow, which shifts the lever from rulemaking comment to congressional oversight: House Financial Services and Senate Banking should ask the OCC directly whether its approval-order discretion — the same discretion that let recent trust-bank approvals run well above the $5 million floor — will be used to scale requirements down for smaller applicants, or only ever up. Congress built GENIUS as an on-ramp. Whether it functions as one now depends on choices these five agencies are making this week, not on the statute they're implementing.
