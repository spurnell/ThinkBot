# The Piracy Line: Why Courts Are Getting AI Copyright Right, and Why Congress Must Lock It In

On May 5, five major publishing houses — including Hachette Book Group, Macmillan, and McGraw-Hill — joined novelist Scott Turow in [filing a class-action suit against Meta](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/articles/publishers-and-authors-file-class-action-lawsuit-against-meta-and-zuckerberg-for-willful-copyright-infringement-to-develop-llama-ai-models/) in the Southern District of New York. The complaint alleges that Mark Zuckerberg [personally authorized the use of pirated books](https://variety.com/2026/digital/news/meta-ai-mark-zuckerberg-copyright-infringement-lawsuit-publishers-scott-turow-1236738383/) scraped from LibGen and Anna's Archive to train the Llama series of models — after Meta's internal teams had considered and then abandoned formal licensing negotiations. The suit lands at the same moment the U.S. Copyright Office released its [Part 3 report on generative AI training](https://www.copyright.gov/ai/Copyright-and-Artificial-Intelligence-Part-3-Generative-AI-Training-Report-Pre-Publication-Version.pdf), which declined to declare AI training categorically fair use and expressed a preference for "voluntary licensing" as the framework of choice.

Read together, these two developments crystallize a pivotal moment for AI policy. The courts are, on balance, drawing the right line. Congress now has the opportunity — and the responsibility — to make that line permanent before the content industry lobbies it into something that functions as an innovation tax on every AI developer in America, regardless of how they sourced their training data.

## The Emerging Judicial Framework

The AI copyright docket is producing a coherent body of law faster than critics predicted. Three cases define the emerging framework.

In [*Thomson Reuters v. Ross Intelligence*](https://www.ded.uscourts.gov/sites/ded/files/opinions/20-613_5.pdf) (D. Del. 2025), Judge Stephanos Bibas rejected the fair use defense for an AI legal research tool that trained on Westlaw headnotes — copyrighted editorial summaries — and then competed directly with Westlaw's commercial product. The fourth fair use factor, market harm, was decisive: Ross was building a substitute for the very product whose content it consumed. This is copyright law working exactly as intended.

In *Bartz v. Anthropic* (N.D. Cal. 2025), [Judge William Alsup issued a split ruling](https://www.afslaw.com/perspectives/alerts/landmark-ruling-ai-copyright-fair-use-vs-infringement-bartz-v-anthropic) that crystallized the emerging standard: training on lawfully acquired books was "quintessentially transformative" and protected as fair use; downloading and training on pirated copies was not. The case subsequently [settled for $1.5 billion](https://www.insidetechlaw.com/blog/2025/09/bartz-v-anthropic-settlement-reached-after-landmark-summary-judgment-and-class-certification) — a figure that reflects Anthropic's exposure on the pirated portion of its corpus, not the lawfully acquired one. The case tracks *Thomson Reuters* precisely: the provenance of training data and the commercial relationship between a model's outputs and the original market for those works are what the doctrine turns on.

*Kadrey v. Meta* remains the most directly instructive pending case. A June 2025 ruling found AI model training to be "highly transformative," but the court deliberately left open whether Meta's method of acquisition — [reportedly torrenting approximately 81.7 terabytes from shadow libraries](https://www.bakerbotts.com/thought-leadership/publications/2026/february/out-of-the-shadow-library) including LibGen and Anna's Archive — constitutes independent infringement. That acquisition question is now being litigated more directly in the May 2026 class action. If the evidence supports what the internal documents suggest — executive authorization for what Meta's own employees described as beyond the company's "ethical threshold" — the case should be decided accordingly.

What unites these cases is not novelty — it is the faithful application of four-factor fair use analysis to a new technological context. Courts are not improvising. They are asking the questions the doctrine has always required: What was the purpose and character of the use? What is the nature of the copyrighted work? How much was taken? And what is the effect on the market for the original? That framework, properly applied, produces sound results.

## Why Pro-Innovation Cannot Mean Pro-Impunity

The allegations in the Turow complaint are serious. [The suit claims](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/05/nx-s1-5812623/scott-turow-meta-lawsuit) Meta leadership was informed of the piracy concerns and proceeded anyway, having concluded that the reputational and legal risks were manageable compared to the competitive pressure to ship capable models. If the evidence supports the claims as pleaded, this is precisely the kind of willful infringement that copyright law was designed to deter.

The pro-innovation case for fair use does not require defending data piracy. A company that chooses to scrape shadow libraries after exploring and rejecting legitimate licensing — and does so with executive authorization — is not a victim of regulatory overreach. It is a defendant in a case that ex-post enforcement exists to address. The lawsuit against Meta is not a crisis for AI development; it is the copyright system working correctly.

The distinction matters enormously for the legislative debate now unfolding. Defenders of robust fair use protection for AI training should not conflate Meta's alleged conduct with the practices of developers who built their corpora through lawful licensing, public domain materials, or original data partnerships. Conflating those two populations is the content industry's preferred rhetorical move — it lets advocates point to egregious piracy allegations and argue that the entire AI training enterprise needs mandatory licensing to keep it honest. That argument should be rejected clearly.

## The Copyright Office's Threat Is More Subtle — and More Dangerous

The Copyright Office's [Part 3 report](https://www.wiley.law/alert-Copyright-Office-Issues-Key-Guidance-on-Fair-Use-in-Generative-AI-Training) is a carefully hedged document that declines to reach broad conclusions about fair use. The Office specifically acknowledged that it cannot make categorical determinations — fair use analysis is always case-specific. But the report signals that the Office views extensive commercial use of copyrighted works to generate "competing" outputs with significant skepticism, and it explicitly endorses voluntary licensing as the preferred framework for the AI training context.

That preference is not a legal holding. It is not binding on courts. But in Washington, policy preferences have a way of becoming legislative mandates when an organized industry constituency shows up to lobby for them. The content industries — book publishers, news organizations, record labels, film studios — have been [lobbying actively](https://ccianet.org/news/2026/04/ccia-urges-court-to-affirm-fair-use-protections-for-generative-ai-training/) for a mandatory licensing framework that would require AI developers to negotiate and pay for any use of copyrighted works in training, regardless of whether the output competes with the original works or the developer acquired the works lawfully.

This would be a fundamental error in policy design. The content industry's argument conflates the legitimate grievance (companies that use pirated material should face liability) with a much broader demand (all companies should pay for the right to train on any copyrighted work, full stop). The first proposition is sound law. The second is a tax on the innovation pipeline dressed up in the language of creator protection.

## Mandatory Licensing Is an Innovation Tax, Not a Creator Subsidy

A mandatory licensing regime for AI training data would impose the same compliance burden on a three-person research team training an open-source model on publicly available scientific literature as on a trillion-dollar technology company that scraped a shadow library. This is not creator protection — it is incumbent protection.

Large foundation model companies can absorb licensing compliance costs. They have legal teams, existing relationships with publishers and content consortia, and the revenue to negotiate at scale. The companies that cannot absorb those costs are exactly the ones the AI ecosystem needs most: academic researchers, open-source developers, domain-specific startups, and non-profit AI safety organizations. A mandatory licensing regime does not just raise prices — it raises barriers to entry, concentrating the AI market further among players who can weather the overhead.

[Morrison Foerster's 2026 AI litigation analysis](https://www.mofo.com/resources/insights/260210-ai-trends-for-2026-copyright-litigation) notes that the litigation landscape has already begun to shift focus from training data to model outputs — reflecting the reality that courts are finding training-data claims difficult to sustain when the model does not directly reproduce protected expression in its outputs. A mandatory licensing regime would reverse that trajectory, creating liability upstream regardless of what the model actually does with the data.

The argument that the market has "failed" to produce voluntary licensing deals at fair prices is asserted more than demonstrated. The market for licensed AI training data is genuinely nascent — measured in months, not years. Declaring market failure before the market has had time to develop, and preempting that development with mandatory regulation, is a textbook case of premature intervention.

## The EU Competitive Dimension Cannot Be Dismissed

American policymakers are fond of expressing alarm about European over-regulation of AI while simultaneously contemplating interventions that would impose heavier compliance burdens on US developers than EU-based developers currently face. The irony deserves to be stated plainly.

The EU's [Text and Data Mining exception under Article 4 of the Digital Single Market Directive](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32019L0790) provides a broad TDM right for commercial entities, subject to opt-out by rights holders. That opt-out mechanism is narrower in practice than it appears on paper — it requires rights holders to affirmatively reserve their rights in machine-readable format, and enforcement is diffuse across 27 member states with inconsistent implementation. The net effect is that European AI developers have operated with a relatively permissive legal environment for training data, even as US developers face mounting litigation uncertainty.

A US mandatory licensing regime would flip this relationship. American AI developers would face negotiated licensing costs and compliance overhead that their European counterparts, operating under TDM exceptions, do not. This is not a neutral policy choice — it is a structural competitive disadvantage conferred by statute. [CSET's research on AI market competition](https://cset.georgetown.edu/publication/promoting-ai-innovation-through-competition/) has documented how regulatory overhead falls disproportionately on new entrants and smaller developers; a mandatory licensing gap would compound that asymmetry internationally as training datasets scale and the number of content categories requiring negotiated clearance grows.

## What Congress Should Do

The legislative solution is narrow and does not require Congress to decide anything the courts have not already been deciding case by case. Congress should codify the following standard:

> Training an AI model on lawfully acquired copies of copyrighted works — including works obtained through licensing, public domain acquisition, or authorized access — constitutes fair use when the model's outputs do not serve as a market substitute for the original works in their primary market.

This formulation does four things simultaneously. It confirms fair use protection for developers who built their corpora responsibly. It excludes from that protection the use of pirated or unlawfully obtained data, because "lawfully acquired" is a predicate condition, not an afterthought. It preserves creator rights where the genuine harm lives — market substitution. And it establishes clear ex-ante guidance that reduces litigation uncertainty without dismantling the fair use doctrine.

What Congress should emphatically not do is enact a mandatory licensing regime — whether framed as a "voluntary licensing" facilitation mechanism, a collective rights organization structure, or a statutory royalty scheme. Each of those structures converts a provenance-based fair use defense into a fee-based license, taxing every developer regardless of their data practices. The piracy cases will be litigated and, if the evidence supports the claims, decided against the bad actors. That is how enforcement is supposed to work.

## Conclusion

The AI copyright debate has been distorted by a false dichotomy: either AI developers receive a blanket exemption from copyright, or they owe licensing fees for every word their training data contains. Neither position is correct, and neither reflects where the courts are heading.

The emerging judicial framework is more sophisticated and more workable than either extreme: lawful acquisition plus transformative use constitutes fair use; piracy and market substitution do not. This is four-factor fair use analysis applied faithfully to a new set of facts. It protects creators from genuine market harm. It deters the conduct alleged against Meta. And it preserves the legal foundation on which responsible AI development rests.

The Judiciary Committees should move on a narrow, targeted fair use codification and resist the pressure to substitute mandatory licensing for a doctrine the courts are already getting right. The window is open. The jurisprudence is there. The competitive stakes are clear.
