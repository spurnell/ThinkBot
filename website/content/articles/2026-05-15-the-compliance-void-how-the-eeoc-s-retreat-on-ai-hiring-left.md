Type `eeoc.gov/ai` into your browser today and you will get a "page not found" error. That broken link is not a technical glitch. It is a policy choice — and its consequences are now reverberating through every boardroom where HR technology decisions get made.

In January 2025, the Trump administration quietly removed the EEOC's suite of AI employment guidance documents from its public website, a move consistent with [Executive Order 14179](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/01/31/2025-02172/removing-barriers-to-american-leadership-in-artificial-intelligence)'s directive to federal agencies to roll back existing AI regulatory frameworks. Gone were the technical assistance documents explaining how Title VII's disparate-impact doctrine applied to resume-screening algorithms, how the Americans with Disabilities Act governed AI-assisted applicant assessments, and how employers could self-audit for algorithmic bias. But what the administration did not remove — could not remove without an act of Congress — was the underlying liability. Title VII, the ADA, and the Age Discrimination in Employment Act remain fully operative. The [EEOC's FY 2024–2028 Strategic Enforcement Plan](https://www.eeoc.gov/strategic-enforcement-plan-fiscal-years-2024-2028) still explicitly identifies "technology-related employment discrimination" as a priority area for investigation and litigation. The agency has authority; it simply no longer has anything to say about how employers should exercise theirs.

This is not regulatory retreat. It is enforcement without guidance — a structurally incoherent posture that serves neither workers, who deserve protection from algorithmic bias, nor employers, who cannot comply with a standard they cannot interpret. And into the vacuum left by a silent federal enforcer, six jurisdictions have rushed forward with their own frameworks — frameworks so procedurally incompatible that satisfying all of them simultaneously may be geometrically impossible.

## The Structural Incoherence

To understand why the current situation is genuinely untenable, consider what the EEOC's guidance documents actually were — and were not. They were not rulemaking. They did not create new substantive obligations. They were technical assistance — agency explanations of how pre-existing law applied to novel technology. When the EEOC published its May 2023 [technical assistance document on AI and Title VII](https://www.eeoc.gov/newsroom/eeoc-launches-initiative-artificial-intelligence-and-algorithmic-fairness), it was not expanding employer liability; it was clarifying that liability already existed under the [Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-29/subtitle-B/chapter-XIV/part-1607) (29 CFR Part 1607) and the adverse-impact doctrine established in *Griggs v. Duke Power Co.* (1971).

Removing that guidance document changed nothing about the underlying doctrine. An employer using an AI resume screener that disproportionately rejects Black applicants at a rate triggering the four-fifths rule is just as exposed today as it was in 2023 — perhaps more so, because it now lacks any agency-blessed methodology for demonstrating good-faith compliance. The [Cooley analysis of the post-guidance landscape](https://www.cooley.com/news/insight/2025/2025-02-21-gone-but-not-forgotten-federal-laws-still-apply-despite-guidance-disappearance-act) put it succinctly: federal anti-discrimination law is "gone but not forgotten" from the EEOC's website, but not from employers' legal exposure.

The Strategic Enforcement Plan makes this tension particularly acute. [HR Dive's coverage](https://www.hrdive.com/news/eeoc-2024-2028-strategic-enforcement-plan-workplace-discrimination/694689/) of the SEP noted that the EEOC explicitly updated its "emerging and developing issues" priority to include algorithmic discrimination — codifying enforcement intent in the strategic plan even as the Trump administration removed the technical assistance documents that told employers how to comply. Maximum enforcement intent, zero interpretive output.

## Two Incompatible Regimes — and Counting

The federal silence has not produced a regulatory vacuum so much as a regulatory cacophony. At least six jurisdictions have enacted or are actively drafting AI employment frameworks, and the two most mature ones cannot be satisfied by a single compliance program.

**Illinois.** Governor J.B. Pritzker signed [HB 3773 (Public Act 103-0804)](https://www.hinshawlaw.com/en/insights/blogs/employment-law-observer/illinois-adopts-new-ai-in-employment-regulations-what-employers-need-to-know-for-2026) on August 9, 2024; it took effect January 1, 2026. The law amends the Illinois Human Rights Act to prohibit the use of AI that results in disparate impact on any protected class — regardless of discriminatory intent — across the full employment lifecycle, from recruitment through termination. Crucially, it also requires affirmative notification to applicants and employees whenever AI is used "to influence or facilitate" an employment decision, whether or not any discrimination occurred. Failure to provide that notice is itself an independent violation of the IHRA. Illinois's framework is outcome-focused and disclosure-mandatory: an employer can use AI in hiring so long as it monitors for disparate impact *and* tells every affected applicant it is doing so.

**New York City.** [Local Law 144](https://www.nyc.gov/site/dca/about/automated-employment-decision-tools.page) takes a fundamentally different approach. Rather than imposing outcome testing on employers, it mandates process: an annual independent bias audit of any automated employment decision tool (AEDT) used in hiring or promotion decisions in New York City. The audit must be conducted by an independent auditor with no financial interest in the tool — vendors cannot audit their own products. Results, including selection rates and impact ratios by demographic category, must be publicly posted. Candidates must be notified before an AEDT is used, informed of what data it collects, and given an accommodation pathway if they object. The [New York City Office of the State Comptroller's December 2025 audit report](https://www.osc.ny.gov/state-agencies/audits/2025/12/02/enforcement-local-law-144-automated-employment-decision-tools) documented persistent compliance gaps, suggesting enforcement will tighten. Civil penalties run $500 to $1,500 per day per violation — potentially multiplied across every affected applicant.

The structural incompatibility is not incidental. Illinois wants employers to self-monitor for disparate impact and notify workers proactively. NYC wants third-party auditors to test tools annually and post the results publicly. An employer operating in both jurisdictions must maintain parallel compliance infrastructures governed by different definitional frameworks, different audit methodologies, different notification triggers, and different enforcement mechanisms. The [Jones Walker analysis](https://www.joneswalker.com/en/insights/blogs/ai-law-blog/whose-rules-govern-the-algorithmic-boss-state-ai-employment-laws-federal-preemp.html) of this jurisdictional collision warns explicitly of a "coming litigation wave" as plaintiffs' counsel exploit definitional gaps between state regimes.

The patchwork is expanding. [Colorado's SB 24-205](https://www.hrdive.com/news/state-laws-regulating-ai-take-effect-in-the-new-year-what-hr-needs-to-know/807125/) takes effect June 30, 2026, requiring risk assessments, worker notice, attorney general notification within 90 days of discovering algorithmic discrimination, and appeal pathways for affected applicants. Texas's [Responsible Artificial Intelligence Governance Act (TRAIGA)](https://www.ailawsbystate.com/blog/ai-hiring-laws-by-state-compliance-map), effective January 1, 2026, takes the narrowest position of any enacted state law — prohibiting only AI deployed with *intent* to discriminate and explicitly clarifying that disparate impact alone does not establish a violation — creating a direct legal conflict with Illinois's intent-agnostic framework. Maryland, New Jersey, and Washington are drafting their own measures. The [National Law Review's tracker](https://natlawreview.com/article/patchwork-ai-hiring-laws-create-rising-compliance-risks-employers) characterizes the resulting landscape as "rising compliance risks for employers" with no harmonization in sight.

## The Standard That Already Exists

Here is the frustrating irony embedded in this entire situation: the substantive legal standard for evaluating AI-driven employment decisions has existed since 1978. The [Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-29/subtitle-B/chapter-XIV/part-1607) (29 CFR Part 1607), jointly adopted by the EEOC, the Department of Labor, the Department of Justice, and the Civil Service Commission, establish adverse-impact doctrine as the governing framework for any employment selection procedure — defined broadly enough to encompass algorithmic scoring systems, ranking algorithms, and automated resume filters.

The four-fifths rule is the operational shorthand: if the selection rate for any protected group is less than 80 percent of the rate for the group with the highest selection rate, adverse impact is presumed and the employer bears the burden of demonstrating job-relatedness and business necessity. The rule is outcome-focused and technology-neutral. It does not care whether a selection procedure is a written test from 1965 or a transformer-based resume embedding model from 2024. It measures what the procedure *produces*, not how it *works*. This is precisely the right frame for AI hiring tools, where the internal mechanisms of ensemble models and scoring distributions are often opaque even to their developers.

The [Mayer Brown analysis](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2023/07/eeoc-issues-title-vii-guidance-on-employer-use-of-ai-other-algorithmic-decisionmaking-tools) of the EEOC's now-deleted guidance confirmed this framing: the agency's 2023 technical assistance applied the four-fifths rule directly to AI selection procedures, emphasizing that employers should conduct self-analyses to assess whether their tools produce adverse impact and, if so, whether alternative tools exist with lesser discriminatory effect. The guidance did not require interpretability mandates, model documentation schemes, or third-party architectural audits. It asked a single question: does the tool produce discriminatory *outcomes* at rates that trigger adverse-impact liability?

The substantive standard is adequate. The application guidance is missing. Those are not the same problem, and conflating them — as state legislatures have done by layering process mandates onto outcome standards — produces compliance complexity without proportionate worker protection.

## Who Gets Priced Out

The distributional consequences of multi-state procedural compliance deserve attention that the policy debate rarely affords them. A bias audit under NYC Local Law 144 is not a bureaucratic checkbox. Third-party auditors with the independence and technical capability to conduct defensible AEDT assessments charge anywhere from $5,000 to $50,000 or more, depending on tool complexity and the volume of historical data required. That figure is annual — it recurs every year, for every covered tool, regardless of whether the audit reveals any discriminatory outcomes. Colorado's impact assessments, worker notice programs, and AG-notification obligations add further fixed-cost layers.

For a Fortune 500 company with a dedicated legal compliance team and established relationships with employment law firms, these costs are manageable overhead. For a 200-person regional employer using an off-the-shelf applicant tracking system with AI-ranking features, they are potentially prohibitive. The [Harris Beach Murtha analysis](https://www.harrisbeachmurtha.com/insights/ai-assisted-hiring-in-2026-managing-discrimination-risk/) of 2026 AI hiring compliance estimates that only a minority of mid-market employers have adequate documentation frameworks to support the audit requirements now in effect — not because they are acting in bad faith, but because the compliance infrastructure required is disproportionate to their operational scale.

The market effect is predictable and perverse. Procedural compliance burdens that large enterprises can absorb become barriers to entry that smaller employers cannot clear. The employers who get priced out of AI-assisted hiring do not stop hiring — they revert to unstructured human review, which carries its own well-documented bias risks and none of the auditable outcome data that algorithmic tools produce. And the vendors who survive the compliance gauntlet are those with the legal resources to navigate multi-jurisdiction risk — incumbent platforms with established enterprise relationships, not the startups whose tools might actually be more accurate and less biased. Fragmented state regulation, in this domain as in others, consolidates market power among incumbents while claiming to protect workers from it.

## The Legislative Dead End

Washington has gestured at solutions without delivering them. The "One Big Beautiful Bill" budget reconciliation package that [passed the House in May 2025](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2025/7/senate-strikes-ai-moratorium-from-budget-reconciliation-bill-in-overwhelming-99-1-vote/8415a728-fd1d-4269-98ac-101d1d0c71e0) included a provision imposing a ten-year moratorium on enforcement of state and local AI laws — a sweeping preemption mechanism that would have suspended Illinois HB 3773, NYC LL 144, and every other enacted state framework pending the development of a federal alternative. The Senate stripped that language in a vote of [99 to 1](https://reason.com/2025/07/01/senate-votes-99-1-to-remove-ai-moratorium-from-big-beautiful-bill/), a rare display of bipartisan consensus that Congress was not prepared to preempt state AI regulation wholesale without first establishing federal protections to replace it. That was the right call. Preemption without a federal floor is not a solution; it is an abdication.

[Executive Order 14365](https://www.presidency.ucsb.edu/documents/executive-order-14365-ensuring-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence), issued in December 2025, directed a Federal AI Litigation Task Force to identify state AI laws suitable for constitutional challenge. That approach may produce some relief from the most aggressive state frameworks, but litigation proceeds slowly, selectively, and unpredictably — and it produces no positive guidance that employers can actually use. The [Pillsbury analysis](https://www.pillsburylaw.com/en/news-and-insights/new-executive-order-national-policy-framework-artificial-intelligence.html) of the order's workforce implications notes the administration's March 2026 "National AI Legislative Framework" is a policy document, not draft legislation, and includes no directive to the EEOC specifically.

The result: two years of federal action have produced maximum confusion and no workable compliance pathway.

## A Framework That Would Actually Work

The solution does not require a new substantive legal theory. It requires Congress to direct the EEOC to do what technical assistance documents exist to do: explain how existing law applies to novel technology.

Specifically, Congress should authorize and direct the EEOC to issue binding guidance — not merely technical assistance subject to deletion by the next administration — tethered to the four-fifths rule and the Uniform Guidelines. That guidance should address three concrete application questions that the current silence leaves unanswered:

**First, how to apply the four-fifths rule to probabilistic AI outputs.** A resume-ranking algorithm does not produce a binary hire/reject decision; it produces a score or percentile ranking from which human decision-makers draw a cutoff. The guidance should specify how selection rates are calculated across continuous scoring distributions and how the analysis extends to multi-stage screening pipelines where AI filters at the top of the funnel and human reviewers make final decisions.

**Second, vendor contractual accountability.** Most employers using AI hiring tools did not build them. They purchased or licensed them from vendors who often disclaim responsibility for discriminatory outcomes in their terms of service. Guidance should clarify that Title VII liability attaches to the employer regardless of vendor contractual structure, and should specify minimum contractual transparency obligations — specifically, the data and demographic performance information employers need from vendors to conduct four-fifths analysis on their own.

**Third, candidate notification on request.** Rather than the affirmative blanket notification regimes Illinois imposes (notify every applicant, for every decision, regardless of outcome), a federal framework should require employers to inform candidates upon request that an AI tool was used in a decision and to provide the general categories of data the tool assessed. This is disclosure as a safeguard, not disclosure as a compliance ritual.

This framework should be structured as a federal floor: employers who satisfy these three requirements are shielded from state-specific procedural mandates that go further — annual third-party audits, AG-notification triggers, appeal-pathway obligations — while remaining fully liable under Title VII and the ADA for substantive disparate-impact violations. Substantive anti-discrimination protection survives intact. What gets preempted is the procedural fragmentation that imposes compliance costs without proportionate worker protection.

## Conclusion

The EEOC's January 2025 guidance deletion was not a deregulatory act. It was a governance failure dressed as one. Deregulation reduces burden. This left the burden fully in place while stripping the interpretive infrastructure employers need to meet it. Workers who face algorithmic discrimination cannot vindicate their rights effectively when the agency responsible for enforcement declines to say what compliance looks like. Employers acting in good faith cannot build adequate compliance programs against a standard that the federal government refuses to interpret.

The four-fifths rule needs no reinvention for the AI era. It measures what selection procedures produce, not how they work — precisely the right frame for algorithmic systems whose internal mechanics are often opaque even to their developers. What it needs is application: durable, binding agency guidance that tells employers how to calculate adverse impact across probability distributions, how to structure vendor contracts to ensure access to underlying demographic data, and when good-faith compliance has been demonstrated. Congress has the authority to direct that work. The Senate HELP Committee has the jurisdiction. The EEOC still has the expertise, even if the current administration has directed it to stand down.

The choice before policymakers is not between federal guidance and worker protection. It is between coherent federal guidance and a state patchwork that protects large employers from competition while leaving workers to navigate six different enforcement regimes depending on their zip code.
