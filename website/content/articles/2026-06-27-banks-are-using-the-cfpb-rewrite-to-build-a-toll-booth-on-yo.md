The CFPB's open banking rule is dead — for now — and the banks are moving fast to reshape what comes next.

In October 2024, the CFPB finalized its [Personal Financial Data Rights rule](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/personal-financial-data-rights/) under Section 1033 of the Dodd-Frank Act, requiring large financial institutions to provide standardized, machine-readable access to consumers' own financial data. The rule included a key competitive provision: banks could not charge data aggregators — the Plaids and MXs of the world — for access to data consumers themselves had authorized. By November 2025, a federal court in the Eastern District of Kentucky had enjoined the CFPB from enforcing the rule. By mid-2026, the Bureau is [racing to finalize an interim replacement](https://www.americanbanker.com/news/cfpb-to-issue-interim-final-rule-on-1033-open-banking) before its [operating funds run dry](https://www.consumerfinancialserviceslawmonitor.com/2025/12/cfpb-signals-issuance-of-interim-final-rules-on-section-1071-and-section-1033-amid-funding-constraints/).

In the vacuum between the injunction and the rewrite, the financial incumbents are erecting exactly the infrastructure the original rule was designed to prevent: a toll road on consumer financial data.

## The Fee Is the Fight

The original Section 1033 rule's prohibition on data-access charges wasn't a minor technical provision. It was the load-bearing wall of the entire open banking architecture.

Open banking works — for consumers and for the fintechs that serve them — only when the data flows freely. Neobanks, personal finance apps, automated savings tools, and fintech lenders all depend on real-time access to account data to deliver their services. If incumbent banks can charge aggregators per-call fees for that access, the economics of the fintech business model collapse. The fees don't punish Plaid and MX in the abstract; they punish the tens of millions of American consumers who use those platforms to manage their money.

The CFPB's [reconsideration docket](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/rules-policy/rules-under-development/personal-financial-data-rights-reconsideration/) lists fee permissibility as one of four open questions. The Bureau has asked whether the fee prohibition represents "the best reading of the statute" and whether permitting fees would obstruct the data-access right Congress contemplated. These are not academic questions. They will determine, in the next 60–90 days, whether America's open banking regime becomes a competitive platform or a bank-controlled rent-collection scheme.

## The Toll Road Is Already Under Construction

The regulatory uncertainty isn't theoretical. It is already producing the market outcome that fee prohibition was designed to prevent.

JPMorgan Chase began notifying data aggregators in mid-2025 of new tiered charges for accessing customer financial data, with fees taking effect in September 2025. [The schedule runs from $0.05 for basic balance queries to $1.25 for new account linking](https://www.ceotodaymagazine.com/2026/02/jpmorgan-data-access-fees-open-banking-explainer/) — each call, not each month. By November 2025, the bank had [reached paid-access agreements with several major aggregators](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/11/14/jpmorgan-chase-fintech-fees.html), including Plaid, Yodlee, Morningstar, and Akoya. For Plaid alone, estimates put the annual cost exposure [as high as $300 million](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/banking-law/jpmorgan-plaid-data-fee-deal-shifts-open-banking-battlefield) — a figure that reportedly exceeds three-quarters of the company's 2024 revenue.

JPMorgan's stated rationale is system load: the bank recorded [1.89 billion API requests from aggregators in June 2025](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/07/28/jpmorgan-fintech-middlemen-plaid-data-requests-taxing-systems.html), of which it claimed only 13% were tied to direct customer activity. But infrastructure cost recovery and incumbency rent extraction produce identical pricing behavior in the short term, and only one of them is consistent with the statute's purpose. The Financial Data and Technology Association called it ["a cynical attempt to take advantage of regulatory uncertainty."](https://www.paymentsdive.com/news/fintechs-blast-jpmorgan-over-data-fees/753021/) The Financial Technology Association's CEO was equally direct: ["Charging for financial data access undermines that freedom and threatens to jeopardize millions of Americans' access to financial services of their choice."](https://www.bankingdive.com/news/fintechs-blast-jpmorgan-over-data-fees-open-banking-dimon-plaid-aggregators-block-mx-dave/753087/)

The competitive dynamic here is textbook. Every other custodian bank is watching. When an interim final rule arrives without a fee prohibition, the behavior will generalize across the industry immediately. The market structure effect would be durable and difficult to reverse.

## The Consumer Welfare Standard Cuts One Way

Not every regulatory mandate serves consumers. The relevant question is whether this intervention advances competition and consumer welfare — or whether it shields producers from market discipline.

Here, the analysis runs in one clear direction. Banks did not build their data infrastructure in a competitive vacuum — they built it from captive customer relationships, retail deposits, and decades of regulatory protection. The consumer data in those systems belongs to the consumer, not the bank. Charging fintech aggregators to access data a consumer has explicitly authorized is not a cost-recovery mechanism; it is a tax on the switching that keeps consumers locked into legacy institutions.

The pro-market, pro-competition position is clear: data portability at zero marginal cost is what genuine competition in financial services requires. Allowing fees inverts the rule's competitive purpose entirely. It transforms a consumer data right into a bank revenue line.

## The Fee Prohibition Has a Real Legal Problem — and a Path Forward

The original rule's legal vulnerabilities were genuine, and the most important one bears directly on fees. [The Eastern District of Kentucky's injunction](https://www.cozen.com/news-resources/publications/2026/section-1033-compliance-date-open-banking-rule-enjoined-and-under-reconsideration) explicitly found that the CFPB likely lacked statutory authority to prohibit interface-access fees, holding that so broad an economic mandate requires clear congressional authorization — authorization the court concluded Section 1033 does not supply.

That finding cannot be dismissed. But it does not foreclose a fee prohibition in a revised rule. A preliminary injunction applies a likelihood-of-success standard, not a binding final judgment. More importantly, the court's objection was to the CFPB's framing, not to fee restrictions as a category. A more targeted prohibition — one that ties fee limits explicitly to the consumer's statutory right to access and transfer her own data, rather than imposing a sweeping economic mandate — stands on firmer textual ground and is less vulnerable to the court's reasoning.

The fee prohibition and the rule's other procedural defects are legally separable issues. The CFPB can remedy the APA compliance failures that doomed the original rule while reconstructing the competitive architecture on a narrower, more defensible statutory hook. The Financial Technology Association has already [intervened in the litigation](https://www.pymnts.com/bank-regulation/2026/data-aggregators-push-secure-access-as-rule-1033-rewrite-looms/) to press this position. That is the right approach.

## America Is Falling Behind Open Economies

The international comparison is instructive — not as a regulatory template to import, but as evidence of what genuinely open data rails produce.

The UK's open banking regime, built without a carrier-fee model, now serves [more than 17 million users](https://www.fca.org.uk/news/news-stories/open-banking-2025-progress) — nearly one in three British adults. A March 2026 analysis commissioned by [Open Banking Limited and EY](https://www.openbanking.org.uk/news/43bn-annual-open-banking-opportunity-for-the-uk-economy/) found the full open banking opportunity could reach £43 billion annually for the UK economy. The EU's PSD2 framework, built on the same principle of mandatory fee-free data access for authorized third parties, has produced hundreds of licensed account information and payment initiation businesses across the European Economic Area — all catalogued in [the European Banking Authority's public register](https://www.eba.europa.eu/risk-and-data-analysis/data/registers/payment-institutions-register) — and is now advancing toward PSD3. Australia's Consumer Data Right is [extending open banking obligations to non-bank lenders from July 2026](https://www.cdr.gov.au/rollout).

These regimes work because they resolved the fee question correctly: data portability is a consumer right, not a bank product. If the U.S. allows its interim rule to introduce a fee regime, American fintechs will face a structural cost disadvantage against competitors building on open data infrastructure in every peer economy.

## What the CFPB Must Do

The Bureau's interim final rule is expected this summer. The answer on fees is not complicated:

**Preserve the prohibition — and build the statutory case for it.** Consumer-authorized data access must not become a bank toll road. The CFPB should anchor the fee restriction explicitly in the consumer's right under Section 1033, rather than framing it as a broad economic mandate. Narrow, transparent allowances for genuine infrastructure maintenance — subject to Bureau review and cost justification — are a workable accommodation. Blanket per-call charges calibrated to fintech revenue are not.

The regulatory window is closing. Once an interim rule lands without a fee prohibition, the market structure will calcify around the incumbent advantage. The CFPB's reconsideration docket will have done the banking lobby's work for it — not through any explicit policy choice, but through silence at exactly the moment it mattered most.
