The Trump White House [released its National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/) in late March 2026 with a direct message to Congress: preempt state AI laws that impose "undue burdens" on the development and deployment of artificial intelligence in the United States. The framework didn't bury the ask in a footnote. It made federal preemption a centerpiece of the administration's AI strategy — recognition that the legislative landscape is fracturing in ways that threaten American competitiveness at the worst possible moment.

The scale of that fracturing is striking. [More than 1,500 AI-related bills](https://www.multistate.ai/artificial-intelligence-ai-legislation) have already been introduced across 45 states in the current legislative session, already surpassing the full-year total for 2024. Indiana, Utah, and Washington have [moved from introduction to enactment](https://www.orrick.com/en/Insights/2026/04/AI-Law-Center-February-March-2026-Updates), adding new sector-specific AI mandates to the books. California continues to advance its own constellation of requirements. Texas, Colorado, and Illinois are not far behind. [AI enforcement is accelerating as federal policy stalls](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2026/04/ai-enforcement-accelerates-as-federal-policy-stalls-and-states-step-in) — state attorneys general are already pursuing enforcement actions under existing consumer protection frameworks while simultaneously pushing for new AI-specific obligations.

Congress has twice had the chance to act on preemption and twice declined, [rejecting provisions in both the National Defense Authorization Act and the "One Big Beautiful Bill."](https://www.fisherphillips.com/en/news-insights/congress-again-drops-bid-to-block-state-ai-laws.html) The administration, for its part, [has signaled openness to a "give and take" on state preemption](https://www.nextgov.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/03/white-house-official-advocates-give-and-take-state-ai-preemption/412407/) rather than a blunt federal override — a pragmatic posture that should give both sides room to reach agreement. The question is whether Congress will take the opening before the patchwork becomes so entrenched that rationalization becomes politically impossible.

Federal preemption with a light-touch national floor is not regulatory overreach. It is the only outcome consistent with preserving the unified national market that made American tech dominance possible in the first place. A balkanized compliance regime of 1,500 inconsistent state bills is not an expression of regulatory federalism — it is a structural barrier to entry that will protect incumbents, suffocate startups, and ultimately cede the AI race to jurisdictions with coherent national strategies.

## The Compliance Burden Falls Hardest on New Entrants

Start with what compliance actually means for an AI company operating at national scale. Under the emerging state patchwork, [a company faces liability exposure under potentially dozens of distinct AI regimes](https://www.wsgr.com/en/insights/2026-year-in-preview-ai-regulatory-developments-for-companies-to-watch-out-for.html), each with its own definition of what constitutes a "high-risk" AI system, its own audit and documentation requirements, its own disclosure mandates, and its own enforcement mechanisms and penalty structures. An algorithm that qualifies as high-risk in Washington might not meet that threshold in Indiana. An audit procedure sufficient in Utah may fall short of what California requires. The definitional variation alone creates a compliance nightmare; the liability exposure compounds it exponentially.

Large technology incumbents — the Googles, Microsofts, and OpenAIs of the world — can absorb this friction. They have legal teams, compliance infrastructure, and lobbying capacity to track and adapt to state-by-state variation. The compliance cost is a line item in a budget measured in billions. For a Series A startup trying to deploy a healthcare AI tool in all 50 states, or a mid-sized enterprise building hiring software for a national client base, the same compliance obligation is not a line item — it is potentially an existential barrier.

This is the innovation tax in practice. When we talk about the burden of a fragmented regulatory landscape, we are not primarily talking about inconvenience to trillion-dollar firms. We are talking about the systematic foreclosure of market entry by the next generation of AI companies. States that believe they are protecting consumers by enacting AI mandates may in fact be protecting market incumbents by erecting barriers that only incumbents can clear.

The Commerce Clause exists precisely to prevent this outcome. The founders understood that allowing individual states to erect idiosyncratic commercial barriers would balkanize the national economy and undermine the competitive dynamism that a unified market produces. AI systems do not respect state borders — a model deployed in one state is instantaneously accessible in all 50. The case for applying the same federal preemption logic that governs telecommunications, financial services, and product safety standards to AI is not merely strong. It is compelled by first principles.

## Europe Already Ran This Experiment

The United States should not have to learn this lesson empirically. Europe already ran the experiment, and the results are instructive.

Before the EU AI Act came into force, European AI development operated under a patchwork of member-state regulations, data protection interpretations, and sector-specific requirements that varied meaningfully across the bloc. The result was not regulatory innovation — it was regulatory uncertainty that chilled investment, slowed deployment, and drove AI development activity toward the United States, where a clearer and more permissive environment prevailed. European entrepreneurs and researchers built AI companies here, not there, precisely because U.S. regulatory coherence made scale achievable.

The EU's eventual solution — a horizontal, comprehensive AI regulation — has its own serious problems. [As scholars have documented](https://www.theregreview.org/2026/03/10/rangone-the-paradoxes-of-the-european-unions-ai-regulation/), horizontal AI laws tend to entrench incumbents, misallocate compliance costs, and substitute bureaucratic categorization for genuine risk assessment. The EU AI Act is not a model to emulate. But the pre-Act fragmentation that preceded it is an even worse model — and that is precisely what the United States is now constructing voluntarily on its own soil.

The strategic irony is acute. American policymakers spent years arguing, correctly, that Europe's regulatory environment was a competitive handicap. Now, through inaction at the federal level and hyperactivity at the state level, the United States is poised to replicate Europe's worst mistake in the jurisdiction that currently leads global AI development. If this patchwork calcifies, the beneficiary will not be American consumers. It will be Beijing.

## Preemption Is Not Deregulation

The strongest objection to federal preemption frames it as a race to the bottom — a federal override that strips states of their consumer protection authority and leaves AI systems operating in a regulatory vacuum. That framing is wrong.

Federal preemption in the AI context means establishing a national floor that addresses demonstrated harms, not eliminating regulation. The [White House framework's explicit carve-outs](https://www.mofo.com/resources/insights/260402-trump-administration-releases-national-ai-policy-framework) for child safety and fraud enforcement are the right model. A coherent federal standard should prohibit AI-enabled fraud with real enforcement teeth, ban nonconsensual deepfake pornography nationwide, and provide clear guardrails against AI systems deployed to exploit children. These are not hypothetical risks — they are demonstrated harms with bipartisan political salience and clear federal jurisdiction.

What preemption opposes is not consumer protection per se. It is the idiosyncratic, inconsistent, and frequently duplicative set of compliance requirements that states are layering on top of each other with no coordination and no common definitional framework. The distinction matters: a light-touch federal floor with genuine enforcement against demonstrated harms is stronger protection than 50 inconsistent regimes that confuse both consumers and firms. Regulatory complexity is not the same as regulatory strength.

Opponents of preemption like to invoke the "laboratories of democracy" framing — the idea that states serve as experimental policy spaces whose innovations eventually percolate up to the federal level. That framing has genuine merit in many policy domains. It does not translate to AI. AI systems operate across state lines instantaneously. A model deployed by a national employer in Minnesota simultaneously applies to workers in Arizona, Florida, and New York. Inconsistent state liability standards for that system don't produce experimental learnings — they produce forum shopping, defensive deployment decisions, and a chilling effect that falls hardest on the smallest entrants. Laboratories of democracy work when the subjects of regulation stay within the laboratory. AI does not.

## The Bipartisan Case for Federal Action

Congressional reluctance on preemption has been driven partly by genuine federalism concerns and partly by political calculation — no member wants to be seen as stripping states of their authority to protect constituents from AI harms. These concerns are not unreasonable, and the [White House's stated preference for a "give and take"](https://www.nextgov.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/03/white-house-official-advocates-give-and-take-state-ai-preemption/412407/) rather than a full federal override reflects an understanding that a workable coalition requires accommodating state interests.

There is a bipartisan deal to be made here if Congress chooses to look for it. Republicans bring the national competitiveness frame — AI preemption as strategic imperative in the competition with China. Democrats bring the consumer protection floor — a federal minimum that ensures AI fraud and deepfake prohibitions are enforceable nationwide, not dependent on the varying resources and priorities of 50 state attorneys general. The overlap is real: a federal standard with genuine enforcement authority against fraud, deepfakes, and child exploitation is simultaneously a pro-innovation measure (clearing the compliance field) and a pro-consumer measure (establishing a reliable national floor).

The telecommunications analogy is instructive. The [Telecommunications Act of 1996](https://www.fcc.gov/general/telecommunications-act-1996) preempted state telecommunications regulations that conflicted with federal standards, establishing national rules for an industry that operated across state lines. It was controversial at the time. It was also correct. The unified national market it helped create enabled the internet economy that followed. Federal preemption of AI standards is the same logic applied to a more consequential technology.

Financial services regulation offers the same lesson. National bank charters preempt state usury laws and licensing requirements, enabling financial institutions to operate at national scale under a coherent regulatory framework. The result is not consumer harm — it is consumer access to financial products that would be economically unviable under 50 differing state regimes. Product safety works the same way. The Consumer Product Safety Commission sets federal standards that preempt inconsistent state requirements, ensuring that manufacturers can serve the national market without redesigning products for each jurisdiction.

AI is more inherently interstate than any of these industries. The Commerce Clause case for federal preemption of AI standards is, if anything, stronger than it was for telecommunications or financial services.

## The Judicial Path Is the Worse Outcome

Congress should also understand what happens if it punts again. The administration has established a [DOJ AI Litigation Task Force](https://www.bakerlaw.com/insights/navigating-the-emerging-federal-state-ai-showdown-doj-establishes-ai-litigation-task-force/) that signals seriousness about challenging state AI laws through preemption and Commerce Clause arguments in federal court. If Congress fails to provide a legislative solution, expect an aggressive federal litigation strategy targeting the most burdensome state AI mandates.

Judicial preemption is a slower, messier, and less democratically legitimate path to the same destination. Court-by-court resolution of state AI law conflicts will take years, produce inconsistent results across circuits, and generate the very uncertainty that is already chilling AI investment and deployment decisions. An adverse Ninth Circuit ruling on California's AI requirements does not automatically invalidate Washington's different framework — it simply adds another data point to an incoherent body of law that companies must navigate.

Congressional action is cleaner. A legislative preemption framework, negotiated with state input and enacted through the democratic process, is more durable, more predictable, and more legitimate than a litigation-driven outcome. It also gives Congress the opportunity to define the federal floor explicitly — rather than leaving that definition to the courts, which are poorly positioned to make the nuanced policy tradeoffs that a functional AI governance regime requires.

## A Narrow Window for Action

The political window for federal preemption is open, but it is not wide. Every additional state law enacted creates a new constituency invested in preserving the state-level status quo — state legislators who voted for the law, agencies with new enforcement authority, advocacy organizations that secured hard-fought provisions. The compliance infrastructure that companies are now building to satisfy Indiana's requirements will, over time, become a sunk cost that reduces the urgency of federal rationalization. Incumbents, once they have absorbed the compliance costs, may quietly prefer a system that new entrant competitors cannot afford.

Congress should act now, while the White House is publicly calling for preemption, while the compliance burden is visible and politically salient, and before the patchwork becomes so entrenched that reform requires dismantling a settled regulatory ecosystem rather than preventing one from forming. The bipartisan deal is available. The constitutional rationale is overwhelming. The competitive stakes could not be higher.

The unified national market is not just a legal concept. It is the structural foundation on which American tech dominance was built. Fifty separate innovation environments, each with its own rules for what AI can and cannot do, is not an expression of American dynamism. It is a voluntary constraint on it. Federal preemption with a light-touch national floor — addressing demonstrated harms while clearing the field of idiosyncratic state barriers — is not a choice between regulation and no regulation. It is a choice between a regulatory framework that enables American AI leadership and one that guarantees American AI fragmentation.

Congress should choose correctly — and it should choose now.
