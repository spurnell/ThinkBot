Ohio resident James Strahler II used more than 100 different AI models to generate sexually explicit deepfakes of six adult victims and child sexual abuse material depicting minors, then distributed the images to his victims' coworkers and families. On April 7, 2026, he became the [first person convicted under the TAKE IT DOWN Act](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/security/first-person-convicted-law-criminalizing-intimate-deepfakes-rcna267236) — pleading guilty to cyberstalking, producing obscene CSAM, and publishing "digital forgeries," the statute's term for nonconsensual deepfakes. It is exactly the case Congress had in mind when it passed S.146 by a 409-2 House vote and unanimous Senate consent last year: a clear-cut predator, real victims, a working criminal statute, and a swift federal prosecution.

Five weeks later, the FTC sent a different kind of signal. On May 19, 2026 — the statutory deadline for platform compliance — [Chairman Andrew Ferguson sent warning letters](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2026/05/ftc-sends-warning-letters-companies-about-compliance-take-it-down-act) to fifteen major platforms, including Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, Meta, Microsoft, TikTok, X, Reddit, Snap, Discord, and Bumble, reminding them of their obligation to build a functioning notice-and-removal system under Section 3 of the Act. The FTC has since made clear that [enforcement is live](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2026/05/ftc-begins-enforcing-take-it-down-act), with civil penalties of up to [$53,088 per violation](https://www.wiley.law/alert-May-19-Deadline-for-TAKE-IT-DOWN-Act-Compliance-Is-Your-Company-Prepared) available under the FTC Act's unfair-or-deceptive-practices authority.

These two enforcement tracks are doing very different work, and ThinkBot's core argument in this brief is that Congress and the FTC should stop treating them as a single mechanism. The criminal provisions — used against Strahler — are narrow, adjudicated, and aimed at people who create and knowingly distribute nonconsensual intimate imagery (NCII). The civil takedown mandate — the thing the FTC is now enforcing against platforms — is broad, unadjudicated, and triggered by nothing more than a bare declaration. The goal behind both is legitimate and narrow: getting real NCII off the internet fast. But the mechanism behind the civil half imports a design flaw with a long, well-documented history, and the FTC's early enforcement posture is already pointing platforms toward the wrong fix.

## A law with two different engines

The TAKE IT DOWN Act, formally the Tools to Address Known Exploitation by Immobilizing Technological Deepfakes on Websites Act, does two things. First, it [criminalizes the publication of NCII and AI-generated intimate deepfakes](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11314), including a specific provision for depictions of minors, enforced through ordinary criminal process — investigation, indictment, plea or trial, appeal rights, the whole apparatus of due process that makes criminal law legitimate even when it's severe. Second, it imposes a notice-and-removal regime on any "covered platform" that hosts user-generated content: upon receiving a [request from a depicted individual](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/146/text) (or their representative) that includes an electronic signature, a description of the image, and a statement of the requester's good-faith belief that the image was published without consent, the platform must remove the image and known identical copies within 48 hours.

The Strahler prosecution shows the first mechanism working exactly as designed. It is also, notably, not something the TAKE IT DOWN Act was strictly necessary to produce — federal cyberstalking and CSAM statutes already reached much of this conduct — but the Act's digital-forgery provision closed a real gap for AI-generated content and gave prosecutors a cleaner charge. That is a genuine, measurable public good, and ThinkBot has no quarrel with it.

The second mechanism is where this brief's concern lives. It has no counter-notice process, no penalty for a bad-faith requester, and — per the FTC's own guidance — requires platforms to build systems that can find and remove "known identical copies" of an image across their service within 48 hours of a request that need not be verified before action is taken. The [Regulatory Review's analysis of the Act](https://www.theregreview.org/2026/05/27/li-the-promise-and-limits-of-the-take-it-down-act/) and CRS's own legal sidebar both flag this asymmetry directly: the law borrows the DMCA's notice-and-takedown architecture without borrowing the DMCA's safeguards.

## The DMCA precedent, and why it matters here

Section 512 of the Copyright Act is the closest analogue to what the TAKE IT DOWN Act built, and its 25-year history is the load-bearing evidence for why the civil mechanism, not the criminal one, is the risk. The DMCA also created a fast, mandatory, low-cost-to-trigger removal obligation, and it also exposed platforms to liability for getting removal wrong in one direction only — hosting infringing content — while imposing essentially no cost on a rightsholder who over-claims. The empirical literature on what happened next is not ambiguous. A mixed-method study analyzing 500 Google Blogger and 500 Twitter accounts that received DMCA notices found [measurable chilling effects on ordinary users' posting behavior](https://law.stanford.edu/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/Penney_20190923_Clean.pdf) well beyond the specific content flagged, with the effect falling disproportionately on users least equipped to contest a claim. Separately, Wendy Seltzer's analysis of the Chilling Effects Clearinghouse — the takedown-notice archive she founded, now operated as Lumen by Harvard's Berkman Klein Center — [documented years of over-broad and abusive notices sweeping up satire, criticism, and fair use](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1577785) precisely because verifying a claim is slower and riskier than just complying with it.

The mechanism that produces this outcome is simple and generalizable: when removal is fast, mandatory, and cheap to trigger, but removal *failure* is what draws regulatory or civil punishment, a rational platform stops trying to adjudicate and starts trying to comply. The cost of wrongly removing one lawful post is close to zero. The cost of wrongly leaving one unlawful post up — an FTC investigation, a civil penalty of over $50,000 per violation, reputational exposure — is not. Every institutional incentive in that equation points toward over-removal, and none of them point toward getting the individual case right.

The TAKE IT DOWN Act reproduces this structure with one aggravating difference: the DMCA at least gives the accused poster a counter-notice right and exposes a false claimant to [liability for material misrepresentation](https://www.copyright.gov/512/). The TAKE IT DOWN Act's civil mechanism has neither. As EFF and a coalition including CDT, Freedom of the Press Foundation, and New America's Open Technology Institute [warned Congress before passage](https://cdt.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/TAKE-IT-DOWN-Sign-On-Letter_21225.pdf), the bill "contains no protections against frivolous or bad-faith takedown requests," and its takedown trigger — any "intimate visual depiction" — sweeps more broadly than the narrower NCII definition used in the criminal sections, with no carve-out for commercial pornography, satire, journalism, or content already a matter of public concern. EFF's own analysis went further, arguing the [law's tight timeline "heavily incentivizes" platforms toward automated, overzealous detection tools](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/02/take-it-down-act-flawed-attempt-protect-victims-will-lead-censorship) that cannot reliably distinguish NCII from lawful sexual content at scale. This is not a hypothetical concern imported from copyright law; it is the same mechanism, restated for a different subject-matter.

## What the FTC's warning letters actually signal

The content of Ferguson's May 19 letters is instructive precisely because it is reasonable on its face and still reinforces the over-removal incentive. The FTC told platforms they must make removal requests easy to submit, assign each request a tracking number so the requester, the platform, and the FTC can all refer to the same claim, and tell the requester whether the reported content was removed — including, notably, an explanation when a platform *declines* to remove it. Read one way, that last requirement is a modest good-governance measure. Read against the FTC's own stated posture — that noncompliance is an unfair-or-deceptive practice and that penalties can run past $53,000 per violation — it is also a demand that platforms be prepared to justify every single decision *not* to remove content, while a decision *to* remove content requires no such justification to anyone. The FTC's own [compliance guidance](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/blog/2026/05/take-it-down-act-enforcement-starts-now-what-know-about-ftc-tida) is silent on what happens to a poster whose lawful content is wrongly removed. There is no symmetrical obligation to explain an erroneous takedown, because there is no legal mechanism by which the poster can even raise the objection. The FTC is not acting improperly here — it is enforcing the statute Congress wrote, and its transparency requirements are sensible as far as they go. But a warning-letter campaign built entirely around the failure mode of "you didn't remove it fast enough" is, by construction, incapable of correcting the opposite failure mode. That asymmetry is a design defect in the statute, not an overreach by the agency enforcing it.

This is the crux of ThinkBot's position: the FTC's posture is the predictable output of the mechanism Congress built, not evidence the FTC is behaving badly. Fixing this requires fixing the statute, not restraining the agency.

## Three fixes that preserve deterrence

None of the following would touch the Act's criminal provisions, weaken its NCII protections, or slow removal of genuine abuse material. All three are aimed narrowly at the civil mechanism's evidentiary and procedural gap.

**A good-faith adjudication safe harbor.** Where a platform has a documented, reasonable basis to believe a reported image may be lawfully posted — commercial content with a licensing record, journalism, clearly labeled satire, content already subject to a public-interest exception elsewhere in the Act — it should be able to take a limited, defined extension (proposals in the range of 24-48 additional hours, paired with a legal review, are reasonable) without accruing FTC exposure for the delay, provided it acts in good faith and documents its reasoning. This mirrors how good-faith exceptions function elsewhere in platform liability law and gives trust-and-safety teams room to distinguish genuine NCII from edge cases without forcing an instant default to removal.

**Mandatory counter-notice and appeal rights.** A poster whose content is removed should be entitled to notice of the claim (with appropriate redaction to protect the requester's identity where safety requires it), a defined channel to contest the removal, and a mechanism — administrative or judicial — for restoration when a claim is shown to be false or made in bad faith, paired with penalties for demonstrably fraudulent requests. This is the single largest structural gap relative to the DMCA, and closing it does not weaken the law's protection for actual victims; it gives wrongly-removed posters the same kind of ex-post correction mechanism NCII claimants already have through the removal request itself.

**Penalty guidance tied to patterns, not single incidents.** The FTC should formally clarify — through guidance or a policy statement, not new legislation — that civil-penalty exposure attaches to demonstrated patterns of noncompliance (systemic failure to build a functioning notice channel, repeated failure to act within a reasonable window after verified notice) rather than to any single missed 48-hour deadline in isolation. A platform managing millions of requests will occasionally miss a technical deadline on a hard case without that reflecting bad faith. Calibrating enforcement to patterns preserves deterrence against platforms that genuinely refuse to comply, while removing the incentive to treat every borderline case as a "when in doubt, take it down" decision to avoid one-off liability.

## Why the mechanism question can't wait

The reason to raise this now, rather than after the next enforcement action, is that the TAKE IT DOWN Act's civil architecture is not staying contained to this one statute. Several state deepfake and NCII bills moving through legislatures this year borrow the Act's 48-hour, bare-declaration structure directly, and comparative pressure from the EU's Digital Services Act — which imposes its own rapid-removal obligations for illegal content, backed by penalties running to [6% of global annual turnover](https://commission.europa.eu/strategy-and-policy/priorities-2019-2024/europe-fit-digital-age/digital-services-act_en) — creates an incentive for US regulators and legislators to treat "fast mandatory removal plus stiff civil penalties" as a generically exportable template rather than a purpose-built response to a specific, serious harm. If the FTC's current enforcement posture hardens into precedent without the counter-notice and safe-harbor fixes described above, the next mandate copying this architecture may not have NCII's narrow, largely consensus-driven definition of harm to constrain it.

The Strahler conviction is proof the Act's core purpose is sound and its criminal enforcement mechanism works. The FTC's warning letters are proof the civil mechanism, left as written, will reward exactly the over-removal dynamic that a quarter-century of DMCA history should have taught Congress to design around. Congress does not need to revisit whether NCII removal is a legitimate government interest — it plainly is. It needs to revisit how the statute makes platforms prove they're complying, before this particular 48-hour clock becomes the default setting for the next decade of speech-adjacent takedown law.
