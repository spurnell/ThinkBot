The Supreme Court [ruled 6-3 on June 29, 2026](https://www.scotusblog.com/cases/trump-v-slaughter-2/) that a president may remove Federal Trade Commission commissioners without cause, [overturning the 91-year-old precedent](https://www.npr.org/2026/06/29/nx-s1-5816232/supreme-court-ftc-independent-agencies-humphreys-executor) established in *Humphrey's Executor v. United States* (1935). The ruling is seismic, the commentary hyperbolic, and the policy implications genuinely complex. Before either celebrating or catastrophizing, let's be precise about what the Court actually decided — and what it did not.

## What *Humphrey's Executor* Actually Protected

Since the FTC's founding in 1914, Congress has provided that commissioners may be removed only for "inefficiency, neglect of duty or malfeasance in office." That "for cause" protection — codified and then judicially blessed in *Humphrey's Executor* — rested on a theory that certain agencies occupy a quasi-legislative, quasi-judicial zone outside the president's direct supervisory authority. Commissioner Rebecca Slaughter was [told her removal](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/29/supreme-court-trump-slaughter-ftc.html) was not for any of those reasons but because her "continued service on the FTC is inconsistent with [the Trump] Administration's priorities." The Court, in an opinion by Chief Justice Roberts, held that was sufficient.

The 6-3 majority — with Justice Sotomayor writing in dissent, joined by Justices Kagan and Jackson — reaches the conclusion most consistent with *Seila Law v. CFPB* (2020), which had already eroded *Humphrey's Executor* by holding that single-director independent agencies cannot be insulated from at-will removal. What remained was a question of intellectual honesty: could a five-member commission claiming to exercise prosecutorial discretion, rulemaking authority, and adjudicatory power truly sit beyond the constitutional chain of command? [The Court says no](https://thehill.com/regulation/court-battles/5935135-supreme-court-trump-independent-agencies-firing-protections/).

## The Structural Anomaly Was Real

Critics who frame today's ruling as a partisan attack on antitrust enforcement are inverting the accountability problem. The more uncomfortable question is whether unelected commissioners who exercise sweeping quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial power over the American economy — largely insulated from electoral correction — represent a feature or a bug of constitutional governance.

The FTC under its recent progressive majority pursued enforcement theories that tested the outer limits of its statutory authority: novel definitions of "unfair methods of competition," sweeping non-compete rulemaking that a [federal court struck down](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=3df63c41-c141-4ce0-951f-6c9093806679) as exceeding the agency's mandate, and a novel standalone Section 5 price-coordination theory targeting Amazon's internally dubbed ['Project Nessie'](https://techcrunch.com/2023/11/02/unredacted-ftc-suit-shows-project-nessie-price-raising-algorithm-made-amazon-1-4b/) algorithm — which the FTC alleged artificially elevated prices across competing platforms, generating an estimated $1.4 billion in excess profits. The FTC's monopolization case against Meta — built on a theory that TikTok and YouTube don't compete with Facebook and Instagram — was [rejected by Chief Judge James Boasberg](https://www.skadden.com/insights/publications/2025/11/ftc-loses-retroactive-merger-challenge) after a bench trial in November 2025 precisely because the agency's market definition had become untethered from economic reality.

None of that enforcement trajectory was subject to democratic correction short of Senate confirmation of new commissioners over a multi-year horizon. The structural accountability deficit was not invented by the current administration. It predates it.

## Why This Still Isn't Categorically Good News

That said, any endorsement of this ruling must be genuinely cautious, because the logic of the majority opinion does not select for *good* antitrust enforcement. It selects for *presidential* antitrust enforcement.

The FTC's consumer welfare mandate does not belong to any party. The same at-will removal authority that could redirect an agency running roughshod over vertical integration norms can — and in a different administration, likely will — be turned toward protecting domestic incumbents from foreign competition, pursuing politically favored theories of harm, or pressuring companies whose executives displease the occupant of the White House. The sword is now in the hands of the executive. Future wielders of that sword are not yet known.

A [companion ruling blocked at-will removal of Federal Reserve board members](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/supreme-court/supreme-court-rules-trump-cannot-fire-fed-member-lisa-cook-grants-powe-rcna234931) — a meaningful carve-out suggesting the Court is not writing a blank check for total executive dominance of every agency. But the FTC, the NLRB, and [approximately two dozen other multimember agencies](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/29/supreme-court-trump-slaughter-ftc.html) now operate under a fundamentally different constitutional regime.

## What Accountability Actually Requires

The correct response to today's ruling is not lamentation and not celebration. It is clarity. If Congress believes the FTC's substantive mandate has been serially abused — and there is evidence it has — the remedy is statutory reform that defines what "unfair methods of competition" means, anchors it explicitly to the consumer welfare standard, and limits non-compete rulemaking authority. Presidential at-will removal is a check on personnel; it is not a substitute for a clear substantive mandate.

The antitrust community should not make the same mistake in reverse that the neo-Brandeisian movement made: substituting structural arguments for substantive ones. "The president can fire the commissioners" does not answer the question of *what standard* those commissioners should apply. The consumer welfare standard — prices, output, quality, innovation — is the right answer. It needs to be written into law, not left to fluctuate with whoever the sitting president has most recently appointed.

## The Bottom Line

*Trump v. Slaughter* closes a structural anomaly that was always in tension with basic principles of democratic accountability. An agency wielding enormous power over the American economy without meaningful electoral correction was never a neutral arrangement — it was a political settlement that happened to favor the commissioners who were in place. Correcting that imbalance is legitimate.

But the correction creates its own risks. The consumer welfare standard that should govern FTC enforcement is now more exposed to political interference than at any point since *Humphrey's Executor* was decided. The right policy response is to take that standard off the political table — through statutory codification — rather than to assume that executive accountability will, on net, produce better antitrust enforcement than the commission structure it replaces.

Accountability is necessary. It is not sufficient.
