In March, the National Republican Senatorial Committee released [an AI-generated video of Texas Democratic candidate James Talarico](https://www.cnn.com/2026/03/13/politics/james-talarico-ai-deepfake-republicans-midterms) — a fabricated version of the candidate, wearing his actual likeness and speaking in his actual voice, reading excerpts from his past statements on transgender issues, religion, and abortion for more than a minute. The words "AI GENERATED" appeared in small, faint text in the corner for a few seconds. The fake Talarico was, by all accounts, uncannily convincing.

It was not an isolated incident. [As *The American Prospect* reported on April 17](https://prospect.org/2026/04/17/american-politics-inundated-with-ai-deepfakes/), American politics is already inundated with AI-generated fabrications — and the 2026 midterm cycle is only accelerating their spread. The question before Congress, the FEC, and anyone who cares about democratic integrity is not whether to respond. It's how.

The answer the First Amendment permits — and the only one that will actually work — is mandatory disclosure. Not prohibition. Not platform-removal mandates. Not a 50-state patchwork of criminalization statutes. A federal rule requiring that AI-generated paid political advertising say so, clearly and prominently, in every placement. Full stop.

**Political Speech Is the Last Thing Government Should Prohibit**

The instinct to ban fabricated political advertising is understandable. Watching a convincing AI simulacrum of a living candidate say things he never said should make any concerned voter uncomfortable. But the First Amendment does not make exceptions for uncomfortable political speech — and for good reason. Political expression sits at the apex of the constitutional hierarchy. The Supreme Court has been consistent on this point for half a century.

Prohibition-based deepfake statutes — and [several of the 31 states that have already enacted deepfake election laws](https://www.citizen.org/article/tracker-legislation-on-deepfakes-in-elections/) have gone this route — face the same doctrinal headwinds as prior restraints on political expression. They will inevitably sweep in protected satire, parody, and critical commentary. A prohibition on "materially deceptive" AI content in the 90 days before an election is a prohibition waiting to be weaponized against attack ads, political cartoons rendered in video, and the entire genre of satirical candidate impersonation that American political culture has tolerated since the republic's founding. Courts applying strict scrutiny will not look kindly on content-based restrictions on political speech, particularly when less restrictive alternatives — like disclosure — are available.

The Supreme Court's decision in *[United States v. Alvarez](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/567/709/)* (2012) is instructive here: even false statements enjoy First Amendment protection, and the government cannot simply criminalize speech it deems false without meeting a demanding constitutional burden. The Alvarez plurality explicitly noted that "an outright ban on false speech" would grant the government "a freewheeling authority to declare certain statements false," a power incompatible with free expression. That logic applies directly to fabricated political content.

**Disclosure Is the Constitutionally Settled Tool**

Here is the good news: the Constitution already tells us what to do. *[Buckley v. Valeo](https://www.fec.gov/legal-resources/court-cases/buckley-v-valeo/)* (1976) upheld campaign finance disclosure requirements precisely because the government has a compelling interest in informing voters about the source and nature of political advertising — and because disclosure achieves that interest without suppressing the underlying speech. The FEC already requires "paid for by" identification on every political advertisement. It is a direct, workable, constitutionally durable regime.

Extending that regime to AI origin is not a radical step. It is a logical one. A federal rule requiring that any paid political advertisement containing AI-generated audio or video imagery include a prominent, standardized disclosure — not faint corner text that disappears after three seconds, but a clear, durable label — would give voters the information they need to evaluate what they're seeing without the government making content judgments about which deepfakes are harmful enough to ban.

This is the model the FEC should have moved on months ago. Instead, [the Commission remains deadlocked on partisan lines](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/artificial-intelligence/regulate-deepfake-ads-election-agency-told-again-after-deadlock), unable to issue even basic interpretive guidance on how AI fits existing campaign rules. That paralysis is the proximate cause of the current crisis. Congress should act by statute — directing the FEC to issue a mandatory AI-origin disclosure standard for paid political content, with enforcement teeth attached.

**The 31-State Patchwork Is Failing Voters**

[According to Ballotpedia's tracking](https://news.ballotpedia.org/2026/04/03/15-deepfake-bills-enacted-so-far-this-year-number-of-states-with-deepfake-laws-remains-the-same/), 15 deepfake bills were enacted in just the first quarter of 2026 alone, bringing the total number of states with deepfake election laws to at least 31. The standards are wildly inconsistent: some states require disclosure, others prohibit AI content outright within specified windows before elections, and eight states have enacted platform liability provisions. A national campaign running ads across 20 states faces a compliance maze with no coherent logic.

This is precisely the scenario federal preemption was designed to address. A single federal floor — mandatory AI-origin disclosure in paid political advertising — would establish a consistent, nationwide standard while leaving states free to experiment on the margins. It would cut compliance costs for campaigns and platforms alike. And it would protect voters more reliably than any patchwork, because federal enforcement is uniform and national campaigns cannot route around it by avoiding particular states.

**What Platforms Can Do Is Not What Government Can Compel**

One critical distinction that gets lost in the current debate: what social media platforms choose to do voluntarily about fabricated political content is entirely different from what government can require them to do.

Platforms labeling AI-generated content, demoting synthetic media in recommendation systems, or removing the most egregious fabrications pursuant to their own policies — all of that is private editorial discretion, protected by the First Amendment (see *[Moody v. NetChoice](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/23pdf/22-555_4857.pdf)*) and consistent with settled constitutional doctrine on platform editorial autonomy. It is good, responsible moderation.

But government-mandated platform removal of political content is a different matter entirely. That is compelled speech, or more precisely compelled silence about speech — and it raises First Amendment concerns on both ends of the transaction. The government cannot commandeer platform editorial discretion to suppress political expression it finds harmful. Courts will be skeptical of any regime that routes government content judgments through platform intermediaries.

The line is real and it matters: government mandates disclosure; platforms choose, voluntarily, what additional measures to take.

**The Clock Is Running**

The 2026 midterms are not hypothetical. They are happening now, with fabricated political advertising circulating across every major platform at scale. The FEC is frozen. Congress has not acted. And [state legislatures have raced](https://ballotpedia.org/Press_release_State_Deepfake_Laws_Hit_Record_Pace) to fill a federal vacuum with inconsistent, constitutionally fragile laws.

The right solution is also the narrowest one. Require disclosure. Empower the FEC to enforce it. Preempt the state patchwork with a uniform federal floor. And resist the temptation to hand government the power to define which fabrications are too harmful to permit.

Voters are capable of evaluating political speech when they have the information they need. Give them the label. Trust the rest to the electorate.
