Apple's App Store was supposed to be "open" in the European Union by now. The Digital Markets Act's app-store provisions took full effect in March 2024. Apple has since rewritten its EU business terms at least three times. It promised, as recently as June 2025, that a new revenue-based Core Technology Commission (CTC) would fully replace its widely criticized per-install Core Technology Fee (CTF) by January 2026, collapsing a bewildering multi-tier fee structure into "one business model" for all developers.

That deadline has come and gone. As of this writing, Apple and the European Commission remain in unresolved talks over the new fee architecture, with Apple publicly [accusing Brussels of "political delay tactics"](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/01/22/apple-europe-political-delay-tactics/) and the Commission countering that Apple still hasn't addressed the "key issues concerning its business terms." A parallel non-compliance investigation into whether Apple's contractual terms — including the CTF's successor — satisfy the DMA's Article 6(4) app-distribution obligations, [first opened in June 2024](https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/detail/en/ip_24_3433), is still open. Apple has now held a [second formal "compliance workshop"](https://legalblogs.wolterskluwer.com/competition-blog/apples-second-dma-compliance-workshop-an-aggressive-defence-of-rights/) with the Commission and stakeholders, mounting what one close observer of the proceedings called an "aggressive defence" of its right to design its own platform.

This is not a transitional hiccup on the way to a settled competitive market. It is the DMA's app-store regime functioning as designed — and that design flaw is the most important data point available to Congress as it weighs whether to import the EU's model.

## A Timeline Without an Endpoint

The sequence is worth laying out in full, because its shape — not any single episode in it — is the finding.

**March 2024**: The DMA's app-store obligations take effect. Apple responds with a compliance package built around the original Core Technology Fee: €0.50 per first annual install above one million, layered on top of new tiered commissions for apps that opt into "alternative business terms."

**June 2024**: The Commission [opens a non-compliance investigation](https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/detail/en/ip_24_3433) into whether that very compliance package — the CTF and its eligibility rules for third-party app stores and sideloading — actually satisfies the DMA's Article 6(4) obligations, or merely simulates compliance while preserving Apple's economics.

**April 2025**: On April 23, the Commission fines Apple [€500 million for a separate anti-steering violation](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/news/commission-closes-investigation-apples-user-choice-obligations-and-issues-preliminary-findings) under Article 5(4) — the first DMA fine against any "gatekeeper" (the DMA's designation for the handful of large platforms, including Apple, Google, and Meta, subject to its heaviest obligations) — while simultaneously closing a third investigation (over browser choice screens) and issuing new preliminary findings that the CTF *still* falls short of Article 6(4). Three parallel proceedings, three different postures, all against one company, all in one announcement.

**June 2025**: Apple unveils a sweeping restructuring: a single business model for all EU developers, built around an Initial Acquisition Fee (2%), tiered Store Services fees (5% or 13%, 10% for Small Business Program members), and — critically — a new 5% revenue-based Core Technology Commission that Apple promises will fully retire the per-install CTF by January 2026. [Developer-tooling analysts](https://www.revenuecat.com/blog/growth/apple-eu-dma-update-june-2025/) immediately flag that the new stack, run through its various permutations, produces combined fees ranging from roughly 10% to 20% before payment processing overhead — comparable to, not categorically lower than, what many developers paid under the old regime.

**July 2025**: At Apple's second compliance workshop, held in Brussels on June 30, the company's representatives argue the Commission's approach forces gatekeepers to build "carbon copies" of each other's business models and complains that App Store products are "being designed by lawyers, instead of engineers." Apple also presents data suggesting a majority of third-party interoperability requests under the DMA are redundant or out of scope — an argument aimed at the Commission's broader enforcement posture, not just the fee dispute.

**January 2026**: The CTC transition deadline arrives. It does not happen. Apple says the Commission has "refused to let us implement the very changes that they requested." The Commission says Apple hasn't rolled out the changes needed to address its concerns. Neither side offers a new date. In the same month, [Setapp Mobile — one of the first alternative iOS app stores built specifically to take advantage of the DMA's sideloading mandate — shuts down](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/01/15/setapp-mobile-eu-app-store-closing-next-month/), its parent company MacPaw citing "still-evolving and complex business terms" as the reason the model was commercially unviable.

**Mid-2026**: The fee architecture remains unresolved. The Article 6(4) investigation remains open. There is no public timeline for either to close.

That is not a market finding its equilibrium. It is a regulator and a platform locked in what has become, structurally, a permanent negotiation — one where the object under negotiation is not a discrete violation but the platform's entire monetization architecture.

## Fee Relabeling Is Not Fee Reform

The Commission's theory of the case has always been that ex ante structural obligations — mandated sideloading, mandated alternative payment processing, mandated interoperability — would force Apple's effective take rate down and let rival distribution channels take root. Two-plus years and three major restructurings later, the evidence for that theory is thin.

Compare the fee stacks directly. Under Apple's pre-2025 approach, a developer accepting the DMA's "alternative business terms" paid a Core Technology Fee (as much as $400,000 per month for a popular free app, according to independent [developer-cost modeling](https://www.singular.net/blog/apples-core-technology-fee/)) plus reduced but still substantial commissions — combined costs that could run as high as 25%. Under the 2026 restructuring, a developer who opts out of Apple's optional "Store Services" tier can get combined fees as low as 10–12%; one who wants the full suite of App Store discovery, review, and hosting tools that used to come bundled with the standard 30% commission is back up around 20%, before the 3–5% in payment-processing and fraud-prevention overhead that Apple's system no longer absorbs. The range moved. The midpoint didn't move much. And the number of line items a developer must model — initial acquisition fee, Tier 1 services, Tier 2 services, Core Technology Commission, rolling six- and twelve-month lookback windows — went up, not down.

This is the pattern the DMA has produced across its app-store enforcement generally: entitlement categories multiply, compliance overhead multiplies, and the net transfer from developers to Apple moves within a band rather than collapsing. Apple isn't the only gatekeeper generating this pattern — Meta's own DMA compliance saga over its "consent-or-pay" advertising model, fined €200 million in the same April 2025 enforcement wave, shows the same dynamic: an initial "compliant" redesign, a Commission finding that the redesign doesn't actually satisfy the obligation, and a second redesign now under review. The specific mechanism differs by platform; the meta-pattern — relabel, dispute, relabel again — is the DMA's actual institutional output.

None of this means EU developers are worse off than before the DMA in every respect; some — particularly large publishers with owned distribution and payment infrastructure — clearly benefit from the option to route around Apple's standard commission entirely. But "some developers with the scale to exploit optionality see gains" is a much narrower claim than "the DMA opened the App Store to competition," and it is the narrower claim the evidence actually supports.

## The Entry That Hasn't Come

The strongest empirical test of the DMA's app-store theory isn't the fee schedule — it's whether new distribution channels have actually taken root. Setapp Mobile is the cleanest data point available, precisely because it was built by a well-resourced, DMA-native developer (MacPaw) for the express purpose of exploiting the sideloading mandate, and it still failed. Its own explanation was not that Apple blocked it outright — the DMA formally prohibits that — but that the underlying business terms remained too unsettled to plan a viable product around.

That is the deeper problem with an ex ante regulatory model applied to a live, evolving multi-sided platform: the fee architecture a challenger has to underwrite is a moving target that the regulator and the platform are still fighting over years into implementation. A conduct-based case resolves into a final judgment that establishes a fixed rule a rival can build against. An ex ante structural mandate administered through iterative compliance workshops and rolling non-compliance investigations does not resolve at all — it just generates new editions of the same argument, and entrants have to underwrite the risk that today's compliant fee schedule is tomorrow's subject of a fresh Commission finding.

## The Direct Answer to "Time for an American DMA?"

This record is squarely relevant to a live debate in Washington. ProMarket [recently asked whether the Apple App Store saga shows "it's time for an American Digital Markets Act,"](https://www.promarket.org/2025/11/13/does-the-case-of-apples-app-store-indicate-its-time-for-an-american-digital-markets-act/) pointing out — correctly — that EU users can access apps and alternative marketplaces Americans cannot, and that many EU developers pay lower headline fees than their U.S. counterparts. The argument, echoed in periodic pushes to revive Open App Markets Act- and American Innovation and Choice Online Act-style app-store mandates in Congress, is that the U.S. has achieved lower fees without the DMA's structural entry provisions, and that pairing lower fees with mandated entry points would be better still.

The comparison proves the opposite of what its proponents intend. Legal availability of an alternative channel is not the same thing as a commercially viable one, and Setapp Mobile's collapse — alongside the broader thinness of third-party iOS marketplaces two-plus years after sideloading became mandatory — shows why: an entrant can be granted a legal right of entry by statute while the regulator and incumbent are still fighting over the economic terms of that entry, and the right is worth nothing until the terms are settled. The DMA gave challengers the right to enter years ago. It has not yet given them terms stable enough to enter *on*. An American DMA modeled on this framework would import the same defect — a federal agency (the FTC, in most legislative drafts) empowered to mandate structural changes to platform business models, without any comparable mechanism forcing the underlying fee and entitlement architecture to actually settle within a defined period.

## The Better Baseline: Epic's Ex Post Model

The United States already has a working comparison case, and it looks nothing like Brussels'. *Epic Games v. Apple* and *Epic Games v. Google* were conduct-specific antitrust suits that produced court-tested, judicially enforceable remedies tailored to proven violations — not agency-administered fee taxonomies imposed prospectively on an entire business model.

The two cases have now reached different but instructive endpoints. In *Epic v. Google*, the Ninth Circuit's affirmance of a jury verdict against Google pushed the parties to a negotiated settlement: as of March 2026, Google has agreed to fees of 20% or less on many transactions and restored Fortnite to the Play Store worldwide. In *Epic v. Apple*, the Ninth Circuit in December 2025 upheld a civil contempt finding against Apple for willfully violating the original 2021 injunction, holding that Apple could charge a commission on off-app purchases only for costs "genuinely and reasonably necessary" to facilitate the transaction — a concrete, judicially administrable standard, not an open-ended entitlement schedule. The Supreme Court declined to stay that ruling in May 2026, and on June 30, 2026 it [granted certiorari](https://www.macrumors.com/2026/06/30/apple-epic-games-supreme-court/) in *Apple Inc. v. Epic Games, Inc.* — but only on one of Apple's two questions presented, declining to take up its broader argument about the scope of nationwide injunctive relief. The single question that survived is narrow and specific: whether a court may ground civil contempt in an injunction's "spirit" or must tie it to conduct the order clearly and unambiguously prohibits. That question will be resolved by next term. It has a clear off-ramp.

Compare that to the Commission's Article 6(4) investigation into Apple's business terms, opened in June 2024 with no resolution in sight two years later, or to the CTC transition that has now missed its own announced deadline by half a year and counting. The U.S. process is slower to reach any single remedy than a legislative mandate would be — that is a real cost, and this brief does not pretend otherwise. But it terminates. Every stage narrows the question in dispute and produces a standard a lower court, and the parties, can apply going forward. The EU process broadens the question in dispute with each new workshop and investigation, because the underlying statute empowers the Commission to keep re-litigating the platform's business model indefinitely rather than adjudicate a specific act and move on.

## What Congress Should Take From This

None of this counsels inaction on app-store competition. Epic's results show conduct-specific antitrust enforcement, tested through the ordinary appellate process, can produce real remedies — lower fees, restored distribution, judicially administrable commission rules — without requiring a standing federal bureaucracy to co-manage a platform's fee architecture in perpetuity. That is the model Congress should build on if it legislates at all: codifying anti-retaliation protections for the kind of steering and linking rights that emerged from the Epic litigation, and preserving courts' authority to impose targeted, evidence-based remedies tied to proven anticompetitive conduct.

What Congress should not do is treat the DMA's stated goals — lower fees, more competing app stores, developer freedom — as evidence of the DMA's actual results. Two-plus years in, Brussels has not settled what "compliance" means for a single gatekeeper's app store, and the regulatory uncertainty that has produced is itself now cited by developers as the reason competing distribution channels can't get off the ground. A U.S. statute that handed the FTC the same open-ended mandate to define and redefine "fair, reasonable, and non-discriminatory" fee structures for dominant platforms would not resolve into a stable competitive market any faster than the DMA has. The honest lesson from the EU's app-store experiment, at the two-year mark, is that ex ante structural regulation of multi-sided platforms doesn't produce competition — it produces a compliance treadmill, with the platform, the regulator, and the developers who were supposed to benefit all still running in place.
