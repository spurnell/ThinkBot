Somewhere between the third and fourth state circular letter arriving in an insurtech's compliance inbox, the insight usually lands: the system wasn't designed for this. The National Association of Insurance Commissioners issued [its Model Bulletin on the Use of Artificial Intelligence Systems](https://content.naic.org/insurance-topics/artificial-intelligence) in December 2023 as flexible, principles-based guidance. It asked carriers to maintain documented AI governance programs, conduct bias testing, and manage third-party vendors responsibly — all enforced through existing market conduct examination authority, with no new statute, no new agency, and no per-decision output mandate. It was, by any reasonable measure, the right architecture.

But "voluntary" without a federal backstop invites exactly what followed. [Twenty-four states and the District of Columbia](https://www.quarles.com/newsroom/publications/nearly-half-of-states-have-now-adopted-naic-model-bulletin-on-insurers-use-of-ai) have now adopted the bulletin, a number NAIC described as "remarkable for a model product." At the same time, [four additional states have adopted insurance-specific regulation or guidance going beyond the bulletin](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2026/04/us-naic-spring-2026-national-meeting-highlights-innovation-cybersecurity-and-technology-h-committee-update) altogether. Illinois [HB 0035](https://legiscan.com/IL/text/HB0035/id/3207940) prohibits insurers from making adverse decisions — denials, reductions, terminations — solely based on AI output and requires meaningful human review with override authority. Connecticut has advanced legislation restricting automated prior authorization denial in healthcare claims.

Layered on all of that: the NAIC is drafting a model law on third-party data and predictive models that could formalize a vendor registry, a twelve-state examination pilot is running through September 2026, and Colorado — which built the most ambitious horizontal AI law in the country — just repealed it.

This is not regulatory evolution. It is regulatory accumulation: a growing stack of bulletin adoptions, sector-specific overlays, and examination initiatives that were never designed to interact. The NAIC's examination-based model was correct. The question now is whether it gets strengthened into something durable before fifty AI disclosure regimes calcify into permanent infrastructure.

## The Model Bulletin Was the Right Architecture

Insurance is not a generic industry. Actuarial underwriting, pricing, and claims evaluation are built on probabilistic risk models — the entire discipline involves calculating who is likely to cost how much, under what conditions, based on what observable characteristics. The question of when that calculation produces unlawfully discriminatory outcomes is genuinely hard and predates machine learning by decades. The question of whether a specific AI model is discriminatory is harder still.

The NAIC's examination-based approach took that complexity seriously. Rather than mandating what models must look like — or demanding that every adverse underwriting decision come packaged with a consumer-readable textual explanation — the Model Bulletin asked regulators to examine whether a carrier's AI governance *program* produces fair outcomes. Examiners assess governance structure, model development and validation procedures, bias testing protocols, and third-party vendor management. The operative question is: does this insurer have the processes, documentation, and accountability structures to catch and correct AI-driven discrimination? That is the right question.

Compare this to the CFPB's approach in credit. [CFPB Circular 2022-03](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/compliance/circulars/circular-2022-03-adverse-action-notification-requirements-in-connection-with-credit-decisions-based-on-complex-algorithms/) and its [2023 follow-on](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/compliance/circulars/circular-2023-03-adverse-action-notification-requirements-and-the-proper-use-of-the-cfpbs-sample-forms-provided-in-regulation-b/) require that adverse-action notices under Reg B include specific reasons an algorithmic model denied credit, regardless of whether that model can generate such reasons without materially distorting the explanation. Applied to simple scorecards, this is workable. Applied to gradient-boosted ensembles and neural networks processing hundreds of alternative-data signals, the mandate effectively penalizes model accuracy in service of output legibility — and the borrowers most harmed by less accurate models are typically thin-file consumers the regulation claims to protect. The insurance parallel is exact: a per-decision explainability requirement does not measure fairness; it measures whether the model can generate text. Those are not the same thing.

Compare also the EU's horizontal approach. The EU AI Act classified life and health insurance underwriting as high-risk under [Annex III](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/annex-3) and imposed technical documentation requirements, conformity assessments, and mandatory explanations on any AI system influencing a consequential decision. Brussels, to its credit, [revised course in May 2026](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/press/press-releases/2026/05/07/artificial-intelligence-council-and-parliament-agree-to-simplify-and-streamline-rules/). The Council and Parliament reached a provisional agreement under the Digital AI Omnibus to defer high-risk AI compliance deadlines — moving Annex III stand-alone systems to December 2027 and Annex I embedded systems to August 2028. Operationalizing several provisions, particularly documentation and third-party assessment requirements, had proven harder than anticipated. The NAIC's examination-based model avoided this cliff by not building the cliff in the first place.

## The Patchwork Is Real, and It Is Accelerating

The Model Bulletin text was adopted with minimal changes across its 24 state jurisdictions — a genuine testament to the NAIC's coordination capacity. But uniformity in the bulletin text is not uniformity in the regulatory environment. According to [NAIC's own Spring 2026 meeting materials](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2026/04/us-naic-spring-2026-national-meeting-highlights-innovation-cybersecurity-and-technology-h-committee-update), four states have adopted insurance-specific regulation or guidance that goes beyond the bulletin's framework. Layered on top: California's AI Transparency Act (effective January 1, 2026) imposes broader system disclosure requirements that interact uneasily with the NAIC standards. New York's circular letters impose supervisory expectations on external consumer data use with different evidentiary standards. Illinois HB 0035 imposes a human-review mandate. And [Connecticut's prior authorization legislation](https://iapp.org/news/a/notable-ai-privacy-bills-hit-finish-line-in-illinois-connecticut-and-new-york) advances automated-decision restrictions in healthcare claims.

The result is a multi-jurisdiction compliance matrix built for no one. A carrier or insurtech operating across all 24 bulletin states plus the four with insurance-specific AI regulation faces not one AI governance framework but a layered interpretive environment in which "fair algorithmic underwriting" means something different depending on the state: human-review mandate in Illinois; conformity assessment expectations in California; third-party vendor scrutiny under development at NAIC; claims decision restrictions in Connecticut. The [Fenwick tracking analysis](https://www.fenwick.com/insights/publications/tracking-the-evolution-of-ai-insurance-regulation) of AI insurance regulation found that 92 percent of health insurers and 88 percent of auto insurers were already using AI as of late 2025, with a third of health insurers failing to regularly test models for bias — a data point that suggests the problem is not too little regulation, but that the regulation in place is not consistently enforced.

## The Innovation Tax Falls Hardest on the Innovators

Large incumbent carriers have compliance infrastructure. They have legal teams, actuarial departments, government affairs functions, and state-by-state regulatory relationships built across decades. When an explainability mandate or a human-review requirement arrives from Springfield or Hartford, they absorb it. Their model development cycles are slow enough, their legacy systems conventional enough, and their per-policy compliance costs diluted enough across enormous policy volumes to make this workable. Expensive, yes. Survivable, yes.

Regional carriers and insurtechs do not have that cushion. A startup deploying modern gradient-boosted ensemble models or neural network underwriting — systems that may genuinely outperform legacy actuarial methods on accuracy and, critically, on fairness for non-standard risks — faces a multi-jurisdiction compliance matrix with no obvious path. Modern ML models typically cannot generate per-decision textual explanations without post-hoc rationalization techniques (SHAP values, LIME) that introduce their own distortions. What such an explanation tells a consumer about why their homeowner's premium increased is often less accurate than no explanation at all — because it attaches certainty to an approximation.

This is what makes mandatory explainability requirements a covert **model architecture mandate**: only models that can generate post-hoc per-decision explanations survive multi-state examination. In practice, that means logistic regression and decision trees — the tools that legacy actuaries have always used — receive a structural regulatory subsidy over methods that are often more accurate and, for underserved populations with non-traditional risk profiles, frequently more equitable. The carriers with the most sophisticated, fairest models face the highest compliance friction. The carriers with the least sophisticated, most legible models get a pass. This dynamic inverts the consumer protection rationale for the requirement.

The NAIC's Big Data and AI Working Group at least asked the right question. Its [2025 Request for Information](https://content.naic.org/committees/h/big-data-artificial-intelligence-wg) on a potential model law on AI use in insurance specifically probed the trade-off between explainability and predictive accuracy. The question was well-posed. The answers coming from state legislatures have not been.

## The 12-State Pilot Is the Fork in the Road

The [NAIC AI Systems Evaluation Tool pilot](https://www.fenwick.com/insights/publications/naic-expands-ai-systems-evaluation-tool-pilot-program-to-12-states-key-updates-for-insurers-and-ai-vendors-supporting-insurers), running from March through September 2026 across California, Colorado, Connecticut, Florida, Iowa, Louisiana, Maryland, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Vermont, Virginia, and Wisconsin, is the most consequential insurance AI policy development of the year — not because of what it will find, but because of what it will force.

The tool is structured around four exhibits: Exhibit A quantifies AI deployment scope across the carrier; Exhibit B assesses governance and risk management frameworks; Exhibit C details high-risk AI implementations; Exhibit D covers data use and accommodation policies. Participating state regulators are conducting examinations across property/casualty, life, and health lines, with monthly coordination calls to standardize approach. Results will inform tool updates in September–October 2026, with formal adoption expected at the NAIC's fall meeting in November.

That timeline is the policy decision point. If the pilot demonstrates that examination-based governance — even without mandatory explainability or per-decision human review requirements — produces consistent, auditable outcomes across carriers and lines of business, the case for strengthening the NAIC framework into a binding model regulation is compelling, and the case for additional state mandates is weak. If the pilot reveals that voluntary coordination produces inconsistent examinations, carrier forum-shopping, or governance programs that are documented but not substantive, the case for federal minimum standards gains weight.

Either way, the evidence will not be available until September at the earliest. Illinois and Connecticut mandates are advancing now. The NAIC [Third-Party Data and Models Working Group](https://www.mondaq.com/unitedstates/insurance-laws-and-products/1718464/naic-fall-meeting-update-third-party-data-and-models-h-working-group-exposes-risk-based-regulatory-framework-for-third-party-data-and-model-vendors) exposed its draft regulatory framework in December 2025, with a model law expected later this year. The sequencing is backwards: the evidence from the most important examination pilot in NAIC history is six weeks away, and state legislatures are writing its conclusion in advance.

## Colorado's Retreat Validates the Thesis

The clearest evidence that horizontal AI mandates are the wrong instrument for insurance AI governance is Colorado's retreat from them.

[Colorado SB 189](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/05/colorado-governor-signs-sb-189), signed by Governor Polis on May 14, 2026, repealed and replaced the 2024 Colorado AI Act — the first U.S. law imposing broad developer-and-deployer obligations on "high-risk" AI systems, with algorithmic discrimination prevention requirements, mandatory risk management programs, and annual impact assessments. The replacement passed 34-1 in the Senate and 57-6 in the House. It stripped the duty to prevent algorithmic discrimination, eliminated mandatory impact assessments, and replaced substantive governance obligations with a [disclosure-only regime](https://www.troutmanprivacy.com/2026/05/colorado-legislature-passes-bill-to-repeal-and-replace-colorado-ai-act/): pre-decision notices, post-adverse-outcome disclosures within 30 days, and the right to request human reconsideration. Effective January 1, 2027.

The retreat accelerated through overlapping pressure in April 2026. [xAI filed a federal constitutional challenge](https://www.hrdive.com/news/colorado-ai-bias-law-unconstitutional-elon-musks-xai/817258/) against SB 205 on First Amendment and commerce clause grounds; the DOJ intervened in support, consistent with [a December 2025 executive order](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/) directing the Attorney General to challenge state AI laws the administration deemed onerous; a federal magistrate stayed enforcement of the original law on April 27. Within weeks, state lawmakers who had voted for SB 205 in 2024 introduced its replacement — acknowledging they could not identify a compliance path to the original law's 2026 effective date. But the deeper lesson is structural: horizontal AI mandates that apply across industries, risk levels, and model types generate compliance burdens that are not proportionate to their consumer protection benefits. Colorado's lawmakers recognized it.

The NAIC's examination-based model survived that pressure precisely because it did not overclaim. It did not promise to catch every instance of algorithmic discrimination through documentation requirements alone. It asked examiners to use existing market conduct authority to assess governance programs — a familiar, tested mechanism. That institutional humility turned out to be durable where SB 205's ambition was not.

It is also worth noting what Colorado retained in its revised regime: the disclosure and human-reconsideration rights that the NAIC examination framework was never designed to provide at the consumer level. This is not a contradiction of the NAIC approach — it is a complement to it. Examination-based governance operates at the program level; consumer notice rights operate at the transaction level. Both have a legitimate role. The error is conflating them into a single mandatory explainability regime that tries to do both and succeeds at neither.

## The Policy Prescription

The NAIC's examination-based framework is the right foundation. But "voluntary" is not sufficient, and the current trajectory — 24 bulletin adoptions, four insurance-specific overlays, state mandates advancing before the pilot concludes, and a third-party data model law in development — will produce the worst of both worlds: an innovation tax on the insurers building more accurate, fairer models, and an accountability gap on actual consumer harm.

Three things need to happen, in sequence:

**First, the NAIC pilot should complete before new state insurance AI mandates take effect.** State legislators in Illinois, Connecticut, and any other jurisdiction considering insurance-specific AI explainability or human-review requirements should allow the September 2026 pilot results to inform their decisions. The pilot was designed precisely to test whether examination-based governance produces adequate consumer protection. Layering mandates before reading those results is not evidence-based policymaking — it is political preemption of an evidence base that does not yet exist.

**Second, the NAIC should upgrade its governance work product from bulletin to binding model regulation.** The gap between a commissioner-issued bulletin (adopted through executive guidance) and a model regulation (adopted by state legislatures as binding law) is the gap between coordination and uniformity. The [Third-Party Data and Models Working Group](https://content.naic.org/committees/h/third-party-data-models-wg) is already developing a centralized vendor registry framework, narrowing its initial scope to pricing and underwriting as a starting point. Moving quickly to a binding model regulation — with uniform examination standards, common bias testing methodologies, and a single third-party vendor registration framework — is the most important thing NAIC can accomplish before the fall meeting. A bulletin that 24 states have adopted with varying supplementary requirements is not a uniform standard. A binding model regulation can be.

**Third, Congress should clarify FIO's authority as a backstop of last resort.** The [Federal Insurance Office at Treasury](https://home.treasury.gov/policy-issues/financial-markets-financial-institutions-and-fiscal-service/federal-insurance-office) already holds statutory authority to monitor all aspects of the insurance industry and identify gaps in state regulation. That authority does not currently extend to setting substantive insurance AI standards — but a narrow, conditional congressional grant could change that: if NAIC coordination produces a binding uniform standard on AI governance within a defined period, the FIO backstop is unnecessary and sits dormant; if it does not, FIO may establish minimum federal standards to fill the identified gap. This is not federal preemption of state insurance regulation. It is a conditional deadline that makes voluntary coordination credible.

The political headwind is real. [H.R. 643, the Federal Insurance Office Elimination Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/643), introduced in the 119th Congress, has support from state insurance regulators who view FIO as an unnecessary federal foothold — and [insurance industry trades have lined up behind it](https://www.insurancejournal.com/news/national/2026/01/20/854885.htm). Any use of FIO authority should be designed with that context explicitly in mind: modest in scope, conditioned on NAIC coordination failure, and framed as reinforcing rather than displacing the state-based system. The argument is not that federal insurance regulation is preferable — it manifestly is not. The argument is that a conditional backstop authority, never exercised if NAIC succeeds, is the instrument that gives state coordination teeth.

## Conclusion

Insurance AI governance is not a question in search of a framework. The NAIC provided one. The question is whether the states will allow it to work, or whether a combination of genuine regulatory concern and competitive political dynamics will bury a functional examination-based system under fifty layers of disclosure mandates, explainability requirements, audit obligations, and human-review rules that no regulator can enforce consistently and no innovator can satisfy uniformly.

Colorado's horizontal AI Act is gone. The EU's high-risk AI deadline has been deferred. The twelve-state pilot is running, and results are six weeks away. The NAIC's fall calendar gives the organization a genuine window to move from voluntary bulletin to binding uniform standard before the patchwork calcifies.

The examination-based approach asked the right question about insurance AI governance. Whether the institution can make the answer stick is the policy question of the fall.
