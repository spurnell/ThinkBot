On June 22, 2026, President Trump signed [Executive Order 14411, "Ushering in the Next Frontier of Quantum Innovation,"](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/06/ushering-in-the-next-frontier-of-quantum-innovation/) which commits the government to "maintain a strategic technical advantage in QIST and lead the development of a robust and trusted quantum ecosystem across QIST research, manufacturing, commercialization, and application." Buried inside that ambition is a directive to "harmoniz[e] research security and export control policies with international allies and partners" and to keep "robust and balanced security controls" from "unduly impacting quantum innovation."

That balancing act was already failing before the ink dried. The export control architecture EO 14411 now elevates to presidential policy is the [Bureau of Industry and Security's September 2024 interim final rule](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/09/06/2024-19633/commerce-control-list-additions-and-revisions-implementation-of-controls-on-advanced-technologies), which draws a bright numeric line through the quantum industry: systems with 34–100 qubits are controlled if their two-qubit gate error rate falls at or below 0.01%, with looser error-rate ceilings applying as qubit count rises through graduated bands, and any system exceeding 2,000 qubits is controlled outright regardless of error rate. Every controlled item — hardware, software, and underlying technology — carries a [worldwide license requirement, with a presumption of denial for exports to Country Group D:1 and D:5 destinations](https://www.bis.gov/press-release/department-commerce-implements-controls-quantum-computing-other-advanced-technologies-alongside) — [the same country groupings](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/15/appendix-Supplement_No_1_to_part_740) used for the most sensitive semiconductor and missile-technology controls.

EO 14411 didn't invent this framework, but it did something arguably more consequential: it made "harmonizing" the qubit-threshold regime with allies a matter of stated national policy, directing the State Department to fold it into international frameworks like the "Pax Silica" initiative within 120 days. A rule written as a technical annex to the Commerce Control List is now the operational core of America's quantum diplomacy. That makes getting the threshold right — not just getting *a* threshold — a first-order policy question, and right now the threshold is wrong.

## The proxy problem: qubit count isn't computational advantage

A qubit-count-and-error-rate cliff is an appealingly bright line for a regulator that wants a rule an export compliance officer can apply in an afternoon. It is also a poor stand-in for the thing the rule is actually trying to control: exploitable computational advantage over classical computing on tasks that matter to national security, principally code-breaking and certain classes of optimization and simulation.

The reason is that raw physical qubit count and headline error rate are inputs to capability, not measures of it. What determines whether a quantum system can actually run Shor's algorithm against a cryptographically relevant key size, or deliver a real simulation advantage in materials or chemistry, is a stack of variables the threshold ignores entirely: qubit connectivity and topology, gate fidelity *distribution* (not just a ceiling), coherence times, and — most importantly — the overhead of quantum error correction needed to turn noisy physical qubits into a much smaller number of reliable logical qubits. A 2,000-physical-qubit noisy intermediate-scale device and a fault-tolerant 2,000-*logical*-qubit machine are separated by orders of magnitude in real capability, and the current rule cannot tell them apart because it was never built to. As a 2025 legal analysis of the U.S. and EU quantum control regimes by Cambridge and Copenhagen researchers [Peter Alexander Earls Davis, Mateo Aboy, and Timo Minssen notes](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5404548), quantum computing's "layered technological stack and dual-use potential" create exactly the kind of regulatory dilemma that a single hardware metric cannot resolve.

This is a Goodhart's Law problem, not a rounding error. Once a specific qubit-and-error-rate number becomes the control trigger, real-world quantum capability can move independently of it in both directions:

- **Adversary-relevant capability can arrive without crossing the line.** Error mitigation techniques, hybrid classical-quantum algorithms, and tensor-network-assisted simulation methods can extract materially more useful computation from a fixed, modest hardware base — the quantum-computing analog of the software efficiency gains that have let AI labs do more with fewer FLOPs. A foreign program that stays under 2,000 physical qubits, or keeps its published error rate a fraction above the control ceiling, faces no licensing barrier at all under the current rule, no matter how much algorithmic sophistication it has layered on top.
- **Commercially benign systems get swept in anyway.** University research clusters built for algorithm development, cloud-quantum-as-a-service offerings sized for enterprise pilot programs, and quantum sensing and metrology devices that use qubit-based hardware for entirely non-computational purposes can cross the numeric line without posing any plausible cryptographic or weapons-relevant risk. The rule cannot distinguish a 40-qubit teaching cluster at a research university from a 40-qubit system purpose-built to attack encryption, because it isn't asking that question.

## Worldwide scope is the real cost driver

The threshold's design flaw is compounded by its geographic reach. This is not a China-specific rule. Because the license requirement is worldwide and the presumption-of-denial tier applies specifically to D:1/D:5 destinations, every other transaction — to Canada, Australia, the UK, Japan, EU member states, and other close partners — still requires an export license, even though it will typically be approved. That licensing burden lands on exactly the allied research collaborations and commercial cloud-quantum relationships that give the U.S. quantum ecosystem its scale advantage in the first place, and on the deemed-export obligations that follow any foreign national working in a U.S. quantum lab.

ThinkBot has made a version of this argument before about semiconductor export controls: a worldwide licensing default with presumption-of-denial architecture, applied indiscriminately to allied and adversary destinations alike, taxes the commercial base that underwrites U.S. technological leadership more than it constrains the adversary it's aimed at. Quantum computing is a smaller, more research-intensive industry than semiconductors, which makes it more exposed to this dynamic, not less — a handful of licensing delays or a chilling effect on a university's ability to bring in a postdoc from a non-adversary country can measurably slow a field that federal officials themselves [have told Congress is already short on trained talent relative to demand](https://fedscoop.com/quantum-workforce-meeting-demand-federal-officials-tell-lawmakers/) — NIST's own director put it to the House Science Committee as "a workforce shortage — plain and simple." BIS has partially recognized this with a [License Exception for Implemented Export Controls (License Exception IEC)](https://www.bis.gov/press-release/department-commerce-implements-controls-quantum-computing-other-advanced-technologies-alongside), which lets transactions with countries that have adopted equivalent national controls skip the license application. That's the right instinct, but it's currently narrow, country-by-country, and disconnected from the capability question — it exempts a destination, not a use case.

## The fix that AI export policy already found

BIS does not need to invent a new regulatory model to fix this. It needs to finish applying one it already started building for advanced computing chips.

Early AI chip export controls relied on a comparably crude proxy: [raw theoretical processing performance](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2022/10/13/2022-21658/implementation-of-additional-export-controls-certain-advanced-computing-and-semiconductor), expressed in FLOPS. That metric proved gameable and imprecise for the same structural reason the qubit-count line is imprecise — headline throughput numbers don't map cleanly onto real-world capability, and chip designers could (and did) architect around a single-number cutoff. BIS's subsequent rules moved toward composite, harder-to-game metrics — [Total Processing Performance combined with memory bandwidth thresholds](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2026/01/bis-revises-export-review-policy-for-advanced-ai-chips-destined-for-china-and-macau) — and, as of the [January 2026 revision](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/01/15/2026-00789/revision-to-license-review-policy-for-advanced-computing-commodities), toward case-by-case license review tied to end-use and end-user conditions rather than a pure hardware trigger. The direction of travel in AI export policy has been away from a single static hardware number and toward multi-factor, capability-sensitive assessment. Quantum export control is starting exactly where AI chip control started, and it should not have to relearn the same lesson through a second multi-year regulatory cycle.

Concretely, BIS should:

1. **Build a capability-based licensing track alongside the bright-line threshold**, using fault-tolerance-relevant benchmarks — logical qubit count after error correction, published quantum volume or algorithmic-qubit scores, and demonstrated performance on cryptographically relevant algorithm classes — rather than physical qubit count and a single error-rate ceiling. A system that clears the numeric threshold but cannot demonstrate meaningful fault-tolerant performance should have a faster path to license approval; a system that stays under the line but demonstrates outsized algorithmic capability should not get a free pass by default.
2. **Expand License Exception IEC into a genuine general license for close allies**, covering routine academic collaboration and commercial cloud-quantum access with Pax Silica partner countries, rather than requiring case-by-case license applications for transactions that are approved as a matter of course. If EO 14411's harmonization directive is going to mean anything operationally, this is where it should show up first.
3. **Create a streamlined deemed-export pathway for foreign nationals from non-adversary countries** working in U.S. university and national-lab quantum programs, so research security obligations don't become a de facto tax on the international talent pipeline that quantum research — like most cutting-edge STEM fields — depends on.
4. **Commit to periodic, published recalibration of the threshold** against the same fault-tolerance and algorithmic-efficiency benchmarks used in the licensing track, so the numeric line doesn't ossify into a fixed target that evolving hardware and evolving algorithms will eventually run past in either direction.

## The stakes are the ecosystem, not just the rule

None of this is an argument against controlling quantum computing exports to strategic competitors. A cryptographically relevant quantum computer in the hands of an adversary is a legitimate and serious concern, and BIS is right to treat quantum hardware, software, and know-how as dual-use technology worthy of export discipline. The problem is that the current threshold recalibrates the wrong variable — punishing raw hardware scale while leaving algorithmic capability effectively unregulated — and does so with a worldwide licensing default that burdens exactly the allied research and commercial relationships the administration says it wants to harmonize and protect.

EO 14411 states the goal plainly: a "robust and trusted quantum ecosystem." An ecosystem's robustness comes from its scale — the breadth of university programs, cloud-access customers, and allied commercial partners that keep U.S. quantum firms at the technological frontier. A qubit-count cliff that treats a Toronto research cluster and a Beijing weapons lab as the same licensing problem doesn't protect that ecosystem; it shrinks it, one delayed license and one discouraged collaboration at a time. BIS has already found the right model for this fix in its own AI chip rules. It should apply it to quantum before the current threshold hardens into the default the rest of the world assumes the United States actually wants.
