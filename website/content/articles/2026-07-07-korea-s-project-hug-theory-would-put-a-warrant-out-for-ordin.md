On July 1, South Korea's Fair Trade Commission sent Google an examiner's report accusing the company of anticompetitively foreclosing rival Android app stores through "Project Hug," internally known as the Games Velocity Program (GVP) — a developer-incentive program that ran from July 2019 to March 2026 ([MLex](https://www.mlex.com/mlex/antitrust/articles/2495887/google-s-project-hug-deals-with-game-publishers-face-south-korea-antitrust-scrutiny)), under which Google reportedly paid Activision Blizzard alone roughly $360 million in cash and other benefits in 2020 to keep its titles on Google Play on terms "at least as good as" any rival store ([PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/cpi-posts/google-under-fire-for-360m-incentives-to-activision-blizzard-amid-epic-games-suit/)). The KFTC says the program touched roughly $9.2 billion in revenue and exposes Google to a fine of up to 6 percent of that figure — as much as $547 million — plus corrective orders ([TechTimes](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/319506/20260702/google-ordered-pay-15b-sweden-korea-accuses-it-project-hug-exclusivity-scheme.htm); [BigGo Finance](https://finance.biggo.com/news/9eac33c7-0a06-4f48-bbe4-93c2029fd974)). Google has eight weeks to respond and maintains that "Google Play competes fairly with other app stores and delivers numerous benefits to developers and consumers in Korea" (statement to Reuters, cited via [MarketScreener](https://www.marketscreener.com/news/south-korean-trade-watchdog-alleges-google-abused-its-position-in-android-app-store-ce7f5fdddd80f427)).

What makes this case novel isn't the target — Google's app-store conduct has been litigated for years — but the mechanism the KFTC has chosen to attack. The GVP was not an exclusive-dealing contract. Developers were free to distribute on Samsung's Galaxy Store, ONE store, or any rival marketplace; nothing barred entry or punished defection. What Google offered instead was cloud credits, ad inventory, and YouTube promotion, conditioned on keeping Play Store terms competitive with whatever rivals offered. That is a payment for favorable treatment, not a penalty for disfavored treatment. The KFTC's theory treats that distinction as immaterial. It shouldn't be — the distinction is the whole ballgame in antitrust law.

**Inducement is not exclusive dealing.** Doctrine built around cases like *[Tampa Electric v. Nashville Coal](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/365/320/)* and *[Standard Oil of California v. United States](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/337/293/)* asks whether a restraint forecloses a substantial share of a market to competition and probably produces anticompetitive effects — not whether a dominant firm made itself more attractive to counterparties. The animating concern in exclusive-dealing law is that a locked-in counterparty *loses* the freedom to deal elsewhere, or is penalized for exercising it. A payment that a developer is free to accept, decline, or later walk away from doesn't produce that harm; it increases the developer's welfare relative to the no-payment baseline. Collapsing "payment for favorable terms" into "foreclosure" would sweep in co-marketing agreements, volume discounts, MDF (market development fund) programs, and cloud/ad bundling that are routine across every platform business — commercial arrangements American courts have generally required plaintiffs to prove cause *actual* exclusionary effect, not merely successful competition for a counterparty's business (see *[Ohio v. American Express Co.](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/585/16-1454/)*, 585 U.S. 529 (2018), on the need to show net anticompetitive effects on the actual relevant market rather than harm to a subset of participants). The closest domestic analogue — the Third Circuit's *[LePage's v. 3M](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/court/us-3rd-circuit/1169758.html)* bundled-rebate decision — is instructive precisely because it has been so widely criticized by antitrust economists for imposing liability without requiring proof that rebates were below cost or that rivals were actually excluded, rather than merely out-competed.

That is the crux of the KFTC's theory: Google's rivals did not disappear. Samsung's Galaxy Store and OEM-preloaded alternatives remained available throughout the GVP's run. The allegation is that Google *won* more developer loyalty than competing stores did, using tools — cloud credits, marketing spend, ad inventory — that are the ordinary currency of platform competition. Antitrust law has never treated out-competing a rival for a counterparty's business as foreclosure; if it did, every discount, rebate, or bundled service arrangement offered by a market leader would be presumptively suspect merely because the market leader has more resources to offer.

**Cutting incentive programs hurts the developers the theory claims to protect.** If payment-for-favorable-terms is treated as legally radioactive, the rational response for any dominant platform — and its more cautious rivals — is to eliminate developer incentive programs altogether rather than risk a foreclosure claim. That is a worse outcome for the small and independent studios that rely on cloud credits, ad credits, and promotional support to compete for attention against triple-A publishers. A rule that chills inducements without regard to their effect on output or price does not protect competition; it protects a particular class of would-be competitors (rival app stores) at the direct expense of developers and, ultimately, the consumers who benefit from a more vibrant catalog of games.

**No US enforcer has followed the KFTC here — and that gap is meaningful, not incidental.** Google's Play Store conduct was already tested in US courts: a jury found Google liable to Epic Games in December 2023, and the Ninth Circuit affirmed that verdict in July 2025 ([Mintz](https://www.mintz.com/insights-center/viewpoints/2025-08-06-ninth-circuit-upholds-jury-verdict-against-and-remedies)). But that case was a private Sherman Act suit resting on the *totality* of Google's exclusionary scheme — anti-fragmentation agreements with device manufacturers, revenue-sharing deals conditioned on excluding rival app stores, and Project Hug payments *combined*. Neither the jury nor any US antitrust enforcer has endorsed the freestanding proposition the KFTC now advances: that inducement payments alone, absent complementary restraints that actually lock out rivals, constitute foreclosure. Neither the FTC nor the DOJ — both of which have separately pursued Google over search defaults and ad tech — has signaled interest in importing a standalone inducement theory. That silence reflects the consumer welfare standard doing its job: US enforcers have consistently required proof of actual market effects — higher prices, reduced output, degraded quality — not merely evidence that a dominant firm's commercial offer was more compelling than a rival's.

**The extraterritoriality problem compounds the doctrinal one.** Google is a US company; the KFTC's theory, if it results in a final order and fine, effectively sets a global compliance floor for how a US platform may structure commercial relationships with developers everywhere, since firms rarely maintain jurisdiction-specific developer-relations architectures. Korea's domestic platforms and marketplaces face no comparable scrutiny of their own developer-incentive practices under this same theory — raising the question of whether the standard is being applied evenhandedly or reserved for the market's most successful foreign entrant.

None of this requires defending Google's conduct on the merits; that question is for the KFTC's forthcoming written findings and Google's response. It requires only that the KFTC hold itself to the same standard countries with mature competition regimes have converged on: show that the challenged conduct actually reduced interstore competition or harmed consumers through higher prices, less choice, or degraded quality — not merely that Google's offer outcompeted the alternatives. Absent that showing, "Project Hug" is a name for ordinary platform competition, not a conspiracy.
