On April 30, 2026, the FCC adopted [FCC 26-27](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/FCC-26-27A1.pdf), a Further Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (FNPRM) that would require every originating voice provider — wireless carriers, VoIP services, and the small resellers, or mobile virtual network operators (MVNOs), that ride on their networks — to collect a customer's verified name, physical address, a government-issued identification number, and a backup phone number before activating or renewing *any* voice service, prepaid included, and to retain that file for four years after the account closes. Violations would carry a [$2,500-per-call base forfeiture](https://www.dwt.com/blogs/broadband-advisor/2026/05/fcc-kyc-rules-voice-providers-robocalls). Initial comments closed June 25; [reply comments are due July 27](https://www.fcc.gov/document/cgb-announces-comment-dates-know-your-customer-fnprm) — ten days from now.

Coverage so far, including a widely shared [Fortune piece](https://fortune.com/2026/07/14/fcc-proposal-burner-phone-privacy-voip-kyc-data/) and a [joint EFF/ACLU comment](https://www.eff.org/files/2026/06/25/2026.06.25_fcc_kyc_joint_comment.pdf), treats this as a straightforward civil-liberties story: the end of the anonymous "burner phone." That framing is correct but incomplete: the FCC has already built — and adopted — a narrower tool for a closely related problem, and this FNPRM never explains why that tool is insufficient before reaching for a much blunter one.

## Two different threats, one blunt instrument

In November 2023, the Commission adopted [FCC 23-95](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/FCC-23-95A1.pdf), a rule targeting SIM-swap and port-out fraud — the practice of tricking or bribing a carrier into moving a victim's phone number to an attacker's device or a new account, typically to intercept SMS two-factor codes and drain bank or crypto accounts. The Commission's fix was deliberately narrow: carriers must use "secure methods of authenticating a customer" before processing a SIM change or port request, bar the use of "predictable or easily obtainable information" like Social Security numbers, and notify customers immediately when a swap or port request is made. Nothing in the order requires carriers to collect a driver's license number or retain it for years; the [rule was built around authenticating an *existing* customer against harm to *that* customer](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/12/08/2023-26338/protecting-consumers-from-sim-swap-and-port-out-fraud), not around vetting the identity of every new subscriber against harms to third parties.

FCC 26-27 targets a different problem — the Commission's own filing points to illegal robocalls, scam-call origination, and, more expansively, "fraud, espionage, or influence operations" and "abuse in text messaging networks." That is a real problem, but a different threat vector requiring a different fix. SIM-swap fraud is solved by authenticating that the person modifying an account is who they claim to be. Illegal robocall origination is a scale-and-traceability problem — the challenge is identifying and shutting down a small number of high-volume bad actors quickly, not confirming the identity of the tens of millions of ordinary people who buy a $20 prepaid line at a convenience store and never place an unlawful call in their lives. Treating "we can't easily trace bad actors" and "we don't have identity records on every subscriber" as the same problem is a category error, and it's the FNPRM's central unexamined premise.

## The tool already on the shelf is underused, not exhausted

The FCC doesn't need to speculate about whether a narrower approach can work against calling fraud — it already has one, and it isn't fully deployed. The STIR/SHAKEN caller-ID authentication framework the Commission mandated years ago reached roughly [85% signed traffic among Tier-1 carriers in 2025 (93% at the highest attestation level) — but only 17.5% among smaller providers](https://cfca.org/tns-2026-robocall-report-whats-next-going-further-than-stir-shaken/), the traceback consortium reports. That gap, concentrated in the small-carrier segment, is a targeting and enforcement failure, not proof that the entire authentication-based model is inadequate. The Commission has, in fact, already recognized this: on May 20, 2026 it separately took up a "[Know Your Upstream Provider](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DOC-421205A1.pdf)" FNPRM tightening attestation standards and closing loopholes that let unsigned traffic pass through intermediate carriers. That is the FCC 23-95 model — proportionate, targeted at the point of actual risk — extended to the robocall context. FCC 26-27 runs in parallel to that effort and, on the current record, doesn't explain why closing the STIR/SHAKEN gap and finishing the upstream-provider work is insufficient, such that every ordinary retail customer also needs a verified-ID file retained for four years.

Notably, FCC 26-27 itself gestures at proportionality without following through: it already proposes heightened scrutiny — collecting intended service use and originating IP address — for "high-volume" and foreign-based customers, and separately asks whether prepaid customers should be treated differently. That's the right instinct. But the baseline the Commission proposes for everyone else is still collect-verify-and-retain, not a risk-tiered exemption. The rule should finish what it started: if high-volume and suspicious-traffic accounts warrant extra scrutiny, ordinary single-line retail and prepaid customers — who plainly are not the source of mass robocall campaigns — warrant less, not the same universal floor.

## Who actually absorbs the compliance cost

The burden of building out identity-verification infrastructure, running documents against ID-verification vendors, and maintaining a four-year records system doesn't fall evenly. It falls hardest on the prepaid carriers and MVNOs that serve cash-pay, rural, and unbanked customers specifically because those providers built their business model around *not* requiring a bank account or credit check. Prepaid is not a niche: [North America's prepaid wireless base tops 120 million accounts](https://www.lightreading.com/5g/the-renaissance-of-prepaid-wireless), heavily concentrated among smaller MVNOs serving [populations with above-average unbanked and underbanked rates](https://www.fdic.gov/household-survey) — precisely the customers a mandatory ID-and-address-verification pipeline is most expensive, relative to revenue, to serve. A universal KYC floor is a fixed compliance cost that scales down badly for small entrants; that's a market-structure problem — a barrier to entry in the segment of the wireless market that most needs low barriers.

And the privacy trade-off is real, even if it isn't the only lens. The EFF/ACLU comment notes that [15 million American adults lack a driver's license and 2.6 million lack any government photo ID](https://www.eff.org/files/2026/06/25/2026.06.25_fcc_kyc_joint_comment.pdf) — a nontrivial share of exactly the low-income population most likely to rely on prepaid service. Anonymous phone access is also a documented safety tool: NNEDV's [Safety Net Project](https://www.techsafety.org/) works with domestic violence survivors precisely because an untraceable line can be the difference between escape and discovery by an abuser, which is also why Congress passed the Safe Connections Act to let survivors [separate phone lines from abusers' accounts](https://www.fcc.gov/consumers/guides/how-request-phone-line-separation-abusers-account). None of this means the FCC lacks a legitimate interest in curbing illegal calling — it plainly has one. It means the record doesn't show the Commission weighed that cost against the marginal benefit above what STIR/SHAKEN enforcement and the Know-Your-Upstream-Provider rule already deliver.

## What reply comments should say

The comparison to financial KYC that the FCC's own filing invokes cuts against the agency's proposal, not for it. Bank Secrecy Act KYC rules target money laundering at high-value transactional chokepoints; they are not applied to every $20 retail purchase. Proportionality, not precedent-matching to a differently structured risk, should govern here.

Reply comments — due July 27 — should press the Commission on a narrow, specific ask: incorporate the FCC 23-95 model. Require the Commission to justify, on the record, why finishing STIR/SHAKEN deployment among smaller carriers and implementing the Know-Your-Upstream-Provider rule is insufficient before adding a blanket identity-and-retention mandate. Where the FNPRM already proposes risk-based tiering for high-volume and foreign-traffic accounts, extend that logic rather than abandon it — exempt ordinary single-line retail and prepaid accounts from baseline ID collection, and reserve verified-identity requirements for the accounts the agency's own traffic-pattern analysis flags as high-risk. That isn't a call to kill the docket; illegal robocalling is a legitimate target and the Commission has every right to regulate it. It's a call to make the agency prove, in this record, that the tool it already built and is still rolling out isn't enough before it reaches for one that reaches every American with a phone.
