The House Energy and Commerce Committee's Subcommittee on Energy advanced a bill the week of June 24 that tells you something important about where the AI-infrastructure debate is heading: the fight is no longer about whether to build data centers, but about who pays for the electrons that power them.

H.R. 9340, the [Ratepayer Protection Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/9340/text), introduced by Reps. Gabe Evans (R-CO) and Kathy Castor (D-FL) and advanced with the backing of Chairmen Brett Guthrie and Bob Latta, would amend the Public Utility Regulatory Policies Act of 1978 to require state regulators to establish a federal-floor "large load standard." Under it, any customer drawing 100 megawatts or more — in practice, almost exclusively hyperscale data centers — would have to pay the full incremental cost of the transmission, generation, and interconnection upgrades its demand requires, rather than having those costs socialized across residential bills. The bill codifies the [White House's March 2026 Ratepayer Protection Pledge](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/03/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-advances-energy-affordability-with-the-ratepayer-protection-pledge/), under which Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft, OpenAI, Oracle, and xAI committed to build, bring, or buy the power they need and cover delivery-infrastructure costs directly.

ThinkBot's view: this is good policy, and Congress should move it, because it heads off a much worse outcome. Cost-causation pricing is not a novel regulatory imposition on the AI buildout — it is ordinary ratemaking, decades old, that state commissions have simply been slow to extend to a new class of industrial mega-user. The real threat to America's AI infrastructure isn't this bill. It's the alternative: a growing wave of state and local moratoriums, sparked by residential bill shock, that could freeze compute buildout far more effectively than any tariff redesign.

## This is regulatory catch-up, not regulatory overreach

Cost causation is the foundational principle of American utility rate design: rates should track the costs a customer class actually imposes on the system. It's the same logic that has long governed how utilities price service to steel mills, aluminum smelters, and refineries — large, lumpy industrial loads that can require dedicated substations or transmission upgrades get dedicated tariffs, not a blended rate spread across every residential customer on the system. As a recent [E3/Halcyon large-load tariff whitepaper](https://www.ethree.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/E3_Large-Load-Tariff-Whitepaper-1.pdf) lays out, the tariff mechanics being proposed for data centers — minimum demand charges, long-term contract terms with exit fees, credit and collateral requirements — are standard tools regulators have used for other large industrial customers for decades. What's new is the scale and speed of the customer class, not the underlying economics.

The market has already been moving this direction without Congress's help. By [May 2026, twenty-three states had approved at least one large-load tariff](https://environmentenergyleader.com/stories/who-pays-for-the-data-center-buildout-23-states-have-already-decided,129803), according to Edison Electric Institute tracking, with seven more pending. Pennsylvania's utility commission [found "broad consensus among stakeholders on the fundamental principle of cost causation"](https://whyy.org/articles/pennsylvania-public-utility-commission-data-center-protection/) in its own large-load proceeding. California, Ohio, and Utah have gone further and enacted statutes. [Florida's SB 484](https://www.flgov.com/eog/news/press/2026/governor-ron-desantis-signs-law-protect-floridians-subsidizing-data-centers), signed in May and effective July 1, requires 50-plus-megawatt users to pay their full cost of service and bars utilities from passing those costs to residential and small-business ratepayers. [Oklahoma followed with a 75-megawatt threshold in HB 2992](https://www.okhouse.gov/posts/News-20260506_2). This is a rate-design correction moving through the normal channels of state utility regulation — H.R. 9340 simply sets a federal floor to make sure every state gets there, rather than leaving it to the states that happen to act first.

That framing matters because it cuts against the instinct to treat any bill touching data centers as either a subsidy grab or a regulatory crackdown on AI. It's neither. It's price accuracy. When a hyperscale campus adds 300 megawatts of load and the local utility must build new transmission and generation capacity to serve it, someone pays for that capacity. If the tariff structure doesn't isolate that cost to the customer that caused it, the cost gets rolled into the general rate base and paid by every household on the grid — a straightforward cross-subsidy. ThinkBot has consistently argued that markets allocate capital better than politics; a cross-subsidy is itself a political allocation, just a quiet one, and correcting it is a pro-market position, not an anti-tech one. The [Center for Data Innovation's assessment](https://itif.org/publications/2026/02/25/the-ratepayer-protection-pledge-is-a-pragmatic-path-forward-says-center-for-data-innovation/) of the underlying pledge — calling it "a pragmatic path forward" — reflects the same judgment: getting the price signal right is compatible with, not hostile to, continued AI buildout.

## The real threat isn't cost allocation — it's the backlash to not having it

Here is the case for treating H.R. 9340 as urgent rather than merely sensible: unpriced externalities are radicalizing the politics of data-center siting in real time, and the fallout is landing on the AI industry far harder than a fair tariff ever would.

The numbers are stark. A [Gallup poll fielded March 2–18, 2026](https://news.gallup.com/poll/709772/americans-oppose-data-centers-area.aspx) found 71 percent of Americans oppose having a data center built near them — worse than the 53 percent who oppose a nearby nuclear plant, a higher opposition level than Gallup has recorded for nuclear plants since it began asking the question in 2001 — with 48 percent strongly opposed. In the first quarter of 2026 alone, local backlash [delayed or blocked at least 75 data-center projects worth a cumulative $130 billion](https://www.datacenterwatch.org/q1-2026). That opposition is not abstract NIMBYism about noise and land use, though those factor in; it is substantially a reaction to the fear, increasingly borne out in utility filings, that data-center demand is going to show up on household electric bills. California's Little Hoover Commission [warned lawmakers in March](https://lhc.ca.gov/wp-content/uploads/LHC-Report-292-Data-Centers-California-Electricity-Policy-FINAL-PUBLIC-3.3.26.pdf) that Pacific Gas & Electric alone has fielded data-center interconnection requests totaling roughly 10 gigawatts — about four times the output of the Diablo Canyon nuclear plant — and urged the state to require large users to prepay for infrastructure and commit to a defined share of proposed demand before those costs land on residential bills.

Where that anxiety goes unaddressed, it hardens into policy that is far more damaging to AI infrastructure than a cost-causation tariff. More than [300 data-center bills have been introduced across 30-plus states](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/2/20/state-data-center-legislation-in-2026-tackles-energy-and-tax-issues) in 2026, and the center of gravity is shifting from incentives toward restriction. At least [twelve states have filed outright moratorium bills](https://goodjobsfirst.org/data-center-moratorium-bills-are-spreading-in-2026/) this year. Maine's legislature passed [a 20-megawatt moratorium that Gov. Janet Mills ultimately vetoed](https://www.maine.gov/governor/mills/news/governor-mills-announces-decision-ld-307-2026-04-24) — but only after signaling she would have signed it absent a carve-out for one specific project. [Seattle's city council](https://council.seattle.gov/2026/06/09/city-council-passes-emergency-data-center-moratorium-and-policy-framework/) and a growing number of municipalities, including [the Lysander, New York town board, which passed a six-month construction pause in May after 350 residents turned out to oppose a local project](https://cnycentral.com/news/local/lysander-town-board-to-vote-on-six-month-moratorium-for-new-data-centers), have moved on their own moratoriums. At the federal level, Sen. Bernie Sanders and Rep. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez introduced an [Artificial Intelligence Data Center Moratorium Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/4214) that would halt construction or upgrades on any data center of 20 megawatts or more nationwide until unspecified "national safeguards" are established.

This is the actual choice facing the AI buildout: fix the price signal now, through ordinary rate design, or watch residential bill anger keep pushing legislatures toward blunt instruments — moratoriums, permitting freezes, outright bans — that don't discriminate between well-sited, fairly priced projects and poorly planned ones. A large-load tariff is a scalpel. A moratorium is a guillotine. Congress and state regulators that want to preserve America's capacity to build AI infrastructure at the pace the technology demands should prefer the scalpel, and prefer it before the guillotine becomes the median voter's answer.

## Federal floor, not federal takeover — and why that's the right call here

H.R. 9340 sets a minimum standard and leaves implementation to state public utility commissions, which retain their traditional authority to design the actual tariff mechanics — rate classes, contract terms, minimum-bill provisions — within the federal floor. That is the correct division of labor, and it is worth being explicit about why, because ThinkBot has argued elsewhere for a very different federalism outcome on a related-looking issue.

In our recent analysis of the [SECURE Data Act](/articles/2026-06-30-secure-data-act-preemption-feature-not-bug), we argued for full federal preemption of the state privacy patchwork, on the grounds that data governance is inherently interstate — a single national platform cannot operate fifty different compliance regimes for the same data flows without crushing overhead that falls hardest on smaller entrants. Utility cost allocation is a different animal. Electricity is generated, transmitted, and priced within physically distinct regional grids and state-jurisdiction utility territories; a large-load tariff in ERCOT reflects different generation mix, transmission constraints, and cost structures than one in PJM or the Pacific Northwest. Utility ratemaking has been state and local jurisdiction since the Federal Power Act itself, for sound reasons — state commissions have the granular cost data and local accountability that a single federal rate design could never replicate. A federal takeover of data-center tariff design would be the wrong tool for this job, however well-intentioned.

That said, a floor is worth having, and 23-states-and-rising should not be read as evidence the floor is unnecessary. The concern isn't a patchwork of large-load tariffs — the compliance burden of tracking one rate class in different jurisdictions is trivial compared to the fifty-jurisdiction data-privacy problem, since hyperscalers already negotiate individualized interconnection agreements state by state as a matter of course. The concern is the states that haven't moved, where the cross-subsidy keeps compounding on residential bills until the backlash described above sets in. A federal floor forces laggard states to catch up to what Pennsylvania, Florida, and Oklahoma have already figured out, without displacing the states already doing this well.

## The guardrail: don't let "ratepayer protection" become the vehicle for something else

The risk in H.R. 9340's path forward isn't the bill's current text — it's what gets attached to it between subcommittee and floor vote. "Ratepayer protection" is popular, bipartisan-coded language, which makes it an attractive vehicle for riders that have nothing to do with cost allocation. Congress and state legislatures should watch for, and reject, amendments that use ratepayer-protection framing to smuggle in:

- **De facto moratoriums dressed as safeguards** — provisions requiring open-ended "impact studies," indefinite permitting pauses, or unspecified "national safeguards" before any large-load interconnection can proceed, of the kind the federal moratorium bill and several state proposals already contain.
- **Discriminatory permitting delays** that single out data centers for review timelines or approval thresholds systematically stricter than those applied to other large industrial loads drawing comparable power.
- **Tax and incentive claw-backs bundled in as cost-allocation fixes** — several states have already frozen data-center tax exemptions this year under the same broad ratepayer-protection banner; that is a separate fiscal-policy question and should be debated on its own terms, not folded into rate-design legislation where it escapes proper scrutiny.

The distinction is not subtle, and it should be policed rigorously: requiring a data center to pay for the substation its own demand necessitates is cost recovery. Requiring a data center to wait years for a study before it can connect at all, or subjecting it to review standards no other 100-megawatt customer faces, is protectionism wearing cost-causation's clothes. ThinkBot supports the former without reservation and would oppose the latter regardless of what it's called.

## The stakes: grid buildout is now an AI-competitiveness constraint

None of this is happening in a vacuum. The Department of Energy's Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory projects that [data centers, which consumed 4.4 percent of U.S. electricity in 2023, could account for 6.7 to 12 percent by 2028](https://eta-publications.lbl.gov/sites/default/files/2024-12/lbnl-2024-united-states-data-center-energy-usage-report_1.pdf) — a demand trajectory that has forced grid planners to revise load forecasts upward for three consecutive years. That growth is the physical substrate of the AI race: every frontier model, every inference deployment, every robotics and biotech application downstream of AI capability runs on a grid that has to be built fast enough to keep up. If interconnection queues, transmission upgrades, and generation buildout can't keep pace, the binding constraint on U.S. AI leadership over the next five years won't be chip export controls or model architecture — it will be whether the grid can physically deliver the power.

Getting cost allocation right now is a precondition for solving that bottleneck, not a distraction from it. Utilities will not move fast on the generation and transmission buildout AI demands if they fear stranded-asset risk from data centers that sign up for capacity and later scale back — which is exactly what well-designed large-load tariffs, with minimum-bill and take-or-pay provisions, are built to prevent. And state legislatures will not hold the line against moratorium bills if residential ratepayers keep discovering AI-driven charges on their electric bills that no one asked them to accept. H.R. 9340 addresses both problems with the same mechanism.

## The recommendation

Congress should move H.R. 9340 to full committee markup and the floor promptly, preserving its core structure: a federal floor requiring large-load cost recovery, with implementation left to state PUCs. State regulators that haven't yet adopted a large-load tariff — and haven't waited for Congress to force the issue — should look to Pennsylvania, Florida, and Oklahoma as templates and move now, both because it's sound ratemaking and because it's the fastest way to defuse the political pressure building toward moratoriums in their own states. And every member of the House Energy and Commerce Committee should scrutinize floor amendments for the difference between paying your own way and being told you can't connect at all. Cost-causation pricing is what lets America build the power grid the AI era requires without a populist backlash burning down the buildout in the process. Congress should pass it, and then get out of the way of the tariffs it authorizes.
