Congress has not passed an AI liability statute. It may not for years. That has not stopped the United States from acquiring a de facto AI liability regime — it just arrived through a federal courthouse in Orlando instead of a committee markup in Washington.

On May 21, 2025, Senior U.S. District Judge Anne Conway denied a motion to dismiss in [*Garcia v. Character Technologies, Inc.*](https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/69300919/garcia-v-character-technologies-inc/), the wrongful-death suit brought by Megan Garcia after her 14-year-old son, Sewell Setzer III, died by suicide following months of immersive conversation with a Character.AI companion bot. The defendants argued that chatbot output was speech, entitled to the same First Amendment protection as a novel or a film script — a defense that, if accepted, would have made most AI harm litigation a non-starter. Judge Conway [rejected it](https://www.courthousenews.com/florida-judge-rules-ai-chatbots-not-protected-by-first-amendment/), writing that the companies "fail to articulate why words strung together by an LLM are speech." She allowed product liability, negligence, wrongful death, and Florida deceptive-trade-practices claims to proceed — and, critically, she allowed a component-manufacturer theory against Google, which had licensed Character.AI's underlying technology and later re-absorbed its founders, to survive as well.

That is not a small ruling. It is a doctrinal hinge. Once a court characterizes model output as a product rather than protected expression, the entire body of American products liability law — strict liability, design-defect analysis, failure-to-warn duties, component-manufacturer exposure — becomes available to plaintiffs suing AI developers. No statute did this. A single Article III judge, interpreting existing tort doctrine against a set of facts nobody anticipated when that doctrine was written, did it.

The fallout arrived fast. On January 7, 2026, Google and Character.AI [disclosed settlements in principle](https://www.cnn.com/2026/01/07/business/character-ai-google-settle-teen-suicide-lawsuit) covering the Garcia case and four related suits filed in Colorado, New York, and Texas — the first major monetary resolutions of AI companion-bot harm litigation, with no admission of liability and confidential terms. Two months later, on March 4, 2026, [Nippon Life Insurance Company of America sued OpenAI](https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/72365583/nippon-life-insurance-company-of-america-v-openai-foundation/) in the Northern District of Illinois over an entirely different fact pattern: a claimant who used ChatGPT to second-guess a settled insurance dispute, generating what Nippon alleges was $300,000 in wasted legal fees and abusive motion practice. Practitioner shops that track this space — [K&L Gates](https://www.klgates.com/AI-Product-Liability-The-Next-Wave-of-Litigation-3-27-2026), [McGuireWoods](https://www.mcguirewoods.com/client-resources/alerts/2026/3/can-social-media-or-ai-be-a-defective-product/) — are now openly describing 2026 as the "next wave" of AI product-liability litigation. They are not describing a hypothetical. They are describing a caseload.

This white paper argues that the caseload is the problem, not just the cases. Absent a coherent federal products-liability framework calibrated to how AI systems actually work, American courts are backing into a strict-liability regime one jury verdict and one settlement at a time — applying doctrine built for lawnmowers and pharmaceuticals to systems that generate a different, probabilistic answer every time they're asked the same question. That mismatch does not protect consumers better than a clear rule would. It produces exactly the kind of jurisdiction-dependent, unpredictable liability exposure that critics of prescriptive, fragmented regulation have long warned against — except this time the uncertainty is arriving through common-law tort doctrine rather than a state statute or an agency rule, which means the normal remedies (comment periods, legislative override, agency guidance) don't apply. You cannot lobby a jury.

This paper is deliberately narrow in scope. It is not about the separate, heavily litigated fight over whether states may enact their own AI safety or transparency statutes and whether Congress should preempt them. This paper is about something categorically different: common-law tort doctrine — negligence, strict liability, design defect, failure to warn — evolving case by case through judicial interpretation, with no state legislature and no Supreme Court ruling in sight. It builds on and extends [an earlier analysis of the AI LEAD Act's strict-liability prong](/articles/2026-05-12-don-t-treat-ai-like-a-toaster-the-case-against-strict-produc), which argued that Congress should reject strict liability in favor of negligence-based accountability. The conclusion here is the same. The problem is that Congress hasn't acted, and courts — quite reasonably, given the tools available to them — are filling the vacuum with the very doctrine that piece warned against.

## I. From Speech to Product: How One Ruling Became the Operative Regime

To understand why the *Garcia* ruling matters this much, it helps to understand what it replaced.

For most of the last decade, the default legal shield for platforms hosting third-party content was [Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/47/230) — and by extension, First Amendment doctrine treating generated expression as speech. Under that framework, a plaintiff harmed by something a chatbot said faced a steep uphill argument: the output was either third-party content the platform wasn't liable for, or the platform's own expression, protected the way an author's words are protected. Either way, the harm had to be litigated as a speech tort — defamation, intentional infliction of emotional distress — with all the doctrinal hurdles those causes of action carry, including, in many jurisdictions, a showing of actual malice or reckless disregard for truth.

*Garcia* broke that frame. Judge Conway's ruling did not have to resolve whether Section 230 applied — Character.AI itself generates the output rather than merely hosting third-party content, which put the case outside Section 230's core protection from the start. What made the ruling doctrinally significant was the *product* characterization layered on top: by declining to treat chatbot output as protected speech and by allowing a products-liability theory (including a component-manufacturer theory against Google) to proceed, the court signaled that a generative AI system's design, guardrails, and deployment choices can be evaluated the way a court evaluates a defective airbag or an unreasonably dangerous drug, rather than the way it evaluates a published sentence.

This is the recharacterization that matters, and it is worth being precise about why it happened through courts rather than legislatures. No statute defines a large language model as a "product" for tort purposes. The [Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability](https://www.ali.org/publications/restatement-law-third/torts-third) — the closest thing American law has to an authoritative products-liability rulebook — was drafted in 1998, years before transformer-based generative models existed, and it addresses software liability only glancingly, treating mass-marketed software as a "good" subject to products liability principles while treating custom software as a "service" outside that framework. Courts applying *Garcia*'s logic are extending a decades-old doctrinal category to a technology its drafters could not have imagined, using the tools of analogical reasoning rather than legislative drafting. That is exactly what common law is supposed to do when statutes are silent. It is also exactly the mechanism by which uncertainty compounds: each subsequent court facing an AI harm case must independently decide how far the *Garcia* analogy extends, with no statute to anchor the analysis and no appellate consensus yet to constrain it.

The California state-court case *[Raine v. OpenAI](https://www.courthousenews.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/raine-vs-openai-et-al-complaint.pdf)*, filed in August 2025 in San Francisco County Superior Court on behalf of 16-year-old Adam Raine, tested the same theory against a different defendant within months of the *Garcia* ruling — alleging that OpenAI shipped GPT-4o with safety protocols that had been weakened relative to earlier versions, and that the system continued providing self-harm-adjacent responses despite its own moderation system flagging hundreds of the user's messages for acute distress. Seven additional suits against OpenAI followed the same template. None of this required a new statute. It required only that one federal judge in Florida establish that the product frame was available, and that plaintiffs' counsel elsewhere notice.

## II. Two Cases, No Limiting Principle

The doctrinal shift would be more defensible if it were being applied to a single, coherent category of harm. It is not. Contrast the two cases at the center of this wave.

**The Character.AI/Google settlement** resolved claims of psychological harm to a minor from sustained, emotionally immersive interaction with a consumer-facing companion chatbot — a product marketed for exactly the kind of open-ended, relationship-simulating engagement that produced the harm. The plaintiffs' theory, credited enough by Judge Conway to survive dismissal, was that the system's design — its persona-based engagement mechanics, its absence of crisis-intervention guardrails, its optimization for continued conversation — created a foreseeable risk to vulnerable teenage users that ordinary negligence and design-defect doctrine could evaluate. This is squarely within the harm profile that [prior analysis has argued](/articles/2026-05-12-don-t-treat-ai-like-a-toaster-the-case-against-strict-produc) is best handled through negligent-design and failure-to-warn liability: a developer who deploys an emotionally engaging system to minors and fails to implement available, known safeguards has a coherent, knowledge-based case to answer.

**Nippon Life v. OpenAI** is a different animal entirely. The underlying facts, as [Nippon's complaint](https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/72365583/nippon-life-insurance-company-of-america-v-openai-foundation/) lays them out, involve a former disability-benefits claimant who had already settled her dispute with the insurer, then fed her attorney's correspondence into ChatGPT and asked it to evaluate the advice she'd received. Nippon alleges the system responded by questioning the settlement's legitimacy and her attorney's competence, then helped her draft motions and subpoenas attempting to reopen a closed federal case — costing the insurer roughly $300,000 in fees fighting what it characterizes as meritless, AI-generated litigation. Nippon's actual causes of action are tortious interference with contract, abuse of process, and unauthorized practice of law under Illinois statute — not, on the face of the complaint, a products-liability count. But a [Stanford CodeX analysis](https://law.stanford.edu/2026/03/07/designed-to-cross-why-nippon-life-v-openai-is-a-product-liability-case/) argues the case is better understood, and more durably litigated, as a design-defect products claim: OpenAI's own marketing of ChatGPT's bar-exam performance establishes both the foreseeability that users would rely on it for legal analysis and the technical feasibility of architectural safeguards against unauthorized-practice-of-law outputs — safeguards the complaint alleges have existed in principle since well before ChatGPT's release.

Put the two cases side by side and the limiting principle disappears. In one, the alleged defect is a design optimized for sustained emotional engagement with a vulnerable minor who had no counterparty capable of catching the harm before it occurred. In the other, the alleged defect is a general-purpose assistant giving overconfident legal analysis to a sophisticated commercial party who had — and disregarded — actual counsel. These are not the same kind of harm, the same kind of user, or the same kind of foreseeability. A doctrine capable of stretching to cover both, without any statutory or appellate guidance on where its boundary lies, is not a liability framework. It is an open question that will be answered fifty different times, by fifty different juries and trial judges, before any coherent boundary emerges — and every one of those answers will bind different companies in different ways in the meantime.

That is precisely the failure mode identified in [an analysis of algorithmic-discrimination liability bills](/articles/2026-06-19-who-gets-sued-when-the-algorithm-gets-it-wrong-congress-is-a): a liability regime with no limiting principle does not deter the specific bad conduct that motivated it. It creates undifferentiated exposure for the ordinary business of deploying probabilistic software, and lets plaintiffs' counsel — not legislators, not agencies, not even judges applying a settled test — decide by complaint-drafting choice how far the product frame will reach in any given case.

## III. Why Fifty Common-Law Experiments Are Worse Than One Federal Standard

It is tempting to treat this as a virtue of the common law: courts working out doctrine incrementally, case by case, is how American tort law has always evolved, and eventually a body of precedent will settle the hard questions. That confidence is misplaced for AI specifically, for three reasons that don't apply equally to, say, the century-long evolution of automobile products liability.

**First, the underlying technology changes faster than precedent can accumulate.** A products-liability doctrine trained on years of litigation over a stable mechanical part — a brake line, a ladder rung — can afford to develop slowly, because the product being litigated in year ten resembles the product litigated in year one. A foundation model deployed in 2026 does not resemble the model the same company deployed in 2024; it has different training data, different fine-tuning, different guardrails, often a materially different architecture. By the time a jurisdiction develops a stable body of case law addressing how negligence or design-defect doctrine applies to a 2024-generation chatbot, developers have shipped several generations of systems the precedent never contemplated. Common-law development assumes the target holds still long enough to be hit. AI development is specifically designed not to.

**Second, the jurisdictional variance is not a temporary transition cost — it is permanent, by design.** Federal courts sitting in diversity apply state substantive tort law. A company deploying one model nationally can, and increasingly will, face materially different standards of liability in Florida (following *Garcia*'s product-and-component-manufacturer framing), Illinois (litigating the boundary of unauthorized-practice-of-law liability in *Nippon*), California, and every other state with an AI harm suit pending, with no federal floor or ceiling reconciling them. This is not a problem that resolves itself as case law matures — it is the permanent structure of a fifty-state common-law system applied to a product that, unlike a car or a drug, is typically deployed identically and simultaneously in all fifty states from a single codebase. A single national deployment decision now carries fifty distinct, independently evolving liability profiles.

**Third, the resulting uncertainty is already visibly repricing into the market, not just into legal risk models.** Major carriers — [Chubb, Travelers, and CNA among them](https://riskandinsurance.com/ai-risk-is-here-and-insurers-are-learning-to-write-the-rules/) — have moved toward broad AI exclusions in standard technology errors-and-omissions policies, forcing companies whose core product involves generative AI to seek narrower, more expensive "affirmative AI" endorsements or standalone AI-liability coverage, a market industry observers describe as still forming its own underwriting conventions from scratch. Underwriters cite the same problem courts are grappling with: AI outputs are probabilistic rather than deterministic, a single model behavior can affect millions of users simultaneously rather than one defective unit, and causation in a "black box" system is difficult to establish with the confidence insurers need to price a policy. When the insurance market — an institution whose entire function is pricing legal risk — cannot yet build stable actuarial tables for AI liability because the underlying doctrine is still being litigated case by case, that is strong evidence the uncertainty is a cost being borne by the real economy, not an abstraction.

The European Union, instructively, chose the opposite path — and its choice is a useful contrast precisely because it is not the AI Act. The [revised EU Product Liability Directive](https://www.pinsentmasons.com/out-law/analysis/revised-eu-product-liability-regime-expands-ai-software-providers) (Directive (EU) 2024/2853), which member states must transpose by December 9, 2026, expressly classifies software and AI systems as "products" subject to a harmonized strict-liability regime across all twenty-seven member states, with common evidentiary presumptions to assist injured plaintiffs. Whatever one thinks of extending strict liability to AI at all — and this paper argues below that Congress should not — the EU's approach at least produces *one* answer instead of fifty. A company operating in the EU knows, as of December 2026, what standard applies regardless of which member state a plaintiff sues in. A company operating in the United States, absent federal action, will not have that clarity for AI liability at any point in the foreseeable future. The single most consequential regulatory-design lesson from the EU's approach — nothing to do with the substance of its strict-liability choice — is that jurisdictional uniformity has independent value that the untethered American common-law process cannot deliver on its own.

None of this is an argument that AI developers should escape accountability. It is an argument that the *mechanism* of accountability matters as much as its existence. A single, calibrated federal standard — even a demanding one — is more compatible with continued AI deployment and iteration than an unbounded, jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction common-law experiment whose eventual shape no company can predict today.

## IV. A Calibrated Federal Standard: Component-Manufacturer and Learned-Intermediary Doctrines, Adapted

A [May 2026 analysis of the AI LEAD Act](/articles/2026-05-12-don-t-treat-ai-like-a-toaster-the-case-against-strict-produc) argued that Congress should strike that bill's strict-liability prong and retain its negligent-design and failure-to-warn prongs, because negligence doctrine's foreseeability analysis — unlike strict liability's demand for a discoverable, reproducible "defect" — can actually accommodate a probabilistic system's behavior. This paper does not revisit that conclusion; it extends it, because *Garcia* and *Nippon* expose a gap the earlier analysis did not need to address: even a negligence-based federal standard needs a mechanism for calibrating *how much* duty a given AI deployer owes, given how radically different a consumer companion chatbot and an enterprise-licensed legal-research tool actually are in terms of who controls the output before it reaches an end user.

Two doctrines already used elsewhere in American product and professional liability law supply exactly that calibration mechanism, and Congress should draw on both rather than inventing a novel AI-specific test from scratch.

**Component-manufacturer doctrine** answers the question of how liability should flow through a supply chain when multiple parties contribute to a final product. As Professor Catherine Sharkey has argued in a detailed treatment of [products liability for AI](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/products-liability-for-artificial-intelligence), the Supreme Court's 2019 decision in [*Air & Liquid Systems Corp. v. DeVries*](https://www.law.cornell.edu/supremecourt/text/17-1104) — a 6-3 ruling, with Justice Gorsuch dissenting on the ground that maritime law should defer to a bright-line rule tying liability to the product actually manufactured — supplies a workable middle ground: a component supplier retains a duty to warn when it knows or has reason to know its component will be integrated into a product likely to be dangerous in its intended use, and knows users are unlikely to independently realize that danger — but not simply because a third party later added a harmful element the supplier neither directed nor could reasonably foresee. Applied to AI, this maps naturally onto the foundation-model-to-application supply chain: a foundation-model developer that licenses its model for integration into a specific high-risk application, with knowledge of that use, retains meaningful exposure (exactly the theory that kept Google in the *Garcia* case as a component-manufacturer defendant). A foundation-model developer with no knowledge of, or practical ability to control, how a downstream licensee configured and deployed its product should not bear the same exposure as the party that actually built the deployed system.

**Learned-intermediary doctrine**, developed in pharmaceutical liability law, holds that a drug manufacturer's duty to warn runs to the prescribing physician — the trained intermediary equipped to evaluate risk in a specific clinical context — rather than directly to the end patient, precisely because the intermediary is better positioned than the manufacturer to translate general risk information into a judgment about a specific person's circumstances. The doctrine's logic transfers cleanly to a meaningful slice of enterprise AI deployment: an enterprise customer that licenses a foundation model, configures its own guardrails, trains its own staff on appropriate use, and deploys it to its own employees or clients under its own supervision is functionally the "learned intermediary" between the model developer and the ultimate user — and should bear correspondingly more of the liability for a bad outcome, not less, because it is the party with actual visibility into and control over the deployment context. A hospital system that deploys a diagnostic-support tool with clinician oversight occupies a different liability position than a consumer chatbot company whose only "intermediary" between model and user is the model itself.

**A hybrid federal standard should therefore calibrate duty to control, not merely to distance from harm.** Concretely:

1. **Direct-to-consumer deployers bear the primary duty of care.** A company that deploys a model directly to consumers with no human intermediary — Character.AI's companion-bot product, OpenAI's consumer ChatGPT — owes the negligence-based duty this paper and prior analysis both endorse: reasonable design safeguards and adequate warnings calibrated to foreseeable use, including foreseeable misuse by vulnerable populations such as minors.

2. **Enterprise integrators that configure, fine-tune, or supervise deployment absorb liability commensurate with the control they exercised.** A licensed enterprise integrator that strips out safety guardrails, fine-tunes a model for a specific high-stakes use case, or deploys it without adequate human oversight cannot point upstream to the foundation-model developer for a harm its own configuration choices caused or enabled — mirroring how a physician's independent judgment, not the drug manufacturer's original warning label, controls liability in the pharmaceutical context.

3. **Foundation-model developers retain component-manufacturer-style exposure only where they had actual or constructive knowledge of a specific dangerous downstream use and failed to act on it** — not for every conceivable downstream application built on a general-purpose model, and not merely because their system was technically capable of producing a harmful output if deliberately misused. This is the *DeVries* middle ground: liability follows knowledge and foreseeability, not mere technical capability.

4. **Marketing claims about system capability create foreseeability, and therefore duty, in proportion to their specificity.** *Nippon*'s complaint leans heavily on OpenAI's own bar-exam-performance marketing to establish that legal-analysis-style reliance was foreseeable. That is doctrinally sound and should be preserved: a developer that markets professional-grade capability should not be surprised when courts treat professional-grade reliance as foreseeable. But this cuts against strict liability, not for it — the remedy is a failure-to-warn or negligent-marketing theory keyed to what was actually claimed, not an assumption of defect merely because an output was wrong.

This framework does not soften accountability relative to the current judicial improvisation — if anything, it sharpens it, by giving plaintiffs a predictable theory to plead and defendants a predictable standard to design against, rather than a jurisdiction-by-jurisdiction guessing game about whether a court will accept the product frame at all this time.

## V. Recommendations

**For Congress and the Senate Judiciary Committee:** A federal AI liability statute should occupy the field currently being filled ad hoc by state common law, not by banning state tort claims outright but by supplying a uniform substantive standard — negligent design, failure to warn, and the component-manufacturer/learned-intermediary calibration above — that state courts apply instead of independently reinventing strict-liability products doctrine fifty different ways. This is a narrower, more surgical intervention than a comprehensive AI statute: it does not require Congress to resolve what "AI" means for regulatory purposes or referee the broader preemption fight over state AI safety statutes. It requires Congress to do what it has done before in specialized tort contexts — vaccine injury compensation, aviation liability, maritime component-parts doctrine — and supply one coherent duty-of-care standard where common law is visibly struggling to produce one on its own.

**For AI developer general counsel:** Do not wait for *Garcia*'s theory to be tested on appeal before building the record a negligence-and-calibration standard rewards. Document red-teaming, log known-risk disclosures to enterprise licensees in writing, and — critically, given *Nippon* — calibrate marketing claims about professional-grade capability to what the system's actual guardrails can support. The foreseeability doctrine cuts both ways: overclaiming capability now generates duty later.

**For insurers pricing AI E&O and product-liability risk:** The component-manufacturer/learned-intermediary framework proposed here, if adopted, would substantially narrow the actuarial uncertainty currently driving blanket AI exclusions — because it ties exposure to a measurable variable (deployment control) rather than an unbounded one (whether a plaintiff's theory persuades a given jury that any output was a "defect"). Underwriters should track the *DeVries*-style knowledge threshold as it develops in the foundation-model licensing context; it will likely become the operative line between insurable and uninsurable AI deployment risk.

**For tort scholars:** The doctrinal question *Garcia* and *Nippon* leave open — how far a "product" characterization extends across radically different harm types with a single AI system — deserves the same sustained academic attention that component-parts and learned-intermediary doctrine received in their formative decades. The stakes of getting the limiting principle wrong are not abstract. They are already being priced into settlements, insurance premiums, and deployment decisions today.

---

Congress's inaction on AI liability has not produced a policy vacuum. It has produced a policy default — one being written, case by case, by federal district judges applying decades-old doctrine to systems that doctrine's authors never imagined, with no appellate consensus yet to bound it and no legislative override available to correct course quickly if courts get the balance wrong. That is not regulatory humility. It is regulatory abdication dressed up as judicial restraint. A calibrated federal negligence standard — built on doctrines American law already uses to solve exactly this kind of supply-chain and intermediary-control problem — would give AI developers, enterprise deployers, insurers, and injured plaintiffs alike something the current common-law improvisation cannot: a rule they can know in advance.
