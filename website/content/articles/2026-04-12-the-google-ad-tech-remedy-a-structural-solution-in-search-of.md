# The Google Ad Tech Remedy: A Structural Solution in Search of a Structural Problem

Judge Leonie Brinkema's remedies ruling in *United States v. Google* (ad tech) is expected any day. When it arrives, it will be among the most consequential antitrust decisions in years — not just for Google, but for how courts think about remedies in complex digital markets. The Department of Justice has asked Judge Brinkema to force Google to divest both its AdX ad exchange and its DoubleClick for Publishers (DFP) ad server. That demand is wrong, and if adopted, risks producing market disruption without competitive gain.

The liability finding was legitimate. The remedy demand is not.

## What Brinkema Actually Found

[Judge Brinkema handed Google a partial defeat](https://www.courthousenews.com/judge-hands-google-partial-defeat-in-ad-tech-monopoly-case/), not a sweeping indictment of its entire advertising stack. Her ruling addressed a specific theory — that Google illegally tied its publisher ad server to its ad exchange, using control of DFP to funnel publishers into AdX and deny rivals the scale they needed to compete effectively. That is a narrow finding about conduct at a specific layer of the ad tech supply chain.

The DOJ's remedy demand inflates that narrow finding into a structural indictment of Google's entire publisher-side business. Forced divestiture of both AdX and DFP is a blunt instrument calibrated not to the harm proved at trial, but to a broader theory of Big Tech power that never fully materialized in Brinkema's liability opinion. Proportionality is a foundational principle of antitrust remedies: courts must tailor relief to the violation, not to the violation they wished they had found. Divestiture of both products does not pass that test.

## The Buyer Problem Is Real

[During closing arguments, Judge Brinkema herself raised the buyer problem](https://www.thecurrent.com/data-privacy-judge-structural-remedies-google-ad-tech-monopoly-case) — who, exactly, is capable of acquiring and operating AdX at global scale? The answer is uncomfortable for divestiture advocates: any entity large enough to maintain AdX's liquidity and technical infrastructure would likely be a major technology or media conglomerate, and that acquirer would face its own antitrust scrutiny the moment the sale closed. A fire-sale transfer of AdX to, say, a major cloud competitor or a media company with its own ad interests does not produce competition. It produces a new concentration problem under different ownership, with the added complication of the asset having been stripped from the integrated ecosystem that made it valuable in the first place.

Structural remedies work when there is a viable, competitive acquirer on the other side of the transaction. In the AT&T divestiture of 1984, the Bell Operating Companies were geographically separable businesses with clear customer bases and independent operational identities. In the Microsoft consent decree context, the structural proposals failed precisely because no acquirer could have run a stripped-down Windows business in a way that preserved consumer value. AdX is closer to the latter than the former.

## Behavioral Remedies Have a Track Record Here

Critics of behavioral relief often invoke the Microsoft consent decree as evidence that behavioral remedies fail. But the lesson from Microsoft is not that behavioral relief is inherently flawed — it is that *poorly designed* behavioral relief fails. The 2001 consent decree lacked third-party technical monitoring, contained ambiguous interoperability definitions, and gave Microsoft too much interpretive latitude over compliance. Those are design failures, not instrument failures.

The better precedent is the Google Search remedies ruling. [A federal court endorsed behavioral remedies and rejected structural relief in the Search antitrust litigation](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11362), adopting a framework built around choice screens and a ban on exclusive default agreements — targeted behavioral rules aimed at the specific exclusionary mechanism the court identified. [The DOJ's choice screen mandate triggered a notable market response](https://markets.financialcontent.com/stocks/article/marketminute-2026-4-8-alphabet-dodges-structural-breakup-doj-choice-screen-mandate-triggers-relief-rally-for-google-parent), suggesting investors recognized meaningful competition-restoring potential in behavioral relief without the collateral damage of forced divestiture.

The same logic applies in ad tech. The actual exclusionary mechanism Brinkema identified was informational: Google used its control over DFP's publisher data to give AdX preferential access to bid opportunities and pricing intelligence that rival exchanges could not match. That mechanism is addressable directly — through mandatory real-time bidding data sharing requirements, open API access obligations, and non-discrimination rules requiring Google to offer rival exchanges the same bid optimization data it reserves for AdX. These tools attack the specific conduct found harmful without dismembering a functioning product ecosystem.

## Integration Isn't the Problem; Foreclosure Is

DFP and AdX are integrated products, and that integration creates genuine efficiencies — lower latency, better yield optimization for publishers, more accurate floor pricing. The court's finding was not that integration is inherently harmful; it was that Google *used* integration as a foreclosure mechanism. That distinction matters enormously for remedy design.

Forced divestiture does not eliminate integration — it transfers it under new ownership, severing it from complementary infrastructure in ways that could degrade the publisher liquidity that makes the exchange valuable to all participants. Publishers benefit from a liquid, well-functioning exchange. A structurally separated AdX, cut off from Google's demand-side relationships and data infrastructure during a transition that could take years, may be a less functional market — not a more competitive one.

## Institutional Humility Is Not Optional Here

Multi-sided advertising markets involve feedback loops, liquidity dynamics, and network effects that make them genuinely difficult to restructure through judicial decree. Courts are not market designers. Even the parties most intimately familiar with the technology have struggled throughout this litigation to predict how separation would affect publisher yield, advertiser reach, and market-clearing efficiency. A court ordering structural separation based on trial testimony about a market that is itself rapidly evolving is making a bet on outcomes it cannot reliably forecast.

The principle antitrust law demands here is the least restrictive means adequate to restore competition — a principle as old as the Sherman Act itself. Behavioral remedies with teeth: mandatory interoperability, real-time bidding data access, third-party compliance monitors, meaningful contempt authority. That is the proportionate response to a proportionate finding.

## The Stakes Beyond This Case

Judge Brinkema's choice will send a signal about where antitrust remedies in digital markets are heading. A well-designed behavioral order demonstrates that antitrust enforcement can be surgical and effective, restoring competitive dynamics without dismembering functioning markets. A forced divestiture that produces market chaos — or simply transfers concentration from one owner to another — would hand ammunition to every critic of tech antitrust enforcement for the next decade.

The liability finding in *United States v. Google* (ad tech) was sound. The court should not undermine that finding's credibility by pairing it with a remedy that overshoots the evidence, ignores the buyer problem, and substitutes structural ambition for competitive analysis.

Calibrate the remedy to the harm proved. That is all antitrust law asks — and in this case, it is enough.
