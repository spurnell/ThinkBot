On March 20, 2026, the White House released its [National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence](https://www.wilmerhale.com/en/insights/blogs/wilmerhale-privacy-and-cybersecurity-law/20260323-white-house-releases-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence), calling on Congress to broadly preempt state AI laws that impose undue burdens on development. Within days, House Democrats circulated the [GUARDRAILS Act](https://democrats-judiciary.house.gov/media-center/press-releases/at-hearing-on-federal-preemption-of-state-ai-laws-subcommittee-democrats-underscore-need-for-guardrails-to-protect-americans-while-promoting-innovation) to block it, and state legislators across the country accelerated their own competing bills to fill the perceived federal void. The battle over who regulates AI — Washington or Sacramento, Austin, Albany — is now the defining governance fight of 2026.

The stakes are not abstract. As Senate markup of federal AI legislation approaches, Congress must choose between two futures: a unified national framework that allows American AI companies to build, scale, and compete, or a compliance labyrinth of 50 overlapping, contradictory regimes that will function as a structural tax on innovation — one paid disproportionately by startups and not at all by China.

Federal preemption of patchwork state AI regulation is not an assault on federalism. It is a strategic necessity for American technological leadership. The counterargument — that states are "laboratories of democracy" best positioned to regulate AI — confuses consumer protection with technical compliance architecture. States are excellent laboratories for consumer protection and criminal law enforcement. They are not equipped to be simultaneous architects of 50 incompatible definitions of "high-risk AI" that American developers must satisfy all at once.

## The Compliance Labyrinth Is Real — and Asymmetric

Proponents of state AI regulation often assume that more rules produce more safety. The actual effect is different: more rules produce more lawyers, and only large incumbents can afford them.

The data on compliance costs is stark. The [U.S. Chamber of Commerce](https://www.uschamber.com/technology/the-hidden-cost-of-50-state-ai-laws-a-data-driven-breakdown) projects that if Colorado's SB 205 AI governance model were applied across all 50 states, the result would be up to 92,000 jobs lost from reduced AI investment, up to $53.7 billion in GDP losses by 2030, and a regulatory burden that falls heaviest on the small businesses least equipped to bear it. Sixty-five percent of small businesses surveyed reported worrying about rising litigation and compliance costs from conflicting AI laws; one-third said they would scale back AI adoption in response.

These are not hypothetical projections. [PerceptIn](https://fortune.com/2026/01/30/americas-ai-regulatory-patchwork-is-crushing-startups-and-helping-china/), an autonomous driving startup, budgeted $10,000 for regulatory compliance. The actual bill exceeded $344,000 per deployment project — more than double the company's research and development costs. PerceptIn went out of business. A [Harvard Kennedy School Student Policy Review analysis](https://studentreview.hks.harvard.edu/why-compliance-costs-of-ai-commercialization-maybe-holding-start-ups-back/) found that a 200 percent increase in fixed compliance costs transforms a startup's operating margin from positive 13 percent to negative 7 percent — the mathematical difference between survival and bankruptcy.

The compliance burden is also structurally asymmetric. Google, Microsoft, and Amazon maintain compliance departments larger than entire AI startups. They can absorb 50-state regulatory variance as routine overhead. A 40-person AI company cannot. When critics characterize federal preemption as "a Big Tech giveaway," they have the logic exactly backward: patchwork state regulation is the Big Tech protection racket. Regulatory fragmentation builds moats around incumbents while blocking the challengers who might otherwise disrupt them.

## Fifty States, Fifty Definitions — and Zero Coherence

The deepest problem with state-by-state AI regulation is not cost — it is structural incoherence. States are not simply regulating AI differently; they are creating obligations that are logically irreconcilable.

Consider "high-risk AI," the category that triggers the most significant compliance obligations across state frameworks. [Colorado's SB 205](https://www.clarkhill.com/news-events/news/colorados-ai-law-delayed-until-june-2026-what-the-latest-setback-means-for-businesses/), now effective June 30, 2026, defines high-risk AI by reference to specific use cases and consequence thresholds — employment, education, housing, credit — and requires developers to provide deployers with statements on reasonably foreseeable uses and known harms, with deployers conducting impact assessments and annual reviews. California's approach, built across multiple bills including [AB 2013's](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billTextClient.xhtml?bill_id=202320240AB2013) training data transparency requirements, ties disclosure obligations to training data characteristics rather than use-case categories. [Illinois](https://themeridiem.com/policy/2026/4/10/ai-liability-shifts-to-legislative-reality-as-openai-backs-illinois-shield), through HB 3773's amendment to the Human Rights Act, doesn't use the "high-risk" framing at all — it targets discriminatory employer use of AI with a $500 million threshold for "critical harm" and distinct duty-of-care rules for deployers versus developers. [Texas's TRAIGA](https://www.millernash.com/industry-news/from-colorado-to-texas-how-states-are-rewriting-ai-laws), effective January 1, 2026, layered in requirements around biometric identifiers, healthcare provider disclosure, and anti-discrimination obligations with its own definitional structure.

A developer building an AI-assisted hiring tool that operates in all four states faces a compliance challenge with no coherent solution. Colorado's definition of what triggers obligations may not map to California's. California's disclosure mandates may be structurally inconsistent with Texas's liability framework. Complying fully with Illinois's duty-of-care regime for deployers may not satisfy Colorado's requirements for developers. These are not gaps that better lawyers can bridge — they reflect fundamentally different theories of what AI governance is for.

The problem compounds as the [volume of state legislation](https://www.glacis.io/guide-state-ai-laws) accelerates. Over 1,100 AI-related bills were introduced in state legislatures in 2025 alone, with roughly 100 becoming law. States define "AI," "high-risk," and "consequential decisions" differently, requiring developers to analyze the same system under multiple incompatible frameworks. The result is not a mosaic of thoughtful state-level experimentation — it is a compliance labyrinth that no innovator can navigate across all 50 jurisdictions simultaneously.

## Preemption Is Not Deregulation — Read the Framework

Critics of the White House's National Policy Framework have characterized it as a blanket nullification of state authority over AI. This is a misreading of the document, and the misreading should not go unchallenged.

The Framework does not preempt state authority broadly. It targets a specific category of state regulation: laws that "impose undue burdens" on AI development, specifically regulations of AI model development itself and state-level liability regimes that hold developers responsible for unlawful conduct by third parties using their systems. These are precisely the categories where state divergence creates the most irreconcilable compliance obligations.

What the Framework explicitly [preserves](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/03/the-white-house-legislative-recommendations-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence-an) is significant: states retain traditional police powers, consumer protection enforcement authority, authority to protect children (including against AI-generated child sexual abuse material), and authority over their own AI procurement and use. A state can still prosecute AI-enabled fraud under its consumer protection statutes. A state can still enforce its own laws governing how state agencies deploy AI. What a state cannot do — under the Framework's preemption approach — is create a bespoke technical compliance architecture that developers must satisfy simultaneously with 49 other bespoke architectures.

This is not federalism nullification. It is the same logic that underlies federal preemption in dozens of other domains — financial products, pharmaceutical labeling, airline safety, telecommunications — where the inherently interstate nature of the regulated activity makes state-by-state divergence unworkable. A foundation model trained in California, deployed on servers in Virginia, used by businesses in Illinois, and affecting consumers in Texas is not a California product amenable to California-only regulation.

## The GUARDRAILS Act Defaults to the Most Aggressive Regulator

House Democrats have introduced [H.R. 8031, the GUARDRAILS Act](https://www.akingump.com/en/insights/alerts/white-house-releases-long-awaited-artificial-intelligence-framework-setting-the-stage-for-federal-preemption-debate-and-further-legislative-action), sponsored by Representative Don Beyer and joined in the Senate by Brian Schatz (D-HI), to repeal the December 2025 executive order that directed development of the White House's preemption framework. The politics are understandable — Democrats see the preemption push as stripping away safety guardrails. The policy logic is flawed.

The GUARDRAILS Act proposes to block federal preemption while offering no federal alternative. That is not a safety policy. That is a decision to let the most aggressive state regulator set effective national AI governance by default.

This is how regulatory geography works in practice. If California imposes liability on AI developers for third-party misuse and other states do not, developers building national products must comply with California's standard or exit California's market entirely. Since California represents [roughly 14 percent of U.S. GDP](https://www.gov.ca.gov/2026/04/09/californias-economy-leads-again-grows-another-5-in-2025-to-record-4-25-trillion-gdp/) and a disproportionate share of AI talent and customers, "exit California" is not a viable option for most companies. California's rules become de facto national rules — without any of the deliberation, democratic accountability, or technical input that would accompany a genuine federal standard.

The GUARDRAILS Act, in other words, does not prevent a national AI regulatory standard from emerging. It simply ensures that standard is set by Sacramento rather than Washington, without Congressional debate, without the [NIST AI Risk Management Framework's](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) technical grounding, and without the input of the other 49 states that might have preferred a different approach. This is not a race to the top for safety — it is regulatory capture by geography.

## The EU AI Act Is the Cautionary Tale

American policymakers debating the preemption question need only look at Europe to understand the cost of getting this wrong.

The [EU AI Act](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/implementation-timeline/) — the world's most comprehensive horizontal AI regulatory framework — has become a case study in how prescriptive, broad AI governance chills foundation model development. The Act's high-risk AI obligations, which were supposed to be supported by technical standards by late 2025, are now operating without those standards — the responsible committee delayed their delivery to April 2027, nearly a full year after enforcement obligations are due. More than [110 major EU companies](https://ttms.com/eu-ai-act-update-2025-code-of-practice-enforcement-industry-reactions/), including Airbus, ASML, and Mistral, have formally asked the Commission to delay high-risk enforcement by two years to account for this regulatory chaos.

The result is a continent-wide AI deployment pause. Every European business using AI must wait for legal uncertainty to resolve before proceeding — pushing Europe further behind in the AI race at precisely the moment when the technology's commercial trajectory is accelerating. Small AI companies in Europe, unable to staff dedicated compliance teams, are either delaying product launches or abandoning the EU market entirely.

The American regulatory fragmentation problem is the domestic equivalent of the EU AI Act problem, scaled to the state level. Both create structural uncertainty that large incumbents can absorb and smaller competitors cannot. Both prioritize up-front prescriptive mandates over after-the-fact harm enforcement. Both chill the fast, iterative development that defines the frontier of AI capability.

The U.S. competitive advantage in AI is not primarily about compute access or talent density, though both matter. It is about speed and flexibility — the ability to deploy, iterate, and improve faster than state-directed competitors. Regulatory fragmentation is a direct tax on that speed. Federal preemption of the compliance labyrinth is how the U.S. preserves the advantage.

## What Good Federal AI Governance Looks Like

None of this means AI should go unregulated. The argument for federal preemption is precisely that AI should be regulated — coherently, proportionately, and through institutions with the technical capacity to do it well.

The right model is sector-specific authority layered over a clear preemption framework. The FDA and HHS should govern AI in healthcare using existing statutory authority. The SEC and CFPB should govern financial AI. The EEOC should enforce existing anti-discrimination law as it applies to algorithmic hiring tools. The FTC has broad authority to address deceptive and unfair AI practices. These institutions have domain expertise, established procedures, and legal authority. They do not need a new horizontal AI law to act — they need clarity that their sector-specific standards are the applicable national standard, not a floor above which 50 states can pile incompatible mandates.

The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) provides a voluntary governance architecture that developers and deployers can implement across all sectors. Congress should codify that framework as the national baseline and explicitly preempt state technical compliance regimes that conflict with it. States would retain enforcement authority under consumer protection and police power — but they would not be permitted to write their own "high-risk AI" definitions that developers must satisfy simultaneously with 49 others.

This is the direction the White House Framework points, even if imperfectly. Congress should use the Senate markup process to refine the preemption scope, strengthen the federal baseline, and give sector regulators clear authority. What it should not do is accept the GUARDRAILS Act's false choice between preemption and safety — a choice that would leave American AI development navigating a compliance labyrinth of its own making while Chinese developers operate under a single national standard.

## Conclusion

The preemption debate is not a philosophical contest between federalism and centralization. It is a practical question about whether American AI developers can build and compete when their compliance obligations are structurally irreconcilable across 50 jurisdictions. The answer, supported by the data on compliance costs, the evidence from state definitional divergence, and the cautionary example of the EU AI Act, is clear: they cannot.

Federal preemption of state AI technical compliance regimes — paired with preserved state enforcement authority and a coherent sector-specific federal standard — is the pro-innovation, pro-safety, pro-competitiveness answer. The GUARDRAILS Act offers obstruction without an alternative, defaulting national AI governance to whichever state legislature happens to be most aggressive. That is not a policy. That is an abdication.

Congress should act before the compliance labyrinth becomes load-bearing infrastructure for the incumbents who benefit from it most. The window for coherent federal action is now.
