The [Department of War's January 9, 2026 AI-First Strategy](https://media.defense.gov/2026/Jan/12/2003855671/-1/-1/0/ARTIFICIAL-INTELLIGENCE-STRATEGY-FOR-THE-DEPARTMENT-OF-WAR.PDF) contains a sentence that should alarm every acquisition lawyer in the Pentagon: the Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Office (CDAO) is directed to establish a delivery cadence enabling "the latest commercial models to be deployed within 30 days of public release." Thirty days. For a department whose Authorization-to-Operate process averages twelve to eighteen months under the Risk Management Framework.

That gap — between the strategy's ambition and the acquisition system's structural capacity — is not a management failure or a personnel problem. It is a regulatory barrier dressed in camouflage. Process requirements that were never designed for the technology they now govern function as market exclusion mechanisms — the same dynamic that plagues FDA drug review timelines and FCC spectrum auctions. In defense AI procurement, the excluded parties are the commercial AI vendors whose models the Pentagon says it needs, and the strategic cost is measured not in foregone efficiency but in degraded national security.

Congress created this acquisition system through statute, and Congress is the only institution that can fix it durably. Agency-level workarounds — however well-intentioned — cannot substitute for statutory reform.

## What the Strategy Actually Says

Credit where it is due. The January 9 AI-First Strategy memo, followed three days later by [Secretary Hegseth's sweeping reorganization of the Department's tech and innovation hubs](https://defensescoop.com/2026/01/13/hegseth-ai-tech-hubs-reorganization-dod-dow/), represents a genuine departure from the incremental AI governance that characterized previous administrations. The strategy mandates seven Pace-Setting Projects accountable directly to the Deputy Secretary, consolidates technical authority under a single Chief Technology Officer, and directs the CDAO to treat barriers to data-sharing and authority-to-operate as "operational risks" requiring active elimination. [Breaking Defense's January analysis](https://breakingdefense.com/2026/01/pentagon-rolls-out-major-reforms-of-rd-ai/) characterized the initiative as the most aggressive AI restructuring the Pentagon has attempted.

The 30-day commercial deployment target is particularly striking. Prior administrations had no equivalent mandate. The [Fluet Law analysis of the AI-First strategy](https://fluet.law/the-pentagons-ai-first-mandate-5-strategic-insights-reshaping-defense-procurement-in-2026/) correctly identifies this as a primary procurement criterion — meaning future model acquisition will be evaluated in part on whether the vendor can actually deliver at that tempo. The strategy also establishes a "Barrier Removal Board," a waiver-authority mechanism with power to escalate non-statutory obstacles directly to the Deputy Secretary.

The vision is right. The implementation machinery is broken.

## The Authorization-to-Operate Bottleneck

The core of the problem is the Department's [Risk Management Framework](https://www.ai.mil/About/Resources/Pathway-to-AI-Readiness/Risk-Management/) (RMF) and the Authorization-to-Operate (ATO) process it governs. For traditional software — a logistics platform, a payroll system — a twelve-to-eighteen-month authorization timeline is administratively burdensome but manageable. For foundation models that ship major capability updates on monthly or quarterly cycles, it is operationally absurd.

[Rise8's documentation of the ATO process](https://www.rise8.us/resources/what-is-authority-to-operate-for-systems) captures why AI creates unique friction: while AI models technically require an "Assess Only Construct" rather than a full ATO, the underlying system infrastructure layer does require authorization — and that infrastructure must be re-assessed when the model running on it materially changes. A commercial AI provider releasing a major new model in month one faces the prospect of running month-one capability on Pentagon infrastructure until the ATO clock resets. The 30-day mandate becomes a shelf-display policy rather than an operational standard.

[Representative Arrington launched an effort in 2025 to eliminate the RMF for DOD software](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/defense-main/2025/05/arrington-kicks-off-effort-to-eliminate-rmf-for-dod-software/) entirely — a maximalist position that reflects genuine frustration. The right reform is not abolition but redesign: a risk-tiered authorization framework that applies proportionate scrutiny to proportionate risk, allowing well-validated commercial AI infrastructure to operate under continuous monitoring rather than periodic re-authorization. The FY2026 NDAA mandated standardized ATO documentation and accountability chains, including [mandatory timelines for authorizing officials on cloud-hosted platforms](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF13197), but stopped short of restructuring the underlying framework for AI-specific authorization cycles. That gap belongs in the FY2027 NDAA.

The challenge is not that security review should be eliminated — it is that the current timeline produces no better security outcomes than a well-designed risk-tiered alternative. Asserting that slower authorization is inherently more secure is a claim that demands evidence, not an axiom that terminates the debate.

## FAR/DFARS: Cold War Rules for a Foundation-Model World

The ATO problem is the most visible acquisition barrier. The Cost Accounting Standards (CAS) regime under the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) Part 230 and the Defense Federal Acquisition Regulation Supplement (DFARS) is the most structurally exclusionary.

CAS compliance requires contractors to maintain government-approved accounting systems that segregate cost categories in Pentagon-specific ways, submit certified cost or pricing data, and accept audit rights that most commercial software companies — operating on SaaS pricing, subscription models, and rapid release cycles — cannot practically accommodate. Full CAS coverage historically triggered at the $50 million contract threshold, meaning any commercially successful AI vendor large enough to have a meaningful Pentagon engagement was potentially on the hook for an accounting transformation whose compliance costs could exceed the contract value.

The [FY2026 NDAA raised the full CAS coverage threshold to $100 million](https://www.insidegovernmentcontracts.com/2025/12/fy26-ndaa-aims-to-raise-the-dollar-thresholds-for-the-applicability-of-cas-and-tina/) and increased the Truth in Negotiations Act (TINA) threshold for certified cost data from $2.5 million to $10 million. The same legislation extended nontraditional contractor exemptions, relieving a broader range of startups and commercial firms from government-unique accounting requirements. Congress deserves credit for these adjustments.

But threshold adjustments do not reach the underlying problem: the FAR/DFARS framework was designed for hardware acquisition — weapons platforms with multi-year production schedules and predictable cost structures. Software-as-a-service, and AI-as-a-service in particular, has none of those characteristics. A language model's "cost" is largely compute time, researcher salaries, and training infrastructure — none of which maps cleanly onto FAR Part 31 cost allowability categories. Every commercial AI vendor engaging seriously with Pentagon procurement faces a compliance translation problem that their commercial competitors — including Chinese AI companies competing for PLA contracts — do not.

## The MOSA Paradox

The Modular Open Systems Architecture (MOSA) requirement deserves particular attention because its failure mode is the opposite of its intent.

MOSA was designed to prevent vendor lock-in: by mandating that Pentagon systems use open interfaces and government-accessible technical data packages, acquisition officials hoped to preserve competitive re-sourcing at the component level. On a hardware platform — a ship's combat management system, an aircraft's avionics suite — this logic is sound. On a commercial AI deployment where the "system" is a foundation model accessed via API, it produces a compliance burden that most commercial AI vendors cannot afford to absorb.

[War on the Rocks' February 2026 analysis of defense innovation reforms](https://warontherocks.com/2026/02/will-these-four-defense-innovation-reforms-improve-industrys-lot/) identified precisely this failure mode: unclear benchmarking standards and policy volatility increase uncertainty for the very firms the Pentagon depends on. The [April 2026 MOSA Summit at NAVAIR](https://www.navair.navy.mil/news/Open-standards-takes-center-stage-2026-MOSA-Defense-Summit/Tue-04282026-1256) convened another round of standards-setting conversations. Meanwhile, commercial AI companies continued to evaluate the Pentagon as a customer and conclude that compliance overhead does not justify engagement at the contract values on offer.

The result is that MOSA — a well-intentioned anti-lock-in mechanism — has produced its own form of lock-in: concentration of the Pentagon's AI vendor base among the handful of large primes capable of absorbing compliance costs and managing the regulatory interface. More friction does not produce more competition when the friction is selective by firm size. [Breaking Defense's April 2026 analysis of multi-sourcing and MOSA reform](https://breakingdefense.com/2026/04/multi-sourcing-mosa-and-producibility-form-next-level-defense-acquisition-reform/) notes that the Pentagon is attempting to course-correct with producibility requirements and multi-source contracting structures — but these operate downstream of the underlying compliance barrier. A commercial AI company that cannot reach contract award because of CAS and MOSA overhead does not benefit from a well-designed multi-source competition it cannot enter.

## The Barrier Removal Board: Waivers Are Not Reform

The January strategy established a "Barrier Removal Board" — a dedicated review body under Research and Engineering with authority to waive non-statutory requirements and escalate to the Deputy Secretary anything obstructing AI acceleration. [DefenseScoop's March 2026 policy gap analysis](https://defensescoop.com/2026/03/26/policy-gap-threatening-pentagon-ai-innovation-pipeline/) documented what was already apparent: the Board represents genuine leadership commitment and can clear discrete obstacles, but it is structurally limited to non-statutory friction. It cannot waive the FAR. It cannot rewrite the RMF. It cannot extend Other Transaction Authority to AI-as-a-service contract structures that the 2018 NDAA's drafters never contemplated.

Monthly waivers of non-statutory requirements are a productive use of executive authority within its proper domain. They are not a substitute for statutory reform — any more than FDA Commissioner guidance can substitute for Congressional updates to FDCA approval pathways. The distinction matters because waivers create uncertainty: a commercial AI vendor that enters the Pentagon market relying on a non-statutory waiver faces the risk that a new administration, a new Deputy Secretary, or a new CDAO eliminates the waiver and reinstates the barrier. Durable market entry requires durable legal clarity, and only Congress can provide that.

## The Strategic Asymmetry Congress Should Treat as Urgent

There is a simple way to frame the stakes for House and Senate Armed Services Committee (HASC and SASC) members who are not acquisition policy specialists: China's PLA is integrating commercial AI into military applications with no procurement friction whatsoever.

[CSET's research on China's military AI wish list](https://cset.georgetown.edu/publication/chinas-military-ai-wish-list/) documents a procurement system in which close to three-quarters of AI-related PLA contracts go to non-state vendors — commercial firms with no formal state ownership ties — routing capability through open procurement into military applications at wartime tempo. [CSET's military-civil fusion analysis](https://cset.georgetown.edu/publication/pulling-back-the-curtain-on-chinas-military-civil-fusion/) maps the institutional infrastructure enabling this: a legal framework that obligates commercial firms to cooperate with military requirements, combined with procurement processes designed for rapid vendor onboarding rather than compliance certification.

[The Diplomat's March 2026 reporting on private firms powering China's military AI push](https://thediplomat.com/2026/03/the-private-firms-powering-chinas-military-ai-push/) documents cases such as a PLA unit contracting for a DeepSeek-powered system as early as August 2025 — performing multilingual translation, key information extraction, and flight path analysis — and describes a commercial-to-military integration pipeline operating at a tempo that the Department's January strategy is explicitly trying to match. [FPRI's January 2026 comparison of US AI acceleration and China's diffusion model](https://www.fpri.org/article/2026/01/the-us-ai-acceleration-plan-vs-chinas-diffusion-model/) frames the core asymmetry: the United States is attempting to redirect a compliance-oriented procurement bureaucracy toward speed, while China is scaling a speed-oriented integration system toward reliability. These are not symmetric challenges.

The United States is asking commercial AI companies to absorb compliance costs that China never imposed on its vendors. The strategic tax is paid in capability gaps measured in months and years. Every Authorization-to-Operate cycle that takes eighteen months instead of six is a capability transfer to the adversary.

## The Reform Agenda

The path forward requires action in three lanes simultaneously.

**Lane one: RMF restructuring for AI.** Congress should direct the CDAO and CIO, in the FY2027 NDAA, to develop a risk-tiered authorization framework specific to AI-as-a-service deployments. The framework should distinguish between infrastructure authorization — which retains full RMF rigor — and model-layer authorization for commercially validated systems, which should operate under continuous monitoring with presumptive re-authorization on model update absent demonstrated new risk. The [FY2026 NDAA's mandate for standardized ATO documentation and timelines](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF13197) is a foundation; FY2027 should build the AI-specific structure on top of it.

**Lane two: Extend OT authority and Middle Tier Acquisition to AI-as-a-service.** [Other Transaction Authority and the Middle Tier Acquisition pathway](https://aida.mitre.org/middle-tier/) — established under Section 804 of the FY2016 NDAA and codified at 10 U.S.C. § 4021 — were designed to accelerate prototype-to-fieldable timelines for innovative systems. The FY2026 NDAA expanded OT authority to include experimentation, which is progress. But neither pathway was explicitly drafted for subscription-based AI model access, where "delivery" is continuous API access rather than a hardware deliverable. [War on the Rocks' FY2026 NDAA industry guide](https://warontherocks.com/2025/12/an-industry-guide-to-the-fy2026-ndaa/) flags this gap. Congress should amend OT and MTA statutory provisions in FY2027 to explicitly cover AI-as-a-service contract structures, treating model update cycles as milestones rather than contract modifications requiring re-authorization.

**Lane three: Commercial AI vendor on-ramp reform.** The FY2026 NDAA's [nontraditional contractor exemptions and raised CAS thresholds](https://www.insidegovernmentcontracts.com/2025/12/fy26-ndaa-aims-to-raise-the-dollar-thresholds-for-the-applicability-of-cas-and-tina/) point in the right direction. Congress should extend this logic to MOSA compliance: commercial AI APIs accessed via standard interfaces should be deemed MOSA-compliant for data portability purposes without requiring bespoke technical data package development. The anti-lock-in goal is achievable through contract terms requiring API interoperability — it does not require imposing hardware-era documentation standards on software providers.

## The Bottom Line

The [Department's AI-First Strategy](https://media.defense.gov/2026/Jan/12/2003855671/-1/-1/0/ARTIFICIAL-INTELLIGENCE-STRATEGY-FOR-THE-DEPARTMENT-OF-WAR.PDF) is not a communications initiative. The Pace-Setting Projects are real, the CDAO mandate is genuine, and the Barrier Removal Board represents meaningful executive commitment. The 30-day commercial deployment target is the most aggressive such mandate in Pentagon history.

But the Pentagon has announced ambitious AI modernization initiatives before — JEDI, JADC2, the 2018 AI Strategy — and the gap between vision and fielded capability has been consistent. That gap is not explained by a lack of leadership ambition. It is explained by a procurement system that functions as a structural barrier to the commercial AI market, imposed through FAR/DFARS cost accounting requirements, RMF authorization timelines, and MOSA compliance burdens that the strategy's authors cannot waive by executive memo.

The CHIPS and Science Act demonstrated that Congress can act with urgency when strategic technology competition demands it. Defense AI procurement is the same category of problem: a structural misalignment between US institutional capacity and the speed of technological competition, with a strategic adversary exploiting the gap in real time. The FY2027 NDAA is the vehicle. The acquisition reform agenda — risk-tiered ATO, OT/MTA extension to AI-as-a-service, commercial vendor on-ramp relief — is the content. The strategic case is already made.

The 30-day mandate will either mean something or it won't. That determination belongs to Congress.
