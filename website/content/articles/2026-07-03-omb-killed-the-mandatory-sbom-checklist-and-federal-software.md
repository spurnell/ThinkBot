On January 23, 2026, the Office of Management and Budget quietly did something federal cybersecurity policy rarely does: it admitted a mandate had failed and replaced it with less paperwork rather than more. [Memorandum M-26-05, "Adopting a Risk-Based Approach to Software and Hardware Security,"](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/M-26-05-Adopting-a-Risk-based-Approach-to-Software-and-Hardware-Security.pdf) rescinded the Biden administration's mandatory "Common Form" secure-software attestation regime, first imposed by [M-22-18 in September 2022](https://bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/M-22-18.pdf) and narrowed by M-23-16 in 2023. In its place, individual agencies now have discretion to build risk-based software and hardware assurance policies tailored to their own mission needs — and the software bill of materials (SBOM), once a document agencies were pushed toward requiring wholesale, becomes something they can request when it actually matters.

This is not a story that has gotten much attention outside federal contracting law circles, but it deserves more. It is a clean natural experiment in mandate design: the government tried a uniform, prescriptive compliance checklist for software supply-chain security, watched it fail on its own terms, and corrected by restoring discretion rather than writing a more detailed checklist. Layer on top of it CISA's [May 2026 joint guidance with its G7 partners](https://www.cisa.gov/resources-tools/resources/software-bill-materials-ai-minimum-elements) on minimum elements for AI-specific SBOMs — voluntary, multistakeholder, explicitly non-mandatory — and a coherent model for extending software transparency norms starts to come into focus. It's also a useful contrast to the direction Brussels has taken with the EU's Cyber Resilience Act, whose SBOM-adjacent reporting obligations become enforceable this September under a considerably more rigid statutory framework.

## What the Common Form actually required — and why it didn't work

M-22-18's logic was straightforward: after SolarWinds and Log4Shell exposed how little the federal government knew about the provenance and composition of the software running its systems, OMB directed every agency to obtain a signed self-attestation from software vendors confirming they followed [NIST's Secure Software Development Framework](https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/218/final) before that software could be used on federal information systems. CISA operationalized this in March 2024 with the "Common Form" — a standardized attestation document every vendor, from a two-person SaaS startup to a critical-infrastructure hardware supplier, had to complete in essentially the same way.

OMB's own rescission memo is candid about what went wrong. As multiple law firms tracking the memo have reported, OMB concluded the attestation regime imposed ["unproven and burdensome software accounting processes that prioritized compliance over genuine security investments and diverted agencies from developing tailored assurance requirements,"](https://www.wiley.law/alert-OMB-Rescinds-Secure-Software-Development-Mandate-in-Favor-of-a-Risk-Based-Approach) while also failing to account for hardware-based supply-chain threats at all. OMB Director Russ Vought put the underlying critique more bluntly: ["There is no universal, one-size-fits-all method of achieving"](https://www.nextgov.com/cybersecurity/2026/01/omb-reverses-biden-era-software-attestation-order/410939/) software security across an agency portfolio that ranges from public-facing informational websites to weapons-adjacent industrial control systems.

That is a fair description of what compliance theater looks like in practice. A signed attestation form doesn't verify that secure development practices were actually followed — CISA had no meaningful capacity to audit the thousands of forms that would flow in, and the form itself asked vendors to self-certify against practices phrased at a level of generality that made "yes" the path of least resistance regardless of underlying rigor. Meanwhile, contracting officers and agency CISOs — the people who actually understand which systems touch classified networks, which touch benefits payments, and which run a public comment portal — were locked into paperwork collection instead of building assurance regimes that matched actual exposure. [Analysis from law firms tracking federal contracting](https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/software-de-simplified-trump-administration-rescinds-standardized-secure-software-development-attestation-requirements) has consistently noted that the Common Form's uniform threshold effectively treated a commodity productivity tool and a SCADA vendor as equivalent risks, which is precisely backwards from how security resources should be allocated.

## The right template: risk-based discretion with a retained backstop

M-26-05's replacement approach gets the structure right. Agencies must now maintain accurate software and hardware inventories and build assurance policies "commensurate with the risk" of the specific system involved — a genuine differentiated-risk-tier model rather than a checkbox applied uniformly. Critically, OMB did not eliminate the underlying transparency tool; it changed who decides when to use it and on what terms. SBOMs shift from a quasi-mandatory expectation to a contractual option agencies can invoke selectively — and OMB specifically preserved the ability of agencies procuring cloud services to require that providers furnish an [SBOM of the runtime production environment "upon request,"](https://www.dwt.com/blogs/privacy--security-law-blog/2026/02/omb-changes-course-on-software-security) which is exactly the category of infrastructure — shared, opaque by default, running code from dozens of upstream vendors — where transparency demands are hardest to substitute with anything else.

That retained backstop matters, and this brief is not arguing for its removal. Full discretion with zero floor would invite the predictable failure mode: budget-constrained agencies quietly deprioritizing software assurance altogether, and a fragmented landscape where a vendor selling into a dozen agencies faces a dozen incompatible ad hoc security questionnaires — which is itself a compliance burden, just a less standardized one. Critics of M-26-05, including some who track secure-by-design policy closely, [have raised exactly this concern](https://www.scworld.com/perspective/the-trump-administrations-recent-omb-memo-walks-back-important-secure-by-design-concepts) — that walking back a mandate without a clear minimum invites underinvestment.

They're not wrong that a floor is necessary. They're wrong that M-26-05 removed one. The memo keeps the CISA form available for agencies that want to keep using it, keeps the on-request SBOM authority intact for the highest-leverage procurement category (cloud), and simply removes the requirement that every agency use the same instrument for every vendor regardless of risk. That's the correct minimum: a backstop authority agencies can invoke, not a universal mandate they must document compliance with regardless of relevance.

The near-term cost is real and worth naming honestly: contractors now face [near-term uncertainty](https://govcon.mofo.com/topics/omb-relaxes-federal-contractor-software-attestation-requirements-but-creates-uncertainty-in-the-process) about which agencies will keep using the Common Form, which will build bespoke requirements, and which will lean primarily on SBOM-on-request. That transition friction is the legitimate price of correcting a badly designed uniform rule — and it's considerably smaller than the compliance drag the uniform rule itself imposed for five years across the entire federal vendor base. Congress and the FAR Council have a role here in the medium term: standardizing *how* agencies request risk-tiered assurance (a shared taxonomy of risk tiers, not a shared checklist) would preserve M-26-05's flexibility while reducing the vendor-facing chaos of fifty different agency-specific forms. That's a worthwhile follow-on rulemaking; it is not a reason to have kept the Common Form.

## The cautionary counterfactual: what the US just avoided

The value of M-26-05's approach is easiest to see against the alternative Washington didn't take — the one Brussels did. The EU's [Cyber Resilience Act](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/cyber-resilience-act) imposes binding, statutory software-transparency obligations on any manufacturer placing "products with digital elements" on the EU market, with reporting duties for actively exploited vulnerabilities and severe incidents taking effect on [September 11, 2026](https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/eu-cyber-resilience-act-countdown-11-september-2026-incidentvulnerability-reporting-deadline-is-less-than-100-days-away) and the full SBOM-and-vulnerability-management regime binding by December 2027. Because manufacturers cannot meet a 24-hour vulnerability disclosure clock without already having component-level visibility into their software, the CRA's practical effect is a de facto mandatory SBOM requirement more than a year before its formal legal deadline — enforced with fines up to €15 million or 2.5% of global turnover, applied uniformly across a small IoT device maker and a critical infrastructure software vendor alike.

That is precisely the design flaw OMB just diagnosed and corrected in the domestic Common Form, except the CRA locks it into statute rather than executive guidance, making a risk-based recalibration far harder to achieve without a full legislative reopening. For US software vendors selling into both markets, the contrast is now a live competitiveness fact: the same company can face a flexible, risk-tiered relationship with federal agencies on one side of the Atlantic and a rigid, uniformly enforced statutory mandate with eight-figure penalty exposure on the other. That's not an argument that the US should race to match Brussels' rigidity — it's an argument that Washington should recognize the design choice it made in M-26-05 as a genuine and defensible point of difference worth preserving, not an oversight to be corrected by future mandate creep.

## The right vehicle for a new domain: voluntary AI-SBOM guidance

If M-26-05 shows how to correct a bad mandate, CISA's [Software Bill of Materials for AI – Minimum Elements](https://www.cisa.gov/resources-tools/resources/software-bill-materials-ai-minimum-elements) guidance — released in May 2026 jointly with its G7 partners — shows how to extend transparency norms into a genuinely new domain without repeating the mistake in the first place. AI systems raise supply-chain questions that traditional software SBOMs don't capture: model provenance, training-data lineage, fine-tuning history, and infrastructure dependencies that traditional package-and-library inventories weren't built to describe. The guidance organizes these into [seven clusters](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2026/06/us-cisa-g7-partners-in-europe-and-asia-release-minimum-elements-for-ai-software-bills-of-materials) — metadata, system-level properties, models, dataset properties, infrastructure, security properties, and performance indicators — and is explicit that the elements are a floor, not a ceiling, expected to expand as the technology and as industry practice matures.

Making this voluntary rather than mandatory is the right call, and not merely as a matter of ideological preference for light-touch regulation. AI supply-chain documentation standards are genuinely unsettled: there is no consensus yet on how to represent training-data provenance in a way that's both meaningful and doesn't force disclosure of trade secrets, no agreement on how granular model-lineage documentation needs to be to be useful for security purposes versus merely burdensome, and rapid ongoing change in how models get built, fine-tuned, and deployed via API versus self-hosted weights. Mandating a specific AI-SBOM schema now, before market practice has converged, risks locking in a format that becomes obsolete or actively counterproductive within eighteen months — precisely the kind of premature standardization that a voluntary, iterative, multistakeholder guidance document is designed to avoid. Industry adoption, procurement pressure from agencies and large enterprise buyers who choose to request AI-SBOMs from vendors, and international alignment among willing governments can do the norm-building work here without the ossification risk of a binding rule.

## The template going forward

M-26-05 and the CISA/G7 AI-SBOM guidance together sketch a coherent federal approach to software supply-chain transparency: use binding requirements sparingly and keep them tied to genuine risk (the retained cloud-SBOM-on-request backstop), use agency discretion to match assurance intensity to actual mission exposure rather than uniform checklists, and use voluntary multistakeholder frameworks to build out transparency norms in fast-moving domains before locking in a standard by mandate. None of this amounts to abandoning software transparency as a policy goal — the thesis here is about mandate design, not about whether the federal government should know what's in the software it runs. It should. The question OMB got right this time is how much of that assurance needs to come from a form everyone fills out the same way, versus from agencies actually empowered to ask for the right thing from the right vendor at the right moment. The FAR Council's next move — if any — should build shared risk-tiering language for agencies to draw on, not resurrect a common checklist by another name.
