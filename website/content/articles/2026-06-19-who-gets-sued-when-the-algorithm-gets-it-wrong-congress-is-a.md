On August 12, [Colorado's HB 26-1195](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/HB26-1195) takes effect. The law prohibits licensed therapists, unlicensed psychotherapists, and anyone else authorized to provide psychotherapy in the state from using AI systems to directly interact with clients in therapeutic communication, generate treatment recommendations or plans without professional review, or detect client emotions or mental states. Governor Polis [signed the bill June 3](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/HB26-1195). It will be followed on January 1, 2027 by [HB 26-1139](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/HB26-1139), which prohibits insurers from basing utilization review decisions solely on AI-generated group data and imposes compliance requirements on any AI system used in prior authorization. Together with [SB 26-189](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb26-189) — which Governor Polis signed May 14, repealing and replacing Colorado's original AI governance law with a disclosure-focused automated decision-making framework also effective January 1, 2027 — health systems operating in the state must navigate a multi-layered state AI governance regime with no coherent federal baseline beneath it.

Both health AI bills are the product of a legislature that, in the absence of federal guidance, felt compelled to do something. That impulse is understandable. But Colorado's laws answer the wrong question. They ask: *what should AI systems be prohibited from doing in clinical settings?* The question Colorado needs Congress to answer — and that other state legislatures will keep trying to answer themselves until Congress does — is harder: *when clinical AI produces a bad patient outcome, who bears legal responsibility?*

That liability allocation question does not yet have a federal answer. What it does have, as of September 2025, is the beginning of one — built not by regulators or legislators, but by the Joint Commission and the Coalition for Health AI (CHAI). Their [Responsible Use of AI in Healthcare (RUAIH) framework](https://www.jointcommission.org/en-us/knowledge-library/news/2025-09-jc-and-chai-release-initial-guidance-to-support-responsible-ai-adoption), converted into a [formal voluntary certification program on June 1, 2026](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/06/01/3304442/0/en/joint-commission-releases-first-of-its-kind-exclusively-designed-for-healthcare-organizations-voluntary-responsible-use-of-ai-in-healthcare-certification.html), is the architecture for a workable liability safe harbor. It is sitting in plain sight. Congress is ignoring it while advancing the Durbin-Hawley [AI LEAD Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2937/text) — a bill that would impose strict products liability on AI developers and whose application to clinical AI would be catastrophic.

## The Liability Gap Is Real, and It Is Paralyzing Both Parties

The current legal landscape governing clinical AI liability has the structure of a paradox. Under settled malpractice doctrine, [physicians bear ultimate liability](https://www.medicaleconomics.com/view/the-new-malpractice-frontier-who-s-liable-when-ai-gets-it-wrong-) for clinical decisions made with AI assistance. If a physician follows an algorithmic recommendation without independent review and a patient is harmed, the duty of care stays with the doctor. Courts have consistently refused to transfer that duty to software or its developer — there is no federal law and no settled common-law doctrine that shifts malpractice liability from clinician to AI tool.

AI developers, by contrast, occupy a comparatively sheltered legal space. They are not licensed practitioners and therefore not subject to malpractice claims. They are potentially reachable through state products liability law — but clinical AI systems are typically sold under contracts that disclaim warranties, cap damages, and describe the software as a "decision support" tool rather than a clinical instrument, creating substantial barriers to successful plaintiff claims. As [Sommers Schwartz's June 2026 analysis](https://www.sommerspc.com/blog/2026/06/ai-misdiagnoses-ai-in-healthcare-and-the-liability-chain-from-software-developer-to-hospital/) of the AI healthcare liability chain concluded, vendors operate in "a comparatively sheltered legal space" while physicians and hospitals absorb disproportionate exposure.

The result, identified precisely by Lawton et al. in a [2024 paper published in the Future Healthcare Journal](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11025047/), is that clinicians risk becoming "liability sinks" for AI — absorbing legal exposure for the consequences of algorithmic recommendations while being disenfranchised from the model's decision logic. Physicians cannot meaningfully audit models they did not design, train, or control. They are nonetheless legally responsible for outcomes those models contribute to.

Neither the physician nor the developer, under this arrangement, has clean incentives for safe deployment. Physicians are incentivized to document independent clinical judgment regardless of how substantially AI shaped their decision. Developers are incentivized to characterize products as supplementary tools and bury liability in contract fine print. Hospitals are caught between them: potentially liable for system selection and deployment, but lacking the technical capacity to independently validate the models they procure. The [Milbank Quarterly's analysis](https://www.milbank.org/quarterly/articles/artificial-intelligence-and-liability-in-medicine-balancing-safety-and-innovation/) of AI liability in medicine identified this three-party misalignment as the core structural problem. Nothing in the current tort framework resolves it.

## The AI LEAD Act Is the Wrong Instrument

Into this gap steps the [AI LEAD Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2937/text) (S.2937), introduced September 29, 2025 by Senators Dick Durbin (D-IL) and Josh Hawley (R-MO). The bill's core mechanism is to classify AI systems as products and apply standard products liability doctrine: negligent design, negligent failure to warn, breach of express warranty, and — critically — strict liability for a "defective condition [that is] unreasonably dangerous." As the [National Law Review's analysis](https://natlawreview.com/article/new-federal-legislation-proposes-product-liability-standards-ai-systems) summarized, the strict liability track would hold a developer liable "notwithstanding that the developer exercised all possible care in the design or distribution of the covered product."

That *notwithstanding all possible care* language is not a drafting quirk. It is the mechanism's point: even a developer that met every reasonable safety standard is liable if the product causes harm and a jury deems it defective. Applied to consumer AI — chatbots producing self-harm content, generative models producing defamatory output — this framework is debatable on its merits. Applied to clinical AI, it would be destructive.

The closest historical parallel is what unchecked tort liability did to the U.S. vaccine industry in the 1970s and early 1980s. As litigation against vaccine manufacturers escalated and jury awards became unpredictable, manufacturers exited the market. [By the end of 1984](https://historyofvaccines.org/vaccines-101/ethical-issues-and-vaccines/vaccine-injury-compensation-programs/), only one U.S. company still manufactured the DPT vaccine. Congress passed the [National Childhood Vaccine Injury Act of 1986](https://www.congress.gov/bill/99th-congress/house-bill/5546) specifically to restore supply by shielding manufacturers from unavoidable-side-effect claims — not because Congress thought vaccines needed less oversight, but because it recognized that strict liability without a design alternative was eliminating the supply of a life-saving product.

Clinical AI is not vaccines. But the market dynamic rhymes. [Hooper Lundy's analysis](https://hooperlundy.com/durbin-hawley-join-to-introduce-products-liability-cause-of-action-bill-for-ai-systems/) of the AI LEAD Act observed that the bill's design defect and failure-to-warn theories would reach clinical decision support products of the kind deployed by Epic, Nuance, and Google Health — products that inform millions of clinical decisions per year. Smaller clinical AI developers — academic medical center spinouts, safety-net-focused startups, rural telehealth vendors — would face extinction-level litigation exposure before reaching institutional scale. The innovation tax falls heaviest on the entrants most likely to serve underserved populations.

The AI LEAD Act's authors are solving a genuine problem: AI developers currently have too little accountability for harm. The solution should be *calibrated accountability through a prospective standard of care* — not blunt strict liability that treats a sepsis risk model and a consumer chatbot as legally equivalent products.

## The Joint Commission-CHAI Framework Already Exists and Works

What Congress is ignoring is that the healthcare sector did not wait for federal intervention to begin solving this problem. On September 17, 2025, the Joint Commission and the Coalition for Health AI released [initial RUAIH guidance](https://www.chai.org/news/joint-commission-and-coalition-for-health-ai-chai-release-initial-guidance) representing — as [Polsinelli's analysis](https://www.polsinelli.com/publications/tjc-chai-release-guidance-responsible-ai-use-healthcare) described it — "the first formal framework from a U.S. accrediting body aimed at helping healthcare organizations safely, effectively, and ethically integrate AI into clinical and operational practice."

The RUAIH framework establishes seven governance pillars: formal governance structure integrated with existing compliance and patient safety processes; multidisciplinary oversight including clinical, technical, legal, and patient perspectives; lifecycle management with model monitoring and drift detection; risk and impact assessment before deployment; algorithmic bias review; clinician trust and transparency protocols; and data governance. On May 27, 2026, CHAI released [eight comprehensive governance playbooks](https://www.chai.org/news/coalition-for-health-ai-chai-releases-comprehensive-governance-playbooks-to) developed through community workshops with 150+ health AI leaders, providing operationalized implementation guidance for more than 100 health systems. On June 1 — just eighteen days ago — the Joint Commission [launched the RUAIH certification](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/06/01/3304442/0/en/joint-commission-releases-first-of-its-kind-exclusively-designed-for-healthcare-organizations-voluntary-responsible-use-of-ai-in-healthcare-certification.html): a voluntary program recognizing hospitals, critical access hospitals, and health systems that demonstrate governance, safeguards, monitoring, and clinician education aligned with the RUAIH standard.

As [Healthcare IT News](https://www.healthcareitnews.com/news/joint-commission-intros-new-voluntary-ai-responsibility-certification) noted, the certification is open to any healthcare organization regardless of existing Joint Commission accreditation status — making it a freestanding governance standard available to every hospital and health system in the country. This is not draft guidance or stakeholder commentary. This is a live certification program operating at the same institutional authority level as other Joint Commission accreditation regimes that health systems already treat as dispositive for market access, CMS reimbursement eligibility, and institutional insurance coverage.

The governance infrastructure is built. The question is whether Congress will use it.

## Map Accreditation to Liability: The Safe Harbor Model

The Joint Commission-CHAI framework maps cleanly onto a federal liability safe harbor structure. The policy mechanism is straightforward: amend federal law to provide that a clinical AI developer or deploying institution that achieves and maintains RUAIH certification — or demonstrates compliance with the [NIST AI Risk Management Framework's](https://airc.nist.gov/RMF) GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE functions for high-risk clinical systems — receives qualified immunity from strict liability claims under the AI LEAD Act's "defective condition" theory.

The critical design choice is *qualified* rather than *absolute* immunity. The NCVIA's design-defect shield was, in retrospect, too broad — it removed accountability for harms that manufacturers could reasonably have foreseen. The clinical AI safe harbor should be defeatable by a showing of actual fraud, intentional concealment of known safety defects, or conscious disregard of post-market monitoring signals. Developers who do not obtain RUAIH certification or demonstrate equivalent NIST AI RMF compliance receive no safe harbor — they are fully exposed to the AI LEAD Act's liability framework, including its strict liability track.

This creates the right incentive structure. Developers who invest in algorithmic bias review, lifecycle monitoring, and clinician transparency protocols get legal predictability in exchange. Those who do not bear unbounded litigation risk. The standard of care is established prospectively by the governance framework that healthcare's leading accreditor built specifically for this problem — not determined retrospectively by a jury evaluating whether an AI sepsis model was "unreasonably dangerous" based on a single bad outcome.

## IRB-Style Review for High-Stakes Clinical AI

The safe harbor framework should also encode a distinction the AI LEAD Act elides: not all clinical AI carries equivalent stakes. Administrative workflow tools — scheduling assistants, medical coding software, billing automation — do not make treatment decisions and should not face the same governance requirements as diagnostic decision-support or psychiatric intervention systems.

Congress should require IRB-style institutional review for pre-deployment clearance of AI systems that directly influence clinical diagnosis, treatment planning, or psychiatric intervention — precisely the category of tools Colorado has now separately barred from autonomous use. The IRB analog would not replicate clinical trial protocols; it would require that a multidisciplinary institutional committee review the system's training data provenance, bias evaluation results, validation studies, deployment protocols, and post-market monitoring commitments before any patient encounter.

This tiered approach — safe harbor for RUAIH/NIST-compliant developers across all clinical AI, plus IRB-style pre-deployment clearance for the highest-stakes applications — allocates accountability in proportion to risk and governance investment. It does not categorically prohibit AI from clinical settings. It creates the conditions under which AI can be deployed safely and with legal predictability for the physicians, hospitals, and developers trying to use it responsibly.

## The State Clock Is Running

Colorado's August 12 deadline is not an isolated event. As [Ropes & Gray has documented](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/05/colorado-scales-back-ai-law-with-targeted-implications-for-health-care) in analyzing Colorado's evolving health AI landscape, the pattern of layered health AI legislation — even as the state replaced its original AI governance framework with lighter disclosure requirements in May 2026 — reflects a broader national dynamic: state legislatures pass locally sensible rules without coordinating with each other, with the FDA CDS framework, or with the Joint Commission-CHAI governance standard that all of them are implicitly trying to operationalize through prohibition. Each new state law that bans or heavily restricts clinical AI — without a federal liability floor beneath it — carves out a different compliance footprint that advantages large integrated delivery networks over community hospitals, prices rural providers out of AI-assisted care, and fragments the national market for tools that could benefit patients uniformly.

Narrow federal preemption is appropriate here: not preemption of state health AI law across the board — sector-specific state authority to regulate disclosure, insurance, and licensure should remain intact — but preemption of state-law liability allocation regimes for clinical AI, coupled with the safe harbor framework described above. States would retain authority to require disclosure, enforce insurance regulatory standards, and condition licensure on AI governance practices. They would not be permitted to impose liability regimes inconsistent with the federal standard or categorical prohibitions that effectively preclude safe, accreditation-compliant deployment.

That is the window the Senate HELP Committee and the House Energy & Commerce Health Subcommittee have before August 12. The Joint Commission has done the substantive work. Congress needs to pick it up.

## Conclusion: The Infrastructure Is Built — Use It

The clinical AI liability question is not intractable. It has an answer that the private sector spent the last nine months constructing. The Joint Commission-CHAI RUAIH framework establishes a governance standard specific enough to be administrable, flexible enough to accommodate innovation, and authoritative enough to anchor federal legislation. The Durbin-Hawley AI LEAD Act mistakes blunt strict liability for accountability — and the [NCVIA episode](https://journalofethics.ama-assn.org/article/national-childhood-vaccine-injury-act-and-supreme-courts-interpretation/2012-01) is the empirical record of what happens when products-liability exposure becomes unbounded in a specialized healthcare market.

Colorado's August 12 deadline is a signal, not a crisis. Prohibition-adjacent state laws proliferate where federal liability standards do not exist. The way to stop Colorado-style fragmentation is not to wait for fifty inconsistent state regimes and then attempt post-hoc preemption. It is to establish a federal clinical AI liability allocation standard now — one that rewards governance investment, punishes negligence, and gives the hospital systems, developers, and physicians deploying AI tools the legal certainty they need to do so at scale.

The accreditation layer built the standard. The Joint Commission certification launched eighteen days ago. Congress is about to miss it.
