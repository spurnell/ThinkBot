On June 3, the House Energy & Commerce Subcommittee on Commerce, Manufacturing and Trade convened a high-stakes first hearing on [H.R. 8413, the SECURE Data Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/8413), a federal framework that would preempt the growing tangle of state privacy laws in favor of a uniform national standard enforced by the FTC and state attorneys general. The subcommittee [split sharply along party lines](https://statescoop.com/house-subcommittee-secure-data-act-preempts-state-privacy-laws-2026/), with Republicans touting the bill as long overdue and Democrats warning it would erase hard-won state protections. No markup has been announced.

That means the window is open — and the right answer is clear. Twenty state privacy laws have not produced a coherent consumer protection regime. They have produced a compliance labyrinth that burdens every company that can't afford a multi-state privacy law department, while data-hoarding incumbents absorb that friction as a structural moat. The SECURE Data Act's preemption architecture is the correct prescription. Congress should resist the chorus calling for a private right of action and trust the FTC-plus-state-AG enforcement model to deliver real outcomes.

## Compliance Theater, Not Consumer Protection

The argument for maintaining the state patchwork rests on a foundational premise that doesn't survive contact with reality: that twenty different state privacy laws, each with slightly different definitions, consent triggers, opt-out mechanisms, and enforcement regimes, collectively add up to meaningful consumer protection.

They don't. They add up to a legal industry.

As of 2026, [20 states have enacted comprehensive data privacy laws](https://iapp.org/resources/article/us-state-privacy-legislation-tracker), with Indiana, Kentucky, and Rhode Island's statutes taking effect on January 1 of this year. On paper, these laws share some DNA — most use similar language defining personal data as "information that is linked or reasonably linkable to an identified or identifiable individual," as [ITIF's June 3 report on state privacy lessons](https://itif.org/publications/2026/06/03/state-of-privacy-lessons-from-state-laws-for-national-framework/) catalogues. But the divergences accumulate quickly: Colorado's Privacy Act is among [the most operationally demanding](https://www.njbusiness-attorney.com/us-state-privacy-laws-2026-saas-compliance-map/) state regimes in practice; Connecticut's CTDPA produced [the state's first enforcement action in July 2025](https://www.gtlaw-dataprivacydish.com/2025/07/connecticut-ag-fines-ticket-marketplace-in-states-first-ctdpa-privacy-law-enforcement-action/) — an $85,000 fine against ticket marketplace TicketNetwork over a "largely unreadable" privacy notice; California's CPRA has stacked on third-party data sharing requirements, employee data obligations, and algorithmic transparency mandates that have no equivalent in most other state frameworks.

The result is an innovation tax spread unevenly across the economy. A [2022 ITIF analysis](https://itif.org/publications/2022/01/24/50-state-patchwork-privacy-laws-could-cost-1-trillion-more-single-federal/) found that a 50-state privacy patchwork would impose more than $1 trillion in additional compliance costs on organizations compared to a single federal standard — a gap that has only widened as additional states have enacted laws since that estimate was published. The drivers are predictable: jurisdiction-specific consent management customization, data subject request workflows tuned to each state's idiosyncrasies, and the cross-state legal opinions required to triangulate compliance across overlapping regimes. A [2026 analysis by Shook, Hardy & Bacon](https://www.shb.com/intelligence/newsletters/pds/hansen-2026-privacy-compliance-obligations) confirms the picture is worsening: settlement costs are rising, cure periods are expiring, and "the era of 'fix it later if we have to' is largely over" for companies operating across state lines.

What do consumers get from this? Cookie banners. Consent pop-ups. Privacy notices that Subcommittee member Rep. Cliff Bentz (R-Ore.) noted consumers [accept without reading](https://rollcall.com/2026/06/03/consent-questions-raised-at-data-privacy-bill-hearing/) — an observation that drew no serious pushback from the hearing's witnesses. Privacy protection that lives entirely in legal documentation, visible only to plaintiffs' lawyers, is not consumer protection. It is compliance theater.

The most efficient compliance strategy available to multi-state businesses under the current system, as legal advisors consistently note, is to [calibrate your entire program to California's CPRA](https://www.njbusiness-attorney.com/us-state-privacy-laws-2026-saas-compliance-map/) and then layer on state-specific configurations for the remaining jurisdictions. California maximalism as the de facto national standard isn't a feature — it's an unelected regulatory regime imposed through litigation risk, without any of the democratic accountability of federal rulemaking.

## The Political Economy of the Preemption Split

The [June 3 hearing split](https://statescoop.com/house-subcommittee-secure-data-act-preempts-state-privacy-laws-2026/) was predictable — not because Republicans are right and Democrats are wrong on privacy, but because the political economy of federal preemption produces predictable coalitions.

Rep. Brett Guthrie (R-KY), a bill champion, framed the federal approach precisely: the goal is "not to compete with Europe to regulate" but "to compete against China to innovate." Rep. Frank Pallone Jr. (D-NJ) countered that the bill "locks in the failed notice and consent status quo" and contains "loophole upon loophole." Rep. Kathy Castor (D-FL) called it "an appalling betrayal of hardworking Americans."

Strong language. But notice who lines up against preemption: state attorney general offices, which currently possess enforcement leverage that federal preemption would redirect toward FTC coordination; consumer advocacy organizations, some of which owe their policy influence to the complexity of the existing regime; and plaintiffs'-bar-aligned interests whose business model depends on forum shopping across state courts. Civil liberties witnesses at the hearing testified that the federal standard would be "weaker than the weakest state law" — a framing that conveniently ignores that state-level variation is precisely what they are defending.

When Caitriona Fitzgerald of the Electronic Privacy Information Center [testified](https://epic.org/documents/testimony-on-h-r-8413-the-secure-data-act/) that the bill allows businesses to "continue collecting and using data however they please as long as they disclose it," she was making a consent-architecture critique that applies with equal force to most of the state laws currently on the books. The patchwork doesn't solve the consent-quality problem; it multiplies the paperwork around it. As the [IAPP's analysis of the SECURE Data Act](https://iapp.org/news/a/secure-data-act-analysis-of-the-new-federal-privacy-bill) notes, the bill faces criticism precisely because it would eliminate leverage — leverage that the opposition coalition has spent years building.

The opposition to preemption is not, at its core, a policy argument. It is a political economy argument dressed in policy language.

## The BIPA Myth and the Case for Precision Enforcement

The most frequently cited evidence that a private right of action outperforms agency enforcement is the Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act. BIPA does have a private right of action, and it has generated an enormous volume of litigation. The EFF's [critique of the SECURE Data Act](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/05/secure-data-act-not-serious-piece-privacy-legislation) cites the absence of a private right of action as evidence the bill "is not a serious piece of privacy legislation."

That argument gets the BIPA story exactly backwards.

BIPA private litigation has not primarily constrained the large technology platforms accumulating the largest troves of sensitive biometric data. It has primarily targeted employers who installed biometric timeclocks and sandwich chains that used fingerprint scanners to log worker hours. Pret A Manger [settled a BIPA case for $677,000](https://cookcountyrecord.com/stories/618765727-pret-a-manger-agrees-to-pay-677k-to-settle-il-biometrics-class-action-over-worker-punch-clock-fingerprint-scans) over fingerprint-based timeclock systems for roughly 800 workers. Hospitals, trucking companies, and logistics firms have faced wave after wave of class actions for systems that create no meaningful privacy harm at scale. BIPA filings peaked at 427 in 2024 before [falling to 150 in 2025](https://www.legalnewsline.com/cook-county-record/reforms-sliced-bipa-class-actions-in-2025-new-report-says/) — not because the privacy problem was solved, but because Illinois Democrats amended the statute to restrict per-scan damages that were producing [potentially catastrophic exposure](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2023/02/illinois-supreme-courts-most-recent-bipa-decision-exponentially-increases-potential-exposure-for-businesses/) for ordinary businesses, not surveillance capitalists.

The BIPA experience illustrates the structural problem with plaintiff's-bar economics as a regulatory mechanism: private litigation is high-volume and indiscriminate. It is not precision-targeted at the data practices that cause the largest consumer harms. It is targeted at the violations most amenable to class certification and the defendants most likely to settle.

FTC enforcement is different in kind, not just degree. The Third Circuit's [landmark decision in *FTC v. Wyndham*](https://www.insideprivacy.com/united-states/federal-trade-commission/third-circuit-upholds-ftcs-data-security-authority-in-ftc-v-wyndham/) confirmed the Commission's authority to regulate companies' data security practices under Section 5's "unfair practices" prong — providing a durable enforcement foundation that does not require Congressional action on every new category of harm. The FTC also brings subject-matter expertise to complex cases that contingency-fee attorneys structurally lack. The EFF's [critique of the bill's 45-day cure provision](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/05/secure-data-act-not-serious-piece-privacy-legislation) as a loophole is more legitimate — but the answer is tightening the cure architecture, not restoring the conditions for BIPA-style volume fire.

As [ITIF's analysis of the bill's enforcement model](https://itif.org/publications/2026/05/15/state-privacy-laws-show-the-secure-data-acts-merits-and-political-appeal/) concludes, FTC and state AG enforcement promotes "consistency, provides expert oversight, deters bad actors, and allows regulators to focus on meaningful violations." That is the right architecture. Private litigation adds volume; it does not add targeting.

## The Startup Innovation Tax

The compliance costs of the current patchwork fall hardest on companies that can't lawyer around them.

A large incumbent — the kind of company the privacy advocacy complex most worries about — has in-house privacy counsel, enterprise-grade consent management platforms, established legal opinions on cross-state compliance, and the ability to amortize costs across a revenue base large enough to absorb rules written by 20 different state legislatures. The compliance burden is a cost of doing business; it is also, structurally, a moat that discourages the data-driven startup that might actually challenge the incumbent.

A seed-stage company building in health-adjacent data — personalized care management tools, mental health diagnostics platforms, wearable health integrations — does not have that runway. California's CPRA demands substantial privacy infrastructure before a product reaches market. Colorado's CPA layers on additional operational requirements. Connecticut's enforcement posture, which [produced an $85,000 fine against a regional ticket marketplace in 2025](https://natlawreview.com/article/no-more-warnings-ignoring-ag-costs-85000) for privacy notice deficiencies, signals that it intends to enforce broadly against smaller digital platforms. Building a data-driven product for users in multiple states now requires navigating a compliance landscape whose fragmentation costs [IAPP has documented](https://iapp.org/news/a/secure-data-act-analysis-of-the-new-federal-privacy-bill) as substantial and growing.

The [Software and Information Industry Association's endorsement](https://www.siia.net/siia-backs-the-secure-data-act-as-a-national-framework-for-consumer-privacy-protections/) of the SECURE Data Act reflects the same reality: the innovation tax is real — not because the industry wants weak privacy rules, but because the industry needs clear privacy rules. A national standard with teeth allows companies to build compliance infrastructure once and deploy at scale. The current patchwork requires building compliance infrastructure for every jurisdiction touched, with variations that serve no consumer protection purpose and every lawyer-billing purpose.

The [ITIF analysis](https://itif.org/publications/2026/05/15/state-privacy-laws-show-the-secure-data-acts-merits-and-political-appeal/) makes the critical point: the SECURE Data Act "avoids overly restrictive European-style frameworks," preserving the data-driven product development that has powered American tech competitiveness. Even European policymakers now acknowledge the GDPR needs reform. Importing consent fatigue at the federal level in the name of fixing the patchwork would swap one problem for a worse one.

## What Congress Should Add — and What It Should Resist

No bill is perfect at the hearing stage, and the SECURE Data Act has legitimate vulnerabilities that opponents will exploit. Congress should address those vulnerabilities through targeted additions — and firmly resist the move that would undermine the bill's entire enforcement architecture.

**Add a minimum breach notification standard.** The SECURE Data Act should include a mandatory breach notification floor: 72 hours to the FTC upon discovery of a breach affecting a threshold number of consumers, and 30 days to affected consumers. This eliminates one of the weakest arguments against the bill — that preemption would wipe out 50 state breach notification regimes — without reopening the preemption debate. The FTC has existing authority to require breach remediation; a statutory notification timeline simply codifies practice that sophisticated data handlers already follow and gives regulators clear investigative triggers.

**Tighten the cure provision.** The EFF's critique of the 45-day cure window has merit as drafted. A guaranteed opportunity to cure should apply only to first-time, non-willful violations — not as a recurring safe harbor for repeat offenders. The [ITIF June 3 report's recommendation](https://itif.org/publications/2026/06/03/state-of-privacy-lessons-from-state-laws-for-national-framework/) for "guaranteed cure opportunities to incentivize compliance" is sound; the drafting should foreclose bad-faith reliance on a provision designed for good-faith actors.

**Resist a private right of action as a bipartisan sweetener.** The pressure to add plaintiff's-bar access as a compromise to attract Democratic votes is predictable and should be rejected categorically. A private right of action does not improve targeting — it improves contingency-fee economics. The BIPA experience, in which a private right of action produced hundreds of class actions against timeclock users while doing nothing to constrain large-scale data aggregation, is the most complete empirical case study available. It argues decisively against replicating the model at federal scale. State AG co-enforcement with the FTC provides the geographic accountability that critics of federal-only enforcement legitimately seek; it does not require layering on the economics that turned BIPA into a small-business tax.

## The Window Is Open

The June 3 hearing surfaced the fault lines clearly. The subcommittee split on preemption reflects the political economy of the existing patchwork — who benefits from complexity, who benefits from forum shopping, and who has standing to oppose a federal floor that eliminates leverage they currently hold. It does not reflect a genuine policy argument that twenty inconsistent state regimes better serve consumers than one national standard with FTC-plus-state-AG enforcement.

The SECURE Data Act is not perfect. Its cure architecture needs tightening, and a breach notification floor would make it stronger. But its core proposition — federal preemption of the compliance labyrinth, precision enforcement in lieu of plaintiff's-bar economics, a national standard that enables innovation rather than taxing it — is right. Rep. John Joyce (R-PA), who introduced the bill, noted at the hearing that its provisions drew from "more than 20 states: red states, blue states, and purple states," pointing to [a genuine bipartisan foundation](https://itif.org/publications/2026/05/15/state-privacy-laws-show-the-secure-data-acts-merits-and-political-appeal/) that the markup process should build on, not squander.

The markup window is open. Congress should move.
