In May 2025, a cyber-extortion group calling itself Kairos stole records — Social Security numbers, financial details, fingerprints, passport data — on roughly 45,000 residents and employees of a county government never officially named, though leaked negotiation files point to [Union County, Ohio](https://www.theregister.com/cyber-crime/2026/07/09/an-unnamed-us-county-perhaps-in-ohio-paid-1m-extortion-demand-to-cybercriminals/5269575). Kairos demanded $3 million not to publish the files; the county negotiated the group down and paid [about $1 million in bitcoin](https://www.theregister.com/cyber-crime/2026/07/09/an-unnamed-us-county-perhaps-in-ohio-paid-1m-extortion-demand-to-cybercriminals/5269575) on June 13, 2025. Kairos never claimed to encrypt any files; this was data-theft extortion, not the file-locking attack the term usually implies. The public didn't learn of the payment until a threat-intelligence researcher published a [case study more than a year later](https://thehackernews.com/2026/07/us-government-entity-paid-kairos-group.html) — this month.

That thirteen-month gap between payment and disclosure is the actual story — a data-transparency failure, not a legal-availability-of-payment one. Yet the case is already being cited by advocates of a different policy instrument: banning ransom and extortion payments outright, the path North Carolina set in 2021, Florida followed in 2022, and the UK is preparing to codify through its forthcoming Cyber Security and Resilience Bill. Australia has gone the disclosure route instead — mandatory reporting of ransom payments under its [Cyber Security Act 2024](https://www.legislation.gov.au/F2025L00278/asmade/text), with active enforcement beginning this year. The U.S. is at the same fork, mid-rollout of its own disclosure regime under the Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act (CIRCIA). Policymakers should read the Union County case as an argument for finishing that rollout, not for jumping past it to prohibition.

## Two Different Instruments, Two Different Problems

Reporting mandates and payment bans get discussed as if they're points on the same spectrum. They aren't. A reporting mandate — CIRCIA's 72-hour incident and 24-hour ransom-payment reporting requirements, or Australia's equivalent — solves a visibility problem: government doesn't know who's being extorted, how much, or by whom. A payment ban solves a different, contested problem: the theory that removing the payment option reduces attacker profitability and, over time, attack volume. The first is a low-cost information intervention that doesn't stop anyone from doing anything, just makes it visible. The second is a prohibition with real distributional costs, falling on whichever victim has no other way to protect residents' data.

Conflating the two lets ban advocates borrow the reporting regime's uncontroversial premise — "government should know about this" — to sell a much more aggressive intervention. CIRCIA's final rule, still unpublished as CISA now [targets a September 2026 finalization](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/cybersecurity/2026/07/circia-other-big-cyber-rules-expected-to-get-finalized-this-fall/) after missing its original October 2025 deadline, hasn't even started generating a full data cycle. That's precisely the wrong moment to leapfrog to a payment ban — Congress would be legislating prohibition before the mechanism meant to inform that decision has switched on.

## A Resilience Failure, Not a Legality Failure

Union County didn't pay because payment was legal. It paid because it apparently had no other way to stop 45,000 residents' Social Security and passport data from hitting a leak site. A ban wouldn't have patched the vulnerability Kairos exploited, funded a backup architecture, or given the county's IT staff the tooling to detect exfiltration — it would only have removed the county's last option once the intrusion had already occurred, leaving the underlying preparedness gap untouched.

That gap is well documented and getting worse. The [2026 NASCIO-Deloitte Cybersecurity Study](https://www.nascio.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/2026-NASCIO-Deloitte-Cybersecurity-Study.pdf) found that the share of state CISOs who describe themselves as "not very confident" in local governments' ability to secure public data rose from 35% in 2022 to 63% in 2026, even as 16% of state CISOs reported budget cuts this year, up from none in 2024. Meanwhile the Multi-State Information Sharing and Analysis Center — the threat-intelligence backbone many local governments relied on — [converted to a paid membership model](https://www.cybersecuritydive.com/news/ms-isac-membership-loss-states-federal-funding-cut/821984/) after Washington pulled its funding, and the State and Local Cybersecurity Grant Program, the main federal vehicle for closing this gap, enters its [final year of active funding in FY2026](https://www.cisa.gov/cybergrants/slcgp), with annual awards already down from $400 million in FY2023 to $100 million in FY2025. Legislators reaching for a payment ban are treating a resource-and-resilience problem as a legal-loophole problem. It isn't one.

## What North Carolina and Florida Actually Show

Proponents of prohibition point to NC and FL as proof of concept. The evidence doesn't cooperate. North Carolina CIO James Weaver has [acknowledged that measuring the ban's effect is difficult](https://www.govtech.com/security/should-state-governments-ban-ransomware-payments) given the absence of a clean counterfactual, and the state recorded 843 ransomware-related data breaches in 2023 — two years into the prohibition. Security researchers, including Emsisoft's Brett Callow, were [skeptical from the start](https://statescoop.com/states-ransomware-payment-bans-curb-threat/) that operators would drop state and local targets over a statute their victims might not even know applies to them. The bans' reliable product isn't a measurable drop in attacks but a [more complex compliance patchwork](https://www.cpomagazine.com/cyber-security/patchwork-of-us-state-regulations-becomes-more-complex-as-florida-north-carolina-ban-ransomware-payments/) for multi-jurisdiction entities — a dynamic ThinkBot has flagged before in the state privacy law context, where 20-plus divergent statutes raise compliance cost without a commensurate consumer-protection gain.

The underground-shift risk is the sharpest problem, and it isn't just intuition — it's the finding of the closest thing to peer-reviewed literature this debate has. A 2025 *International Cybersecurity Law Review* study on [banning ransomware payments](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1365/s43439-025-00137-5) concludes that a blanket prohibition risks exactly the outcome U.S. officials cited when they declined to pursue a federal ban in 2022: victims keep paying, they just stop reporting it, "resulting in even less transparency and reporting of attacks." A ban that suppresses reporting doesn't just fail to reduce harm — it degrades the threat-intelligence base CIRCIA and Australia's Cyber Security Act are designed to build. The study's own proposed alternative — a conditional safe harbor tied to minimum security investment and prompt disclosure, rather than a flat prohibition — is worth Congress studying once CIRCIA data exists to calibrate it, not a case for banning payments now.

## The Sequencing Congress Should Follow

Australia offers a workable template: a seven-month [education-first enforcement posture](https://www.gadens.com/legal-insights/new-mandatory-ransomware-payment-reporting-obligations-now-in-force/) before penalties kicked in, building compliance capacity before punishment capacity. CISA should apply the same logic to CIRCIA's rollout, and Congress should resist grafting a payment ban onto CIRCIA before its ransom-payment reporting stream has run for even a full year. Three concrete steps:

**First**, finish CIRCIA. The September 2026 target should hold, and CISA should segment ransom-payment data by entity type and size once reporting begins, so Congress has an actual empirical base — who's paying, how much, and whether payers are disproportionately under-resourced local governments — before any prohibition debate starts in earnest.

**Second**, states should stop treating NC and FL as a template to replicate absent that data. A legislator worried about the next Union County-style incident would do more good funding SLCGP match requirements or MS-ISAC membership than passing a prohibition statute with no demonstrated deterrent effect.

**Third**, Congress should reauthorize and adequately fund the State and Local Cybersecurity Grant Program before it lapses. A ban is cost-free for the legislators who pass it and a real cost for the counties who can't refuse a ransom without it. Resilience investment is the opposite: it costs money up front, and it's the only lever that actually closes the gap a ban merely papers over.

The lesson from the Union County case isn't that payment should have been illegal. It's that a county with 45,000 residents' data on the line had no better option — and that the country still doesn't know, systematically, how often that happens. CIRCIA is built to find out. Let it.
