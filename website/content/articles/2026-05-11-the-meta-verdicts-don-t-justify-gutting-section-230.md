The Senate Judiciary Committee convenes this Wednesday to hear proposals for reforming [Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R46751). The occasion is the [March 2026 jury verdicts](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/25/nx-s1-5746125/meta-youtube-social-media-trial-verdict) finding Meta and YouTube liable for teen addiction harms — and Meta's subsequent move to invoke Section 230 to unwind those judgments on appeal. Congress is watching the maneuver and reaching the wrong conclusion: that Section 230 is broken.

It isn't. The verdicts are proof that it works.

The accountability those juries delivered came through existing state consumer protection and product liability law — without touching Section 230 at all. Meta's post-verdict invocation of the statute is a standard appellate tactic, not evidence of a policy failure. Gutting the legal foundation of the open internet in response to courts enforcing state law through exactly the mechanisms Congress designed is a solution in search of a problem — one that would devastate user-generated content, small platforms, and online speech far beyond the narrow child safety context legislators are citing.

## What the Courts Actually Decided

The California verdict in [*KGM v. Meta*](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/classroom/daily-news-lessons/2026/03/jury-finds-meta-and-youtube-liable-in-landmark-youth-addiction-case) — finding Meta and YouTube liable on negligent design grounds — rested on product liability theory. New Mexico's [$375 million judgment](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/25/new-mexico-meta-lawsuit-jury-verdict.html) rested on the state's Unfair Practices Act, which prohibits deceptive and unconscionable trade practices. Courts in both cases found that platforms' own engineering decisions — infinite scroll, engagement-maximizing algorithms, notification architectures designed to exploit developing brains — could give rise to liability independently of what users posted.

This is the crucial distinction. [Section 230(c)(1)](https://publicknowledge.org/what-section-230-is-and-does-yet-another-explanation-of-one-of-the-internets-most-important-laws/) shields platforms from being "treated as the publisher or speaker" of third-party content. It was never designed to immunize a company from liability for its *own* deceptive conduct, its own product design choices, its own concealment of internal research showing harm to minors. The verdicts didn't require carving out an exception to Section 230; they demonstrated that the statute, properly understood, [never barred these claims in the first place](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/03/meta-google-under-attack-court-cases-bypass-30-year-old-legal-shield.html).

## Meta's Appeal Is Not a Policy Argument

[Meta has asked a California judge to overturn the verdict](https://thedailyrecord.com/2026/05/06/meta-asks-california-judge-to-overturn-social-media-verdict/) and has signaled it will raise Section 230 on appeal. The Senate Judiciary Committee is treating this as evidence that the statute needs legislative repair. That logic inverts causation. A defendant invoking a legal defense is how litigation works. The correct remedy for an overreaching Section 230 argument is a court rejecting it — a process currently underway.

Meanwhile, the [New Mexico Phase 2 trial](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/04/meta-new-mexico-child-safety-facebook-instagram.html) — seeking [$3.7 billion and sweeping platform redesign mandates](https://www.claimsjournal.com/news/national/2026/05/05/337354.htm), including algorithmic overhauls, elimination of infinite scroll, and mandatory guardian-linked accounts for minors — is in final arguments before a judge who has [already warned prosecutors he won't "overreach."](https://sourcenm.com/2026/05/04/judge-warns-new-mexico-prosecutors-he-wont-overreach-as-bench-trial-against-meta-begins/) Congressional intervention now would disrupt active judicial proceedings that are delivering exactly the accountability platform critics have demanded. If courts ultimately hold that Section 230 *does* shield this conduct, that would be a genuine signal worth examining. We are nowhere near that determination.

## Carve-Outs Always Metastasize

Congress has been here before, and the lesson is consistent: every Section 230 amendment sold as surgical expands far beyond its stated target. SESTA/FOSTA — passed in 2018 to combat online sex trafficking — provides the template. The law's passage triggered an immediate chilling effect, as platforms shuttered entire categories of content before it was even signed. The documented downstream harms were extensive: reduced income, increased violence, loss of the community networks and safety-screening tools that sex workers relied on to vet dangerous clients. [The Hacking//Hustling "Erased" report](https://hackinghustling.org/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/HackingHustling-Erased.pdf) captured the casualties in affected communities' own words. The law did not measurably reduce trafficking; it did measurably harm the people it nominally protected. [The Electronic Frontier Foundation has continued challenging the law](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2022/09/fight-overturn-fosta-unconstitutional-internet-censorship-law-continues) as unconstitutional precisely because its scope was never actually narrow.

Child safety framing is politically irresistible. That is precisely why the history of Section 230 carve-outs demands skepticism, not goodwill. The EARN IT Act — pitched as a focused intervention against child sexual abuse material (CSAM) — [expanded in markup to threaten end-to-end encryption](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2023/04/earn-it-bill-back-again-seeking-scan-our-messages-and-photos) and impose government-approved scanning obligations across the internet. The pattern is not a bug. It is the nature of open-ended liability regimes.

## Age Verification Is a Surveillance System

Several reform proposals circulating ahead of Wednesday's hearing would condition platform access for minors on age verification — requiring platforms to collect government-ID-quality documentation from users at scale. The [EFF has catalogued the dangers in detail](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/12/10-not-so-hidden-dangers-age-verification): at its core, age verification is a surveillance architecture that connects a person's online activity to their legal identity. The risks are not hypothetical. A third-party age verification vendor handling ID checks for Discord in the United Kingdom [was hacked in late 2025, with the government ID photos of 70,000 users potentially exposed](https://www.techradar.com/vpn/vpn-privacy-security/age-verification-changed-the-internet-in-2025-heres-what-it-means-for-your-privacy-in-2026). [Critics across the political spectrum](https://www.marketplace.org/story/2026/02/26/age-verification-rules-on-social-media-spark-privacy-concerns) have warned that mandating such systems at scale means one breach away from exposing the browsing history of hundreds of millions of Americans — tied to their legal identities.

The cure — a national biometric infrastructure tethered to social media access — is a privacy catastrophe that dwarfs the harms it purports to address. Adults lose anonymity. Marginalized communities lose safe spaces. And the government acquires, through private-sector mandate, surveillance capacity that would be unconstitutional if asserted directly.

## The Courts Are Already Doing the Work

This is the point that gets lost in the hearing-room rush to legislate: the accountability system Congress built is functioning. [More than 42 state attorneys general](https://ag.ny.gov/press-release/2023/attorney-general-james-and-multistate-coalition-sue-meta-harming-youth) have filed coordinated suits against Meta. [Massachusetts's case survived a motion to dismiss in April 2026.](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/10/meta-must-face-youth-addiction-lawsuit-by-massachusetts-court-rules.html) New Mexico has extracted $375 million in a jury verdict and is now actively pursuing injunctive relief that would restructure how Meta operates for minors in the state. The California verdict will reshape product design decisions across the industry for years. [More than 2,000 additional cases are pending](https://fortune.com/2026/03/26/meta-youtube-lawsuit-verdict-what-happens-next/). Platform behavior is already changing under litigation pressure — without a single amendment to Section 230.

## If Congress Must Act: Surgical Precision, Not a Sledgehammer

If Congress cannot resist the impulse to legislate, there is a path that avoids the worst damage: a narrow clarification that Section 230's immunity does not extend to a platform's *own* knowing and deceptive conduct toward minors — as distinct from liability for third-party content users post. That is precisely the distinction the verdicts turned on. It would codify what courts are already holding, without expanding liability to cover algorithmic curation of user speech, without imposing platform design mandates by congressional fiat, and without building the surveillance infrastructure that age verification mandates require.

Broad platform design liability — the approach embodied in the Kids Online Safety Act and similar proposals before this Congress — does not thread this needle. It obliterates it — producing the kind of regulatory overreach that chills speech, crushes smaller platforms that cannot absorb compliance costs, and ultimately benefits the large incumbents the proposals purport to discipline.

---

The jury that found Meta liable in March did not need Congress's help. Neither did the New Mexico jury that ordered $375 million in civil penalties. What they needed was a plaintiff willing to litigate, state laws robust enough to reach the conduct, and a Section 230 properly understood — as a shield for third-party content, not a license for platforms to engineer addiction and conceal it from regulators and parents. That is what they got. Wednesday's hearing should not become the occasion to dismantle the statute whose proper operation just delivered the accountability everyone claims to want.
