Weeks from now, the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency is expected to finalize the most sweeping federal cybersecurity mandate in American history. The [Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act](https://www.cisa.gov/topics/cyber-threats-and-advisories/information-sharing/cyber-incident-reporting-critical-infrastructure-act-2022-circia) (CIRCIA) final rule will require covered organizations to notify CISA within 72 hours of discovering a cyber incident and within 24 hours of making a ransomware payment. The clock starts when an entity "reasonably believes" an incident has occurred — not when its investigation confirms it.

The goal is sound: give the federal government real-time visibility into cyber threats targeting the nation's most essential systems. The implementation is not. CISA's [proposed rule](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/04/04/2024-06526/cyber-incident-reporting-for-critical-infrastructure-act-circia-reporting-requirements), published in April 2024, would apply to an estimated [316,000 entities](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/cybersecurity/2024/03/cisas-cyber-incident-reporting-rules-will-apply-to-316k-entities/) across all 16 critical infrastructure sectors — a number that dwarfs anything Congress contemplated when it passed CIRCIA in March 2022. Industry coalitions, sector regulators, and members of Congress from both parties have raised alarms. CISA scheduled [town hall meetings in March 2026](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/02/13/2026-02948/cyber-incident-reporting-for-critical-infrastructure-act-circia-rulemaking-town-hall-meetings) to take last-minute feedback on scope and burden — meetings that were then [disrupted by a government funding lapse](https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/dhs-announces-virtual-town-halls-on-circia-final-rule) — and appears poised to finalize before the feedback is digested.

The final rule must be narrowed. CISA has transformed a targeted congressional directive into a sprawling compliance apparatus. Unless the agency course-corrects before the rule drops, the primary effect will be to divert finite security resources from actual defense to paperwork, punish breach victims with liability exposure, and undermine the voluntary threat-sharing infrastructure that is the real backbone of American cyber resilience.

## What Congress Actually Authorized

CIRCIA was signed into law on March 15, 2022, as part of the [Consolidated Appropriations Act (P.L. 117-103)](https://www.sidley.com/en/insights/newsupdates/2022/03/congress-passes-cyber-incident-reporting-for-critical-infrastructure-act-of-2022). The legislative context matters: it was passed in the wake of the Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack that disrupted fuel supplies across the East Coast and the SolarWinds supply-chain compromise that infiltrated federal agencies. Congress was responding to specific, high-profile attacks on true critical infrastructure — pipeline operators, federal networks, defense contractors, power grids.

The statutory mandate reflects that specificity. CIRCIA directed CISA to promulgate rules covering "covered entities" — a term the law tethered to entities operating within critical infrastructure sectors as defined by [Presidential Policy Directive 21](https://www.cisa.gov/topics/cyber-threats-and-advisories/information-sharing/cyber-incident-reporting-critical-infrastructure-act-2022-circia). The statutory text and legislative history do not contemplate a universe of 316,000 entities. As the [Congressional Research Service noted](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48025) in its analysis of the NPRM, the proposed rule applies to significantly more entities than a close reading of the statute would suggest, using expansive definitional choices to extend coverage far beyond true critical infrastructure operators.

Congress did not include an explicit statement of purpose in the statute — a drafting gap CISA has exploited. But the gap does not confer open-ended regulatory authority. The targets of the law were entities whose compromise would threaten national security, economic security, or public health and safety: pipeline operators, electric utilities, water systems, financial market infrastructure, and defense contractors. A mid-sized commercial real estate firm or a regional food distributor that clears the SBA's small-business size threshold is not what the post-SolarWinds congressional majority had in mind.

## The Scope Explosion: 316,000 Entities and Counting

CISA arrived at its 316,000-entity estimate by combining two coverage pathways: entities that meet sector-specific criteria, and entities that simply exceed the [Small Business Administration's small-business size standards](https://www.venable.com/insights/publications/2024/04/circia-cyber-incident-reporting-for-practically). That second pathway is the problem. SBA size standards — measured in revenue or employee count, varying by industry code — were designed to determine eligibility for small-business procurement preferences. They were not designed to define which organizations operate critical infrastructure.

The result is definitional overreach wearing the costume of statutory interpretation. Under CISA's proposed approach, an entity that generates above the SBA revenue threshold for its sector can be swept into CIRCIA's mandatory reporting regime regardless of whether it operates anything remotely resembling critical infrastructure. This is not what Congress authorized — and [bipartisan pushback from the House Homeland Security Committee](https://homeland.house.gov/2024/05/01/implementation-of-circia-is-more-important-than-ever-chairmen-green-garbarino-deliver-opening-statements/) confirms it. Subcommittee Chair Andrew Garbarino (R-NY) stated plainly that "the scope of the proposed CIRCIA rule went far beyond congressional intent," while Ranking Member Eric Swalwell (D-CA) expressed frustration that CISA failed to adequately incorporate private-sector feedback. When an agency's definitional choices draw criticism from the oversight committee's majority and minority simultaneously, the definition is wrong.

The scale problem compounds at CISA's intake end. The agency is not resourced to process intelligence from 316,000 reporting entities. Signal-to-noise ratio collapses at that volume. A narrower, well-defined covered-entity set produces actionable threat intelligence; a mass-filing regime produces an inbox no analyst corps can meaningfully clear. CISA's own threat analysis mission is undermined by a rule that floods the agency with low-quality filings from entities that were never the intended targets.

## The Duplication Trap

CIRCIA was sold to Congress and industry alike as a harmonization vehicle — a way to rationalize the fragmented patchwork of sector-specific cyber reporting obligations into a coherent federal regime. The proposed rule delivers the opposite.

Critical infrastructure operators already carry substantial mandatory reporting obligations. Public companies face [SEC cyber incident disclosure requirements](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2023-139) that took effect in December 2023, requiring Form 8-K disclosure of material cyber incidents within four business days of a materiality determination. Energy sector operators comply with NERC CIP reliability standards that include mandatory cyber incident reporting to the Electricity Information Sharing and Analysis Center (E-ISAC) and the Department of Energy. Financial institutions report to the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency and the Federal Reserve under interagency cyber incident notification requirements finalized in 2021. Healthcare entities face HIPAA breach notification obligations to HHS with their own timelines and definitions. State attorneys general across dozens of jurisdictions have enacted breach notification laws with their own triggering events and windows.

CIRCIA was supposed to streamline this landscape. Instead, [as legal analysts have documented](https://www.governmentcontractslaw.com/2024/05/cisas-circia-proposed-rule-another-player-enters-the-reporting-regime/), the proposed rule creates a new standalone federal reporting layer that competes with every existing regime rather than consolidating them. The definitions of "covered cyber incident" do not align with SEC materiality standards. The 72-hour CIRCIA window differs from the four-business-day SEC window. The covered-entity definitions do not map onto sector-regulator jurisdictional boundaries.

The result for general counsel at a covered entity is paralysis: which definition governs? Which window controls? Does a single incident trigger simultaneous obligations to CISA, the SEC, OCC, and three state AGs — with inconsistent deadlines, inconsistent definitions, and potentially inconsistent investigative findings? [Congressional oversight staff and industry coalitions](https://www.dwt.com/blogs/privacy--security-law-blog/2025/03/cisa-sec-cyber-incident-reporting-rules-congress) have repeatedly flagged this problem. CISA's own March 2026 town hall agenda acknowledged "harmonizing CIRCIA reporting requirements with existing federal and state requirements" as one of three core areas needing resolution — an implicit concession that the proposed rule failed its central purpose.

## Why 72-Hour Windows Produce Bad Intelligence

The 72-hour reporting requirement sounds reasonable in theory. In practice, it produces the opposite of useful threat intelligence.

The clock begins when an entity "reasonably believes" a covered incident has occurred. In the fog of an active attack — when security teams are simultaneously triaging affected systems, isolating compromised credentials, and assessing whether data has been exfiltrated — the scope, origin, and severity of an incident are typically unknown. Attribution is uncertain. The blast radius is unconfirmed. Whether the incident meets CIRCIA's definition of "covered" may itself be contested.

Mandatory reporting before incident scope is understood does not produce accurate intelligence. It produces inaccurate, legally defensive filings. Organizations facing simultaneous reporting obligations and potential regulatory penalties for under-disclosure will file early, file broadly, and file conservatively — not to help CISA understand the threat landscape, but to create a legal record demonstrating timely compliance. General counsel will review and shape every filing. Security teams will spend incident-response hours on paperwork rather than containment.

The outcome is a CISA inbox flooded with filings that are overbroad, potentially inaccurate, and shaped by litigation strategy rather than operational intelligence. Speed without accuracy enables misattribution, wasted response resources, and potentially harmful government intervention based on preliminary, incomplete data. Investigations typically revise initial assessments significantly in the weeks following an incident — meaning the intelligence CISA receives first is the least reliable it will ever get.

The 24-hour ransomware payment requirement compounds the problem. Ransom negotiations are sensitive, rapidly evolving, and often involve active law enforcement coordination. A hard 24-hour window for payment disclosure — before the organization understands whether law enforcement has advised delay, whether decryption keys will be delivered, or whether the payment resolves or escalates the situation — again prioritizes compliance theater over genuine threat visibility.

## How Mandatory Reporting Undermines Voluntary Sharing

The voluntary threat-sharing architecture that actually underpins American cyber defense took decades to build and depends on trust. The 16 critical infrastructure Information Sharing and Analysis Centers (ISACs) — including the Financial Services ISAC, the Electricity ISAC, and the Health ISAC — aggregate and distribute threat intelligence among sector operators in real time, outside formal legal reporting frameworks, with informal norms protecting contributors. CISA's own Cybersecurity Collaboration Center has built a similar voluntary intelligence-sharing relationship with defense industrial base operators. These arrangements work precisely because participation is voluntary, disclosures are non-adversarial, and contributors face no legal jeopardy for sharing incomplete or evolving information.

A punitive mandatory reporting framework with ambiguous scope, compressed timelines, and no robust liability protection inverts these incentives. Under the proposed CIRCIA regime, the legally rational choice for a covered entity is to share less voluntarily — because voluntary disclosures made outside the mandatory framework may be inconsistent with mandatory filings, creating regulatory exposure. ISACs have already [raised concerns](https://www.ibm.com/think/insights/circia-feedback-update-critical-infrastructure-providers-weigh-in-nprm) that CIRCIA's design, if not corrected, will chill the informal sharing that currently makes the voluntary system valuable. When mandatory reporting becomes the legally safest channel, voluntary sharing becomes the legally risky one. That is backwards.

The history of cybersecurity information sharing is instructive. The [Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act of 2015](https://www.congress.gov/bill/114th-congress/senate-bill/754) provided liability protection for voluntary threat-indicator sharing — and voluntary participation in information-sharing programs expanded materially as a result. The lesson is straightforward: incentives drive behavior. CIRCIA as proposed provides no comparable protection for good-faith incident reports, meaning the incentive structure runs precisely the wrong direction.

## The Right Model

None of this is an argument against cyber incident reporting. The goal — giving the federal government timely visibility into threats to critical infrastructure — is legitimate and important. The argument is about scope, design, and incentives.

The final rule should narrow the definition of covered entities to true critical infrastructure operators: entities whose compromise would have national-security-level consequences. That means sector-specific criteria based on operational criticality — not SBA size standards imported from procurement policy. CISA's own sector risk management agencies, which have existing relationships with and regulatory authority over true critical infrastructure operators, should anchor the covered-entity definitions.

The final rule should harmonize rather than duplicate. CISA should function as a downstream aggregator of intelligence from sector-specific reporting regimes — receiving and synthesizing reports that covered entities already make to the SEC, NERC, OCC, HHS, and other sector regulators — rather than establishing a parallel disclosure regime with inconsistent definitions and competing timelines. Where sector-specific frameworks already exist, CIRCIA reporting obligations should be satisfied by compliance with those frameworks plus a forwarding copy to CISA.

Reporting timelines should reflect operational reality. A 72-hour initial notification — from "reasonable belief" that an incident occurred — should require only basic, confirmed information: that an incident appears to have occurred, affected systems, and available severity indicators. Detailed supplemental reports, informed by actual investigation, should follow within 30 days. This mirrors the approach taken by thoughtful sector regulators and produces better intelligence than a compressed deadline that forces speculation.

Finally, the final rule must include robust liability protection for good-faith incident reports. Without explicit protection, organizations face legal jeopardy for every disclosure — chilling precisely the honest, complete reporting that makes CIRCIA valuable.

## Policy Recommendations

CISA and Congress should act on five specific points before and after the final rule drops:

1. **Narrow covered entities to operational criticality, not SBA size.** Eliminate the SBA size-standard pathway and replace it with sector-specific criteria developed in coordination with each sector's risk management agency. True critical infrastructure operators — those whose compromise would threaten national security, economic security, or public health — are the statute's intended targets.

2. **Harmonize with existing sector frameworks.** Compliance with a sector-specific reporting regime (NERC CIP, SEC cyber disclosure, OCC cyber notification, HIPAA breach notification) should satisfy CIRCIA's parallel obligation. CISA should be a recipient and synthesizer of intelligence, not a competing mandatory disclosure principal.

3. **Reform the reporting timeline.** Replace the 72-hour full-incident report with a 72-hour notice of incident and 30-day confirmed report. The initial notice should require only information known at the time; detailed analysis follows investigation. The 24-hour ransomware payment window should be extended to 72 hours and explicitly coordinated with law enforcement notification protocols.

4. **Establish robust liability protection for good-faith disclosures.** Congress should legislate explicit safe harbors for incident reports made in good faith under CIRCIA, analogous to the liability protections provided by the Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act of 2015 for voluntary threat-indicator sharing. Without comparable protection, the filing incentives run against quality and candor.

5. **Resource CISA as an analyst, not a regulator.** CISA's comparative advantage is technical analysis and threat correlation, not compliance enforcement. The final rule should reflect this by directing CISA to publish aggregated threat intelligence derived from CIRCIA reports — giving covered entities something of value in return for their compliance costs — rather than treating the agency primarily as an enforcement body.

## Conclusion

CIRCIA's core purpose is sound. The federal government needs real-time visibility into threats against the systems that underpin American economic and national security. The 2022 statute, passed with genuine bipartisan urgency in the aftermath of Colonial Pipeline and SolarWinds, authorized a targeted, sensible reporting regime to serve that purpose.

What CISA has proposed is something different. By stretching the covered-entity definition to 316,000 organizations, layering a new federal regime on top of existing sector-specific frameworks, compressing reporting timelines past the point where accurate intelligence is possible, and providing no liability protection for good-faith disclosures, the agency has designed a rule that will divert security resources from defense to paperwork, punish breach victims, and chill the voluntary threat-sharing infrastructure that is the real backbone of US cyber resilience.

Effective cybersecurity requires trust, speed, and good intelligence. The CIRCIA proposed rule undermines all three. CISA has weeks to get this right.
