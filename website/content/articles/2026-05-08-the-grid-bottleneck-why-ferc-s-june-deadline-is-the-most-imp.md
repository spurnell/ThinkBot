Washington's tech policy community is fighting a war on the wrong front. Congressional staff are tracking preemption clauses in state AI bills. Lobbyists are mapping votes on foundation model disclosure mandates. Academics are debating liability thresholds for autonomous systems. Meanwhile, the actual binding constraint on American AI preeminence — the one that will determine where compute clusters get built and at what pace — is being resolved right now by commissioners at the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC), on a deadline almost no one in the tech policy world has circled on their calendar.

FERC has committed to act on [Docket No. RM26-4-000](https://www.ferc.gov/rm26-4) — its large-load interconnection rulemaking — by the end of June 2026. That deadline, driven by a [formal directive from Secretary of Energy Chris Wright](https://www.gibsondunn.com/secretary-of-energy-directs-ferc-to-initiate-rulemaking-to-expedite-data-center-and-large-load-interconnection/), is not a speech or a study. It is a regulatory milestone that will set the terms under which data centers — the physical infrastructure of the AI race — connect to the American power grid for the next decade. Congress has no bill with comparable near-term probability of becoming law. No AI governance proposal in either chamber comes close to this level of consequence for AI infrastructure deployment. FERC's June ruling is the most important AI policy moment of 2026, and it is happening almost entirely outside the tech policy conversation.

## The Grid Ceiling Is the Binding Constraint

American AI leadership has a compute dimension, a talent dimension, and an energy dimension. Policy debates have focused almost exclusively on the first two. That is a mistake.

The [International Energy Agency's latest projections](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/energy-demand-from-ai) are unambiguous: global data center electricity consumption will nearly double — from 485 TWh in 2025 to approximately 945 TWh by 2030. The United States alone accounts for an increase of roughly 240 TWh, a 130 percent surge over 2024 levels. AI-focused data centers are the primary driver: their electricity demand surged 50 percent in 2025 alone and is growing at roughly 30 percent annually — more than four times the rate of total U.S. electricity consumption.

This is not a soft forecast. It is the rate at which hyperscalers are already announcing buildouts. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta have collectively committed hundreds of billions of dollars in capital expenditure to AI infrastructure over the next several years. The question is not whether these facilities will demand enormous amounts of power — they already do. The question is whether the American transmission grid can physically absorb that load in time to keep the buildout on U.S. soil rather than losing it to jurisdictions with faster permitting, cheaper power, or less congested queues.

Right now, the answer is: not at the current pace.

## The Queue Crisis in Numbers

The Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory's annual [*Queued Up* report](https://emp.lbl.gov/publications/queued-2025-edition-characteristics) — the authoritative dataset on grid interconnection backlogs — is damning. As of the end of 2024, approximately 10,300 projects were actively seeking grid interconnection in the United States, representing roughly 1,400 GW of generation capacity and another 890 GW of storage. The median time from interconnection request to commercial operation has more than doubled — from less than two years for projects built in the early 2000s to nearly five years for projects built in 2023. These are not preliminary study timelines. This is the full clock, from first filing to power flowing.

For AI infrastructure development, a five-year interconnection timeline is prohibitive. A data center that breaks ground today expecting to be operational in 2027 cannot absorb a queue-driven slip to 2030 or beyond. Power infrastructure is not fungible on the margin: a hyperscaler cannot simply reroute a campus to a different substation the way a logistics company reroutes a truck. The queue is the bottleneck, and the bottleneck is binding.

Transmission permitting compounds the problem. New transmission lines — the infrastructure that moves power from generation to load — face permitting timelines of seven to ten years under current law and practice. The interconnection queue and transmission permitting timelines are not independent constraints. They interact: a project that clears its interconnection study may still face years of delay waiting for the transmission upgrades that study identified as necessary. [DOE's own Transmission Interconnection Roadmap](https://www.energy.gov/sites/default/files/2024-04/i2X%20Transmission%20Interconnection%20Roadmap.pdf) acknowledges this compounding dynamic explicitly.

The AI compute race has a grid ceiling. The United States is approaching it.

## Washington's False Binary

Congressional responses to the data center power surge have split into two sharply opposed frameworks. Neither one actually solves the problem.

Senator Tom Cotton introduced the [Decentralized Access to Technology Alternatives (DATA) Act](https://www.utilitydive.com/news/senate-bill-exempts-fully-isolated-large-loads-from-ferc-doe-regulation/809330/) in January 2026. The bill would create a new utility category — "consumer-regulated electric utilities," or CREUs — for companies that build entirely off-grid power infrastructure to serve their own load. Qualifying facilities would be completely disconnected from the transmission system and exempt from FERC jurisdiction under the Federal Power Act, including rate regulation, reliability standards, interconnection rules, and transmission planning requirements.

Cotton's framing is politically appealing: "American dominance in AI and other crucial emerging industries should not come at the expense of Arkansans paying higher energy costs." The bill is superficially consistent with a pro-market worldview — let developers build their own infrastructure, get out of their way, problem solved.

But the DATA Act is not a market solution. It is a cost externalization bill dressed up as deregulation.

A fully off-grid data center that builds its own generation and never connects to the transmission system does not pay for the transmission infrastructure it does not use — and captures none of the scale economies that make shared grid infrastructure efficient. More importantly, it reduces the pool of load-serving customers who fund the grid through their rates, increasing per-unit costs for everyone who remains on the system. Stranded transmission assets, reduced scale economies, and degraded grid resilience are not priced into the developer's buildout decision. They are negative externalities distributed across ordinary ratepayers and future grid users. The DATA Act does not eliminate the costs of AI infrastructure's power demand; it reroutes them.

There is also a long-run reliability problem. A grid that progressively loses its largest load customers to off-grid islands becomes a thinner, less stable system. The more hyperscalers opt out, the weaker the economic and physical foundation of the shared grid becomes — making it harder to justify the transmission investments that the remaining system needs. This is a race-to-the-bottom dynamic, not a market equilibrium.

Representative Paul Tonko's [Power for the People Act](https://tonko.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=4569), introduced in April 2026, is directionally sounder on cost allocation. The bill would direct FERC to require data centers to pay the full cost of transmission upgrades they require — a correct intuition about who should bear the marginal costs of new load. It would also create a data center load queue and require data centers to prioritize projects that bring their own generation capacity.

The problem is execution. The Power for the People Act would add substantial procedural layers to the interconnection process — new review requirements, new queue management rules, additional FERC rulemaking mandates — that would slow the process rather than accelerate it. Ratepayer protection is a genuine policy goal, and full cost internalization by data centers is the right mechanism. But mandating more reviews of an already choked queue does not protect ratepayers; it delays projects while existing grid upgrade costs continue to mount. A prescription that slows the AI infrastructure buildout without meaningfully containing cost spillover onto ratepayers is not a ratepayer protection bill — it is a project-delay bill.

Neither bill addresses the root cause: the interconnection queue itself.

## The Lever That Actually Matters

While Congress debates frameworks, FERC is moving. The commission's large-load docket is not theoretical. It has already produced concrete action.

In December 2025, [FERC ordered PJM](https://www.klgates.com/FERC-Orders-PJM-to-Reform-Tariff-for-Co-Located-Generation-and-Load-1-15-2026) — the nation's largest grid operator, serving 65 million people across 13 states and the District of Columbia — to establish clear, nondiscriminatory co-location rules for data centers and generation facilities. The order created three distinct transmission service categories for co-located loads, providing a workable template for how large loads can queue, receive firm interconnection rights, and operate alongside generation resources without bypassing grid reliability requirements.

In January 2026, [FERC approved the Southwest Power Pool's High Impact Large Load initiative](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-act-large-load-interconnection-docket-june-2026) — the HILL framework — establishing new protocols to accelerate large-load interconnection across SPP's 17-state footprint while protecting existing customers.

On April 16, 2026, [FERC announced](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-act-large-load-interconnection-docket-june-2026) that it will complete the broader Docket No. RM26-4-000 rulemaking by the end of June. The commission has the legal authority, an active proceeding, real institutional momentum from the DOE directive, and two working regional models — PJM and SPP — to draw on. This is not a long-shot regulatory aspiration. This is a proceeding that is going to conclude.

What FERC does with it will matter enormously. The right framework includes three elements.

**Transparent, rules-based co-location standards.** Co-location — siting generation capacity adjacent to large load — is already happening at scale. The question is whether the rules governing it are predictable and nondiscriminatory. Opaque, case-by-case treatment of co-location requests creates competitive inequality between large hyperscalers with legal resources to navigate bespoke proceedings and smaller data center developers who cannot. Clear, published standards — similar to what FERC directed PJM to develop — level the field and reduce study times by giving applicants a defined process to follow.

**Full cost internalization by data centers, not cross-subsidization by ratepayers.** Data centers seeking interconnection should pay the full marginal cost of the transmission upgrades their load requires. This is the correct pro-market, pro-equity position: the entity that creates the need for infrastructure investment should bear the cost. It also neutralizes the core political objection to fast-tracking large-load interconnection. FERC has existing authority to require this under the Federal Power Act; the June rulemaking is the vehicle to establish it clearly. A [voluntary Ratepayer Protection Pledge](https://www.energy.gov/articles/secretary-wright-acts-unleash-american-industry-and-innovation-newly-proposed-rules) signed by major data center developers in March 2026 commits companies to covering new generation costs — FERC rules should cement what pledges only promise.

**Expedited study timelines for defined large-load pathways.** The five-year median queue wait is partly a function of serial rather than parallel study processes. Clustering studies, standardized environmental reviews, and dedicated processing tracks for large loads above a defined threshold can materially reduce time-in-queue without compromising the technical rigor that grid reliability requires.

## The State Patchwork Problem

FERC is not acting in a vacuum. It is acting in a policy environment where more than 300 data-center-related bills have been introduced in 30 states in the first six weeks of 2026 alone. Legislative activity has run well ahead of any coherent federal framework.

California has enacted [Senate Bill 57](https://sd18.senate.ca.gov/news/legislation-protect-california-ratepayers-paying-data-centers-energy-costs-signed-law), directing its Public Utilities Commission to study data center energy impacts on ratepayers. The legislature is also advancing [SB 886](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260SB886), which would create a tariff regime requiring data centers with peak demands of at least 25 MW to pay for transmission and distribution infrastructure. Ohio and Utah have enacted their own data center energy cost mandates. The [MultiState tracker](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/4/14/federal-ai-data-center-policy-meets-resistance-from-state-lawmakers) documents a decisive pivot in state legislatures from incentive-focused data center policy — tax abatements, permitting streamlining — toward cost-assignment mandates, often without a coordinating federal standard to anchor them.

This is the infrastructure-layer equivalent of the state AI regulatory fragmentation already visible across other tech policy domains. Fifty different cost-allocation frameworks, fifty different interconnection review processes, and fifty different definitions of "large load" will not produce a coherent national AI infrastructure policy. They will produce a patchwork that advantages states with permissive rules, disadvantages states with protective ones, and creates forum-shopping rather than sound investment.

The solution is not to preempt state siting authority — states have legitimate interests in how and where large facilities are permitted within their borders. The federal-state distinction that matters here is between cost allocation (which is FERC's domain under the Federal Power Act) and land-use siting (which is properly a state function). FERC has the authority and the obligation to set uniform cost allocation standards that apply across its jurisdictional transmission systems. Clear federal rules on who pays for what will do more to prevent ratepayer harm than 50 state experiments — and will do so without blocking states from exercising their genuine siting authorities.

## The Pro-Innovation Prescription

The argument for FERC acting boldly in June is not an argument for subordinating grid reliability or ratepayer interests to the preferences of large technology companies. It is an argument for rules-based regulatory clarity that channels private investment toward outcomes that are good for the grid and the economy simultaneously.

American AI leadership is as much an energy infrastructure problem as it is a chips or model problem. The U.S. cannot build the AI infrastructure needed to sustain technological preeminence if the grid cannot absorb new load in a reasonable timeframe. The CHIPS and Science Act mobilized federal capital to rebuild domestic semiconductor manufacturing capacity. Broadband programs created priority pathways for digital connectivity. A clear, transparent federal framework for large-load interconnection — with full cost internalization by data centers, nondiscriminatory co-location standards, and expedited study timelines — is the grid-layer equivalent.

FERC's June 2026 rulemaking is a rare instance where the policy lever that matters most is also the one closest to being pulled. The tech policy community should stop fixating on congressional bills that have no path to enactment and start engaging with the proceeding that will actually shape where AI infrastructure gets built for the next decade.

The deadline is the end of June. The docket is open. The window for shaping it is closing.
