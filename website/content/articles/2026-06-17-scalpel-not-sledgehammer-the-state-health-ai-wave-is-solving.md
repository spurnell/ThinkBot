On July 1 — two weeks from now — Indiana's [HB 1271](https://legiscan.com/IN/bill/HB1271/2026) takes effect. The law prohibits health insurers from using artificial intelligence as the sole basis for downcoding a claim without a healthcare professional's independent review of the patient's medical record. It sounds modest, sensible, and protective of patients. It probably is. It is also, at this point, largely redundant — and as one of [nearly 240 health AI bills introduced across 43 states](https://www.manatt.com/insights/newsletters/health-highlights/manatt-health-health-ai-policy-tracker) this year, it represents exactly the kind of well-intentioned, uncoordinated policy proliferation that produces compliance labyrinths without safety gains.

The state legislators writing these bills are working from a reasonable premise: AI is increasingly embedded in clinical and coverage decisions, patients deserve to know when it is used, and no machine should be the last word on a medical outcome. But the FDA's [revised Clinical Decision Support Software Guidance](https://www.fda.gov/regulatory-information/search-fda-guidance-documents/clinical-decision-support-software), issued January 6, 2026, operationalizes precisely those concerns — with a risk-proportionate framework that distinguishes transparent, clinician-reviewable tools from opaque, judgment-substituting systems. The federal architecture is in place. What the state wave is building on top of it is not a safety net. It is friction.

## What the FDA Actually Built

The January 2026 guidance is worth examining carefully, because the public discourse around it has been distorted in both directions. Some coverage framed it as ["FDA cuts red tape"](https://www.arnoldporter.com/en/perspectives/advisories/2026/01/fda-cuts-red-tape-on-clinical-decision-support-software) — technically accurate but misleading as a characterization of its logic. Other coverage treated it as a green light for black-box clinical AI. Neither is right.

The guidance is best understood as a *transparency accountability* framework. Under the [21st Century Cures Act](https://www.fda.gov/patients/learn-about-drug-and-device-approvals/21st-century-cures-act), Congress established that certain clinical decision support (CDS) software does not constitute a medical device subject to FDA premarket review — but only when the software meets a four-part test. The January 2026 revision expanded enforcement discretion by clarifying when AI-enabled CDS, including [certain generative AI features](https://www.cov.com/news-and-insights/insights/2026/01/5-key-takeaways-from-fdas-revised-clinical-decision-support-cds-software-guidance), qualifies as non-device software.

The operative criterion is clinician reviewability. If a CDS tool provides a single, clinically appropriate recommendation *and* enables the treating clinician to independently review the underlying data, logic, and clinical guidelines behind that recommendation — what [Covington & Burling's analysis](https://www.cov.com/news-and-insights/insights/2026/01/5-key-takeaways-from-fdas-revised-clinical-decision-support-cds-software-guidance) calls the "glass box" requirement — FDA will exercise enforcement discretion. The software is effectively self-explaining, and the clinician retains the decision authority.

What stays in scope is equally important. [As Ropes & Gray summarized](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/01/fda-adapts-with-the-times-on-digital-health-updated-guidances-on-general-wellness-products), FDA retains full device authority over opaque models where the reasoning cannot be independently verified, time-critical decision tools (including those used in emergency settings), and software that substitutes for rather than informs clinical judgment. Radiology AI that generates diagnostic recommendations from medical images remains subject to FDA device oversight regardless of the guidance expansion.

This is not deregulation. It is *calibration*. The framework differentiates between the AI tool that flags a potential drug interaction for a physician to review — transparent, supplementary, the physician is in the loop — and the AI system that outputs a diagnosis recommendation from a black-box model that no clinician can interrogate. The former gets more room. The latter does not.

## The Patchwork Is the Problem

Against this backdrop, [240 health AI bills have been introduced across 43 states](https://www.manatt.com/insights/newsletters/health-highlights/manatt-health-health-ai-policy-tracker) in 2026 alone — nearly as many as were introduced in all of 2025. The most common structure mandates clinician review before an AI recommendation can inform a clinical decision, requires disclosure when AI is used in coverage or care determinations, and prohibits AI from serving as the "sole basis" for adverse outcomes.

On paper, this resembles the FDA framework. In practice, these bills are not interoperable with each other, with the FDA standard, or with existing state insurance law.

Indiana's HB 1271 focuses on insurance claim adjudication — specifically, it bars AI from driving claim downcodes without human review of the medical record. Utah's [SB 319](https://utahafp.org/utah-sb-319-health-insurance-prior-authorization-amendments-implications-for-family-physicians/) requires insurers to disclose to the state Insurance Department, to providers, and to enrollees whether AI is used in prior authorization review — it takes effect January 1, 2027. Texas's [TRAIGA Act](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2025/06/texas-enacts-comprehensive-ai-governance-laws), in effect since January 1, 2026, requires licensed healthcare practitioners to provide patients with written disclosure of AI use in diagnosis or treatment before interaction and prohibits AI discrimination on the basis of protected characteristics. Colorado, after a bruising back-and-forth, [repealed and substantially rewrote its AI Act in May 2026](https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/colorado-hits-reset-on-ai-regulation-sb-26-189-repeals-and-reenacts-the-colorado-ai-act), shifting from a broad duty-of-care framework to a narrower "automated decision-making technology" regime effective January 1, 2027.

None of these regimes are harmonized with each other. The disclosure trigger in Texas is clinical interaction. The disclosure target in Utah is the insurance department. The reviewability standard in the FDA guidance is about the clinician's ability to understand the model's reasoning. Indiana addresses downcoding specifically but says nothing about prior authorization or diagnostic support. A hospital system operating across Texas, Indiana, Utah, and Colorado — plus the dozens of other states with pending legislation — must now map each of these frameworks independently, determine how they interact with FDA device determinations, and build separate compliance workflows for each jurisdiction.

[Holland & Knight's May 2026 survey](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/05/states-continue-efforts-to-regulate-ai-in-healthcare) of health AI legislation enacted in 2026 confirms what practitioners already know: "healthcare payers and providers operating across multiple states now face a maze of conflicting requirements around transparency, bias testing, and human oversight." Ropes & Gray's [Health AI Atlas](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/sites/healthai-atlas-state-ai-laws-healthcare) — a dedicated fifty-state compliance map built specifically for health systems navigating this patchwork — is itself evidence of the problem. When a leading law firm must construct a specialized tool just to track the requirements of a single sector, geography has become a compliance variable. That maze has real costs — costs that do not fall equally.

## Who Actually Pays

The compliance economics of multi-state health AI regulation are not neutral with respect to who deploys AI-assisted care. A large integrated delivery network with a compliance team can absorb the cost of 43 separate jurisdictional analyses, build state-specific disclosure workflows, and maintain documentation that varies by geography. A community hospital in rural Indiana or a federally qualified health center in Utah cannot.

The practical effect of layered state compliance obligations is predictable: AI adoption is compressed into large, well-resourced institutions and withdrawn from smaller, under-resourced ones. Governance under the current regime is as much about where a system is deployed as how well it performs — a formula that turns geographic footprint into a compliance liability.

This is precisely the access equity outcome that healthcare AI advocates on both sides of the aisle say they want to prevent. AI diagnostic support tools have shown strong performance in early detection of diabetic retinopathy, sepsis risk, and radiology findings in underserved settings. If the compliance tax on deploying these tools is lower in major academic medical centers than in rural critical access hospitals, the population that misses out is the one that was supposed to benefit most.

The state bills do not cause this outcome through malice. They cause it through incoherence — each state pursuing a locally reasonable goal without coordination with neighboring states or the federal framework already in place.

## Existing Law Can Reach Demonstrated Harms

The deeper problem with the state wave is that it is solving for a causal theory that does not map cleanly onto how AI-related patient harm actually occurs or is remedied.

The implicit theory behind "no AI as sole basis" mandates is that harm flows from AI automaticity — from a machine making a consequential medical decision without human review. This is a real risk worth guarding against. But the *mechanism* for remedying that harm when it occurs is not a new categorical prohibition. It is the liability frameworks that already exist.

Medical malpractice law attaches to bad clinical outcomes regardless of whether those outcomes were produced by human judgment, algorithmic assistance, or some combination. A physician who blindly implements an AI recommendation without independent clinical review has violated the standard of care under existing negligence doctrine. A hospital that deploys an opaque, inaccurate diagnostic model and fails to disclose its limitations may face corporate liability. An insurer that uses AI to systematically deny medically necessary claims exposes itself to bad-faith insurance litigation and ERISA claims.

HIPAA's minimum necessary and safeguard requirements govern data inputs into AI clinical systems. The FDA's retained authority over opaque and time-critical tools ensures that the highest-risk AI applications still face premarket oversight. These are not theoretical backstops — they are active enforcement mechanisms that create accountability at the point of harm rather than at the point of method.

The state mandates get the causal chain backwards. Liability should attach to *outcomes* — to the patient who was harmed, the claim that was wrongly denied, the diagnosis that was missed. It should not attach categorically to the *use of a computational method*, particularly when that method is subject to federal oversight requirements that already include clinician reviewability and transparency of underlying logic.

## What Congress Should Not Do

The failure of federal AI preemption in 2025 has left the patchwork intact and accelerating. [The Senate voted 99-1 to strip AI preemption provisions from the One Big Beautiful Bill](https://time.com/7299044/senators-reject-10-year-ban-on-state-level-ai-regulation-in-blow-to-big-tech/), and a second attempt in the FY2026 NDAA also failed. The [White House's March 2026 national AI legislative framework](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/03/the-white-house-legislative-recommendations-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence-an) called for Congress to preempt unduly burdensome state AI laws, but no legislation has followed. A [leading bipartisan House discussion draft](https://broadbandbreakfast.com/ai-preemption-battle-lands-in-congress-with-substantive-discussion-draft/) would freeze state laws for three years — a third attempt at preemption through a different vehicle.

None of these federal approaches are right for healthcare AI specifically — and the reason is that healthcare AI is not a horizontal problem requiring horizontal solutions.

The FDA CDS framework, CMS coverage determinations, state insurance regulatory authority, and HIPAA together constitute a sectoral architecture for governing AI in healthcare. The right federal action is not a new AI-in-healthcare statute layered on top of all of it. The right action is HHS clarification of the jurisdictional boundary between FDA's CDS framework and state insurance regulators — specifically, which AI coverage-determination tools are subject to FDA device oversight versus state insurance law, and how the federal transparency standard interacts with state disclosure mandates.

That clarification would not require new legislation. It would require an HHS interagency guidance document — ideally coordinated between FDA's digital health center and CMS — that maps the existing framework against the most common state legislative structures. Insurers and hospital systems would then have a coherent compliance baseline. State legislators pursuing genuinely novel concerns — AI bias in coverage decisions, for example — would have a clear floor to build from rather than a blank slate.

Broad federal preemption of state health AI law is not the answer; neither is congressional silence. The middle path is the one agencies actually have authority to take: use existing law, clarify the boundaries, and let the FDA's transparency-anchored framework do the work it was designed to do.

## The July 1 Lesson

Indiana's HB 1271 will not harm patients. Its prohibition on AI-only claim downcoding is consistent with sound clinical governance and with what the FDA's reviewability standard already operationalizes at the federal level. The problem is not the bill in isolation. It is HB 1271 as one entry in a wave of individually sensible, collectively incoherent legislation constructing a compliance architecture no one designed.

The state legislators passing these bills are right that AI in healthcare needs oversight. They are wrong that their state capital is the right venue for establishing it. The FDA spent years developing a framework calibrated to the actual risk profile of clinical AI tools — one that distinguishes transparency from opacity, supplemental from substitutive, safe harbor from deregulation. The appropriate policy response is to let that framework work, support HHS in clarifying its jurisdictional reach, and reserve legislative action for the genuine gaps the FDA framework cannot fill.

The scalpel is already in the room. The state legislatures are reaching for the sledgehammer.
