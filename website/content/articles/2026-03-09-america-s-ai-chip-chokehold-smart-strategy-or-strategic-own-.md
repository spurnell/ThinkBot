The Trump administration is [preparing sweeping new rules](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/us-govt-preps-sweeping-export-controls-for-nvidia-amd-ai-hardware-worldwide-licensing-system-would-give-trump-admin-broad-authority-to-block-global-sales) that would require US government approval for virtually every significant AI chip export on the planet — to allies and adversaries alike. Under the draft framework, any foreign firm seeking a large-scale AI infrastructure buildout would need a Commerce Department license, government-to-government security assurances, and commitments to invest in US AI. The rules, [first reported March 5](https://www.usnews.com/news/top-news/articles/2026-03-05/us-mulls-new-rules-for-ai-chip-exports-including-requiring-investments-by-foreign-firms-in-us), are being fast-tracked ahead of the Trump-Xi summit on March 31–April 2.

This is one of the most consequential compute governance decisions in years. It is also, in its current form, a policy that risks undermining the very US technological preeminence it purports to protect.

## The Case for Compute Leverage — and It's Real

The Trump administration is right that AI chips are strategic infrastructure. Whoever controls the compute controls the trajectory of frontier AI development — and, by extension, enormous economic and military leverage for decades to come. The US retains a meaningful lead in advanced chip design that is worth defending. A licensing framework that conditions large-scale AI infrastructure on security guarantees and investment commitments is a legitimate national security instrument.

The [proposed tiered structure](https://aidisruption.ai/p/us-drafts-global-ai-chip-export-controls) has logic to it: shipments of 1,000 GPUs or fewer receive cursory review; medium deployments require pre-clearance; massive clusters of 200,000 or more GPUs require government-to-government certification, strict security standards, and US investment commitments. The architecture is designed to give Washington gatekeeper authority over who gets to build frontier AI infrastructure — and at what price.

That ambition is sound. The Cold War's multilateral export control regime was built on the same insight: strategic technologies require strategic controls. The question is never *whether* to use such tools, but *how* — and this draft gets the "how" badly wrong.

## The Ally Problem Is Not a Minor Implementation Detail

The defining flaw in the Trump framework is its categorical departure from the Biden-era tiered approach, which [exempted close US partners](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2026/01/administration-policies-on-advanced-ai-chips-codified) — the UK, Japan, South Korea, the Netherlands, and other Tier 1 allies — from the most burdensome restrictions. The new rules reportedly abolish that exemption, expanding the licensed-destination list from roughly 40 countries to the entire world.

Treating the Netherlands — home to ASML, the sole global producer of the extreme ultraviolet lithography machines without which no advanced chip can be fabricated — as a licensing supplicant in the same bureaucratic queue as neutral states is not a show of strength. It is an act of strategic carelessness.

The alliances that underpin US chip dominance are not incidental to the story — they *are* the story. TSMC fabricates in Taiwan. ASML supplies from Eindhoven. Samsung and SK Hynix produce the HBM memory that powers every advanced AI accelerator. Treating these partners as potential adversaries requiring US supervision corrodes the coalition that makes American AI dominance structurally durable. A former national security official quoted in reporting on the draft [put it plainly](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/us-govt-preps-sweeping-export-controls-for-nvidia-amd-ai-hardware-worldwide-licensing-system-would-give-trump-admin-broad-authority-to-block-global-sales): the license requirements are "overly broad, applying globally, raising concerns that the administration intends to use the controls as negotiation leverage with allies rather than for security."

## Broad Restrictions Accelerate What They Aim to Prevent

Overbroad compute controls manufacture the adversarial competition they're trying to prevent.

Huawei is [preparing to ship roughly 600,000 Ascend 910C chips in 2026](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-29/huawei-to-double-output-of-top-ai-chip-as-nvidia-wavers-in-china) — double its 2025 volume — with a [multi-year chip roadmap](https://www.globalneighbours.org/huawei-unveils-three-year-ai-chip-roadmap-as-nvidia-faces-setbacks-in-china/) stretching to 2028. The [910C is a dual-die design combining two 910B chips](https://blog.heim.xyz/huawei-ascend-910c/) to deliver performance competitive with Nvidia's H100 in aggregate configurations. Yes, manufacturing yields remain constrained — around [30% at SMIC's 7nm node](https://www.ainvest.com/news/huawei-ai-chip-breakthrough-implications-global-semiconductor-market-2509/) — and a meaningful performance gap with Nvidia's latest persists. But Huawei has already secured [ByteDance and China Mobile as anchor customers](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/semiconductors/huaweis-ascend-ai-chip-ecosystem-scales), and it [open-sourced its CANN software stack](https://www.trendforce.com/news/2025/08/07/news-huawei-open-sources-cann-toolkit-for-ascend-ai-gpus-taking-aim-at-nvidias-cuda/) in late 2025 to accelerate ecosystem adoption.

Every market the US effectively closes to Nvidia and AMD — whether through adversarial restriction or allied collateral damage — is a market Huawei steps into. That is not hypothetical risk. It is the observed dynamic of the past three years. Each incremental Huawei sale funds the next design cycle and the incremental improvement that narrows the gap. Restricting allies' access to US chips does not contain China's AI ambitions. It subsidizes them.

The revenue lost from overly restricted markets is not an abstract commercial concern — it finances the next generation of US chip R&D. Semiconductor design at the frontier requires billions in sustained capital investment. Eroding that revenue base to score diplomatic leverage points is a compounding strategic cost, not a one-time trade-off.

## What Targeted, Conditional Controls Actually Look Like

The right framework already has a working template: the Biden-era tiered structure that the Trump administration [recently rescinded](https://www.bis.gov/press-release/department-commerce-announces-rescission-biden-era-artificial-intelligence-diffusion-rule-strengthens). Whatever its specific flaws, the underlying architecture — full access for vetted treaty allies, conditional access for strategic partners, hard caps for adversaries — was built on sound strategic logic.

An improved framework would look something like this:

**Allied-tier access:** Full, streamlined access for treaty allies meeting verified security standards — no licensing overhead, robust end-use verification, bilateral re-export controls. The diversion risk that motivates the blanket global restriction is real; it should be addressed through end-use enforcement, not by treating the UK like Belarus.

**Conditional access for strategic partners:** Countries outside the treaty-ally tier that want large-scale AI infrastructure access should face investment-linked conditions, government-to-government assurances, and security certifications. The framework as drafted includes this logic for the largest deployments — the problem is applying it universally rather than reserving it for genuinely ambiguous cases.

**Hard limits for adversaries:** Clear, durable restrictions on frontier AI compute for adversarial states, with well-defined and consistently enforced thresholds. This is where maximum coercive force belongs.

Existing frameworks — Wassenaar Arrangement commitments, bilateral Export Administration Regulations agreements, established end-use verification procedures — provide the institutional machinery to implement this without reinventing global trade administration from scratch.

## The Summit Wildcard — And Why Congress Must Act

The timing here is too pointed to ignore. Unveiling sweeping global chip controls three weeks before a Trump-Xi summit is not standard regulatory procedure. It is negotiating positioning.

That is not automatically disqualifying — bilateral summits have always involved economic leverage. But using export controls as a transactional bargaining chip rather than durable national security policy creates serious governance pathologies. Controls that can be dialed up or down based on bilateral deal-making provide no stable framework for US companies, partner governments, or the innovation ecosystem that depends on predictable access rules.

[Congress has largely ceded export control authority to the executive](https://capstonedc.com/insights/ai-2026-preview/) on AI compute. That needs to change. Durable compute governance must be anchored in statute — with clear criteria, defined review processes, and allied consultation requirements — not wielded as presidential leverage at summits. The Senate Commerce Committee and House Foreign Affairs Committee should treat this as a prompt for legislative action, not a development to observe from the sidelines.

## The Bottom Line

The US has every right — and a genuine obligation — to maintain leverage over frontier AI compute exports. The Trump administration's instinct to use that leverage aggressively is correct in the abstract: AI chips are the new strategic infrastructure, the US lead is real but eroding, and a world in which adversaries access frontier compute without restriction is worse than the alternative.

But the blunt "license everything, everywhere" framework is the wrong tool. It weaponizes commerce against allies who are structural partners in US tech dominance. It accelerates the adversarial domestic substitution it aims to prevent. And it risks being remembered less as a national security triumph than as the policy that handed Huawei the market access it needed to close the gap.

Sharpen the scalpel. Preserve the alliance tier. Enforce hard limits where they belong. And let Congress — not a bilateral summit — set the rules that govern the world's most strategic technology.
