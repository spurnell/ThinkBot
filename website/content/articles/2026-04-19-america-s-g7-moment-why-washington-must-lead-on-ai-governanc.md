Eight weeks from now, heads of state will gather in Évian, France for the [2026 G7 Summit](https://focus2030.org/en/g7-france-2026-focus-on-development-issues-understanding-the-challenges-keeping-up-with-the-latest-news/) — the most visible multilateral moment for AI governance this year. The EU AI Act is now in full application, actively shaping regulatory discourse in allied capitals and at the UN. And the United States arrives at this table with no coherent multilateral AI governance posture, having spent the past eighteen months absorbed in domestic preemption fights between Congress and the states.

That vacuum is not neutral. It is an open invitation for someone else to set the terms.

The question facing Washington isn't whether global AI governance norms will be established — they will be. The question is whether the United States leads that process or watches from the sidelines as the EU's precautionary model becomes the default template for the dozens of democracies now writing their first AI governance frameworks. The US has the credibility, the institutional tools, and — for now — the window. But that window closes at Évian.

## The Brussels Effect Is a Governance Export Problem

The EU AI Act's most consequential impact isn't what it does inside the European Union. It's what it does outside it.

The mechanism is familiar from GDPR: when a major trading partner imposes regulatory requirements touching cross-border data flows, supply chains, and technology products, firms that operate internationally often comply globally rather than maintain dual compliance architectures. Regulators in allied jurisdictions, meanwhile, face political pressure to demonstrate they're "keeping pace" — and the EU's ready-made framework is the path of least resistance.

The [global AI regulatory update for April 2026](https://www.eversheds-sutherland.com/en/global/insights/gloabl-ai-bulletin-april-2026) documents where this dynamic is and isn't playing out. Among US allies, the picture is more complicated than a simple Brussels Effect story. Japan's [Act on Promotion of Research and Development of AI-Related Technology](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/japans-first-ai-legislation-becomes-law-focus-promoting-research-and-development-no), enacted in 2025, is explicitly innovation-first — Japanese policymakers looked at the EU's approach and deliberately rejected its prescriptive logic. Canada's proposed AI legislation died when Parliament was prorogued in January 2025, and its successor government has explicitly moved toward what the responsible minister called a "light, tight, right" regulatory philosophy. The UK is developing a sector-based framework it has publicly designed to diverge from Brussels.

But these are the countries most closely aligned with the United States. The real Brussels Effect runs through everyone else.

[South Korea's AI Basic Act](https://www.trade.gov/market-intelligence/south-korea-artificial-intelligence-ai-basic-act) — signed in January 2025 and now in full effect — establishes a "high-impact AI" classification structure that closely mirrors EU risk-tier logic, making Seoul the second government after Brussels to enact comprehensive horizontal AI regulation. Across the OECD, national AI strategies increasingly reference the EU Act as a governance template. At the UN, proposed AI governance frameworks skew heavily toward precautionary framing that tracks EU priorities rather than the voluntary, risk-calibrated approach the US has championed domestically.

Most importantly: dozens of emerging economies and smaller democracies are writing AI governance frameworks for the first time right now. For those governments, the EU AI Act comes with chapters, annexes, conformity-assessment templates, and a dedicated multilateral assistance program. The United States offers voluntary frameworks with no export strategy and, increasingly, no diplomatic presence at the tables where these decisions are being made.

As the [Atlantic Council has observed](https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/new-atlanticist/the-g20-is-moving-forward-on-global-ai-governance-and-the-us-risks-being-left-out/), the G20 moved forward on AI governance at Johannesburg without the United States, which skipped the summit and declined to join its AI governance declaration. Whatever the merits of that decision domestically, the signal it sent internationally was unmistakable: Washington is not interested in multilateral AI governance. Brussels is.

The Brussels Effect isn't a problem of EU ambition. It is a problem of American disengagement.

## The Hiroshima Process Is the Right Vehicle

Not all multilateral AI governance frameworks are created equal, and the choice of venue matters enormously.

The [Hiroshima AI Process](https://www.mofa.go.jp/files/100573500.pdf), launched under Japan's 2023 G7 presidency, produced a Code of Conduct for advanced AI developers and eleven Guiding Principles governing responsible AI deployment. Its architecture reflects exactly the governance approach the United States should be championing: voluntary commitments among democratic-ally governments, interoperable rather than harmonized, fast-moving rather than treaty-bound, and scoped to the countries actually driving frontier AI development.

This is the vehicle. Not a UN treaty process.

The case against a UN AI governance framework isn't primarily procedural — it's substantive. A UN process would be negotiated at the pace of the least cooperative member state, giving authoritarian governments that deploy AI for mass surveillance and social control an effective veto over democratic governance norms. China has actively advocated for UN AI governance precisely because it can shape UN outputs in ways it cannot shape G7 outputs. The "inclusivity" of a UN process is not a governance virtue when inclusion means elevating the preferences of states whose AI deployment practices are fundamentally incompatible with democratic values.

The [RAND Corporation's assessment of the 2025 G7 summit](https://www.rand.org/pubs/commentary/2025/06/the-g7-summit-missed-an-opportunity-for-progress-on.html) identified the failure to operationalize the Hiroshima Process into durable standards architecture as the most significant missed opportunity of that cycle. The criticism lands: producing guiding principles is not the same as building the interoperability frameworks that make those principles actionable. France's presidency offers the opportunity to correct that.

The US should arrive at Évian with a concrete Hiroshima Process agenda: convert the Code of Conduct commitments into bilateral technical standards MOUs, establish mutual recognition frameworks for AI conformity assessments among G7 partners, and create a working group mechanism that allows rapid standard-setting as frontier capabilities evolve — without requiring treaty-level consensus that would take a decade to achieve.

## Export NIST, Not Concessions

The most important thing the United States could do at Évian requires no new legislation: actively champion the [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) as the international governance standard of choice for G7 partners.

The NIST AI RMF 1.0, released in January 2023 and developed through an extensive multi-stakeholder process, is the most technically sophisticated, sector-flexible voluntary AI governance tool in existence. Unlike the EU AI Act's ex-ante mandate structure — which requires conformity assessments before deployment based on use-case classification — the NIST framework is risk-calibrated across the full AI lifecycle, scalable from small deployers to frontier model developers, and designed to evolve with the technology rather than lock in compliance obligations based on capability assessments that will be obsolete within years.

The framework has already achieved meaningful international uptake. Japan's AI Safety Institute has incorporated NIST RMF elements into its evaluation methodology. The UK's AI Safety Institute cross-references it in published guidance. Several OECD member states cite it as a reference framework in their national AI strategies.

That's a foundation. The US should be building on it aggressively — formally proposing NIST RMF adoption as the G7's shared baseline for AI risk management, offering technical assistance to partner governments implementing NIST-aligned frameworks, and establishing a G7 AI Standards Working Group that uses NIST as its reference architecture.

The alternative — allowing EU AI Act harmonization pressure to become the default among allied partners — doesn't just export a different regulatory model. It exports a fundamentally different theory of governance: precautionary rather than risk-calibrated, ex-ante rather than adaptive, horizontal rather than sector-specific. Once that logic is embedded in allied regulatory architecture, unwinding it is a generational project.

## Bilateral Agreements Move Faster and Matter More

The most actionable near-term governance strategy doesn't run through Évian at all — it runs through bilateral channels that can move faster than any multilateral forum.

The [US-UK AI Safety Institute Memorandum of Understanding](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/uk-and-us-launch-partnership-on-artificial-intelligence-safety-testing), signed in 2024, is the proof-of-concept: two advanced-economy partners establishing shared evaluation methodologies, test protocols, and information-sharing arrangements for frontier AI systems. No treaty process, no multilateral consensus requirement, no authoritarian-state veto. Just two allied governments with complementary capabilities agreeing to work together on technical governance standards.

That model should be replicated urgently with Japan and Australia, both of which have functional AI safety evaluation programs and deep technology partnerships with the United States. A network of bilateral AI governance MOUs — covering conformity assessment mutual recognition, shared frontier model evaluation protocols, and coordinated incident reporting — would create the functional equivalent of an interoperability zone among democratic AI developers, without the deadweight of a formal multilateral treaty.

This bilateral-first approach also has a strategic function: it crowds out EU harmonization pressure by giving US partners an alternative anchor. When a partner government is deciding whether to align its implementing regulations with EU technical standards or US NIST-derived frameworks, the existence of a functioning bilateral MOU changes the calculus. Bilateral engagement is not a substitute for multilateral leadership — it's the foundation that makes multilateral leadership credible.

## The Strategic Stakes

This is not a regulatory process story. It is a standards competition story with decade-long consequences.

Whoever establishes the reference architecture for global AI governance shapes the incentive environment for AI development, deployment, and investment for the foreseeable future. Technical standards determine which systems can be deployed in which markets, what conformity demonstration costs, and which governance approaches attract international investment. They also shape what AI capabilities are considered presumptively safe and which trigger precautionary intervention — a determination with profound implications for where frontier research happens and who benefits from it.

The EU AI Act's precautionary logic, if it becomes the global default, imposes what amounts to a tax on AI capability development: every new use case that triggers "high-risk" classification generates compliance costs, delays, and regulatory uncertainty that disadvantage the most innovative applications. In a world where the US and China are in genuine strategic competition over AI capabilities, regulatory frameworks that slow the development and deployment of advanced AI in democratic economies are not neutral governance choices — they are strategic concessions.

The global AI governance landscape is not a blank slate. Frameworks are consolidating, governments are making alignment decisions, and the multilateral conversation is advancing with or without the United States. France's G7 presidency and the Évian summit represent the clearest opportunity Washington will have this year to redirect that conversation.

## What Washington Must Do Before June 17

The agenda is tractable. It requires political will, not new legislation.

**First**, the administration should arrive at Évian with a formal proposal to operationalize the Hiroshima AI Process — converting the Code of Conduct into a standing G7 AI Standards Council with a NIST-anchored technical baseline and a mandate to produce interoperability frameworks on an accelerated timeline.

**Second**, the US Trade Representative and Department of Commerce should accelerate bilateral AI governance MOU negotiations with Japan and Australia before the summit, so the US can point to a functioning network rather than a theoretical framework.

**Third**, NIST should be directed to release an internationalized version of the AI RMF — explicitly designed for adoption by allied governments — with technical assistance resources attached. This is an export strategy, not just a domestic tool.

**Fourth**, the US should formally oppose any expansion of the UN's AI governance mandate into binding treaty territory, and enlist G7 partners in that position at Évian. The Hiroshima coalition is the right venue; the UN is not.

The EU will be at Évian pushing AI Act harmonization as the model for allied coordination. China will be absent from the room but present in every discussion about what global governance frameworks must address. The United States has eight weeks to develop a coherent answer to both.

Showing up without one isn't a neutral position. It's a concession — and one the democratic AI development ecosystem will be paying for long after the Évian summit ends.
