Washington has spent two years building an elaborate internal rulebook for how federal agencies are supposed to adopt artificial intelligence. Executive orders, three successive Office of Management and Budget memoranda, mandatory use-case inventories, agency AI strategies, Chief AI Officer designations, risk-management checklists for "high-impact" systems. By any reasonable measure, the federal government has *governance* in abundance.

What it does not have, according to three separate Government Accountability Office reports released between March and April 2026, is a federal workforce equipped to execute that governance, or even confidence that the governance is buying the protection it promises. Read together, [GAO-26-107681](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107681), [GAO-26-107522](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107522), and [GAO-26-107859](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107859) describe an agency-facing compliance stack that has grown faster than agencies' capacity to comply with it — and that, even where it functions as intended, is not delivering the risk reduction it is designed to produce. This is not a story about whether AI needs more rules. It is a story about a government that has written itself rules it cannot yet follow.

## The stack, and how it got here

Federal agencies' internal AI governance obligations have been rewritten twice in three years. The Biden administration's [Executive Order 14110](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/11/01/2023-24283/safe-secure-and-trustworthy-development-and-use-of-artificial-intelligence) produced [OMB Memorandum M-24-10](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/M-24-10-Advancing-Governance-Innovation-and-Risk-Management-for-Agency-Use-of-Artificial-Intelligence.pdf), "Advancing Governance, Innovation, and Risk Management for Agency Use of Artificial Intelligence" (March 2024), which required agencies to inventory their AI use cases, designate Chief AI Officers, and apply minimum practices to systems deemed "rights-impacting" (AI that serves as a principal basis for decisions affecting civil rights, equal opportunity, or access to benefits) or "safety-impacting" (AI that could affect human life, critical infrastructure, or the environment).

The Trump administration's [Executive Order 14179](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/01/31/2025-02172/removing-barriers-to-american-leadership-in-artificial-intelligence), "Removing Barriers to American Leadership in Artificial Intelligence," revoked EO 14110 in January 2025 and directed agencies to identify and unwind AI-related rules that inhibited innovation. OMB responded in April 2025 with [M-25-21](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-21-Accelerating-Federal-Use-of-AI-through-Innovation-Governance-and-Public-Trust.pdf), "Accelerating Federal Use of AI through Innovation, Governance, and Public Trust," which formally rescinded M-24-10 — and a companion memo, [M-25-22](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-22-Driving-Efficient-Acquisition-of-Artificial-Intelligence-in-Government.pdf), "Driving Efficient Acquisition of Artificial Intelligence in Government," governing procurement.

The rhetoric shifted decisively from risk-management to acceleration. The underlying architecture did not shrink to match. M-25-21 still requires enterprise AI strategies, still requires Chief AI Officers, still requires public use-case inventories, still requires minimum risk-management practices for high-impact systems. What changed was emphasis and terminology, not the number of boxes an agency's AI office has to check before a model goes into production. Each successive memo has added its own reporting cadence, its own inventory refresh cycle, its own certification requirement — without a parallel mechanism for retiring the obligations layered on by its predecessor. That is the pattern GAO's 2026 reporting season now documents in granular, agency-level detail: not recklessness, but accretion.

## Case study: mandate without means at the IRS

The clearest illustration is [GAO-26-107522](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107522), published in March 2026, examining the IRS's AI program. The IRS had 126 active AI use cases in its inventory as of June 2025 — audit selection, fraud detection, taxpayer-service chatbots — reflecting a genuine, sustained push toward AI-enabled tax administration. But GAO found the agency lacks the workforce plan to sustain it. Staffing reductions in 2025 hit the IRS's AI capacity directly: the Research, Applied Analytics and Statistics group alone lost 63 employees who had been working full- or part-time on AI, according to [Nextgov/FCW's reporting on the release](https://www.nextgov.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/03/irs-faces-ai-skills-gaps-after-pushing-tech-talent-out-watchdog-finds/412337/). GAO's conclusion was blunt: the agency does not have a workforce plan to identify or close the skills gaps its AI ambitions require, and its inventory itself has quality-control problems — incomplete entries, inconsistent internal reporting on which systems even belong in it.

GAO made eight recommendations. The IRS agreed with all eight. That is the diagnosis in miniature: an agency directed, at the political and legal level, to modernize with AI at scale, that was simultaneously stripped of the people who would have to do the modernizing, and that was never given a remediation plan to close the gap. This is not an agency defying its mandate. It is a mandate issued without the means to execute it — the paperwork architecture (use-case inventories, strategic-alignment reporting) kept running even as the underlying technical capacity to responsibly operate what's in the inventory eroded.

## The sharper problem: the paperwork isn't even working

If the IRS case shows the compliance stack outrunning agency capacity, [GAO-26-107681](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107681) — also released in March 2026 — shows something more damaging to the case for the stack itself: even where agencies do comply, the governance framework does not deliver the protection it is sold as providing. GAO examined the government-wide privacy risks and challenges associated with federal AI use and assessed how well OMB's guidance addresses them. Convening an expert panel, GAO identified ten major privacy-related challenges federal AI use poses. OMB's guidance, GAO found, meaningfully addresses only two of them — enhancing workforce skills and enabling agencies to scale AI with privacy protections in place. The other eight are left substantially unaddressed: no direction on evaluating or auditing AI systems that process sensitive information, no standards for separating sensitive data out of training sets, no standardized metrics for measuring privacy protection, inadequate guidance on user-consent transparency, and thin coverage of privacy considerations in AI procurement itself.

This is the finding that should worry anyone inclined to defend the current stack on public-trust grounds. Agencies are being required to build and maintain use-case inventories, file risk assessments, and designate accountable officials — real institutional effort, measured in staff-hours and delayed deployments — while GAO's own audit shows that the guidance underlying those requirements still does not resolve the privacy risks it was written to manage. Agencies are paying the compliance cost without collecting the corresponding safety benefit. GAO's two recommendations to OMB — update the guidance directly, or use existing venues like the Chief AI Officer Council and the Federal Privacy Council to disseminate best practices — are modest asks precisely because the gap it identified is not about insufficient ambition in the rules. It is about rules that were never built to answer the questions agencies actually confront when they put a model into production.

## The acquisition blind spot compounds it

The third report, [GAO-26-107859](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107859), released in April 2026, closes the loop by showing agencies aren't even learning from the AI they've already bought. GAO found that federal agencies more than doubled their AI use between 2023 and 2024, acquiring capability through a range of contracting vehicles. But officials at four agencies examined — the General Services Administration, and the Departments of Defense, Homeland Security, and Veterans Affairs — told GAO they were not systematically collecting lessons learned from those acquisitions, because their internal policies did not require it, notwithstanding OMB guidance calling for exactly that kind of knowledge-sharing. The result: agencies are repeatedly encountering the same friction points — data-rights disputes, testing requirements misaligned with how AI systems actually get updated, acquisition timelines that don't match AI development cycles, opaque vendor pricing — without a mechanism to avoid repeating each other's mistakes. All four agencies agreed to fix their policies; DHS and VA set completion targets of July and August 2026, respectively.

Put the three reports side by side and the pattern is unmistakable. The compliance architecture generates inventories, strategies, and use-case documentation in volume. What it has not reliably generated is the thing all of that paperwork is nominally in service of: agencies that can deploy AI competently, protect the privacy of the people whose data it touches, and get smarter with each subsequent purchase. Process has scaled. Capability has not kept pace.

## Why this is a competitiveness problem, not just a management one

It is tempting to file these findings under routine GAO good-government housekeeping. That undersells the stakes. Every quarter an agency's AI office spends reconciling a use-case inventory against a superseded memo's format, or waiting on a Chief AI Officer sign-off duplicated across two overlapping approval gates, is a quarter the private sector is not losing. Commercial AI deployment cycles — model selection, fine-tuning, evaluation, rollout — now move in weeks. The IRS took years to accumulate 126 use cases and still lacks the workforce plan to operate them safely. That gap does not stay contained to tax administration. It shows up in every mission area where the federal government is a meaningful AI adopter and, increasingly, a meaningful AI *purchaser* whose contracting practices shape how vendors build for the public sector — a role that competing state-directed AI programs, Beijing's foremost among them, do not have to win by out-executing a layered internal compliance process of their own making. A federal government that cannot execute its own internal AI governance is not a cautious government. It is a government ceding adoption speed — and with it, institutional AI fluency — to actors who don't carry the same paperwork tax.

This is a fundamentally different critique from the one dominating most AI policy debates in Washington this year, which concerns whether states or the federal government should set binding rules for *private-sector* AI development and deployment. That fight is about external regulation of industry. This is about whether the federal government can manage its *own* adoption of the same technology it is deciding whether to regulate elsewhere — and GAO's reporting says, as of mid-2026, that it largely cannot, not for want of rules, but for want of the workforce and institutional design to execute the rules already on the books.

## What GAO's findings do — and don't — justify

None of this is an argument for stripping away oversight of government AI use. Federal agencies handling taxpayer data, benefits determinations, and law-enforcement-adjacent functions should have real guardrails, and GAO's privacy findings if anything argue for *better-targeted* guidance, not less of it. The failure mode documented here is not too much scrutiny of high-risk uses; it's too much duplicative process applied indiscriminately, layered without retirement, while the guidance that actually matters for privacy protection remains thin. Three concrete fixes follow directly from GAO's own findings:

**Sunset overlapping requirements when new guidance supersedes old.** M-25-21 rescinded M-24-10's core framework but preserved its structural elements — inventories, strategies, high-impact-system reviews — without OMB conducting a systematic audit of which specific reporting artifacts from the M-24-10 era remain duplicative under the new regime. Every future OMB AI memo should include a mandatory sunset review of the specific inventory and reporting requirements it replaces, not just the ones it explicitly rescinds.

**Pair every AI adoption mandate with dedicated workforce funding.** The IRS finding is the clearest evidence that directing agencies to scale AI adoption while their technical workforce is shrinking is not a governance failure at the agency level — it is a design failure at the mandate level. Congress and OMB should require that any directive expanding an agency's AI use-case portfolio be accompanied by a workforce-gap assessment and a funded remediation plan, not left to the agency's existing (and often shrinking) budget.

**Give GAO's acquisition lessons-learned findings a mandatory response mechanism.** GAO-26-107859's four agencies agreed to fix their policies only because GAO asked. That is not a systemic fix — it is four voluntary commitments. Congress should require agencies to report annually to OMB on lessons-learned collection and require OMB to certify compliance as a condition of continued high-impact AI procurement authority, similar to the reporting the [Federal Information Technology Acquisition Reform Act (FITARA)](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R44843) already imposes on major IT investments.

The through-line across all three fixes is the same: fewer, better-enforced requirements beat more, loosely enforced ones. That is not a call for deregulation — it is the same discipline routinely urged for AI oversight of private industry, turned inward on the government's own house. An agency that cannot staff its AI workforce, whose privacy guidance leaves eight of ten known risks unaddressed, and that cannot learn from its own procurement mistakes is not a government exercising caution. It is a government that has confused documentation with governance — and Congress's oversight committees, not another round of OMB memoranda, are best positioned to force the distinction.
