Somewhere in a study section right now, a program officer is staring at an AI-detection score attached to someone's R01 application and trying to decide what it means. Nothing in NIH policy tells them. That is the quiet, structural defect sitting underneath a rule that got a lot more consequential this spring: what began as [NOT-OD-25-132](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/notice-files/NOT-OD-25-132.html), a temporary notice titled "Supporting Fairness and Originality in NIH Research Applications," has now been folded into the permanent [NIH Grants Policy Statement](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/policy/nihgps/html5/toc_test_417.htm) as Section 2.3.7.13, "Appropriate Use of Artificial Intelligence," in the [March 2026 NIHGPS revision](https://res.research.northeastern.edu/revised-nih-grants-policy-statement/). The provision disqualifies as "non-original" any application NIH determines was "substantially developed by AI" — enforced by detection tooling NIH says it will keep updating, with no published standard for what "substantial" means and no dedicated appeals process for a wrongful call.

ThinkBot has already argued that NIH's companion policy — a flat six-application-per-year cap adopted in the same notice — is a blunt volume proxy for a fraud problem NIH itself estimates affects [roughly 1.3 percent of applicants](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/faculty-issues/research/2025/07/22/nih-limit-ai-use-cap-grant-applications-6-year). That critique was about throttling quantity. This one is about something more corrosive: NIH has built an enforcement regime around forensic content-detection technology with a well-documented, population-skewed error rate, and it has now made that technology's verdict permanent grounds for disqualification without building in the procedural safeguards that any detection instrument with a real error rate requires. The underlying worry — AI-generated grant mills gaming federal science funding — is legitimate. The instrument NIH chose to police it is not fit for purpose, and the researchers most likely to be wrongly caught in it are not the fraudsters NIH is trying to stop.

## From temporary notice to permanent law, with no new safeguards

The timeline matters because it shows the stakes rising while the rule's design stood still. NOT-OD-25-132 took effect for applications submitted on or after September 25, 2025, as one part of a broader response to [reported cases of individual PIs filing more than 40 applications in a single round](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/faculty-issues/research/2025/07/22/nih-limit-ai-use-cap-grant-applications-6-year), some apparently AI-generated. As a temporary notice, it functioned as an emergency brake — reversible, subject to revision, implicitly provisional. Folding that same language into Section 2.3.7.13 of the Grants Policy Statement changes its legal character entirely. The NIHGPS is NIH's standing terms and conditions of award; provisions codified there become durable compliance obligations woven into every institution's grants-management infrastructure, reviewed by auditors and referenced by Offices of Research Integrity as a matter of course, not a notice institutions can expect to sunset.

Nothing about the underlying enforcement mechanism was strengthened to match that upgrade in permanence. NIH's own guidance still states only that it "will continue to employ the latest technology in detection of AI-generated content" — no named tools, no published accuracy benchmarks, no disclosure of update cadence beyond a promise to keep improving. Post-award detection can trigger [Office of Research Integrity referral, cost disallowance, award suspension, or termination](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/notice-files/NOT-OD-25-132.html) — the single harshest set of consequences NIH has available short of debarment. NIH does maintain a general peer-review appeals process for [procedurally flawed initial scientific review](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/policy/nihgps/html5/section_2/2.4.2_appeals_of_initial_scientific_review.htm), but that mechanism exists to contest reviewer conduct and scoring irregularities — it was never designed to adjudicate whether a forensic AI-detection score was itself wrong, and neither NOT-OD-25-132 nor the March 2026 NIHGPS revision creates a parallel channel for that specific dispute. An applicant flagged and disqualified pre-award, before the application ever reaches a study section, may have no forum at all in which to contest the machine's verdict. NIH raised the stakes to permanent, standing policy without raising the due-process floor to match — and it did so for a mechanism that independent research says is not reliable enough to bear that weight.

## The detectors don't work the way NIH is treating them

The empirical case against forensic AI-text detection is not new, contested, or industry-funded — it is peer-reviewed and specific about who it misclassifies. In a 2023 study published in *Patterns* (Cell Press), Stanford researcher Weixin Liang and coauthors ran seven widely used GPT-detectors against two sets of human-written essays: TOEFL essays from non-native English speakers and essays from US eighth-graders. The detectors performed [near-perfectly on the native-English essays but misclassified an average of 61.3 percent of the non-native-English essays as AI-generated](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10382961/) — with at least one detector flagging nearly 98 percent of the TOEFL set. The mechanism is not mysterious: detectors key on statistical measures of lexical predictability and sentence-structure variance, and those same measures are exactly what characterizes non-native and formulaic writing. The Liang team found the misclassification rate dropped sharply when they had an LLM simply enrich the vocabulary of the same human-written essays — meaning the tools are, in a real sense, detecting a writing register rather than detecting machine authorship.

That finding generalizes further than TOEFL essays. A growing body of evidence on generative-AI detection in academic and assessment contexts has reached the same conclusion from other angles: reviews of detector performance describe the tools as ["ineffective, unreliable and harmful"](https://www.emerald.com/etpc/article/doi/10.1108/ETPC-07-2025-0155/1353142/AI-writing-detectors-are-ineffective-unreliable-and-harmful) when used for high-stakes determinations, and a [2024 Bloomberg Businessweek investigation](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-10-18/do-ai-detectors-work-students-face-false-cheating-accusations) documented the same disproportionate burden falling on non-native English speakers and neurodivergent writers — including a student with autism spectrum disorder who was accused of academic dishonesty for writing in the formulaic register that characterizes her own voice, and others flagged after using nothing more than standard grammar-assistance software. That is the same category of researcher NIH's own broadening-participation goals are supposed to protect, not screen out.

Translate that error profile into NIH's applicant pool and the population at risk comes into focus immediately: international postdocs and foreign-born faculty writing in a second or third language, researchers with disabilities who rely on dictation, grammar-correction, or other assistive writing software, and simply distinctive prose stylists whose sentence-level unpredictability reads to a classifier as "too clean" or "too formulaic" depending on the tool. These are not edge cases NIH can wave away as rare; non-native English speakers make up a substantial and growing share of the US biomedical research workforce NIH itself funds. A rule intended to catch grant-mill fraud will, on the evidence, land disproportionately on exactly the researchers NIH has spent two decades trying to recruit and retain.

The institutional response outside NIH has already tracked this evidence: universities including [Vanderbilt](https://www.vanderbilt.edu/brightspace/2023/08/16/guidance-on-ai-detection-and-why-were-disabling-turnitins-ai-detector/) and [UCLA](https://dts.ucla.edu/news/turnitins-ai-writing-detection-preview-feature-opted-out) have declined to deploy Turnitin's AI-detection product for high-stakes determinations, citing unresolved accuracy concerns and the equity implications for non-native English speakers. NIH is moving in the opposite direction — deploying undisclosed detection tooling for the single highest-stakes determination in a researcher's career, funding eligibility itself, with permanent codification and no equivalent institutional skepticism built into the rule.

## An undefined standard makes the reliability problem worse

Section 2.3.7.13's operative language does not give reviewers a test they can apply consistently. NIH permits AI use to "assist in application preparation for limited aspects or in specific circumstances" — without naming a single example of what those aspects or circumstances are — but draws no line between that and prose "substantially developed by AI." Does running a literature review through an LLM to reorganize a PI's own citations cross the line? Does using AI to tighten a Specific Aims page cross it? NIH's guidance does not say, and it has declined to operationalize "substantially" in the months since the original notice took effect in September 2025. Pairing a vague standard with an unreliable detection instrument compounds rather than offsets the risk: a program officer with no bright line to apply will lean on whatever number the detection software reports, and that number — per Liang et al. and the subsequent literature — is least trustworthy precisely for the applicants whose writing least resembles a native-English academic register. Vagueness was already a problem when NIH used it to justify a volume cap; layered under a detection instrument this unreliable, it becomes the mechanism by which forensic false positives convert into career-ending funding decisions with no defined channel to contest them.

## NSF shows the more administrable path

NIH did not need to invent this problem from scratch, because its sister agency already solved a version of it. NSF's own [generative-AI guidance](https://www.nsf.gov/news/notice-to-the-research-community-on-ai) targets a narrow, well-specified harm: reviewers uploading confidential proposal text into public generative-AI tools, which NSF treats as a breach of the reviewer confidentiality pledge because that information effectively enters the public domain the moment it is typed into an unapproved tool. That is a compliance test anyone can run before acting — is this information confidential, and is this tool approved. For proposers, NSF encourages disclosure of how generative AI was used in preparing a proposal, and it holds proposers accountable for the accuracy of the result under existing research-misconduct standards (fabrication, falsification, plagiarism) — not a forensic re-adjudication of who typed which sentence. NSF never attempts to forensically determine what share of a proposer's own prose was "substantially" machine-authored, because it recognizes that determination is not reliably administrable at the scale of a federal research agency. NIH is now attempting exactly that determination, for the harshest possible consequence, using tooling with a documented bias problem NSF's model was built to avoid.

## What NIH should do

**Replace detection-triggered disqualification with disclosure and attestation.** NIH should require applicants to affirmatively state, in a standardized field analogous to a conflict-of-interest disclosure, what generative-AI assistance was used in preparing an application and how. This gives program officers a fast, verifiable signal without asking anyone — human or algorithmic — to forensically reconstruct authorship after the fact.

**Reserve disqualification and ORI referral for provable misconduct.** Fabricated data, plagiarized text, and false attestations are already investigable under existing NIH research-integrity machinery. That machinery, triggered by evidence rather than a classifier score, is the appropriate tool for the grant-mill behavior NIH is actually worried about — and it already carries the severe penalties NIH wants available for real fraud.

**Publish a defined appeals mechanism specifically for AI-detection determinations before the March 2026 codification produces its first wrongful disqualification.** NIH's existing peer-review appeals process was not built for this dispute and should not be stretched to cover it by default. A researcher flagged by an undisclosed detector, under an undefined standard, with no dedicated forum to contest the result, is a due-process problem NIH can still get ahead of — or one it can wait to have handed to it by the first PI willing to litigate a wrongful disqualification.

NIH is right that generative AI creates new fraud vectors in federal grantmaking. It is wrong to police that risk with forensic tools that peer-reviewed research shows misfire against non-native English speakers and other legitimate scientists, under a standard so vague that reviewers are left guessing, now codified as permanent policy with no appeals channel built for the mistake everyone studying these tools already knows is coming. Detect the fraud. Don't blackball the scientists the detector gets wrong.
