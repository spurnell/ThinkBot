On June 22, House Energy & Commerce Committee Chairman Brett Guthrie and Ranking Member Frank Pallone [announced a landmark bipartisan deal](https://energycommerce.house.gov/posts/chairman-guthrie-and-ranking-member-pallone-announce-agreement-on-bipartisan-kids-safety-package) on the Kids Internet and Digital Safety Act — a sweeping omnibus consolidating KOSA, COPPA 2.0, a data broker registry, and more than a dozen related child safety measures. The Senate fight is already escalating: KOSA's original co-authors have declared key provisions a "nonstarter." Before that battle hardens into competing bills that move in opposite directions, Congress needs to understand what this deal actually trades away — and what it gets right.

The short version: the House got the hardest part correct and the most consequential part dangerously wrong.

## The Duty of Care Removal Is a Genuine Win

KOSA's "duty of care" provision would have required platforms to "exercise reasonable care" to prevent documented harms to minors across a sprawling list of categories — eating disorders, substance abuse, sexual exploitation, and more. Critics from across the ideological spectrum consistently identified the same structural defect: no workable definition emerged that didn't ultimately delegate to courts the power to second-guess platform design decisions based on a government-defined conception of child wellbeing.

The [Center for Democracy & Technology documented](https://cdt.org/insights/recent-court-opinions-cast-additional-constitutional-doubt-on-kosas-duty-of-care/) how recent court opinions were already casting constitutional doubt on KOSA's duty of care even as the bill's advocates insisted it just needed better drafting. The [EFF warned throughout 2024](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/12/kids-online-safety-act-continues-threaten-our-rights-online-year-review-2024) that the provision's vagueness would produce litigation-driven over-moderation — particularly of LGBTQ content, mental health resources, and anything a plaintiff's attorney could characterize as insufficiently cautious for a minor. Years of markup failed to fix the core indeterminacy. The removal of the duty-of-care provision is the correct outcome.

## Universal Age Verification Is Something Else Entirely

Under [the KIDS Act's replacement framework](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/7757/text), covered platforms must in practice verify the age of every user — not just minors — because enforcement obligations hinge on knowing whether a user is under 17. As [the EFF has explained](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/06/kids-act-would-require-age-checks-get-online), the bill's requirements "come at the expense of privacy, free expression, and the ability of people of all ages to use the internet without revealing sensitive data." Every adult who wants to access a covered platform may need to hand government-issued identification to a third-party verification company. That is not a child safety measure. It is identity infrastructure for the American internet.

The constitutional record on universal age verification mandates is instructive — and more nuanced than the bill's supporters are letting on. In April 2025, a federal district court [permanently enjoined Arkansas's social media age verification law](https://netchoice.org/netchoice-wins-court-declares-arkansas-age-verification-law-unconstitutional-permanently-halts-it-to-protect-arkansans-rights-privacy-online/) as a First Amendment violation, finding that requiring adults to produce state-approved documentation "imposes significant burdens on adult access to constitutionally protected speech." These rulings reflect the baseline established in *[Packingham v. North Carolina](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/582/15-1194/)* (2017): the government cannot broadly restrict access to the modern internet, which the Court called "the modern public square."

KIDS Act proponents will invoke last June's *[Free Speech Coalition, Inc. v. Paxton](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/24pdf/23-1122_3e04.pdf)*, in which the Supreme Court upheld Texas's age verification mandate for websites where more than one-third of content is "sexual material harmful to minors." The analogy does not hold. The [Center for Democracy & Technology immediately flagged](https://cdt.org/insights/fsc-v-paxton-made-bad-law-but-its-not-carte-blanche-for-age-verification/) that this ruling is "not carte blanche for age verification" on general-purpose platforms. *Free Speech Coalition* applied intermediate scrutiny to a specific content category occupying its own constitutional space. A universal verification mandate applied to social platforms — which carry protected speech on every conceivable topic for users of every age — occupies entirely different terrain. Courts evaluating the KIDS Act will apply *[Moody v. NetChoice](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/23pdf/22-277_d18f.pdf)* — the 2024 ruling confirming that platforms' editorial discretion is First Amendment-protected — not the adult-content carve-out of *Free Speech Coalition*. The strict scrutiny analysis that sank Arkansas's law will govern here.

## The Constitutionally Workable Alternative Already Exists in the Bill

The KIDS Act's specific feature mandates — [no ephemeral messaging for users under 17, no direct messaging for users under 13, AI chatbot safeguards for minors](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/7757/text) — belong in a different legal category entirely. They regulate specific, enumerable design choices rather than conditioning all platform participation on identity disclosure. Targeted design standards can survive the constitutional review that open-ended liability frameworks and blanket identity mandates cannot. The bill models the right approach in its feature mandates and the wrong approach in its verification provisions. Congress should preserve the former independent of the latter.

On verification architecture, there is a constitutionally workable path. [NIST's SP 800-63-4 digital identity framework](https://pages.nist.gov/800-63-4/sp800-63.html) describes identity assurance approaches that can establish a user attribute — "this person is over 17" — without disclosing actual identity to the platform itself. Privacy-preserving credential systems built on this framework accomplish exactly what the KIDS Act's verification provisions claim to target, without constructing a surveillance layer in the process. Congress should mandate the outcome — age-appropriate access controls — not the most invasive possible mechanism for achieving it.

## The Senate Has a Narrow Window

The KIDS Act's preemption structure — a federal floor permitting states to go stricter — is sound architecture. State-level experimentation has produced enjoined laws, compliance chaos, and constitutional confusion. A coherent federal standard can resolve all of that, provided the standard itself is constitutionally workable. The case for federal action on children's online safety is strong. The case for exporting the defects of state age verification laws into federal statute is the opposite.

Sen. Blackburn and Sen. Blumenthal's [insistence that the duty-of-care removal is a nonstarter](https://thehill.com/policy/technology/5938935-house-breakthrough-on-kids-online-safety-faces-long-odds-in-senate/) points the Senate in precisely the wrong direction. The leverage KOSA's original authors hold should not be applied to restoring a liability standard that years of legislative history demonstrated cannot be drafted into workability. It should be applied to redesigning the verification architecture before the package advances.

The bipartisan deal Congress just struck removes a provision that would have produced litigation-driven over-moderation and chilling effects on innovation — and replaces it with a provision that courts will strike down while it simultaneously constructs exactly the surveillance infrastructure that civil libertarians and constitutional scholars have long warned against. Take the win on duty of care. Fix the verification architecture. The Senate has a narrow window to do both.
