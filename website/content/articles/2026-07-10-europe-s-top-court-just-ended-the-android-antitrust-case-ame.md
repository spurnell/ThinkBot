On July 2, the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) dismissed Google and Alphabet's final appeal in [Case C-738/22 P](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-02/google-loses-eu-court-fight-over-4-1-billion-android-fine), making permanent the €4.125 billion fine over Android's licensing terms — the amount the General Court set in 2022 after trimming the European Commission's original €4.34 billion penalty from 2018. There is no further appeal. Six days later, the EU General Court delivered a second blow to a second American platform, [upholding Apple's designation as a "gatekeeper"](https://www.jurist.org/news/2026/07/eu-general-court-upholds-apples-gatekeeper-designation-under-digital-markets-act/) under the Digital Markets Act in Joined Cases T-1079/23, T-1080/23, and T-214/24, rejecting Apple's argument that its device lines should be counted separately rather than as a single sprawling ecosystem.

Two American platforms, two final losses, one week. Brussels will treat this as vindication of its enforcement model. Washington should treat it as a warning — and a more urgent one than when ThinkBot [first flagged the Android ruling's doctrinal risk](/2026-07-04-ecj-google-android-fine-eu-regulatory-export) on July 4, because the same structural-presumption logic is now the model some U.S. officials are eyeing for AI and cloud markets.

## Structural presumption, not consumer harm

The Android case is worth pausing on precisely because of what the Commission never had to prove. Its theory, [affirmed by the General Court in 2022](https://www.cliffordchance.com/insights/resources/blogs/talking-tech/en/articles/2022/09/the-google-android-european-court-judgment-and-its-wider-implica.html) and now by the CJEU, was that Google's practice of bundling Search and Chrome into the free Android licensing agreements offered to device manufacturers constituted illegal leveraging of dominance — full stop. What the Commission did not need to demonstrate, because EU competition law does not require it, was that consumers paid higher prices, received a worse product, or lost meaningful choice as a result. Device manufacturers could still install rival browsers and search defaults; users could still switch. Android's free licensing model was a significant reason smartphone prices fell and access expanded globally over the past fifteen years. None of that mattered under a legal standard that infers abuse from market share and the structural fact of bundling itself.

Contrast this with the framework American courts apply. In [*Ohio v. American Express*](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/17pdf/16-1454_5h26.pdf), the Supreme Court required plaintiffs to show actual anticompetitive effects — on price, output, or quality — across the relevant market before a restraint could be condemned, rejecting the theory that harm to individual rivals or merchants was enough. The consumer welfare standard asks a factual question: are consumers worse off? EU dominance doctrine, as applied to Android, asks a structural one: is the firm big and did it bundle? Those are not the same inquiry, and they do not produce the same answers. A legal system that convicts on the second question without evidence on the first isn't protecting competition — it's presuming guilt from success.

## Fifteen years, no European Android

If structural-presumption enforcement against American platforms were actually cultivating rival dynamism, the evidence should show up somewhere. It hasn't. There is no EU-based mobile operating system. There is no European app storefront, cloud hyperscaler, or foundation-model lab operating at anything resembling the scale of the firms Brussels has spent a decade and a half fining.

The innovation gap shows up in the data, too. [McKinsey and Boardwave's 2025 analysis of Europe's tech ecosystem](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/europes-moonshot-moment-fueling-its-tech-ecosystem-for-scale) finds European software scale-ups take roughly fifteen years to reach €100 million in annual recurring revenue — five years longer than U.S. peers. [Startup Genome's 2026 Global Startup Ecosystem Report](https://startupgenome.com/report/the-global-startup-ecosystem-report-2026/europe-s-startup-ecosystem-resilience-reorientation-and-reform) adds that Europe captures 37 percent of global exit volume but just 11 percent of exit value, and [McKinsey's separate analysis of corporate-scale-up partnering](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/how-corporate-scale-up-partnering-can-boost-europes-tech-competitiveness) finds only about 20 percent of European corporates actively partner with scale-ups, versus roughly half in the United States. Enforcement aimed at American platforms was never going to manufacture a European Android by itself, but the absence of any homegrown alternative after this much regulatory pressure should at least caution against the theory that punishing scale is what generates it.

## The contagion risk

Here is why a Luxembourg judgment should interest congressional staff and enforcers who have no jurisdictional stake in it. FTC and DOJ officials have begun describing AI infrastructure and cloud computing in the same register once reserved for search and mobile — control of "the next layer," chokepoints, gatekeeping. That framing is not inherently wrong; foreclosure theories can be legitimate if the facts support them.

The risk is evidentiary shortcut creep: importing the EU's practice of inferring abuse from market share and vertical structure into U.S. remedy design, without Congress ever having voted to change the legal standard. If a firm's AI model defaults, cloud bundling arrangements, or chip licensing terms become presumptively suspect merely because the firm is large and the arrangement is exclusive — rather than because switching costs have been shown to actually rise or output to actually fall — American antitrust will have absorbed Europe's doctrine through rhetorical drift rather than legislative choice. The FTC's [proposed policy statement on AI model accuracy](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2026/07/ftc-seeks-public-comment-policy-statement-addressing-ai-accuracy), open for public comment through July 31, is a separate matter and not the concern here, but it reflects the same broader appetite for precautionary intervention ahead of demonstrated harm.

## What should happen instead

None of this requires U.S. enforcers to stand down. Ex-post antitrust enforcement against proven exclusionary conduct — actual foreclosure, actual price effects, actual innovation harm — remains available and sufficient, in AI and cloud markets as much as in search and mobile. What should not migrate across the Atlantic is the evidentiary shortcut: treating market share and vertical integration as self-proving offenses. Any AI or cloud antitrust theory that reaches the enforcement stage should be required to show, with real evidence, that consumers are paying more, getting less, or losing access to innovation — not merely that a firm is large and integrated. Brussels closed its book on Android this month. Washington should read the verdict as evidence of a framework's limits, not a set of instructions.
