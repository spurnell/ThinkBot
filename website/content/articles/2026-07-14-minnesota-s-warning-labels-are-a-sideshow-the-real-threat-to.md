Minnesota's first-in-the-nation social media "mental health warning label" law took effect on paper July 1, 2026 — and remains unenforced, because [NetChoice sued Attorney General Keith Ellison](https://netchoice.org/netchoice-v-ellison-minnesota-censorship-labels/) weeks earlier, arguing the state is compelling platforms to display "the government's preferred message" on a contested scientific question. [NetChoice is now pressing a federal judge](https://www.mediapost.com/publications/article/416112/netchoice-presses-court-to-block-minnesota-warning.html?edition=143042) to block the law before it can be enforced at all. Colorado's near-identical statute is already dead in the water — [a federal court halted it in November 2025](https://www.coloradopolitics.com/2025/11/07/federal-judge-blocks-colorado-law-requiring-social-media-platforms-to-provide-warnings-to-children/), and Colorado has appealed to the Tenth Circuit to revive it. New York and California have passed their own variants. All four are converging on the same constitutional problem.

Meanwhile, in a Los Angeles courtroom, something more consequential happened. On March 25, 2026, [a jury found Meta and YouTube liable](https://www.npr.org/2026/03/25/nx-s1-5746125/meta-youtube-social-media-trial-verdict) under a negligent-design theory, awarding $6 million in the first bellwether trial to reach verdict out of the roughly 350-family, 250-school-district [JCCP 5255 coordinated proceeding](https://www.techpolicy.press/tracker/jccp-re-social-media-adolescent-addictionpersonal-injury-products-liability/). The jury didn't find that any specific content harmed the plaintiff. It found that infinite scroll, autoplay, and push notifications made the *products themselves* defective. A parallel federal multidistrict litigation — [MDL No. 3047](https://mdlupdate.com/mdl/3047-social-media-adolescent-addiction/), consolidated in the Northern District of California — has swelled to nearly 2,900 cases, more than double its size at the start of 2025.

Both fronts target the same underlying grievance — that platforms are engineered to hold attention — and both are being sold to legislators and litigators as the accountability tool that finally reaches Big Tech. They are not equivalent threats. ThinkBot's view: policymakers and litigators tracking this space are spending far too much energy on the warning-label fight, which is likely to collapse on its own constitutional weight, and far too little scrutinizing the design-defect tort theory, which is built to survive exactly the defenses that have protected online speech for three decades — and which generalizes to nearly every attention-holding interface in the consumer software economy.

## Compelled speech is the weaker vehicle — let the First Amendment do the work

Warning-label statutes are a compelled-commercial-speech problem, governed by the *Zauderer*/*NIFLA* line of doctrine: the government may compel "purely factual and uncontroversial" disclosures, but not messaging on a matter of genuine dispute. Minnesota Statute [§ 325M.335](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/325M.335) requires a label describing "potential mental health harms of social media" every time a user opens the app — a claim that is anything but factually settled. A [2024 National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine consensus report](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10898856/) concluded that the research literature "is quite far from being able to provide confidence" that specific patterns of social media use cause adverse mental health outcomes, pointing to a scarcity of longitudinal studies and a reliance on correlational designs that cannot establish causation. Meta chief executive Mark Zuckerberg made the same point under oath, [testifying before the Senate Judiciary Committee](https://www.techpolicy.press/transcript-us-senate-judiciary-committee-hearing-on-big-tech-and-the-online-child-sexual-exploitation-crisis/) in January 2024 that "the existing body of scientific work has not shown a causal link" between social media use and worse adolescent mental health. Whatever one makes of that dispute on the merits, its existence is precisely what *NIFLA* says makes a disclosure "controversial" rather than "uncontroversial" — and courts have already struck down comparable compelled-warning schemes (on chemical and pesticide risk, for instance) on that exact ground.

Colorado's law is already stayed for this reason. Minnesota's is following the same path. That is not an accident of bad lawyering by the states; it's the doctrine working as designed. ThinkBot doesn't need to spend credibility relitigating the wisdom of warning labels — the compelled-government-messaging problem does the work on its own, and state legislators drafting the next version of this bill should recognize they are pouring resources into a vehicle the First Amendment is built to stop.

## The design-defect theory is the one that scales — and that's the danger

The JCCP verdict took a different, more durable route. Presiding Judge Carolyn Kuhl's earlier order in the coordinated proceeding [dismissed the plaintiffs' content-based claims on Section 230 and First Amendment grounds](https://www.beneschlaw.com/insight/californias-social-media-litigation-poses-potential-to-reshape-the-legal-landscape/) but allowed negligent-design and fraudulent-concealment claims to proceed, reasoning that they target "the function of the platforms" rather than exposure to any specific piece of content. That distinction traces to the Ninth Circuit's 2021 ruling in [*Lemmon v. Snap*](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/20-55295/20-55295-2021-05-04.html), which allowed a negligent-design suit over Snapchat's Speed Filter to proceed because it targeted "a duty to design a reasonably safe product," independent of any third-party content. What was a narrow foothold in a car-crash case is now the architecture for thousands of lawsuits against the core interface decisions of every major platform.

The doctrinal sleight of hand is worth naming plainly: infinite scroll, autoplay, and notification cadence are not incidental hardware — they are how a platform decides what to surface, when, and to whom. That is curation. It is the same editorial judgment Section 230 was written to shield, wearing a different label. The tension shows up starkly in the Third Circuit's 2024 decision in [*Anderson v. TikTok*](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca3/22-3061/22-3061-2024-08-27.html), which held — citing the Supreme Court's *Moody v. NetChoice* reasoning — that an algorithm's recommendations are the platform's own "expressive product," and therefore *outside* Section 230's third-party-content shield precisely *because* they are the platform's speech. Design-defect plaintiffs want the opposite characterization of the identical feature: that ranking and engagement mechanics are "conduct," not speech, so neither Section 230 nor the First Amendment applies. Courts cannot coherently hold both. Whichever way this settles, treating engagement-optimizing design as legally distinct from editorial judgment is a fiction that only holds until the next circuit looks closely.

The reason this matters beyond social media is straightforward: nothing in "addictive by design" liability is content-specific. A jury instructed that infinite scroll and autoplay are negligent design features has been handed a standard applicable to any product that uses variable rewards, streaks, autoplay, or push notifications to hold attention — a fair description of most consumer software built in the last fifteen years, streaming services and mobile games very much included. If "engagement-optimized design equals negligence" hardens into settled law without a rigorous limiting principle, plaintiffs' firms and state legislators now have a portable template that reaches any interface choice a jury finds unsettling in hindsight, evaluated with the benefit of exactly the causal certainty the underlying science doesn't support.

## What courts and legislators should actually do

The correct response is neither blanket Section 230 immunity for product-design claims nor open-ended tort exposure decided feature-by-feature by lay juries applying "addictive by design" as a vibe rather than a standard. Appellate courts reviewing the JCCP verdict, and legislators drafting the next wave of design-liability bills, should insist on: specific causation tying an identified design choice to identified harm in an identified plaintiff, not platform-wide indictment of engagement optimization as a category; feature-level foreseeability grounded in evidence the defendant possessed at the time, not hindsight informed by today's contested literature; and rejection of strict liability for engagement-optimizing design generally, reserving negligence exposure for design choices a plaintiff can show were known to cause the specific harm alleged and left uncorrected. Platforms' own voluntary adoption of teen accounts, default screen-time limits, and notification throttles — done well ahead of any of this litigation — shows that reputational and market pressure already push toward safer defaults without a jury verdict rewriting product-liability law for the entire software industry.

The warning-label statutes will likely die of First Amendment causes on their own timeline; ThinkBot need not spend capital hastening it. The design-defect wave will not die on its own — it will either calcify into a durable innovation tax on any product that competes for attention, or courts will impose the causation discipline this moment requires. That is the fight that matters, and it is the one getting the least scrutiny.
