When the American Bar Association convenes its [third virtual AI and the Practice of Law Summit](https://www.americanbar.org/news/abanews/aba-news-archives/2026/05/third-ai-and-practice-of-law-summit/) on May 18–19, the organizing materials will carry a phrase that understates the stakes: AI has moved "from experiment to infrastructure" in legal practice. That framing is accurate as far as it goes. What it doesn't capture is the policy inflection point the profession is now crossing — one where well-meaning bar associations, acting on incomplete analysis and institutional inertia, are at risk of locking AI-enhanced legal services inside the cost structures of large law firms, where wealthy clients already have access to everything. The clients who would benefit most — the unrepresented, the underserved, those facing eviction or custody disputes without a lawyer — stand to lose the most if bars get this wrong.

The good news is that bar associations don't need new rules. The professional responsibility architecture already in place is fully adequate to govern AI-assisted legal practice. The urgent task at this summit is not to build new doctrine but to resist the institutional temptation to layer redundant, restrictive AI-specific regulations on top of frameworks that, properly applied, already do the job.

## The Architecture Already Exists

The foundation of AI governance in legal practice is older than generative AI. In 2012, the ABA amended [Model Rule 1.1's Comment 8](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/professional_responsibility/publications/model_rules_of_professional_conduct/rule_1_1_competence/comment_on_rule_1_1/) to include the duty to "keep abreast of changes in the law and its practice, including the benefits and risks associated with relevant technology." The amendment was explicitly framed not as a new obligation but as a clarification: the duty of competence already encompassed technological awareness. It has been adopted or adapted in the majority of U.S. jurisdictions.

Then came [ABA Formal Opinion 512](https://www.americanbar.org/news/abanews/aba-news-archives/2024/07/aba-issues-first-ethics-guidance-ai-tools/), issued July 29, 2024 — the ABA's first comprehensive ethics guidance specifically addressing generative AI. The opinion addressed six areas: competence, confidentiality, client communication, candor toward the tribunal, supervisory responsibility, and fees. Its central holding on each was the same: the existing rules apply, and they are sufficient. A lawyer using AI must understand the tool well enough to exercise professional judgment, must protect client confidences when using AI systems that could expose them, must not submit AI-hallucinated content to a tribunal, and must not bill clients for the time spent learning a technology adopted for general practice efficiency. Each of these obligations derives directly from existing doctrine, not from any AI-specific rule.

The opinion's authors were deliberate about this. They could have called for new AI-specific ethics rules. They didn't. The [UNC Law Library's analysis](https://library.law.unc.edu/2025/02/aba-formal-opinion-512-the-paradigm-for-generative-ai-in-legal-practice/) of Opinion 512 characterizes it as establishing the paradigm for generative AI governance in legal practice — one grounded in existing professional responsibility rather than new doctrine. This was the right call.

The implication is direct: any state bar that enacts AI-specific restrictions beyond the framework established by Rule 1.1, Opinion 512, and the existing rules on confidentiality, candor, and client communication is not filling a gap. It is creating one — a regulatory gap between large firms capable of absorbing compliance infrastructure and everyone else.

## When "Client Protection" Means Pricing Out the Poor

The [ABA's December 2025 Task Force report](https://www.americanbar.org/news/abanews/aba-news-archives/2025/12/aba-ai-task-force-report-examines-opportunities-challenges/) found that 44 percent of law firms still have no formal AI governance policy — a figure some commentators read as a governance crisis requiring bar intervention. The correct reading is different: it is evidence of a market in the process of self-organizing around new tools, as markets do. The relevant baseline is not "what percentage of firms have formal AI policies" but "what percentage of lawyers using AI tools are causing client harm that existing malpractice and professional responsibility doctrine cannot reach." That number is not 44 percent.

Meanwhile, the scale of the problem AI can address is not abstract. The Legal Services Corporation's [2022 Justice Gap Report](https://justicegap.lsc.gov/resource/2022-justice-gap-report/) found that 92 percent of the civil legal problems experienced by low-income Americans receive inadequate or no legal help. Nearly three-quarters of low-income households face at least one civil legal problem per year — housing instability, benefit denials, family disputes — and most navigate those problems without any professional assistance. The access-to-justice gap is not a marginal policy concern. It is a systemic failure of the legal market, and AI represents the most significant structural cost-compression opportunity the profession has seen.

The evidence is already accumulating. Thomson Reuters launched its [AI for Justice program](https://www.thomsonreuters.com/en/press-releases/2025/september/from-promise-to-proof-thomson-reuters-ai-for-justice-program-helps-legal-nonprofits-serve-as-many-as-50percent-more-clients-daily) in 2024, deploying its CoCounsel AI assistant at legal nonprofits. The results: attorneys saving up to 15 hours per week, organizations scaling their daily client capacity by as much as 50 percent, and urgent case materials prepared up to 75 percent faster. In December 2025, the Legal Services Corporation awarded [$4.2 million to 32 technology initiative grant projects](https://www.lsc.gov/press-release/lsc-awards-42m-technology-grants-and-releases-tech-summit-report) supporting AI-assisted legal service delivery. These are not marginal improvements. They represent a structural shift in the economics of legal aid.

State bars that impose security certification requirements, prohibited-use categories, or mandatory compliance infrastructure on AI-assisted practice are not protecting these clients. They are writing rules that solo practitioners and legal aid organizations cannot afford to follow, and large firms can absorb without adjusting their billing rates by a single dollar. The compliance cost of restrictive AI regulation is not distributed neutrally across the legal market. It falls disproportionately on the practitioners who serve cost-sensitive clients — the very population the access-to-justice rationale is supposed to protect.

## Three States Setting the Wrong Precedent

The [patchwork of state bar AI guidance](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/centers_commissions/center-for-innovation/state-ai-task-force-information/) that has emerged since Opinion 512 illustrates precisely this dynamic. California, Florida, and New York — the three largest legal markets in the country — are each moving toward requirements that exceed what the existing framework demands, and in ways that impose disproportionate costs on smaller practices.

California has gone furthest. In March 2026, the State Bar's Committee on Professional Responsibility and Conduct [approved proposed amendments](https://www.calbar.ca.gov/public/public-meetings-comment/public-comment/public-comment-archives/2026-public-comment/proposed-amendments-rules-professional-conduct-related-artificial-intelligence) to six rules — 1.1, 1.4, 1.6, 3.3, 5.1, and 5.3 — specifically addressing AI. The [LawSites analysis](https://www.lawnext.com/2026/05/california-bar-proposes-rule-requiring-lawyers-to-verify-every-ai-output-and-five-other-ai-focused-ethics-changes.html) of the proposed amendments flags that Rule 1.1 would require independent review and verification of "any output generated by the technology" used in connection with client representation — with no carve-out for routine tasks or low-stakes matters. Rule 1.4 would trigger mandatory client disclosure whenever AI use "presents a significant risk or materially affects the scope, cost, manner, or decision-making process" of representation. The confidentiality amendment treats exposure of client data to AI tools as "revealing" client information even without breach.

Some of these requirements are reasonable restatements of existing obligations. Others are not. A blanket verification mandate covering all AI output regardless of task type imposes record-keeping and process documentation costs that are invisible to a large firm and potentially prohibitive to a solo practitioner with a $500-per-matter flat-fee practice. A mandatory disclosure trigger tied to whether AI "materially affects the scope or manner" of representation is a litigation magnet: every disgruntled client with a malpractice claim will argue their lawyer should have disclosed more.

Florida's [Opinion 24-1](https://www.floridabar.org/etopinions/opinion-24-1/) requires disclosure of AI use when it affects client billing — a narrower and more defensible standard. New York's [AI and Emerging Technologies Committee](https://nysba.org/committees/task-force-on-artificial-intelligence/) has issued guidance on AI use in client-facing work and emphasized continuing competency obligations around the technology. These are not equivalent interventions. Florida's billing-impact disclosure requirement has a clear client-protection rationale and a defined scope. New York's approach is grounded in professional development, not use restriction. California's proposed amendments are of a different character — they are the leading edge of a restrictive bar rulemaking wave, and if adopted, they will become the reference point for less careful jurisdictions to follow.

## UPL Is Not a Software Licensing Regime

One regulatory theory circulating in bar association discussions deserves direct rebuttal: the argument that AI-generated legal drafts constitute unauthorized practice of law by the AI system, and that bar associations should classify certain AI outputs as per se UPL.

[Rule 5.5](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/professional_responsibility/publications/model_rules_of_professional_conduct/rule_5_5_unauthorized_practice_of_law_multijurisdictional_practice_of_law/) governs the unauthorized practice of law by non-lawyers. Its purpose is to protect the public from unqualified persons holding themselves out as legal practitioners. It classifies who is practicing law, not which software a licensed attorney uses. The attorney who uses AI to draft a motion, reviews that draft, exercises professional judgment about its accuracy and appropriateness, and files it under their own name is practicing law. The AI system is a tool, in the same doctrinal category as legal research databases, document assembly software, or dictation transcription — all of which generate output that a lawyer must review before use.

Treating AI-generated drafts as per se UPL would not protect clients from unqualified practitioners. It would protect existing legal market incumbents from competition, suppress cost-compressing tools that expand meaningful representation, and invite bar associations to function as gatekeepers of attorney tool selection rather than enforcers of professional standards. That is not what Rule 5.5 is for.

## The Right Governance Model: Output Accountability, Not Tool Licensing

The governance architecture that actually works — for clients, for the profession, and for access to justice — is built on three principles that already have full legal support under existing doctrine.

**First, output-verification accountability.** Rule 1.1, as clarified by Comment 8 and Formal Opinion 512, already requires lawyers to understand AI tools well enough to exercise professional judgment over their outputs. A lawyer who submits an AI-hallucinated case citation to a court is not the victim of defective technology — they have failed their competence obligation. The duty to verify AI output is an existing obligation, not a gap that new rules must fill. Courts are already enforcing this directly: the [sanctions in *Mata v. Avianca*](https://www.arstechnica.com/tech-policy/2023/06/lawyer-who-used-chatgpt-to-write-brief-with-fake-cases-gets-sanctioned/) and similar cases demonstrate that existing malpractice and court-sanction doctrine responds to AI-assisted negligence without new rules.

**Second, contextual disclosure under existing communication rules.** Rules 1.4 and 3.3 already require lawyers to communicate material facts affecting representation and to avoid misleading courts. Where AI use is genuinely material to a client's interests — because it affects cost, scope, or the nature of the work product — disclosure is already required. A mandatory blanket disclosure regime that applies regardless of materiality goes beyond informed consent and into administrative burden generation.

**Third, malpractice accountability as the backstop.** Existing negligence doctrine holds lawyers accountable for the quality of their work product, regardless of the tools used to produce it. The lawyer who relies on an AI draft without review and produces an incorrect contract has committed malpractice. No new AI liability framework is needed; the existing one just needs to be applied.

This is a tool-neutral competence standard: what matters is whether the lawyer competently served the client, not which software assisted them. It is enforceable under current law, requires no new licensing infrastructure, and allows the market to develop AI governance best practices faster and more accurately than state rulemaking cycles permit.

## A Narrow, Targeted Role for Congress

Bar rulemaking is not the only lever available. Congress has a narrow but high-value role to play — not in regulating AI in legal practice, but in funding the access-to-justice infrastructure that AI enables.

The DOJ's Office for Access to Justice and the Legal Services Corporation are the natural vehicles for piloting AI-assisted legal intake for civil matters — housing, family law, public benefits, consumer debt — *before* state bar rulemaking hardens a restrictive baseline. The LSC's [Technology Initiative Grants program](https://www.lsc.gov/grants/technology-initiative-grant-program) is already moving in this direction, but the scale of the justice gap (92 percent of civil legal needs unmet) warrants a proportionate federal investment.

The political logic is also favorable. Funding AI-assisted access to justice is not a partisan issue. It is a direct investment in legal system legitimacy, a response to bipartisan concern about the civil justice gap, and a way to generate real-world evidence about how AI tools perform in high-stakes, cost-sensitive legal contexts before bar associations entrench governance frameworks based on speculation. Federal pilots provide the empirical foundation for sensible rulemaking; restrictive bar rules enacted without that evidence base do not.

## What the ABA Summit Should Produce

The third AI and the Practice of Law Summit arrives at the right moment to set a governance direction before the California COPRAC amendments and their imitators harden into the new baseline. The [ABA's Center for Innovation](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/centers_commissions/center-for-innovation/artificial-intelligence/access-to-justice/) has recognized the access-to-justice dimension of AI in legal practice — the summit should make that the organizing frame for its governance recommendations.

The practical agenda is clear. First, affirm that ABA Formal Opinion 512 is the ceiling, not the floor, for AI-specific ethics guidance — existing rules apply; new AI-specific mandates are not warranted. Second, resist UPL expansions that would classify AI-assisted attorney work product as per se unauthorized practice. Third, develop voluntary AI competency benchmarks — what does it mean to "understand" a generative AI tool well enough to exercise professional judgment over its outputs? — and share them as educational guidance rather than enforceable mandates. Fourth, convene bar associations, legal aid organizations, and legal tech vendors to design AI intake tools calibrated to the specific needs of unrepresented civil litigants.

The legal profession's foundational commitment is to equal justice. The [access-to-justice gap documented by the Legal Services Corporation](https://justicegap.lsc.gov/) is a standing rebuke to that commitment. AI does not guarantee the gap closes — but it is the most powerful cost-compression tool the profession has ever had. Bar associations that layer restrictive governance on top of already-adequate professional responsibility frameworks will have made a choice about which clients they are protecting. It will not be the ones who need protection most.
