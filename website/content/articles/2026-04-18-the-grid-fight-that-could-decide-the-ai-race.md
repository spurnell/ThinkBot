# The Grid Fight That Could Decide the AI Race

America is losing the AI race one power application at a time. [Nearly half of planned data center projects are stalled](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/half-of-planned-us-data-center-builds-have-been-delayed-or-canceled-growth-limited-by-shortages-of-power-infrastructure-and-parts-from-china-the-ai-build-out-flips-the-breakers) not for want of chips, talent, or capital — but because they cannot get a grid interconnection. The binding constraint on the United States' ability to build the compute infrastructure that will determine AI leadership is not algorithmic, not diplomatic, and not financial. It is physical: the electricity grid.

That problem is now being decided in two venues simultaneously. The [Department of Energy has directed FERC to complete its advance rulemaking on large-load interconnection by April 30](https://www.gibsondunn.com/secretary-of-energy-directs-ferc-to-initiate-rulemaking-to-expedite-data-center-and-large-load-interconnection/) — twelve days from today. And Congress is advancing two diametrically opposed bills that would reshape, or shatter, the federal framework for how AI data centers access power. The choices made in the next few weeks will reverberate for a decade of AI development.

The stakes are not abstract. China is building compute infrastructure at state-directed speed, with centralized land allocation, subsidized grid access, and no permitting drag. The United States is building it through a patchwork of state utility commissions, FERC interconnection queues that run three to five years, and a Congress tempted to substitute legislative mandates for sound grid policy. Left uncorrected, that combination is a strategic liability.

## The Scale of the Problem

Start with the numbers, because they are extraordinary. [US data center power demand surged 22% in 2025](https://www.spglobal.com/energy/en/news-research/latest-news/electric-power/101425-data-center-grid-power-demand-to-rise-22-in-2025-nearly-triple-by-2030) alone, and [Bloom Energy's 2026 Power Report projects total data center load reaching 150 gigawatts by 2028](https://www.bloomenergy.com/wp-content/uploads/2026-power-report.pdf). For reference, the entire installed generating capacity of the United Kingdom is roughly 90 GW. The United States is being asked to build, in two years, the equivalent of the UK's entire grid — and dedicate it substantially to AI compute.

The queue problem compounds this. FERC's interconnection backlog has become a national embarrassment: [projects representing over 2,600 GW of proposed generation and load capacity are waiting in interconnection queues, with average wait times approaching five years](https://rmi.org/interconnection-reform-ai-data-centers-generator-queues/). Construction timelines that should run eighteen months are stretching to four or five years. California projects are stretching beyond nine.

This is a regulatory failure with strategic consequences. Every month of delay in bringing a large-scale AI training facility online is a month of competitive ground ceded to adversaries who face no such friction. Grid capacity is not a background condition for the AI race — it is a front-line battleground.

## Two Bills, Two Wrong Answers

Congress has noticed the problem. Unfortunately, the legislative responses on offer range from counterproductive to catastrophically wrong.

**The GRID Act (S. 3852)**, introduced by Senators Blumenthal and Hawley, would mandate full off-grid self-sufficiency for any new facility drawing 20 megawatts or more, backed by [$1 million-per-day penalties for noncompliance](https://tech-insider.org/senate-grid-act-data-center-energy-regulation-2026/). The theory is that large loads that generate their own power cannot shift costs to ratepayers or destabilize the grid. The practice would be something far worse.

Private generation at 20 MW scale — the threshold for a modest data center, far below the gigawatt-class facilities hyperscalers are building — requires either captive nuclear capacity (which takes a decade to permit and build), large-scale gas generation (which invites a separate set of regulatory battles), or battery installations that do not exist at the required scale. The [CSIS analysis of the FERC large-load proposal](https://www.csis.org/analysis/whats-stake-fercs-large-load-proposal) makes clear that the economics of co-location and grid-connected operation are far superior to isolated self-generation for most operators. Imposing a statutory mandate changes none of that underlying math — it simply prices out everyone without existing captive generation.

More importantly, the GRID Act is structurally anti-competitive. The facilities that can comply with a mandatory off-grid requirement are those that already have captive generation — the hyperscalers with utility-scale solar farms, negotiated nuclear power purchase agreements, and dedicated transmission corridors. Microsoft, Google, and Amazon can write the check. A mid-tier AI compute provider, a university research computing center, or a defense contractor standing up a classified AI facility cannot. The GRID Act, dressed in the language of grid protection, functions as a moat for the largest incumbents and a barrier to new entrants. That is not grid policy — it is industrial concentration by regulatory mandate.

**The DATA Act** (Senator Cotton) goes in the opposite direction, [exempting isolated large loads from FERC jurisdiction entirely](https://www.utilitydive.com/news/senate-bill-exempts-fully-isolated-large-loads-from-ferc-doe-regulation/809330/). The appeal is obvious: if federal interconnection rules are creating bottlenecks, removing federal jurisdiction removes the bottleneck. But wholesale deregulation here creates a different kind of problem — a jurisdictional vacuum that would be immediately contested in court, produce inconsistent state-level outcomes, and strand investment as legal uncertainty accumulates. The [Data Center Dynamics analysis of the bill](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/us-senator-proposes-bill-permitting-ai-data-centers-to-bypass-federal-power-rules-via-off-grid-energy-infrastructure-development/) notes that the exemption applies to "isolated" loads — a term that will be litigated extensively and defined inconsistently across jurisdictions.

The DATA Act solves the permitting drag problem by eliminating the federal framework entirely. That is not the right answer. Federal jurisdiction over large-load interconnection exists for good reasons: interstate grid stability is not a problem any single state can solve unilaterally, and the investment signals for transmission infrastructure require regulatory certainty that only federal coordination can provide.

## The Right Framework: Let FERC Finish Its Work

The [DOE's directive to FERC](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/doe-directs-ferc-accelerate-interconnection-data-centers) and [FERC's subsequent order to PJM and other grid operators](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-directs-nations-largest-grid-operator-create-new-rules-embrace-innovation-and) to develop large-load rules represent exactly the right policy instrument — applied by the right institution, with the right incentive structure. The April 30 deadline reflects a deliberate DOE judgment that the grid interconnection problem is urgent enough to demand expedited rulemaking, with a final rule to follow in the coming months.

The FERC large-load framework has four features that make it superior to either legislative alternative:

**Participant funding protects ratepayers.** The leading objection to data center grid access — that hyperscalers shift infrastructure costs to residential ratepayers — is addressed directly by requiring participant funding for interconnection upgrades triggered by large-load requests. The GRID Act's mandatory off-grid requirement is a sledgehammer for a problem the rulemaking addresses with precision. If the concern is ratepayer cost-shifting, the FERC rule solves it without mandating uneconomic private generation.

**Binding study timelines are the biggest acceleration lever.** The interconnection queue backlog is primarily a process problem, not a physical infrastructure problem. Current queues run years because they are managed sequentially, with each new request triggering re-studies of the entire affected system. Under [FERC's Order No. 2023 cluster framework](https://www.ferc.gov/explainer-interconnection-final-rule) — now being extended to large loads — transmission providers face mandatory 150-day cluster study windows and escalating daily financial penalties for missed deadlines. This structural shift treats study delays as a cost failure rather than an administrative inconvenience. Grid operators who have historically faced no consequences for slow processing now face real financial exposure for each day of delay.

**Co-location flexibility unlocks stranded assets.** One of the underappreciated features of the FERC framework is its treatment of co-location — situations where a data center and a generating facility are physically adjacent and can operate without using the broader transmission network. [FERC's directive to PJM](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-directs-nations-largest-grid-operator-create-new-rules-embrace-innovation-and) explicitly addresses co-location arrangements, creating a regulatory pathway that allows the economics of self-generation to work where they actually pencil out — without mandating them everywhere regardless of economics.

**Federal jurisdiction ends the state patchwork.** This may be the most important feature of the FERC framework. [Twenty-seven states are advancing divergent data center energy mandates](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/4/14/federal-ai-data-center-policy-meets-resistance-from-state-lawmakers) — some requiring renewable energy sourcing percentages, some imposing efficiency standards, some creating permitting processes that duplicate or conflict with FERC review. A project that has cleared FERC interconnection may still be held up by a state-level proceeding running on a different schedule, with different evidentiary standards, and potentially inconsistent outcomes.

Federal coordination via the FERC rulemaking is both constitutionally appropriate — interstate transmission and wholesale power markets are squarely within federal jurisdiction — and practically urgent. The state fragmentation problem in data center energy policy mirrors the patchwork that has already emerged in AI regulation: fifty different regulatory regimes, each individually plausible in its own terms, collectively producing a compliance maze that delays investment and advantages large incumbents who can afford legal teams in every jurisdiction.

## The Competitive Dimension

None of this analysis is complete without confronting the strategic context. China's compute infrastructure buildout is proceeding at a pace that reflects centralized state direction. [Goldman Sachs estimates that China's AI providers will invest $70 billion in data centers in 2026 alone](https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/chinas-ai-providers-expected-to-invest-70-billion-dollars-in-data-centers-amid-overseas-expansion), backed by state-allocated land, subsidized grid access, and compressed environmental review timelines. [RAND's analysis of China's AI industrial policy](https://www.rand.org/pubs/perspectives/PEA4012-1.html) documents how Beijing has treated compute infrastructure as a strategic input deserving the same state attention as highway networks or port capacity.

This matters because the compute bottleneck is real. Access to large-scale training compute is a primary determinant of frontier model capability, and the facilities being planned today will host the training runs that produce the AI systems of 2028 and 2030. A data center delayed by two years of interconnection queue is not an abstract regulatory inefficiency — it is a concrete setback in the development of capabilities that will shape economic competitiveness and national security for decades.

The United States' answer to China's centralized buildout cannot be to replicate centralization. It should be to remove regulatory friction while preserving the market-based incentive structure that drives efficient capital allocation. The FERC large-load rulemaking does exactly this: it streamlines the federal interconnection process without picking winners, requires participants to fund the infrastructure they need without shifting costs to ratepayers, and creates flexibility for co-location arrangements where they work economically.

The GRID Act, by contrast, imposes a technology mandate that only the largest players can sustain — concentrating AI compute infrastructure in the hands of the hyperscalers while regulatory friction eliminates everyone else. That outcome deepens the concentration problem in AI infrastructure rather than reversing it.

## What Congress Should Do

The FERC April 30 deadline is twelve days away. The most important thing Congress can do right now is nothing — let the rulemaking proceed. But if Congress is going to act, the right actions are clear:

**Fund grid modernization through DOE programs.** The transmission buildout required to support 150 GW of data center load by 2028 will require substantial capital investment. [DOE's Title 17 Clean Energy Financing program](https://www.energy.gov/lpo/title-17-clean-energy-financing) and the [Transmission Facilitation Program](https://www.energy.gov/gdo/transmission-facilitation-program) are the appropriate instruments for catalyzing private transmission investment at scale. This is basic research infrastructure funding, not picking winners.

**Clarify FERC's large-load jurisdiction to foreclose legal challenge.** The DATA Act's premise — that FERC's jurisdiction over isolated large loads is contestable — creates legal risk that will delay investment regardless of whether the Act passes. Congress could preemptively clarify that FERC's large-load rules apply to any facility that physically connects to the interstate transmission grid, eliminating the ambiguity without the DATA Act's deregulatory overreach.

**Reject the GRID Act.** The mandatory off-grid requirement is not a workable grid protection mechanism — it is a compliance burden that advantages incumbents, delays construction, and substitutes a technology mandate for sound interconnection policy. The ratepayer protection problem the GRID Act purports to solve is already addressed by the FERC rule's participant funding requirement.

**Encourage voluntary industry commitments on ratepayer cost-shifting.** Industry has a clear interest in demonstrating that large-load data center development does not impose costs on residential ratepayers. Voluntary commitments — backed by participant funding agreements in interconnection contracts — are a more credible and flexible protection mechanism than a statutory mandate, and they remove the political argument that drives legislation like the GRID Act.

## Conclusion

The AI race is a race to build physical infrastructure: chips, fiber, and above all, power. The United States has the capital, the talent, and the technology to win that race. What it lacks — right now, in April 2026 — is a coherent federal framework for connecting large compute loads to the grid at the speed the competitive moment demands.

The FERC large-load rulemaking is that framework. It gets the incentive structure right, protects ratepayers without mandating uneconomic technology choices, ends the state patchwork, and imposes binding timelines that treat interconnection delays as the strategic failures they are. Congress should let FERC finish its work, fund the transmission infrastructure the buildout requires, and put the GRID Act aside.

The grid fight is the AI race. It is time to treat it that way.
