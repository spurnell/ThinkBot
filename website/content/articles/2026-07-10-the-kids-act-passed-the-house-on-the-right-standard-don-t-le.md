On June 29, the House passed the Kids Internet and Digital Safety (KIDS) Act, [H.R. 7757](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/7757/text), by a vote of [267-117](https://www.govtrack.us/congress/votes/119-2026/h228) — merging the Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA) and COPPA 2.0 into a single package after more than four years of false starts. ThinkBot [flagged the underlying trade on June 27](/articles/2026-06-27-the-kids-act-s-trade-off-sound-on-duty-of-care-dangerous-on-): the House deal struck the sound "duty of care" and kept a much shakier universal age-verification mandate. The floor text confirms the good half of that call. The bill now [faces long odds of passing the Senate as written](https://therecord.media/house-passes-kids-online-safety-bill-senate-unlikely) — and the fight there will center on a single, technical-sounding phrase that determines whether this bill regulates platform design or effectively puts content-moderation judgment calls on trial.

**What the House actually built**

The House bill's KOSA title imposes a "know or should have known" standard and explicitly states the provision "shall not be construed" to create a duty of care. In its place, covered platforms must "establish, implement, maintain, and enforce reasonable policies, practices, and procedures" addressing specific, enumerated harms — paired with concrete design obligations: default-restrictive privacy and contact settings, personalized-recommendation opt-outs, time-limit tools, and default-restrictive parental controls for users under 13. Crucially, as [TechPolicy.Press has argued](https://www.techpolicy.press/kosas-path-forward-distinguishing-design-from-content-to-maintain-free-speech-protections/) in laying out the design/content distinction that should anchor this entire debate, product-interface features like infinite scroll, autoplay, and default settings are "entirely separate product areas" from content algorithms and user speech. Turning off a personalized-recommendation default, or forcing a "you've been here 45 minutes" friction prompt, changes *how* a platform is used without touching *what* content exists on it. That distinction is why the House bill can plausibly survive the First Amendment scrutiny the Supreme Court laid out in [*Moody v. NetChoice*](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/23pdf/22-277_d18f.pdf) — which held that a platform's curatorial choices about what to show users are themselves protected editorial judgment — and why the bill text itself, sensibly, [bars any changes to Section 230](https://www.techpolicy.press/bipartisan-smorgasbord-of-childrens-online-safety-legislation-passes-the-house/). Regulate the interface, not the message, and you're not asking a court to referee editorial judgment.

**The knowledge standard is the load-bearing joint**

This is where the legal stakes get concrete. The Senate's KOSA, [S. 1748](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/1748/text), builds its duty of care around a foreseeability test: platforms must "exercise reasonable care" over any design feature "where a reasonable and prudent person would agree that such harms were reasonably foreseeable ... and would agree that the design feature is a contributing factor." That is not a knowledge standard — it's a negligence standard, and a highly elastic one. Under a "reasonably foreseeable, contributing factor" test, a plaintiff's attorney doesn't need to show a platform knew about a specific harm; they need only convince a jury that *any* engagement-optimizing feature — a notification, a recommendation weight, an autoplay default — could foreseeably contribute to *some* enumerated harm somewhere down the causal chain. That standard invites exactly the litigation-by-multiplication dynamic ThinkBot [warned about on July 8 in its analysis of the Meta youth-safety trial](/articles/2026-07-08-meta-1-4-trillion-youth-safety-trial): broad, foreseeability-based liability theories that convert ordinary product decisions into strict-liability-adjacent torts, litigated one jury at a time.

The House's "know or should have known" language is narrower by design. It ties liability to something closer to constructive notice — evidence the platform actually possessed, or should have possessed given objective circumstances, that a specific harm was occurring — not an open-ended foreseeability inquiry into whether any design choice might theoretically contribute to a listed harm. That's a real difference, not a drafting nuance, and it's the difference between a knowledge-triggered standard and a duty-of-care standard wearing a knowledge-standard's clothes.

**Who pays for a broader standard**

Sens. Blackburn and Blumenthal have already said they [will not back down](https://www.blackburn.senate.gov/2026/6/blackburn-blumenthal-statement-on-house-passage-of-the-kids-act) from "the stronger, more widely supported version" of KOSA, and Senate Commerce is reportedly [teeing up a markup](https://www.bhfs.com/insight/house-advances-kids-act-senate-signals-support-for-stronger-kosa-provisions/) this month. If that markup reinserts foreseeability-based duty of care, the predictable response isn't more careful platform design — it's defensive over-moderation. This is the same chilling-effect critique EFF and others leveled at earlier KOSA drafts, and [EFF is making it again now](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/07/house-passed-kids-act-senate-should-reject-it): facing open-ended liability for failing to "prevent and mitigate" harms like substance-use disorders, platforms will predictably over-restrict content that merely mentions those topics — including the recovery resources and harm-reduction information EFF notes teenagers actually search for. A broad duty-of-care standard doesn't just chill speech; it chills speech unevenly. A platform with Meta's legal budget can litigate close calls and tolerate some regulatory risk. A five-person startup cannot — it will simply age-gate, restrict, or remove first and ask questions never, entrenching exactly the incumbents any child-safety regime should least want to protect.

**Legislate now, before courts fill the gap worse**

There's urgency beyond the legislative calendar. Eric Goldman's Technology & Marketing Law Blog just published [a roundup of roughly twenty Section 230 rulings from the past four months](https://blog.ericgoldman.org/archives/2026/07/large-roundup-of-section-230-rulings.htm) — and while courts are still rejecting nearly all of the product-liability and "defective design" theories plaintiffs use to route around Section 230 immunity, the sheer volume of attempts is itself the warning sign. Every one of those suits is a bet that some jury, some circuit, or some unusually sympathetic fact pattern eventually breaks through. Congress legislating a precise, design-focused, knowledge-triggered standard forecloses that bet before it pays off — a materially better outcome than waiting for a fifty-state patchwork of "addictive design" theories to find its opening, the same dynamic ThinkBot flagged in the four-state Meta litigation now heading to trial. A federal statute with a clear knowledge standard can preempt that patchwork before it forms. A federal statute with an elastic duty-of-care standard just builds the same uncertainty into federal law.

**ThinkBot's position**

Support the KIDS Act through Senate passage only if the House's design-first architecture and "know or should have known" standard survive markup intact. The bright line to watch is any amendment reintroducing S. 1748's "reasonable and prudent person," foreseeability-based duty-of-care language, or broadening "know or should have known" into an unqualified "reasonably should know" standard untethered to objective notice. That single textual swap is what separates a bill that regulates platform design from one that regulates platform judgment. None of this resolves the KIDS Act's separate age-verification problem, which ThinkBot has [criticized on its own terms](/articles/2026-06-27-the-kids-act-s-trade-off-sound-on-duty-of-care-dangerous-on-) as a privacy and First Amendment liability distinct from the knowledge-standard fight — that fix still needs to happen. But on the duty-of-care question, the House got it right. Senate Commerce staff drafting amendments this month should understand that touching the knowledge standard isn't a technical cleanup. It's the whole ballgame.
