Six weeks after President Trump signed [NSPM-11](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/06/national-security-presidential-memorandum-nspm-11/) on June 5, 2026, the numbers look like a policy success story. Roughly [5,000 new firms joined the defense industrial base in FY2024 and another 5,000 in FY2025](https://www.csis.org/analysis/industrial-base-wartime-footing-progress-report) — a genuine reversal after years of decline — and venture capital has followed, chasing what Secretary Pete Hegseth has branded the "[Warfighting Acquisition System](https://www.defensenews.com/pentagon/2025/11/07/hegseth-to-slash-red-tape-empower-program-heads-in-acquisition-revamp/)." NSPM-11 gave that overhaul its AI-specific mandate: within 120 days, agencies must "review and update procurement processes to ensure the rapid onboarding of the most advanced AI models from multiple vendors," organized around four pillars — Adoption, Adaptation, Assurance, and Accountability.

This is, in large part, the right template. It is also not the whole story. Nontraditional defense obligations have grown from [$60.9 billion in FY2015 to $122.6 billion in FY2025](https://www.csis.org/analysis/industrial-base-wartime-footing-progress-report) — real progress, but nowhere near proportional to the doubling of entrants. Even [CSIS's own progress report](https://www.csis.org/analysis/industrial-base-wartime-footing-progress-report) tempers the entrant numbers with a caution worth repeating: "industrial plans are not the same as results, and isolated bursts of investment are not the same as institutionalized industrial power." Layer on Sen. Elizabeth Warren's [July 7 letters](https://www.warren.senate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/warren-presses-dod-for-answers-on-military-ai-contracts-demands-release-of-ai-contracts/) demanding that seven AI firms and the Pentagon disclose the full text of their contracts, and NSPM-11's own clause authorizing "[termination for default or for convenience](https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/national-security-memorandum-aims-to-accelerate-deployment-of-ai-and-streamline-procurement-aligned-to-administration-policies)" of any vendor that "limits how the government uses their products," and a second story emerges alongside the first: a reform built to tear down entry barriers is now generating new ones, just further along the pipeline.

## The reform that got government-as-customer right

Start with what NSPM-11 and the Hegseth acquisition reforms got right, because it's substantial. For years, ThinkBot has argued that the federal government's most effective innovation-policy lever isn't subsidy or mandate — it's procurement. When government acts as a demanding, fast-moving customer rather than a prescriptive regulator, it pulls private capital and technical talent toward priority missions without picking winners in advance. NSPM-11 and the parallel [Warfighting Acquisition System](https://www.nationaldefensemagazine.org/articles/2026/7/14/commentary-pentagon-should-take-advantage-of-expanded-acquisition-authority) do exactly that: expanded Other Transaction Authority, now extended by [Section 1823 of the FY2026 NDAA](https://www.nationaldefensemagazine.org/articles/2026/7/14/commentary-pentagon-should-take-advantage-of-expanded-acquisition-authority) to cover production contracts rather than just prototypes; a shift from cost-plus to fixed-price, time-indexed contracts that reward early delivery; and a mandate to onboard multiple frontier AI vendors simultaneously rather than sole-sourcing a single incumbent's model. Compare that to the FTC's and the European Commission's default instinct on emerging technology — write the rule before the market exists, then litigate compliance for a decade. NSPM-11's model is ex-post and outcome-focused: let vendors compete for the mission, measure delivery, and adjust. Other agencies experimenting with AI procurement should be studying this, not the DMA.

The entrant numbers back this up. Roughly 10,000 new defense-tech firms over two years, [a record $35.6 billion in defense-tech venture funding in the first half of 2026 alone](https://news.crunchbase.com/defense-tech/startup-venture-funding-all-time-record-ai-anduril/) chasing Commercial Solutions Openings and OTA vehicles, and a Pentagon acquisition workforce explicitly instructed to prefer commercial products over bespoke, requirements-heavy development. That is a textbook demonstration that procurement rules — not subsidies, not five-year technology roadmaps drafted by a planning office — are the innovation-policy instrument government actually controls well.

## Where the reform stalls: entrants aren't converting to contracts

But market entry is not market competition, and here the data get uncomfortable. Ten thousand new entrants sounds like a competitive market being built. It is also consistent with a defense-tech ecosystem where most new firms win small pilot contracts, Commercial Solutions Openings, or SBIR-style awards — real, but marginal relative to the multi-hundred-million and multi-billion-dollar programs where incumbents remain the default awardee. CSIS's own analysts flag exactly this gap: entrant counts and nontraditional-obligation growth are not, in their words, "the same as institutionalized industrial power" until they translate into sustained, program-of-record spending rather than one-off pilots.

This is not evidence of a conspiracy, and it would be a mistake to read it that way. It's a structural story about what actually gates large-dollar defense contracts, and procurement-speed reform alone doesn't touch most of it:

- **Past-performance requirements.** Large solicitations routinely score bidders on prior contract performance — a criterion that mechanically favors firms that already hold contracts, regardless of technical merit. A five-person AI lab with a better model has no past-performance record to point to.
- **Security clearance lag.** The [Section 809 Panel](https://discover.dtic.mil/section-809-panel/) — the DoD's own blue-ribbon acquisition reform commission — flagged years ago that the Pentagon defaults to blanket, maximal clearance requirements rather than tailoring clearance levels to what a given contract actually needs. That default disproportionately burdens firms too small to carry a bench of cleared staff on overhead before winning a single award.
- **Classified-network integration costs.** Frontier AI models don't run on unclassified infrastructure by default; integrating into SIPR- or JWICS-adjacent environments requires accreditation, secure facilities, and engineering investment that established primes have already sunk and startups have not.

None of these are line items in NSPM-11. They are the actual bottleneck between "10,000 new entrants" and "10,000 new competitors for contract dollars," and no amount of onboarding-speed reform closes that gap unless it's addressed directly — through tailored (not blanket) clearance requirements, past-performance scoring that credits technical demonstrations over incumbency, and DoD-subsidized accreditation pathways for classified-network integration, an idea the [Section 809 Panel](https://discover.dtic.mil/section-809-panel/) and successive nontraditional-contractor working groups have floated for years without full adoption.

## The new risk: assurance and accountability as a compliance moat

That would be the whole brief if NSPM-11 stopped at procurement speed. It doesn't. The memo's third and fourth pillars — Assurance and Accountability — require that all adopted AI systems be "[reliable, robust, steerable, and controllable](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/06/national-security-presidential-memorandum-nspm-11/)," operating under government approval and subject to strict controls against unauthorized modification. Paired with that is the memo's most consequential enforcement tool: agencies are directed to pursue "[termination for default or for convenience](https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/national-security-memorandum-aims-to-accelerate-deployment-of-ai-and-streamline-procurement-aligned-to-administration-policies)" against any vendor whose contract terms limit how the government can use its model.

Assurance and control over military AI systems is a legitimate government interest — this is not a case for lighter-touch security standards in national-security AI deployment. The problem is design, not intent. A uniform assurance obligation — the same documentation, auditability, red-teaming, and control-attestation regime applied to a five-person foundation-model startup and to a defense prime with an existing compliance division — does not fall evenly. The prime already employs the compliance staff, already holds the facility clearances, and already has a government-relations office fluent in translating "controllable and steerable" into a certification package. The startup does not, and building that capacity from scratch is exactly the kind of fixed cost that falls harder on small entrants than large ones — the same dynamic ThinkBot has flagged in uniform data-protection mandates like GDPR, just relocated from Brussels to the Pentagon.

The [Council on Foreign Relations](https://www.cfr.org/articles/what-trumps-national-security-ai-memo-gets-right-and-leaves-unresolved) has already flagged the underlying problem: NSPM-11's Accountability pillar anchors enforcement in the existing chain of command rather than a new oversight body, and outside experts cited in that analysis have questioned whether the current relationship between the White House and Congress gives that chain-of-command approach real teeth. ThinkBot's own reading of the text turns up a further gap: no scaled reporting tiers, and no threshold distinguishing a pilot contract from a program of record. That ambiguity cuts both ways. Left undefined, "assurance" could mean whatever a program office's risk-averse contracting officer decides it means, and the safest interpretation — for a contracting officer facing career risk, not commercial incentive — is to apply the maximal standard uniformly. That is how a speed reform quietly regrows the very barrier it was designed to remove, just moved from "can you enter" to "can you survive the paperwork."

## Congressional oversight faces the same design choice

Sen. Warren's July 7 letters to Google, SpaceX, Nvidia, Reflection, Microsoft, AWS, and Oracle, along with her [parallel letter to Secretary Hegseth](https://www.warren.senate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/warren-presses-dod-for-answers-on-military-ai-contracts-demands-release-of-ai-contracts/), demand full contract disclosure and raise real questions — about autonomous-weapons safeguards, about contractual restrictions on domestic surveillance uses, about a Pentagon that is [simultaneously taking equity stakes in the companies it contracts with](https://www.govexec.com/defense/2026/06/pentagons-growing-role-investor-scrutiny-capitol-hill/414304/). That oversight is legitimate and Congress should exercise it; a $122 billion shift in nontraditional obligations without transparent contract terms is exactly the kind of spending that deserves scrutiny.

But oversight, like assurance, has a design choice embedded in it. A disclosure regime built around "release the full contract" as a uniform demand imposes a materially different burden on a company with a dedicated federal-contracts legal team than on a startup whose entire legal function is one general counsel juggling IP filings, SEC exposure, and now congressional production requests. If disclosure obligations scale with contract dollar value and sensitivity — a tiered framework where a $2 million pilot OTA faces a lighter reporting burden than a $500 million program of record — oversight and dynamism coexist. If they don't, disclosure compliance becomes another fixed cost that only well-resourced primes can absorb without disruption, and the practical effect is the same as an assurance moat: incumbents shrug it off, entrants either avoid federal contracts or get quietly filtered out by their own compliance limitations.

## The metric that matters

The headline number in this story — 10,000 new defense-tech entrants — is the wrong one to chase if it isn't converting into contract-dollar competition. Entrant counts measure whether the door is open. They don't measure whether anyone gets past the front hallway. NSPM-11's procurement-speed reforms and Hegseth's OTA expansion opened the door; that's real progress worth defending against reflexive re-regulation. But if assurance mandates and disclosure obligations are applied as uniform, undifferentiated burdens rather than risk-proportionate ones scaled to contract size and sensitivity, the reform will still show up as a startup-formation success story in press releases while the contract-dollar data keeps telling the cautionary story CSIS is already documenting.

**Recommendations:**

1. **Tier assurance requirements to contract value and system sensitivity.** A pilot OTA integrating an off-the-shelf commercial model into an unclassified logistics tool should not face the same documentation burden as a program of record touching classified weapons-targeting systems. NSPM-11 implementing guidance should specify these tiers explicitly rather than leaving contracting officers to default to maximal compliance out of institutional risk-aversion.
2. **Fund clearance and classified-network accreditation pathways for small entrants**, following the Section 809 Panel's tailored-clearance recommendation, rather than leaving startups to absorb those fixed costs before they can compete for anything beyond unclassified pilots.
3. **Scale congressional disclosure obligations to contract size**, so oversight targets the multi-hundred-million-dollar programs where public accountability interest is highest, not a uniform production demand that a lean startup and a defense prime must meet identically.
4. **Track contract-dollar share by entrant cohort, not just entrant count**, as the actual success metric for NSPM-11 implementation. CSIS's obligations data should become a standing dashboard metric for the Section 809-successor oversight bodies and House/Senate Armed Services staff — the number that matters is whether new entrants are winning programs of record, not whether they're winning press mentions.

Speed got the Pentagon this far. Converting speed into a genuinely competitive AI supplier base requires getting the compliance architecture right — proportionate, tiered, and scaled to risk — rather than assuming the procurement clock alone finishes the job.
