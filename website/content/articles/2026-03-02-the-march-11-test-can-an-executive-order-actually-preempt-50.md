# The March 11 Test: Can an Executive Order Actually Preempt 50 State AI Laws?

Nine days from now, two federal deadlines arrive simultaneously — and neither will do what the Administration hopes.

On March 11, 2026, the Federal Trade Commission must publish a policy statement explaining how Section 5 of the FTC Act preempts state laws that require alterations to "truthful" AI outputs. That same day, the Commerce Department must release its list of state AI laws deemed sufficiently "onerous" to warrant referral to the DOJ's [AI Litigation Task Force](https://www.paulhastings.com/insights/client-alerts/president-trump-signs-executive-order-challenging-state-ai-laws). Both deadlines flow from Trump's [December 11, 2025 executive order](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/) directing federal agencies to establish national supremacy over AI governance.

The Administration is right about the problem. It is reaching for tools that cannot solve it.

## The Compliance Fragmentation Crisis Is Real

Before dismissing the Administration's objectives, consider what the alternative looks like. In 2025, [all 50 states introduced AI legislation](https://www.ncsl.org/technology-and-communication/artificial-intelligence-2025-legislation) — over 1,100 bills in a single year, with roughly 40 states enacting at least one AI law. These laws span algorithmic bias audits, automated decision-making disclosures, synthetic media labeling requirements, and civil liability regimes. They do not form a coherent system. They form a compliance labyrinth.

The innovation tax imposed by this fragmentation falls hardest not on Big Tech giants — firms with armies of compliance lawyers — but on the startups and mid-size AI companies that lack those resources. [BSA | The Software Alliance](https://www.bsa.org/news-events/news/2025-state-ai-wave-building-after-700-bills-in-2024) has documented the cost-prohibitive compliance challenges inconsistent state requirements impose on smaller firms. The Computer & Communications Industry Association estimates that federal preemption could yield a [$600 billion economic dividend](https://ccianet.org/articles/600-billion-ai-abundance-dividend-from-federal-preemption-of-state-laws/) through 2035 — a figure that reflects how severely fragmentation constrains AI deployment at scale.

A company building a general-purpose AI system cannot tailor its model architecture to satisfy Idaho's bias audit methodology, California's disclosure requirements, Colorado's risk-tier classifications, and New York's automated employment decision rules simultaneously. The result is not safer AI. It is slower AI, or AI deployed only in states large enough to justify the compliance overhead. Federal uniformity is not a regulatory preference. At this stage of AI development, it is a strategic imperative.

## Why the EO's Legal Mechanisms Fall Short

None of that changes the fundamental legal reality: the instruments the Administration has deployed cannot preempt state law.

A policy statement is non-binding guidance. It reflects an agency's views; it does not carry the force of law, and it does not displace duly enacted state statutes. To create binding preemptive effect through the FTC Act, the Commission would need to promulgate a formal trade regulation rule under heightened [Magnuson-Moss Act procedures](https://www.techpolicy.press/the-ftcs-ai-preemption-authority-is-limited/) — advance notice, full comment periods, regulatory analyses, fact-finding hearings. A policy statement issued to meet a White House deadline clears none of those bars.

The Commerce Department's list carries even less legal weight. It is a political document. The DOJ Litigation Task Force can bring lawsuits — but litigation is slow, expensive, and far from guaranteed to succeed.

Courts apply a strong presumption against preemption in domains traditionally regulated by the states. In [*Wyeth v. Levine* (2009)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/555/555/), the Supreme Court held that federal agency approval alone does not automatically displace state law remedies — Congress must speak clearly if it intends preemption. AI governance sits squarely in the consumer protection and civil rights domains where that presumption applies with full force. The Administration's best legal analogy, [*Geier v. American Honda Motor Co.* (2000)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/529/861/) — in which federal regulatory flexibility preempted a state tort mandate — required an actual federal regulation reflecting a deliberate policy balance. A deadline-driven policy statement is not that.

## The "Truthful Outputs" Theory Won't Survive a Courtroom

The Administration's most aggressive legal move is the FTC's forthcoming argument that state-mandated bias mitigation is *per se* deceptive under Section 5 — on the theory that requiring AI systems to alter their outputs makes those outputs less "truthful."

This theory conflates statistical correction with consumer deception. Bias mitigation — adjusting a model's output distributions to reduce disparate impact on protected classes — is not the same as a company making false factual claims to consumers. Section 5's prohibition on deceptive acts targets misrepresentations of material fact, not methodological choices in algorithmic design. [Legal analysts tracking the EO](https://iapp.org/news/a/a-view-from-dc-can-the-ftc-preempt-state-ai-laws-) note that the FTC Act does not expressly preempt state law and does not occupy the entire field of consumer protection. Conflict preemption requires an actual, operational conflict — not a philosophical disagreement about regulatory design.

A [bipartisan coalition of 36 state attorneys general](https://www.naag.org/press-releases/bipartisan-coalition-of-36-state-attorneys-general-opposes-federal-ban-on-state-ai-laws/) has formally opposed the preemption effort, and California AG Rob Bonta has signaled the state is prepared to litigate. This is not a fight the Administration can win through guidance documents.

## The Legislative Path Forward

The Senate voted [99-1 to strip a 10-year AI moratorium](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/2025/7/senate-strikes-ai-moratorium-from-budget-reconciliation-bill-in-overwhelming-99-1-vote/8415a728-fd1d-4269-98ac-101d1d0c71e0) from last year's budget reconciliation package — a bipartisan rejection driven by civil society concerns and Republican governors who viewed the provision as federal overreach. The moratorium is dead. But that vote does not mean federal inaction is acceptable.

The moratorium failed because it was blunt — a decade-long freeze with no federal standards to replace what it displaced. A targeted preemption provision with genuine federal standards is a different and far more defensible legislative ask. Congress must pass a narrow, risk-tiered federal AI framework that establishes enforceable baseline standards and preempts inconsistent state regimes through Supremacy Clause authority — the only mechanism that can actually do the job. The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/artificial-intelligence) provides the technical architecture for such an approach. What is missing is the congressional will to codify it.

## Watch the Carve-Outs

The December EO explicitly exempts child safety, state procurement rules, and data center infrastructure. These carve-outs were the price of political viability — but they also define the real battleground. When Commerce releases its list on March 11, the critical question will not be which laws appear on it. It will be which laws are conspicuously absent. Those absences will signal where the Administration believes it cannot win, and where states retain room to build regulatory programs that could later expand.

## March 11 Is a Starting Gun

The twin deadlines arriving next week are not a resolution of the AI preemption debate. They are the opening salvo in a legal and political conflict that will play out over years in federal courts, state legislatures, and — if Congress finally acts — on the Senate floor.

The Administration has correctly identified compliance fragmentation as an existential threat to US AI leadership. Nonbinding guidance and agency lists cannot preempt 40 state legislatures. Only Congress can — and it must.
