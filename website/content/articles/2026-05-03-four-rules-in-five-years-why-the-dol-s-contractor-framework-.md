The comment period on the Department of Labor's proposed independent contractor rule closed April 28. For the next sixty to ninety days, DOL staff will process thousands of public submissions before issuing a final rule that will govern the legal status of tens of millions of American workers. That window — between comment close and final rule — is precisely when the policy argument matters most.

The proposed rule should be finalized without weakening amendments. The two-factor economic reality test — centering on worker control and profit-or-loss opportunity — is the correct analytical framework for a platform-mediated labor market that has outgrown the 20th-century employee/contractor binary. More importantly, the rule alone is not enough. The deeper problem afflicting independent contractor policy is durability, and only a congressional statute resolves it.

## The Pendulum Problem Is the Real Innovation Tax

The most important thing to understand about the 2026 proposed rule is that it is the fourth distinct federal classification standard in five years. In January 2021, the Trump DOL published a streamlined "core factor" rule that elevated control and profit-or-loss opportunity over other considerations. The incoming Biden administration promptly delayed, then rescinded it. In January 2024, DOL finalized a six-factor, totality-of-the-circumstances analysis that deliberately avoided weighting any single factor — [a framework that drew five separate federal lawsuits](https://www.wageandhourlawupdate.com/2024/03/articles/department-of-labor/independent-contractor-rule-takes-effect-but-legal-challenges-mount/) before it had been in effect a year. The Trump administration returned to office in January 2025 and immediately stopped enforcing the 2024 rule. On February 26, 2026, the [DOL announced the current proposed rule](https://www.dol.gov/newsroom/releases/whd/whd20260226), which would rescind the Biden standard and replace it with a framework [closely resembling the 2021 approach](https://www.paulhastings.com/insights/client-alerts/dol-proposes-to-rescind-biden-era-independent-contractor-rule-and-restore-core-factor-analysis) — this time with a five-factor test in which the two core factors carry decisive weight when they point in the same direction.

Four frameworks. Five years. The cost is not any single rule — it is the rulemaking carousel itself.

For a platform company deciding whether to expand into a new market, the relevant question is not "what does the DOL rule say today?" but "what will the DOL rule say in three years?" When the answer changes with each administration, expansion decisions get deferred, compliance infrastructure gets written off, and contracts get structured around legal ambiguity rather than business logic. For a freelance graphic designer or independent logistics driver, the uncertainty is equally corrosive: classification status determines whether a working relationship is legally viable, whether a platform can extend certain arrangements at all, and whether the financial structure of their work survives the next election cycle.

This is industrial policy by regulatory attrition — and it has no democratic mandate. No Congress passed a law establishing the six-factor test. No Congress passed a law repealing it. The entire cycle runs through notice-and-comment rulemaking, which means it resets every time the White House changes hands. The argument for finalizing this rule must also be an argument for removing the question from the regulatory carousel permanently.

## The Two-Factor Core Test Is the Right Framework

The proposed rule reinstates a five-factor economic reality test, but — crucially — establishes that two "core" factors carry dispositive weight: the nature and degree of control over the work, and the worker's opportunity for profit or loss based on initiative or investment. [If both core factors point toward independent contractor status](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/flsa/misclassi­fication/2026rulemaking), there is a "substantial likelihood" the classification is correct. Secondary factors — skill required, permanence of the relationship, and integration into the potential employer's production — enter the analysis only when the core factors are inconclusive.

This is a significant improvement over the Biden 2024 rule, and not merely because it is more favorable to platforms. It is better law. The six-factor totality approach, by design, made every classification decision a multi-variable judgment call with no predetermined weighting. That structure did not protect workers — it produced litigation. When no factor is controlling and all facts must be balanced, the party with the superior litigation budget wins. The Biden rule perversely benefited plaintiff attorneys at the expense of both workers and platforms: it was simultaneously harder to comply with and harder to enforce.

Control and profit-or-loss opportunity are not arbitrary choices. They are the economic substance of the independent contractor relationship. A worker who sets her own hours, declines clients, prices her services, and invests in her own tools is, as a matter of economic reality, in business for herself — regardless of whether she uses a platform to find clients. A worker who must show up at set times, follow detailed instructions on method and process, and cannot share in upside or mitigate downside is economically dependent on the engaging entity, regardless of what the contract says. The core factors track this distinction cleanly. The secondary factors provide a tiebreaker when the picture is genuinely mixed.

Critics argue the two-factor framework is too easy to game. The objection is weaker than it sounds. Gaming control and profit-or-loss opportunity requires restructuring the actual economic relationship, not just rewriting a contract. A platform that wants its workers to qualify as independent contractors under the proposed rule must actually give them control and genuine upside — which is precisely what a healthy independent contractor relationship looks like. That outcome is the goal of the rule, not a vulnerability in it.

## Flexible Work Is a Revealed Preference, Not Exploitation

The strongest argument for the proposed rule is not doctrinal — it is empirical. American workers are choosing independent arrangements at scale, and when surveyed, they say so clearly.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics [Contingent Worker Supplement](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/conemp.htm), based on a nationally representative survey of households conducted in July 2023 and released in November 2024, found that **80.3 percent of independent contractors prefer their work arrangement** over traditional employment. Only 8.3 percent said they would prefer a traditional job. That gap — 80 to 8 — is not a rounding error. It reflects a consistent pattern across multiple years of data: workers who choose independent contracting, by and large, genuinely prefer it.

This is not a platform industry talking point. [ADP Research's analysis of gig economy labor markets](https://www.adpresearch.com/the-gig-economy-a-tale-of-two-labor-markets/) finds that full-time independent workers — those working more than 15 hours per week on independent arrangements — grew 6.5 percent in 2024, reaching 27.7 million people. [According to Upwork's 2026 freelance workforce data](https://www.upwork.com/resources/freelancing-stats), approximately 76.4 million Americans freelanced in 2025, representing 36 percent of the total U.S. workforce. The population of high-earning independents (over $100,000 annually) [grew from 3 million in 2020 to 5.6 million by 2025](https://www.demandsage.com/gig-economy-statistics/).

Mandatory reclassification does not expand the option set for these workers — it forecloses it. If a worker prefers independence, portable schedule control, and the ability to serve multiple clients over employment benefits and the protections of FLSA, a reclassification mandate does not improve her position; it eliminates the arrangement she chose. The paternalism is the point, and it should be named as such.

The appropriate response to workers who lack benefits is not to eliminate the work model. It is to decouple benefits from employment classification — precisely what the Senate HELP Committee's [portable benefits package](https://www.help.senate.gov/rep/newsroom/press/chair-cassidy-scott-paul-release-legislative-package-empowering-independent-workers-to-access-portable-benefits) introduced by Senators Cassidy, Scott, and Paul is designed to do.

## The Gig Economy Is Infrastructure

The frame of "worker misclassification" undersells what is actually at stake. The independent contractor model is not a classification edge case to be litigated into extinction — it is load-bearing economic infrastructure.

Independent contracting underpins last-mile logistics for e-commerce and same-day delivery, healthcare staffing in rural and underserved markets where hospitals cannot sustain full-time equivalents, professional services (design, development, copywriting, consulting) for the small businesses that cannot afford full-time headcount, and skilled trades work across construction and manufacturing. The total income generated through freelance and independent contracting arrangements reached approximately [$1.5 trillion in 2026](https://www.demandsage.com/gig-economy-statistics/), equivalent to roughly five percent of GDP — on par with the construction and transportation sectors combined.

Regulatory attrition of the independent contractor model is de facto industrial policy. It reallocates economic activity from flexible platform-mediated arrangements toward traditional employment — not because the market has failed, but because rulemakers prefer it. There is no democratic mandate for that reallocation, and no honest accounting of the costs it imposes on the workers and businesses that depend on flexible arrangements.

The rural dimension deserves specific attention. In markets where population density does not support full-time employment in specialized roles — agricultural consulting, physical therapy, specialized equipment repair — the independent contractor model is often the only economically viable staffing arrangement. Federal reclassification mandates that make sense in San Francisco impose unworkable compliance costs in markets where they were never designed to apply.

## California's Warning

The predictive evidence from California's Assembly Bill 5 — the country's most aggressive worker reclassification law — should inform every aspect of the current federal debate. A [Mercatus Center study](https://www.mercatus.org/research/working-papers/assessing-impact-worker-reclassification-employment-outcomes-post) by Palagashvili, Suarez, Kaiser, and Melo found that self-employment in affected occupations declined by **10.5 percent** following AB5's enactment, while overall employment in those occupations fell by 4.4 percent. Traditional W-2 employment did not increase to compensate — the work simply disappeared. On average, one in ten self-employed workers in affected California occupations lost their independent work opportunity, with no corresponding traditional job to replace it.

California voters delivered their own verdict. In November 2020, [Proposition 22 passed with 59 percent of the vote](https://ballotpedia.org/California_Proposition_22,_App-Based_Drivers_as_Contractors_and_Labor_Policies_Initiative_(2020)), exempting app-based gig platforms from AB5's most aggressive requirements. Prop 22 was financed by platform companies, but its margin of victory came from workers. The political economy of reclassification is not what its proponents expect.

The AB5 experience also generated an immediate legislative cleanup: by September 2020, California lawmakers had to pass Assembly Bill 2257 to expand exemptions for dozens of professions that had been inadvertently captured. Broad-based classification mandates routinely harm the workers they are designed to protect, require emergency legislative intervention, and generate legal uncertainty for years afterward. The federal government should study California, not emulate it.

## The Durable Fix: Statute

The strongest argument for finalizing the proposed rule is also an argument for going further. Executive rulemaking cannot entrench a classification standard. The next administration — regardless of party — will face the same temptation to reverse it that every previous administration has faced. The rulemaking carousel will continue until Congress removes the question from it.

The 119th Congress has a legislative vehicle. [H.R. 100, the Protect the Gig Economy Act of 2025](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/100/all-info), and the Senate HELP Committee's [portable benefits and safe harbor package](https://www.help.senate.gov/rep/newsroom/press/chair-cassidy-scott-paul-release-legislative-package-empowering-independent-workers-to-access-portable-benefits) introduced by Senators Cassidy, Scott, and Paul in July 2025 represent the legislative building blocks of a durable solution. The Cassidy-Scott-Paul package is particularly well-designed: it creates a legal safe harbor for companies that provide portable benefits to independent contractors, resolving the perverse dynamic in which platforms avoid offering voluntary benefits because doing so creates misclassification risk. It includes expanded association health plans for independent workers and retirement plan access through pooled employer plans — genuine worker protections that do not require reclassification to deliver.

This is the correct policy sequence: finalize the administrative rule to restore classification certainty now, then legislate to make it durable. The two moves are complementary, not alternatives.

## Three Asks

**For DOL:** Finalize the proposed rule without amendments that restore multi-factor complexity. The core-factor architecture is analytically sound and practically administrable. Resist pressure to add back the six-factor diffusion that plagued the 2024 rule — each additional open-ended factor is an invitation to litigation, not a protection for workers.

**For Congress:** Move the HELP Committee's portable benefits and safe harbor package through committee and to the floor before the 2027 rulemaking window opens. A statutory safe harbor codifying the two-factor core test removes this question from the regulatory carousel permanently. Pair it with the portable benefits provisions to deliver genuine worker protections without forcing the false choice between independence and benefits access.

**For both:** Recognize that legal uncertainty is itself a policy outcome — and a bad one. Four classification standards in five years is not a sign of rigorous policy refinement. It is a sign that the question has been delegated to a forum — notice-and-comment rulemaking — that cannot resolve it durably. The DOL can stop this round of the carousel. Only Congress can take it apart.

The gig economy is not going away. The 76 million Americans who participate in independent work have demonstrated, through their labor market choices and their votes, that they value what it offers. Policy should work with that revealed preference, not against it. The proposed rule is a step toward getting this right. Finalizing it — and locking it in by statute — is how the step becomes progress.
