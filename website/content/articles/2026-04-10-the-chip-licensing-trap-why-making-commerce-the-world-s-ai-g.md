In early March 2026, Bloomberg and Tom's Hardware reported on a draft rule circulating inside the Commerce Department that would represent the most sweeping expansion of US export control authority in modern history. Under the proposal, [virtually every global shipment of advanced AI accelerators](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/us-drafts-rules-for-sweeping-power-over-nvidia-s-global-sales) — regardless of destination country — would require prior US government approval. An allied democracy purchasing a single server rack for a university research cluster would need Commerce Department sign-off. A hyperscaler in Tokyo building out a data center would need sovereign government certifications. The Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) would become the real-time licensing gatekeeper for the entire global AI compute market.

The rules have not been finalized. A White House official [told Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/03/05/trump-ai-chip-clash-white-house) that the draft "does not reflect what President Trump has said on export controls nor does it reflect the direction of the Trump administration on encouraging export of the American AI stack." That internal dissent is the correct instinct. The proposal should be rejected on national security grounds, not merely commercial ones — because a global licensing regime would actively undermine the US strategic position it purports to protect.

## What the Draft Rule Would Actually Do

The [proposed framework](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/us-govt-preps-sweeping-export-controls-for-nvidia-amd-ai-hardware-worldwide-licensing-system-would-give-trump-admin-broad-authority-to-block-global-sales) operates on a tiered structure. Shipments of 1,000 GPUs or fewer would receive cursory review. Medium-scale deployments would require pre-clearance before a license application can even be filed. Deployments of 200,000 or more GPUs — the scale at which serious hyperscale AI infrastructure operates — would require certifications from government officials in the host country.

This is not an incremental tightening of existing China-specific controls. It is a categorical transformation of US technology export policy: from adversary-specific restrictions targeting genuine security threats, to a universal permission-and-approval regime covering the entire planet. Japan, the United Kingdom, Australia, Singapore, South Korea — Five Eyes partners and treaty allies — would all become subject to the same licensing logic as adversary nations.

The stated intent, per administration officials, is not to ban exports but to ensure American AI infrastructure spreads on American terms. The mechanism, however, is one of friction and gatekeeping — and friction compounds.

## Problem One: An Administrative Chokepoint That Cannot Scale

Before evaluating whether a global licensing regime is the right *policy*, it is worth asking whether it is even operationally *feasible*. The evidence strongly suggests it is not.

BIS is already struggling under existing export control responsibilities. Following internal policy reviews that began in February 2025, the bureau [paused processing of new export license applications](https://www.bipc.com/department-of-commerce-pauses-processing-of-all-new-export-license-applications) — not once but twice — creating [backlogs growing at roughly 400 new applications per day](https://exportcompliancedaily.com/article/2025/02/20/bis-bombarded-with-questions-export-licensing-backlog-amid-policy-review-2502190018). Processing times that BIS's statutory framework targets at 90 days stretched to [120–180+ days through much of 2025](https://www.lenzo.ai/blog/export-license-processing-times-in-2026). As of February 2026, exporters continue to report [inconsistent and lengthy processing times](https://exportcompliancedaily.com/article/2026/02/27/exporters-still-seeing-lengthy-bis-licensing-times-lack-of-advisory-opinions-2602260047) and uncertainty in the interagency referral process.

Now consider the volume that a *global* licensing regime would add. The global data center GPU market is one of the fastest-growing segments in the technology economy. US firms — led by the American AI compute stack — [dominate approximately 86–92 percent](https://carboncredits.com/nvidia-controls-92-of-the-gpu-market-in-2025-and-reveals-next-gen-ai-supercomputer/) of the data center accelerator market. Licensing every commercial transaction in that market would require BIS to review thousands of applications per month, many involving sovereign government certifications for allied nations' infrastructure investments. There is no realistic staffing or process model under which this works. The result would not be a security screen. It would be an indefinite administrative delay on global AI infrastructure deployment — with US companies bearing the competitive cost.

## Problem Two: The Self-Harm of Throttling US AI Compute Revenue

American AI hardware leadership is not a given. It is the product of decades of R&D investment, ecosystem development, and a global customer base that provides the revenue to fund the next generation of research. In [fiscal year 2025](https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/nvidia-announces-financial-results-for-fourth-quarter-and-fiscal-2025), the US data center AI compute sector generated extraordinary revenues — with international markets accounting for [roughly 53 percent of total sales](https://stockdividendscreener.com/technology/nvidia-revenue-by-country/). That international revenue is the financial engine of US AI chip leadership.

A global licensing regime that creates friction for every international sale — delayed approvals, sovereign government certification requirements, pre-clearance procedures — does not make that revenue disappear. It redirects it. Allied customers who cannot get timely Commerce Department approval for legitimate commercial deployments will source alternatives. And alternatives exist. Huawei's Ascend AI accelerator line has advanced meaningfully. China's domestic semiconductor ecosystem, however far behind it remains, is actively developing competitive products and is strongly motivated to close the gap. Every friction point the US imposes on allied procurement is a business development opportunity for that ecosystem.

The goal of US AI compute leadership is not to maximize licensing revenue for the Commerce Department. It is to ensure that the infrastructure powering global AI development runs on American technology, subject to American standards, integrated into American technology partnerships. A global licensing regime undermines that goal by making American technology harder to buy than the alternative.

## Problem Three: Export Controls Have a Documented Track Record of Accelerating Chinese Self-Sufficiency

There is a structural problem with the premise underlying broad export controls on AI hardware: the historical evidence shows that such controls accelerate, not retard, Chinese domestic semiconductor investment. This is not a prediction — it is the observed result of Biden-era controls.

Following the Biden administration's [October 2022 and subsequent semiconductor export restrictions](https://www.csis.org/analysis/where-chips-fall-us-export-controls-under-biden-administration-2022-2024), China's domestic semiconductor ecosystem responded with significant investment acceleration. CXMT, China's leading DRAM developer, [grew production capacity from 70,000 wafers per month in 2022 to 200,000 wafers per month by 2024](https://americanaffairsjournal.org/2024/11/the-evolution-of-chinas-semiconductor-industry-under-u-s-export-controls/), with targets of 300,000 WPM and 11 percent of global DRAM market share. CXMT began [acquiring equipment for HBM production in February 2024](https://rhg.com/research/slaying-self-reliance-us-chip-controls-in-bidens-final-stretch/). Huawei and SMIC [doubled down on 7nm production](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48642). China's AI chip designers stockpiled high-bandwidth memory in anticipation of further tightening.

The mechanism is straightforward: export controls on advanced chips signal to Beijing that technological self-reliance is a strategic imperative, justify massive domestic semiconductor investment on national security grounds, and give Chinese chipmakers a protected home market. A global licensing regime will produce the same effect — but amplified. If allies can no longer reliably access US AI hardware, they will invest in developing alternatives and will be more receptive to Chinese technology partnerships. That outcome directly contradicts US strategic objectives.

## Problem Four: The DeepSeek Complication

The policy rationale for choking *global* AI hardware supply rests on an assumption that chip availability is the binding constraint on Chinese AI development. The [House Select Committee on the CCP's DeepSeek report](http://selectcommitteeontheccp.house.gov/media/reports/deepseek-unmasked-exposing-ccps-latest-tool-spying-stealing-and-subverting-us-export) — which documents China's efforts to evade export restrictions — simultaneously undermines the case for hardware-supply-focused controls as the primary tool.

The committee found that DeepSeek had access to an estimated [10,000 restricted H100 chips](https://www.techpolicy.press/us-house-select-committee-report-accuses-deepseek-of-spying-and-circumventing-export-controls-on-chips/), with SemiAnalysis estimating a total fleet of 60,000 Nvidia chips of various restricted models. But DeepSeek also demonstrated the capacity to [compensate for hardware constraints through algorithmic efficiency](https://www.sdxcentral.com/news/deepseek-defies-us-chip-restrictions-with-new-v32-model-that-may-just-close-the-ai-gap/) — longer, more intensive training runs, architectural innovations that reduce per-parameter compute requirements, and inference-time optimization techniques that wring more capability from constrained hardware.

This does not mean hardware controls are irrelevant. Chip access imposes real costs and timeline penalties on Chinese AI development. The committee is right that diversion of restricted chips to China represents a genuine enforcement failure that demands tighter controls. But it does mean that global supply restriction — extending licensing requirements to Japan, Germany, and Australia — is not proportionate to the actual security problem. The binding constraint on Chinese AI development is not that allied democracies are buying Nvidia hardware. The constraint is that China itself cannot access unrestricted advanced AI chips. Adversary-specific controls address the actual problem. A global licensing regime does not.

## Problem Five: Ally Alienation as a Strategic Liability

Perhaps the most underappreciated cost of the proposed global licensing regime is what it would do to the US technology partnership architecture. The Five Eyes intelligence alliance, the Quad, bilateral technology security frameworks with Japan and South Korea, and the EU-US Trade and Technology Council all rest on a foundation of shared technology access and mutual trust. Requiring sovereign government certifications from Japan's Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry or the UK's Department for Science, Innovation and Technology before their companies can purchase commercial server hardware reframes American technology partnership as a bureaucratic burden to be managed — not a strategic relationship to be deepened.

This is precisely what Beijing wants. China's international technology diplomacy is premised on the argument that American technology partnerships come with unacceptable conditions, that US technology suppliers are unreliable partners, and that countries should hedge toward Chinese alternatives. A global AI chip licensing regime validates that argument empirically. Every news story about a South Korean data center waiting for Commerce Department approval is a ready-made propaganda gift to Beijing.

The allies subject to this regime are not passive observers. They have industrial policy objectives of their own. Japan's CHIPS Act analog is investing in domestic semiconductor capacity. The EU has its own chip sovereignty agenda. A global US licensing regime will accelerate those trends, fragmenting the international market for US AI technology at precisely the moment when the US should be consolidating its position as the trusted core of the global AI infrastructure stack.

## The Right Framework: Targeted, Verified, and Alliance-Compatible

None of this argues against export controls as a tool. Adversary-specific controls targeting China are legitimate, necessary, and the correct model. The existing framework restricting high-end AI accelerator exports to China, along with end-use controls on advanced manufacturing equipment, appropriately targets the actual security threat. The enforcement gap — evidenced by DeepSeek's reported access to restricted chips — is a diversion and transshipment problem, not a demand for global extension of the same logic.

The right framework has three components.

**First, strengthen end-use verification and diversion monitoring for existing restricted destinations.** The enforcement failures documented in the DeepSeek report — chips transshipping through third countries to reach restricted Chinese entities — are the genuine policy problem. This requires better pre-shipment diversion risk assessment, expanded post-export verification requirements for large deployments, and closer coordination with customs authorities in transshipment hubs. These are the legitimate tools to close the enforcement gap.

**Second, negotiate bilateral technology security commitments with trusted allies as a precondition for streamlined market access.** Rather than subjecting allied democracies to the same licensing friction as adversary nations, the US should offer fast-track access programs for Five Eyes members, Quad partners, and EU allies who agree to end-use certification and diversion monitoring commitments. This approach secures US strategic interests — ensuring chips don't flow from allied markets to restricted end-users — without treating allies as threats. It also gives allied governments a positive reason to invest in the US technology partnership rather than building around it.

**Third, calibrate hardware controls to genuine weapons-relevant capability thresholds.** Not all AI compute is equally relevant to military applications. Export control regimes should target the specific hardware configurations and performance thresholds that matter for weapons systems, autonomous military platforms, and strategic intelligence applications — not the general availability of commercial cloud computing hardware. Broad controls that sweep in university research deployments in democratic countries impose real costs on global AI development without meaningfully advancing security objectives.

## The Policy Window Is Now

The draft global licensing regime has not been finalized. White House officials have already signaled internal skepticism. When the comment period opens, it will be the critical opportunity for Commerce, NSC, and Congressional staff to hear from allied governments, research institutions, and the broader technology ecosystem about the strategic costs of the proposal.

The administration's stated goal — ensuring the world uses American AI — is the right goal. A global licensing regime is the wrong instrument to achieve it. Treating every foreign buyer of US AI hardware as a potential adversary will not spread American AI. It will drive customers toward alternatives, accelerate Chinese domestic investment, and hand Beijing a ready-made narrative about American technology unreliability.

The correct approach is surgical: adversary-specific controls, rigorously enforced, with allied technology security partnerships that offer streamlined access in exchange for diversion monitoring commitments. That framework advances US AI compute leadership. The global licensing regime would erode it.
