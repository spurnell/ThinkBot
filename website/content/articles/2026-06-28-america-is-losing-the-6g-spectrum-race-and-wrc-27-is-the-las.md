China will walk into WRC-27 in Shanghai with sixteen months of 6G deployment data in the upper 6 GHz band. The United States will walk in with Wi-Fi incumbents and an interagency working group. That asymmetry is a policy choice — and it is still reversible, but not for long.

[MWC Shanghai closed on June 26](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/319169/20260626/mwc-shanghai-2026-closes-huawei-pushes-u6-ghz-first-commercial-5g-launches-loom.htm) with Huawei delivering a message the US policy community should receive as a five-alarm signal: the upper 6 GHz band (6.425–7.125 GHz) is the cornerstone of China's commercial 6G strategy, and the world is following its lead. [Huawei unveiled a comprehensive U6 GHz hardware stack](https://www.huawei.com/en/news/2026/3/mwc-5ga-u6ghz-6g) — macro-site base stations, Gigantic MIMO antenna arrays (a form of massive beamforming technology enabling high-capacity coverage from a single site), microwave backhaul equipment — while the UAE simultaneously became [the world's first country to launch a commercial U6 GHz network](https://www.mobileworldlive.com/uae-launches-worlds-first-commercial-u6ghz-network-advancing-path-to-10gbps-connectivity/). Operators in Hong Kong and Macao are initiating their own deployments. China, Brazil, and several European nations are actively pursuing spectrum identification in the band. The upper 6 GHz is becoming a global licensed mobile standard — without the United States.

The United States is not a passive bystander by accident. In April 2020, the FCC [opened the entire 6 GHz band to unlicensed Wi-Fi](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-opens-6-ghz-band-wi-fi-and-other-unlicensed-uses), releasing 1,200 megahertz for unlicensed use in a decision that made the US the global leader in Wi-Fi 6E. It was a reasonable call at the time. Six years later, the competitive landscape has shifted in ways that make revisiting that decision urgent — because in October 2027, the [World Radiocommunication Conference](https://www.itu.int/wrc-27/) convenes in Shanghai to negotiate global 6G spectrum standards, and America is walking into its geopolitical rival's house without the hand it needs.

## The Upper 6 GHz Split Is a Strategic Decision — Not Just a Technical One

The tendency to frame spectrum allocation as a domestic "Wi-Fi vs. telecom" regulatory dispute misses what the upper 6 GHz decision actually represents: a strategic choice about the world's last remaining wide-block mid-band spectrum. Mid-band spectrum — roughly 1 to 8 GHz — is the coverage-capacity sweet spot for mobile networks. It propagates far enough for practical urban and suburban coverage density and carries enough bandwidth for the gigabit-class throughput 6G will demand. The upper 6 GHz, at 700 megahertz of contiguous bandwidth, is the widest such block still in play globally. Whoever fields deployments in that band first generates the propagation data, interference studies, and engineering evidence that defines what the rest of the world builds.

China recognized this dynamic early and moved accordingly. [As CSIS has documented](https://www.csis.org/analysis/spectrum-allocation-contest-china), China's approach is systematic: dedicate a band domestically, generate real-world deployment experience, then present that evidence at WRC as the technical basis for international identification. The playbook worked during 5G. During that buildout, the United States lagged in licensed mid-band allocations while China moved faster — and [Huawei grew into a dominant global infrastructure provider](https://www.csis.org/analysis/spectrum-6g-and-future-ai-leadership) across Africa and the Middle East as a result. The 6G replay is now visible from 16 months out.

The 2020 FCC decision was made before 6G's competitive dynamics were fully visible, before China's U6 GHz commitment was established, and before WRC-27's agenda crystallized. The question for 2026 is not whether the FCC made a defensible decision in a different environment. It is whether the United States should allow that decision to harden into international standards by default at a conference its geopolitical rival is hosting.

## WRC-27: Playing in the Opponent's Stadium, Against the Opponent's Coalition

The geopolitical dimension of WRC-27 is not subtle. The conference [convenes in Shanghai from October 18 to November 12, 2027](https://www.itu.int/wrc-27/) — a venue that grants China logistical, diplomatic, and psychological advantages the US delegation will not enjoy. But the venue is the least of it. The real advantage is the bloc China is already building.

At WRC-23, the pattern was documented in real time: [China supported new mid-band IMT (International Mobile Telecommunications) allocations totaling 1,490 megahertz](https://www.csis.org/analysis/spectrum-allocation-contest-china) across five candidate bands; the United States backed proposals yielding just 100 megahertz. Brazil and Mexico shifted alignment toward China's spectrum positions. BRICS delegations became more cohesive within the ITU. China leveraged infrastructure deals, vendor relationships, and technical assistance to cultivate developing-nation delegations into a reliable coalition — nations whose WRC votes now track Chinese preferences on contested IMT identifications.

[WRC-27 Agenda Item 1.7](https://www.gsma.com/connectivity-for-good/spectrum/the-road-to-wrc-27-a-new-cycle-begins/) — the conference's central IMT spectrum item — governs new mobile broadband identifications in the 4.4–4.8 GHz, 7.125–8.4 GHz, and 14.8–15.35 GHz bands, directly covering the upper 6 GHz. These are not ceremonial votes. Nations arrive at WRC with pre-negotiated regional positions, coalition commitments, and technical submissions grounded in real-world data. The delegation presenting the most empirical evidence in a contested band does not merely persuade — it sets de facto standards, because other delegations have no technical basis to contradict. China will arrive with field deployments dating to 2026. The United States will arrive arguing for incumbency protection on behalf of Wi-Fi — a position that carries no weight against measured propagation data from live 6G network deployments.

## The Standards Clock Is Already Running

[3GPP approved timelines for Release 21 in June 2026](https://techblog.comsoc.org/2026/06/16/3gpp-approves-timelines-for-release-21-which-will-specify-6g-ran-and-5g-advanced/) — the first set of formal specifications that will define 6G radio access network (RAN) architecture, new air interfaces, and initial frequency band parameters. The first functional freeze is scheduled for March 2027, with a second freeze in June 2028 and a final specification freeze in December 2028. Commercial 6G deployments are expected from 2030 onward.

WRC-27 falls directly inside this specification window. ITU-R Working Party 5D — the technical body within the International Telecommunication Union that develops the IMT identification basis WRC formally adopts — feeds directly into 3GPP's frequency band work. Spectrum bands formally identified at WRC become the global reference against which equipment vendors design chipsets, antennas, and radio access networks for the next decade. Losing the upper 6 GHz at WRC-27 is not a negotiating setback that can be recovered at WRC-31. It means US carriers, US vendors, and US-allied networks will spend the 6G decade operating at a structural equipment and density disadvantage against networks built on the widest available mid-band block.

## The 2.7 GHz Model Works — Now Scale It

The recent [NTIA Spectrum Relocation Fund (SRF) Technical Panel approval of the 2.7 GHz relocation plan](https://www.ntia.gov/press-release/2026/plan-repurpose-27-ghz-band-clears-key-milestone-fueling-us-6g-leadership) is exactly the right model, and deserves recognition as such. NTIA, chairing the SRF Technical Panel alongside OMB and FCC, approved relocation plans for the 2.7 GHz band (2.69–2.90 GHz) and transmitted them to Congress, triggering a 60-day committee review before relocation funding is released. NOAA and FAA — primary users of the band for weather radar and aviation safety systems — are incorporating spectrum repurposing into ongoing radar modernization procurement. This is, as [R Street Institute has noted](https://www.rstreet.org/commentary/the-6g-race-has-a-bureaucratic-bottleneck/), a genuine win-win rather than a forced relocation: incumbents are cooperative, the technical path is clear, and the band is moving toward auction on a predictable schedule.

That model works because it has three elements operating together: a congressional mandate with a specific spectrum target, funded relocation from the SRF, and time-bounded agency compliance. Congress's 500 MHz clearing mandate gave NTIA a forcing function; the SRF gave incumbents a financial path out; and clear deadlines prevented the quiet stretching of timelines that has killed previous spectrum deals.

But 2.7 GHz alone will not close the mid-band gap before WRC-27. [NTIA has also cleared 12 agencies' study plans for the 4.4–4.94 GHz band](https://broadbandbreakfast.com/ntia-clears-study-plans-for-4-4-ghz-band/), forwarding cost estimates to OMB for the congressional review clock. The 7.125–7.4 GHz study, directed by [the President's December 2025 National Security Presidential Memorandum on 6G](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/national-security-presidential-memorandum-nspm-8-0bda/), is on track for a December 2026 report. These are productive steps. They are not a pipeline that produces usable licensed mid-band before WRC-27 negotiations lock in. The gap between "study underway" and "band cleared and auctioned" routinely spans years. WRC-27 is 16 months away.

## The Funding Chokepoint: How Spectrum Pipelines Die Quietly

The operational lesson of every US spectrum clearing effort of the past decade is consistent: unfunded relocation mandates are silent spectrum killers. When Congress directs bands for study without providing full relocation funding upfront, agency timelines slip. The 60-day congressional review window — designed as a procedural guardrail — becomes the first in a sequence of delays that stretches from months into years. NOAA weather radar modernization and FAA air traffic control radar upgrades are legitimate federal missions with legitimate costs. If those costs are not capitalized, incumbents cannot move and bands cannot clear.

[R Street Institute has documented this structural failure](https://www.rstreet.org/commentary/the-6g-race-has-a-bureaucratic-bottleneck/) in detail: SRF rules that tie research and development funding availability to auction notification timing have historically created perverse incentives that slow rather than accelerate clearing. The 2.7 GHz case is progressing precisely because incumbents are not obstructing — NOAA and FAA have been constructive. But constructive incumbents without adequate modernization funding still cannot move on schedule. Congress must appropriate DoD, NOAA, and FAA radar modernization funding in FY2027 — tied to spectrum clearing milestones — or the pipeline deals it has mandated will slip past WRC-27 without producing a usable band.

## Five Actions Before Shanghai

**First: Reopen the upper 6 GHz for a licensed/unlicensed hybrid framework.** The FCC should initiate a rulemaking to evaluate a hybrid framework for the 6.425–7.125 GHz band — licensed mobile in portions, unlicensed where sharing is technically viable — before WRC-27 locks international standards. A hybrid approach has precedent in other jurisdictions. Wi-Fi stakeholders will argue that coexistence studies conducted during the 2020 rulemaking already validated the all-unlicensed approach — a position that deserves engagement, not dismissal. But those studies predate any U6 GHz commercial deployments and were completed before WRC-27 Agenda Item 1.7 crystallized as the decisive venue. The goal is not to displace Wi-Fi incumbents wholesale. It is to create the licensed-band presence that allows the US to arrive at WRC-27 with more than an unlicensed occupancy claim against the majority of the world's mobile operators.

**Second: Accelerate the 4.4–4.94 GHz and 7 GHz pipelines in parallel, not in sequence.** Congress should treat the mid-band pipeline as a portfolio — simultaneous mandates with simultaneous funding — not a sequential queue of studies. Waiting for 2.7 GHz to complete before fully funding 4.4 GHz studies, and for 4.4 GHz to complete before acting on 7 GHz, guarantees arrival at WRC-27 with empty hands.

**Third: Fully capitalize DoD, NOAA, and FAA radar modernization in FY2027.** Dedicated relocation funding tied to spectrum clearing milestones should not compete with general discretionary spending. The cost of adequate SRF capitalization is trivially small relative to the economic value of cleared spectrum and the strategic cost of losing the WRC-27 negotiation.

**Fourth: Direct NTIA to submit unified US positions on WRC-27 Agenda Item 1.7 by Q1 2027.** Advisory committees and interagency working groups are not substitutes for a congressionally-mandated negotiating posture with real deadlines. Congress should require NTIA — in consultation with FCC and DoD — to deliver a unified US position to the preparatory processes of the Asia-Pacific Telecommunity (APT) and the European Conference of Postal and Telecommunications Administrations (CEPT) no later than March 2027, with interim position papers in place for regional preparatory meetings in late 2026.

**Fifth: Execute the presidential coalition-building mandate with the urgency it demands.** The President's 6G memorandum already directs the Secretary of State to build a coalition of industry and foreign partners behind the US position on Agenda Item 1.7. That directive must not be processed as routine diplomacy. The National Security Council should treat WRC-27 coalition-building as a priority analogous to semiconductor export controls and AI governance alignment — engaging US allies in the EU, Japan, Korea, Australia, and the United Kingdom through the same senior-level channels. China is assembling its coalition now. Matching that effort requires executive attention, not working-group management.

## Conclusion

The 2020 FCC decision was made when Wi-Fi's strategic upside was clear and 6G's competitive dynamics were not. That context has changed. The competitive landscape is now unambiguous: the upper 6 GHz is becoming the de facto 6G mobile foundation across a majority of the world, the first commercial deployments are live, and the conference that will codify global standards convenes in 16 months in the capital of the nation driving that standard.

None of the actions required to change this outcome demands a new agency, a new regulatory philosophy, or a departure from market principles. They require strategic prioritization within existing authority — NTIA and FCC authority, congressional appropriations authority, and executive branch coalition-building capacity — applied with a clarity of purpose that treats spectrum allocation as the national competitiveness decision it has always been.

The alternative is straightforward: show up in Shanghai in October 2027 having done none of this, and watch China set the 6G spectrum standards on its home field, with its deployment data and its coalition assembled, for the wireless decade that will underpin the global AI economy. That outcome is still avoidable. The window, however, is closing faster than the proceeding dockets suggest.
