On July 2, Rwanda's President Paul Kagame, Salesforce chair and CEO Marc Benioff, and ITU Secretary-General Doreen Bogdan-Martin [announced the launch](https://www.itu.int/en/mediacentre/Pages/PR-2026-07-02-AI-for-Good-Global-Commission.aspx) of the AI for Good Global Commission — a body of more than 40 founding members that pairs heads of state and government with senior executives of frontier AI developers. Kagame and Benioff co-chair; Bogdan-Martin vice-chairs. Founding members include leaders from Estonia, Iceland, and Kazakhstan alongside Amazon CEO Andy Jassy, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang, Cohere CEO Aidan Gomez, Microsoft President Brad Smith, and Anthropic co-founder Jack Clark, among others. The Commission held its inaugural meeting this week at the [ITU AI for Good Global Summit](https://aiforgood.itu.int/summit26/) in Geneva (July 7–10), alongside the first UN-mandated Global Dialogue on AI Governance.

The structure is the story, and it is a genuine departure from the last three years of multilateral AI governance. The [Bletchley](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/ai-safety-summit-2023-the-bletchley-declaration) and Seoul AI safety summits, the UN's [High-Level Advisory Body on AI](https://www.un.org/en/ai-advisory-body), and the EU's outreach around the AI Act have all treated industry as a stakeholder to be consulted, managed, or regulated — rarely as a co-governor with a seat and a signature. The AI for Good Commission inverts that default. Frontier developers are founding members, not witnesses called to testify. That is worth welcoming, and the US government should engage with the Commission rather than treat it as UN pageantry to be politely ignored.

## Vocabulary Is Not Yet Policy — But It Sets the Runway

The Commission's stated mission, per the [ITU press release](https://www.itu.int/en/mediacentre/Pages/PR-2026-07-02-AI-for-Good-Global-Commission.aspx), is to "identify practical pathways to strengthen trust, support responsible innovation, and deliver broad-based economic and social benefits" while helping "bridge digital divides." Read charitably, that is a capacity-building mandate. Read as a preview, it is nearly identical to the vocabulary that preceded the EU AI Act's risk-tiered compliance regime and the UN Advisory Body's own [Governing AI for Humanity final report](https://www.un.org/sites/un2.un.org/files/governing_ai_for_humanity_final_report_en.pdf), both of which opened with "trust" and "responsible" language before hardening into audits, conformity assessments, and — in Brussels's case — a licensing-adjacent regime for "high-risk" systems.

That history doesn't mean the Commission is destined to follow the same path, and this is not an accusation of bad faith. The Commission has no binding authority, no rulemaking power, and no enforcement mechanism — it is explicitly a convening and advisory body. But non-binding bodies matter precisely because they set the Overton window that binding instruments later inherit. The EU AI Act didn't spring from nowhere; years of OECD and UNESCO "trustworthy AI" principles primed the vocabulary regulators later codified into law. The fight over whether "trust" means *interoperable and auditable* or *pre-approved and licensed* is happening now, while the Commission's first work program is still being written — not after its first report ships.

## What "Good" Looks Like

The Commission's most defensible, highest-leverage output is not a safety framework — it's market expansion. The gap is stark and well-documented: AI adoption among working-age adults runs at roughly 24.7 percent in Global North economies compared with 14.1 percent in the Global South, and none of the world's top 100 high-performance computing clusters capable of training large models is hosted in a developing country, according to [ITU's own AI for Good analysis](https://aiforgood.itu.int/who-controls-frontier-ai-building-a-plan-for-diffused-access/). Closing that gap is precisely the work the UN's [Global Digital Compact](https://www.un.org/global-digital-compact) was designed to catalyze. A body that can convene ministers of Kazakhstan and Estonia with Nvidia and Microsoft has real capacity to move compute access, interoperability standards, and technical capacity-building for the Global South — the kind of infrastructure work that expands the addressable market for competitive AI providers rather than gatekeeping who is allowed to use them. That agenda also has an unavoidable geopolitical backdrop: [China's Digital Silk Road](https://www.cfr.org/china-digital-silk-road/) is actively offering AI infrastructure and standards to the same low- and middle-income countries this Commission is targeting. That's a real reason for the US to show up — but it's a supporting argument, not the thesis. The thesis is that interoperability and access, not a new layer of ex-ante approval, is what "good" should mean here.

The failure mode is a Commission that spends its institutional capital drafting risk taxonomies and "trustworthy AI" certification schemes that read like a soft preview of a global licensing regime arrived at by consensus rather than legislation — no single government voting for it, but every major AI jurisdiction citing "the Commission's framework" as the reference standard within two years. That is exactly how the EU AI Act became a de facto global template for firms that found it cheaper to build once to the strictest standard than to maintain jurisdiction-specific versions. A body with Brussels absent from its founding membership is a chance to prevent that dynamic from repeating at the UN level — but only if someone is actively offering an alternative vocabulary in the room.

## The Recommendation

State Department and USTR should not treat Commission participation as a box-checking diplomatic courtesy. Over its first year, US delegates — not just the American executives sitting as founding members, but formal government representation — should push the Commission's early work program toward two deliverables: (1) technical interoperability standards that let AI systems and data move across borders without triggering conflicting compliance regimes, and (2) a capacity-building fund or facility, modeled on multilateral development bank structures, that gets compute and technical training to the Global South without conditioning access on adopting a specific regulatory template. In parallel, US officials should actively export the [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) — a voluntary, sector-agnostic, outcomes-based standard — as the reference model the Commission cites, rather than leaving that vacuum to be filled by the EU AI Act's risk-tiered compliance architecture by default. Absence doesn't produce neutrality in institutions like this one; it produces a vacuum that the most organized existing framework fills. Right now, that's Brussels's to lose, and Washington's to contest.

The Commission is six days old. It has produced no work product, no framework, and no binding text — only a founding declaration and a first meeting. That is exactly the moment when engagement is cheap and framing is still up for grabs. The read here: welcome the structure, watch the vocabulary, and treat the next twelve months as the window in which the difference between capacity-building and compliance-building gets decided.
