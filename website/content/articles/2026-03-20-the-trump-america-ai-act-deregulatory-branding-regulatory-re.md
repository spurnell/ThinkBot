# The TRUMP AMERICA AI Act: Deregulatory Branding, Regulatory Reality

Senator Marsha Blackburn released the [discussion draft of the TRUMP AMERICA AI Act](https://www.blackburn.senate.gov/2026/3/technology/blackburn-releases-discussion-draft-of-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence/3b3b6458-b6c7-478b-9859-374949586765) on March 18 — a 291-page legislative framework positioned as the pro-innovation answer to America's [growing patchwork of conflicting state AI laws](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/03/examining-the-landscape-and-limitations-of-the-federal-push-to-override-state-ai-regulation). The branding is deliberate: this is the TRUMP AMERICA AI Act, invoking the president's deregulatory mandate. The pitch is federal uniformity over 50-state chaos.

On that narrow point, Blackburn is right. The patchwork is real, it is costly, and it is getting worse. [Over 1,000 AI-related bills were introduced across states in 2025 alone](https://www.bakerbotts.com/thought-leadership/publications/2026/january/us-ai-law-update), and multiple state regimes are now in effect or taking effect this year across California, Colorado, Texas, New York, and Florida. [ThinkBot has consistently supported](https://www.mlstrategies.com/insights-center/viewpoints/54031/2026-02-04-_026-ai-policy-and-semiconductor-outlook-how-federal) a single national AI framework over this regulatory cacophony, and that position has not changed.

But read the fine print. What Blackburn has drafted is not clean federal preemption. It is preemption *plus* a compliance edifice — mandatory bias audits, algorithmic transparency mandates, a "duty of care" standard for AI developers, expanded private rights of action, and one deeply alarming provision that has nothing to do with AI governance: a full sunset of Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act.

Congress should not mistake federal preemption for regulatory restraint. This bill delivers neither.

## The Bill's Genuine Win — and Its Limits

Credit where it is due. [The Trump administration's December 2025 executive order](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/ai-executive-order-targets-state-laws-and-seeks-uniform-federal-standards) made federal preemption of state AI laws an explicit policy objective, and Blackburn's bill attempts to deliver it through statute — the only mechanism that can actually achieve it. Courts have been clear: an executive order cannot preempt state legislation; [only Congress can do that](https://www.steptoe.com/en/news-publications/steptechtoe-blog/executive-order-on-ai-federal-preemption-or-federal-pressure.html). Blackburn is doing the necessary legislative work.

The problem is that she has loaded the preemption vehicle with cargo that transforms it into something else entirely. A clean preemption bill — one that establishes a single national framework, sets a low federal floor, and prevents states from layering on conflicting requirements — would be a genuine policy achievement. What Blackburn has drafted is a new federal regulatory regime that replaces the state patchwork with its own dense compliance requirements. Trading 50 bad laws for one complicated federal law is not progress.

## An Innovation Tax on Startups

The bill imposes a "duty of care" on AI developers, requiring them to prevent "reasonably foreseeable harms" from their systems — language that [legal analysts have already flagged](https://www.govtech.com/artificial-intelligence/federal-ai-bill-would-put-duty-of-care-on-developers) as broad and undefined. It mandates bias audits and algorithmic transparency reporting. It creates new private rights of action, allowing individuals to sue AI developers for "defective design" and "failure to warn."

The innovation economics here are straightforward: large incumbent AI developers — OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, Meta — have the legal teams, compliance infrastructure, and capital reserves to absorb these requirements. Startups building the next generation of AI applications do not. Mandatory audit regimes and expanded liability exposure function as an innovation tax that falls disproportionately on smaller developers. The predictable result is not a safer AI ecosystem; it is a more concentrated one, with compliance costs serving as a moat around incumbents.

The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) already provides voluntary guidance that allows developers to calibrate risk management to their actual risk profile. Mandatory prescriptive requirements do not improve on that model — they calcify it.

## The Section 230 Catastrophe

The most alarming provision in this bill has nothing to do with AI governance. Buried in Blackburn's discussion draft is a full sunset of [Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act](https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/USCODE-2011-title47/pdf/USCODE-2011-title47-chap5-subchapII-part1-sec230.pdf) — the liability shield that has enabled the modern internet — set to expire two years after enactment.

This is seismic liability restructuring tucked into an AI bill.

Section 230 enables platforms to host user-generated content without being treated as the publisher of every post, video, and comment. Without it, every platform becomes a potential defendant in litigation over content it did not create. The [Roll Call analysis of the bill](https://rollcall.com/2026/03/19/ai-draft-bill-would-revamp-online-landscape/) is blunt: phasing out those protections could expose platforms to significantly more litigation over user posts and, depending on how the legislation is written, over AI-generated outputs as well.

Blackburn incorporates Sen. Lindsey Graham's Section 230 sunset legislation and adds a "Bad Samaritan" provision that inverts the foundational logic of current law. Under Section 230 today, platforms can seek quick dismissal of lawsuits over third-party content. Under the "Bad Samaritan" framework, platforms must *prove at trial* that they did not facilitate illegal content. The burden shifts to the defendant. The quick-dismissal protection disappears. Every aggrieved user becomes a potential plaintiff with discovery rights.

This is not a measured AI safety measure. It is a structural reorganization of platform liability that has been debated on its own merits — and found wanting — for years. Inserting it into an AI bill bypasses the deliberation this question demands and links it to the preemption argument to make it harder to strip out. That is legislative malpractice.

Whether or not you have views on Section 230 reform, it does not belong in an AI governance bill. It is a separate question of enormous consequence that deserves its own hearing, its own markup, and its own vote.

## What Congress Should Do

The window to shape this discussion draft is open now. Here is what the AI policy community should be telling Hill staffers and Commerce Committee members:

**Strip the Section 230 sunset.** Full stop. This provision should not travel in any AI bill. It belongs in standalone legislation where it can receive appropriate deliberation.

**Strip the "Bad Samaritan" burden-shifting provisions.** Shifting the burden of proof to defendants at trial is not a proportionate AI safety measure. It is a plaintiff's attorney's dream packaged as consumer protection.

**Replace the mandatory audit and duty-of-care mandates with a voluntary standards framework.** Build on the NIST AI RMF. Allow sector-specific regulators — FDA for healthcare AI, CFPB for financial AI, EEOC for employment AI — to enforce existing law against demonstrated harms. Do not create a new horizontal compliance regime.

**Keep the preemption.** A clean federal preemption provision that establishes a low national floor and prevents state-level regulatory proliferation is the right policy. Save it by separating it from the rest of this bill.

## The Danger of the False Binary

Supporters of the bill will argue that the alternative to this legislation is the 50-state patchwork — and that critics are implicitly defending regulatory fragmentation. That is a false binary, and the AI policy community should reject it.

The answer to bad state law is clean federal preemption. It is not bad federal law. If Congress accepts the premise that any federal bill is better than the status quo, it will pass whatever Blackburn can move — and the innovation economy will pay for it for decades.

The TRUMP AMERICA AI Act has the right name on the wrapper and the wrong contents inside. The goal should be to fix it, not kill it — but fixing it requires a frank acknowledgment that deregulatory branding is not the same thing as deregulatory policy.
