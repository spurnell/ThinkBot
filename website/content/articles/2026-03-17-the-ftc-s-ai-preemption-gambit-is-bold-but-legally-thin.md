On March 11, 2026, the Federal Trade Commission released what may be the most aggressive federal assertion of AI governance authority to date: a [policy statement applying Section 5 of the FTC Act](https://regulations.ai/regulations/RAI-US-NA-ENFORCE-2026) to AI systems, targeting state-mandated bias mitigation requirements as potentially *per se* deceptive trade practices. Simultaneously, the Commerce Department published its list of "burdensome" state AI laws for DOJ litigation referral.

The federal-vs.-state AI regulatory battle has officially begun. The administration is fighting the right war with the wrong weapon.

## The Problem Is Real

Start where the administration is correct: the 50-state patchwork of AI regulation is an innovation tax, and it is getting worse. As of January 1, 2026, [more than seventeen states have AI bias laws on the books](https://www.kslaw.com/news-and-insights/new-state-ai-laws-are-effective-on-january-1-2026-but-a-new-executive-order-signals-disruption), with divergent definitions, compliance timelines, covered-system thresholds, and audit requirements. A mid-sized AI developer deploying a hiring tool across state lines now faces a compliance matrix that rivals GDPR in complexity — without GDPR's single point of accountability.

This is not a theoretical concern. Compliance fragmentation doesn't just impose costs; it concentrates market power. Large incumbents can absorb 50-state legal departments. Startups cannot. If you want to know who benefits most from a chaotic state-law patchwork, look at the companies that can afford to navigate it.

The diagnosis is correct. The cure is where things get complicated.

## The Section 5 Theory Is Administratively Fragile

The FTC's core claim is that state-mandated bias mitigation requirements, when applied to AI outputs, constitute a form of compelled deception — and that the FTC's authority to prohibit deceptive practices under Section 5 therefore preempts those state mandates.

This theory has [serious legal problems](https://www.techpolicy.press/the-ftcs-ai-preemption-authority-is-limited/), and expert observers across the ideological spectrum have flagged them.

First, **policy statements don't preempt state law.** Preemption is a constitutional doctrine requiring either an express congressional command or a direct conflict between federal and state law. An agency's interpretive statement about its own enforcement priorities is neither. Courts routinely decline to give preemptive effect to agency policy documents that lack the force of law — and this statement, issued without notice-and-comment rulemaking, almost certainly lacks it.

Second, the **Major Questions Doctrine** poses a formidable obstacle. In *West Virginia v. EPA* (2022), the Supreme Court held that when an agency claims authority to resolve questions of vast economic and political significance, it must point to clear congressional authorization. [Ropes & Gray's analysis](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/03/examining-the-landscape-and-limitations-of-the-federal-push-to-override-state-ai-regulation) makes the point directly: the FTC's attempt to use a 1914 consumer protection statute to restructure the entire field of state AI regulation is precisely the kind of major question that requires congressional action, not administrative reinterpretation.

Third — and this is the doctrinal inversion that should alarm everyone — **calling bias correction "deceptive output" turns consumer protection logic on its head.** Section 5 was designed to protect consumers from harm. Framing a company's attempt to make its AI system less discriminatory as deception doesn't protect consumers; it exposes them. The FTC's traditional role in AI has been *ex-post* enforcement against companies that deploy demonstrably harmful systems — pursuing discriminatory lending algorithms, deceptive chatbots, and fraudulent AI marketing claims. That's legitimate and necessary. This new theory abandons that lane for something far more ambitious and far less defensible.

## The Weaponization Risk

Here is the political economy argument the administration should be taking seriously: legal theories don't expire with administrations. A future FTC — one that views AI with considerably more skepticism than the current one — could pick up this same Section 5 theory and run it in the opposite direction.

If bias mitigation mandates are deceptive under Section 5, a future commission could argue that *failure* to implement bias mitigation is an unfair or deceptive practice under the same provision. The administration is building doctrinal infrastructure it will not always control. Administratively fragile theories are dangerous precisely because they are reversible — and reversibility cuts both ways.

## What Congress Must Do

The durable solution to AI regulatory fragmentation is a lightweight federal AI framework with **explicit preemption language** — not an aggressive reinterpretation of a statute that predates computing by decades. [Federal deadlines reshaping the AI regulatory landscape](https://www.mondaq.com/unitedstates/new-technology/1755166/march-2026-federal-deadlines-that-will-reshape-the-ai-regulatory-landscape) are multiplying, and the window for congressional action is narrowing.

Such a framework doesn't need to be prescriptive. It needs to do three things: establish a federal floor for AI-related consumer protection, provide clear preemption of conflicting state mandates, and vest primary enforcement authority in agencies with technical competence and sector-specific jurisdiction — working alongside FDA for health AI, the CFPB for financial AI, and the EEOC for employment AI. That's the architecture of federal uniformity without federal overreach.

The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) already provides a voluntary foundation. Congress could make a streamlined version of it the basis for federal preemption — offering states a cooperative federalism off-ramp while establishing national consistency.

## A Warning to Corporate Counsel

Do not assume state laws are preempted. The FTC's policy statement carries no legal weight in a courtroom. State attorneys general retain full enforcement authority over their AI statutes until a federal court issues an injunction or Congress acts. Companies that treat this statement as a compliance holiday are taking on significant legal exposure. The statement may shift federal enforcement priorities; it does not change what state law requires.

## The Bottom Line

The FTC's AI preemption gambit reflects genuine and warranted frustration with the innovation tax that 50-state AI regulation imposes. On that diagnosis, ThinkBot agrees. But the vehicle — a legally novel Section 5 theory issued via policy statement, built on the claim that bias mitigation is deception — is administratively fragile, doctrinally inverted, and ultimately a gift to future regulators who view AI as a threat rather than an opportunity.

Washington should want federal uniformity in AI governance. It should want it badly enough to get Congress to enact it.
