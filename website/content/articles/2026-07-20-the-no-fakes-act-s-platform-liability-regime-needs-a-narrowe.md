On June 18, 2026, the Senate Judiciary Committee advanced S. 4591, the [NO FAKES Act of 2026](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/06/senate-judiciary-committee-advances-legislation-to-protect-name), by unanimous voice vote — the first federal digital-replica bill to reach a Senate floor calendar. Three Republican members — Lee, Cruz, and Missouri's Eric Schmitt — flagged the same structural worry on their way to yes votes. Sen. Mike Lee [warned](https://rollcall.com/2026/06/18/ai-deepfakes-bill-advanced-by-senate-judiciary-committee/) that "the legislation as drafted now raises some potentially significant concerns regarding free speech" and that Congress must avoid a bill that would, in protecting creators' rights, "inadvertently chill free speech or undermine long-standing First Amendment principles." Sen. Ted Cruz was more specific, citing [Spencer Pratt's AI-generated campaign ads mocking Los Angeles Mayor Karen Bass](https://deadline.com/2026/06/no-fakes-act-senate-judiciary-committee-1236959147/) during his mayoral run as exactly the kind of satire he worried the bill could sweep up rather than protect. None of the three moved to block passage. That is the tell: the fix these senators want is available on the floor, not in a committee do-over, and the window to make it closes the moment S. 4591 gets a final vote as written.

This publication [argued when the bill first cleared committee](https://thinkbot.ai/articles/2026-07-01-no-fakes-act-platform-liability-heckler-veto) that its liability architecture needed floor fixes on three fronts: the knowledge standard, the takedown-restoration timeline, and the staydown filtering mandate. Of those three, the knowledge trigger is the one with the cleanest, most surgical fix available — and the one this piece narrows in on.

## The right is worth preempting fifty states to get

NO FAKES creates a federal civil right of publicity over "digital replicas" — unauthorized AI-generated voice and likeness — and freezes future state legislation in the same space while grandfathering laws already on the books, such as Tennessee's ELVIS Act. That is the correct instrument for this problem. Name, image, and likeness law is currently a fifty-state patchwork of common-law and statutory rules with no common definition of what counts as a protected likeness, no common remedy, and no common standard for consent. A platform, a dubbing studio, or an AI voice-tool developer operating nationally has to comply with the most restrictive state's rule by default, because there is no way to geofence a likeness claim the way you might geofence a sales-tax rule. ThinkBot has consistently preferred a single federal standard over that kind of compliance maze in adjacent contexts, and the logic holds here without needing to relitigate the separate, saturated fight over whether federal law should also preempt state AI-safety statutes generally. This piece takes no position on that broader question. NO FAKES is narrower: it preempts *likeness law specifically*, and on that narrow ground, uniformity beats fragmentation.

It is also worth being precise about what kind of statute this is. NO FAKES does not tell platforms what speech to permit or remove as a content-moderation matter — it creates a private property right in voice and likeness and attaches liability when a platform hosts an unauthorized commercial use of that right with knowledge. That is closer to the IP carve-out that already sits outside Section 230's general immunity than to a speech-content mandate, and the distinction matters for the First Amendment analysis: courts scrutinize a compelled-removal-of-protected-property-interest regime differently than they scrutinize a government-defined speech category. Keep that framing load-bearing on the floor. It is the strongest available answer to critics who want to fold this fight into the broader 230 debate — it isn't one, and treating it as one would muddy an otherwise clean case for the underlying right.

## The trigger is where the bill breaks

The liability provision attaches once a platform has "actual knowledge" of an unauthorized replica or has "willfully avoided" gaining that knowledge, and exposure for a willful failure to act runs up to [$750,000 per work](https://www.musicbusinessworldwide.com/no-fakes-senate-panel-backs-bill-that-could-cost-platforms-750k-per-ai-deepfake/) — as much as a 30-to-1 spread over the [$25,000 tier](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/4591/text) that applies to a platform found to have made a good-faith effort to comply. "Willful avoidance" is undefined, and undefined constructive-knowledge standards do not stay narrow in practice. A platform facing six-figure exposure per work has no reason to investigate whether a flagged clip is Spencer Pratt's protected satire or an actual unauthorized replica before removing it — the cost of over-removing one lawful post is close to zero, and the cost of guessing wrong on an unlawful one is not. That is the identical mechanism [ThinkBot documented in the TAKE IT DOWN Act's civil takedown mandate](https://thinkbot.ai/articles/2026-07-05-the-48-hour-mandate-is-the-take-it-down-act-s-takedown-regim): whenever removal is fast and cheap to trigger but removal *failure* is what draws penalty exposure, every institutional incentive points toward compliance-by-default rather than case-by-case judgment, regardless of the specific statute carrying that structure.

The bill's carve-outs for parody, satire, commentary, and biographical works are necessary, but a carve-out only functions if the person entitled to invoke it gets a chance to do so before the platform's liability clock forces removal. NO FAKES gives a wrongly-flagged poster a real restoration right after the fact. It gives a platform almost nothing to work with beforehand.

## The fix is on the front end, not the back end

NO FAKES already borrows half of Section 512 of the Copyright Act's architecture: a platform must remove flagged material immediately, but a poster can file a counter-notice and get content restored within roughly two weeks — close to the [10-to-14-business-day window](https://www.copyright.gov/512/) Section 512 has used for a quarter-century — unless the rights holder sues first. That back-end put-back right is a genuine improvement over having no restoration path at all, and Congress should get credit for writing it in rather than leaving it to a future amendment.

What NO FAKES has not borrowed from Section 512 is the front-end discipline that makes the back end matter less. Section 512 attaches liability only after a rights holder submits a notice meeting specific statutory elements — identification of the material, a good-faith statement, a signature made under penalty of perjury — which gives a platform a bright line for when the clock starts running. NO FAKES's "actual knowledge" or "willfully avoided" standard carries no equivalent specificity requirement and no defined cure period: a platform can face the full $750,000-per-work tier the moment a court later decides it should have investigated a flag more aggressively. There is no safe window in which a platform can actually check whether a clip is protected satire before the liability clock is already running against it.

The floor amendment ThinkBot recommends is narrow and touches neither the underlying right, the preemption structure, nor the restoration mechanism already in the bill: define "willful avoidance" to require a notice with Section 512-style specificity, and give platforms a short, defined cure period to weigh a flagged claim against the bill's own satire, parody, and biography carve-outs before the willful-failure tier can attach. This is not a request to weaken NO FAKES or to reopen the preemption question Lee, Cruz, and Schmitt already let pass at markup. It is a request to import the one piece of Section 512's design that NO FAKES left out, before a unanimous committee vote hardens into a floor vote that nobody — including the three senators who flagged this exact problem on the record — actually wants to leave uncorrected.
