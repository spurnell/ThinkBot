On January 6, 2026, the FDA issued [revised final guidance on clinical decision support software](https://www.fda.gov/media/191560/download) that did something Washington rarely does well: it drew a coherent, defensible line. The guidance sharply narrowed the agency's oversight footprint over AI-enabled clinical decision support tools, exempting software that allows clinicians to independently review the basis of a recommendation from full medical device regulation — while maintaining rigorous FDA review for autonomous diagnostic systems where human oversight is absent or structurally compromised.

Four months later, the healthcare industry is still digesting what the "glass box" transparency standard means in practice. Meanwhile, a coalition of medical device incumbents, risk-averse hospital systems, and patient advocacy organizations has begun pressuring Congress to legislate in the opposite direction — expanding FDA's oversight footprint over clinical AI back to where it was before the guidance was issued. Congressional health committee staff have begun scheduling hearings. The window to cement the FDA's framework is narrowing.

The FDA got this one right. Congress should not undo it. And because administrative guidance can be reversed overnight, Congress should codify the framework into law before the next administration gets the chance to try.

## What the Guidance Actually Does

Critics have characterized the January 2026 CDS guidance as a blanket deregulation of AI in medicine. It is not. It is a risk-stratified framework anchored to a single, clinically meaningful question: can the clinician actually evaluate what the AI is telling them, and why?

The guidance operationalizes the four-factor non-device CDS test that Congress embedded in section 520(o)(1)(E) of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act through the [21st Century Cures Act in 2016](https://www.akingump.com/en/insights/alerts/the-21stst-century-cures-medical-software-provisions-additional-clarity-for-digital-health-but-also-more-questions). Under that framework, a CDS software function escapes device classification — and the full premarket review obligations that come with it — only if it satisfies all four conditions: it does not acquire or analyze medical images, in vitro diagnostic (IVD) signals, or signal patterns; it displays medical information about the patient or clinical reference information; it provides recommendations to a licensed healthcare professional; and — crucially — it enables the HCP to independently review the basis of that recommendation rather than simply accept the software's output.

The 2026 guidance refined how FDA interprets these criteria in practice. [Covington & Burling's analysis of the guidance](https://www.cov.com/en/news-and-insights/insights/2026/01/5-key-takeaways-from-fdas-revised-clinical-decision-support-cds-software-guidance) identifies two especially significant updates: FDA will now exercise enforcement discretion for CDS tools that generate a singular output when only one clinically appropriate option exists — a change from the 2022 guidance — and it expanded what counts as "medical information about a patient" beyond the prior standard of information "commonly discussed in a clinical conversation" to include information grounded in well-understood, accepted clinical sources. As [Ropes & Gray notes](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/01/fda-adapts-with-the-times-on-digital-health-updated-guidances-on-general-wellness-products), this brings the guidance into alignment with how modern EHR-integrated AI actually works — pulling from structured clinical databases, validated drug interaction indices, and published guidelines, not just the narrow slice of information a physician might verbally surface in a patient encounter.

None of this exempts high-risk AI from FDA review. Clinical tools that analyze imaging data, interpret IVD signals, or operate without a reviewing clinician in the loop remain fully subject to device regulation. The guidance narrows FDA's footprint precisely where the rationale for FDA oversight was always weakest: software that gives a clinician information and lets the clinician decide, rather than software that substitutes for clinical judgment entirely.

## Why the Glass Box Standard Is the Right Policy Lever

The fourth factor — independent clinician reviewability — is doing the heaviest policy work in the guidance, and it deserves defense on its merits.

The evidence on automation bias in clinical settings is substantial and instructive. [Research in computational pathology](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.00998) documents that even AI-enhanced workflows produce automation bias at meaningful rates: correct clinical evaluations were overturned by erroneous AI advice at a 7 percent rate in one study, a rate that worsened in severity under cognitive time pressure. [A review published in the AI and Ethics journal](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43681-025-00825-2) found that adding explainability features alone did not eliminate automation bias — what mattered was whether clinicians had a genuine basis for exercising independent judgment, not just a checkbox disclosure.

The glass box standard operationalizes precisely this distinction. A CDS tool that shows its reasoning — here is the clinical guideline this recommendation draws on, here is the patient-specific data element that triggered the alert, here is the evidence base underlying this drug interaction flag — equips the clinician to agree, disagree, or override. A CDS tool that produces an output without surfacing its rationale structurally predisposes clinicians toward deference even when deference is inappropriate. The former supports clinical judgment; the latter erodes it.

This is also why the FDA's approach is analytically superior to the EU's. The [EU Medical Device Regulation](https://healthcarelifesciences.bakermckenzie.com/2025/05/19/the-eu-ai-act-and-medical-devices-innovation-or-insanity-ahead/), which went into force in May 2021 and imposed conformity assessment requirements on software as medical devices, has created well-documented problems for clinical AI deployment across Europe. Manufacturers have evaluated the return on investment and left the EU market; notified bodies — the third-party certification organizations that handle MDR conformity assessment — are operating at capacity limits with long waitlists, [a bottleneck that disproportionately burdens smaller digital health companies](https://www.medtecheurope.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/mte_report_ivdr_mdr_2024-v7.pdf) that cannot afford to queue for years. The EU AI Act's [August 2027 compliance deadline for medical device AI](https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/implementation-timeline/) — which the Digital Omnibus provisional agreement has since extended to August 2028 — adds a second conformity assessment layer on top of MDR, compounding the burden. This is what blanket conformity assessment regimes produce: not safety, but delay, market exit by smaller innovators, and a clinical AI ecosystem dominated by the large incumbents who have the legal resources to clear multiple certification processes simultaneously.

The FDA's transparency-anchored approach rejects that architecture. It asks not "has this software gone through our certification process?" but "can the clinician see what this software is doing?" That is the right question because it aligns developer incentives toward interpretability without mandating specific technical architectures. A developer who wants their tool to qualify as non-device CDS has strong market incentives to build it to be transparent — to surface its reasoning in clinically legible terms, to make its evidence base auditable, to design for oversight rather than deference. That is better safety engineering than conformity assessment against a static checklist.

## The Incumbent Capture Risk

The political economy of the pushback against the January guidance deserves direct scrutiny.

The medical device industry has a structural interest in maintaining high FDA clearance barriers. Established manufacturers — those who have already navigated the 510(k) clearance and premarket approval (PMA) pathways for their existing product lines — benefit from a regulatory environment in which new entrants face identical clearance costs. When CDS software integrated directly into EHR workflows can qualify as non-device under a four-factor transparency test, it undercuts the compliance moat that established device manufacturers have built over decades of navigating FDA clearance. The guidance doesn't harm patients. It threatens incumbents.

This is a familiar dynamic in technology regulation, and it should be named clearly rather than laundered through patient safety rhetoric. When established players argue that new regulatory frameworks are dangerous for patients, the right analytical question is: dangerous compared to what? The alternative to the FDA's risk-stratified framework is not zero clinical AI — it is clinical AI governed by a clearance system that creates systematic barriers to entry for smaller EHR-integrated developers and startups. Patients in multi-hospital health systems do not benefit from a regulatory architecture optimized to protect established device manufacturers' market position.

None of this means patient advocates who have raised concerns about clinical AI are acting in bad faith. The concerns about automation bias and algorithmic errors in clinical settings are real and documented. The question is whether reimposing 510(k) or PMA requirements on CDS tools that clinicians can independently review actually addresses those concerns. It does not. Conformity assessment evaluates whether a device was safely designed and manufactured at a point in time; it does not govern how the tool is used in clinical practice, how clinicians are trained to interact with it, or whether institutional protocols encourage appropriate scrutiny of AI recommendations. The glass box standard addresses the mechanism through which automation bias occurs. Conformity assessment does not.

## The Statutory Fragility Problem

The January 2026 CDS guidance is administrative guidance, not statute. FDA guidance can be rescinded, revised, or reinterpreted by a subsequent administration with no congressional action required. The agency that issued this framework on a Tuesday can begin a new rulemaking to undo it the following Monday.

This is not hypothetical vulnerability. The FDA's approach to CDS software has oscillated across multiple guidances — a 2019 proposed guidance that interpreted the four-factor test narrowly, a 2022 final guidance that narrowed it further, and now the 2026 updated guidance that expands the non-device zone. A future administration that is more sympathetic to established device manufacturers, or simply more predisposed to precautionary regulation of AI, could plausibly reverse the 2026 guidance and reimpose device-clearance requirements on EHR-integrated CDS tools. Every company that has made product roadmap decisions based on the current guidance would face regulatory whiplash. Every health system that has deployed non-device CDS tools would face retroactive compliance questions.

The statutory fix is available. The [21st Century Cures Act](https://www.fda.gov/media/191560/download) already contains the four-factor non-device CDS criteria in section 520(o)(1)(E). What it does not contain is explicit direction on how FDA must interpret those criteria. Congress could amend the Cures Act to codify the interpretive framework the January 2026 guidance establishes — specifically, to confirm that the independent reviewability standard is satisfied when a CDS tool displays the clinical basis for its recommendation in a form that an HCP can evaluate, and to codify FDA's enforcement discretion approach to single-output CDS recommendations when only one clinically appropriate option exists. Doing so would make the current guidance's interpretive choices law, not policy — subject to congressional action to change, not a future FDA administrator's discretion.

This is not a novel legislative move. Congress has codified agency interpretations into statute across a range of domains when the underlying policy stakes warranted durable protection. The digital health industry is making long-cycle infrastructure investment decisions — EHR integration, clinical validation studies, provider training programs — that require regulatory certainty over multi-year horizons. Administrative guidance cannot provide that certainty. Statute can.

## The State Preemption Imperative

Even a durable federal framework faces a second threat: state-level clinical AI legislation that operates on different premises and imposes inconsistent requirements.

California's Privacy Protection Agency finalized [Automated Decision-Making Technology regulations](https://www.skadden.com/insights/publications/2025/10/california-finalizes-cppa-regulations) that took effect January 1, 2026, imposing pre-use notice requirements, opt-out rights, and mandatory independent cybersecurity audits for automated systems used in significant decisions — including healthcare. California has separately enacted legislation governing AI representations in healthcare contexts, with additional proposals under consideration targeting algorithmic recommendations in clinical settings. [New York's RAISE Act](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-hochul-signs-nation-leading-legislation-require-ai-frameworks-ai-frontier-models), enacted in December 2025 and effective January 2027, imposes safety policies and risk-mitigation frameworks on developers of advanced AI systems, with enforcement by the state AG and the Department of Financial Services. Neither state framework is designed with the FDA's four-factor non-device CDS distinction in mind.

A multi-state health system deploying EHR-integrated CDS tools that qualify as non-device under the federal standard may nonetheless face California audit requirements, New York safety-policy mandates, and inconsistent explainability standards across the states in which it operates. This is the compliance nightmare that preemption would prevent. A CDS tool that satisfies the FDA's glass box standard — one that surfaces its clinical basis so a physician can independently evaluate it — satisfies the underlying policy objective that California and New York's explainability mandates are trying to achieve. States imposing additional, inconsistent requirements on top of a federal transparency standard are not adding safety; they are adding compliance costs that burden multi-state deployers without improving patient outcomes.

Congress should include an express preemption provision in any Cures Act amendment codifying the four-factor non-device CDS test. The preemption clause should be narrow and targeted: state requirements that impose explainability, transparency, or audit mandates on CDS software that qualifies as non-device under the federal four-factor standard should be preempted to the extent they impose requirements inconsistent with that standard. States retain authority to regulate CDS tools that fall outside the non-device zone — high-risk AI that remains subject to FDA review — through their standard consumer protection and health regulatory authority.

## What Congress Should Do

The legislative agenda is specific and achievable within the current Congress.

**First, codify the four-factor non-device CDS test into the Cures Act.** Amend section 520(o)(1)(E) to explicitly define the independent reviewability standard and confirm FDA's enforcement discretion approach to single-output recommendations. Make the January 2026 guidance's core interpretive moves statute rather than policy.

**Second, add a targeted preemption provision.** Protect the federal framework from state-level inconsistency without displacing legitimate state health regulatory authority. The preemption clause should apply only to CDS software that qualifies as non-device under the four-factor test and only to state requirements that are inconsistent with the federal transparency standard.

**Third, authorize — and fund — FDA's post-market surveillance of non-device CDS.** Exemption from premarket review is not exemption from accountability. Congress should direct FDA to develop a post-market signal-gathering framework for non-device CDS tools, allowing the agency to identify safety signals from deployed systems without reimposing premarket clearance. This closes the accountability gap without restoring the compliance moat.

**Fourth, direct GAO to study EU MDR's clinical AI deployment effects.** The EU now requires AI-powered software medical devices to navigate both MDR and EU AI Act high-risk requirements — a dual conformity assessment burden whose market effects are only beginning to be measured. A GAO study tracking how many digital health companies have withdrawn from EU markets, how long certification applications queue with notified bodies, and what the lag time is between FDA clearance and EU market access would provide empirical grounding for the congressional debate over whether to import a conformity-assessment approach to the US.

## Conclusion

The FDA's January 2026 clinical decision support guidance is not a deregulatory gesture. It is a risk-stratified framework — calibrated oversight anchored to the question that matters most: can the clinician evaluate what the AI is recommending, and why? The glass box standard at the framework's center is the right policy lever because it aligns incentives toward interpretable AI, guards against automation bias at the mechanism level, and does so without mandating specific technical architectures or building the certification bottlenecks that have hamstrung clinical AI deployment in Europe.

The political threat to this framework is real. Incumbent medical device manufacturers have structural incentives to oppose it, and patient advocacy organizations are providing cover for that opposition without examining whose interests it actually serves. Administrative guidance is not durable enough to withstand that coalition across administration changes. The solution is a targeted statutory fix: codify the four-factor test, preempt inconsistent state mandates, and fund post-market surveillance to close the accountability gap.

Congress does not often get the opportunity to lock in a regulatory architecture that is already working. This is one of those moments. The window is open. It will not stay that way.
