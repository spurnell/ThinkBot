The Trump administration promised to tear up the Biden-era AI executive order, dismantle the safety theater of model evaluations, and unleash American AI innovation. It largely delivered on those promises. But over the past month, something else has been quietly happening — something that cuts directly against the deregulatory script.

In June, Anthropic was [compelled to take its Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models offline](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/security/anthropic-fable-5-ai-offline-trump-order-administration-claude-rcna350117), marking the first time the federal government has forced a major AI company to retract systems already in public deployment. Then, on June 26, [OpenAI released GPT-5.6 under a structure in which the U.S. government vets each customer](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2026/06/26/openai-says-us-government-will-vet-users-its-latest-ai-model/) — with only about 20 companies approved so far. As [Axios reported](https://www.axios.com/2026/06/26/openai-gpt-sol-terra-luna-trump), this is not a voluntary arrangement.

This is not deregulation. This is the most direct form of AI access control the federal government has ever exercised.

## The Paradox at the Center

Let's be precise about what is happening. The Biden administration tried to regulate AI through process: safety evaluations, reporting requirements, testing mandates. The Trump administration has bypassed process entirely and gone straight to *control* — not of how models are built, but of who is permitted to use them. The former approach was annoying and inefficient. The latter is categorically more powerful.

When the government mandates safety disclosures, a company can argue about methodology. When the government decides who your customers are, there is no argument. The Commerce Department has moved from referee to gatekeeper.

The irony is that [Anthropic actively sought regulatory engagement](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/17/anthropic-ai-regulation-trump.html) — and Washington went considerably further than the company anticipated or invited. There is a lesson here for AI companies that believe they can shape regulation by leaning into it: the government, once invited to the table, does not stay in the seat it was offered.

## The Legal Question No One Is Answering

The critical question hanging over all of this is: what is the legal authority?

The Commerce Department's International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) authority — the same statute undergirding the export control regime — permits broad executive action when a national emergency has been declared. But IEEPA was designed for foreign adversaries and cross-border transactions, not for determining which domestic companies may access a domestically-deployed AI model.

If the administration is relying on IEEPA, it is interpreting that statute in a genuinely novel way. If it is relying on some other Commerce authority — perhaps the Bureau of Industry and Security's emerging technology controls — the legal foundation is even murkier. Neither framework was designed with a "which American companies may use GPT-5.6" question in mind.

This matters enormously because the legal theory used to justify this action will determine its scope. A broad IEEPA reading could, in principle, extend to any AI capability deemed strategically sensitive. That is not a hypothetical slippery slope — it is the straightforward implication of the precedent being set right now.

Congress has not authorized this regime. No rulemaking has occurred. No notice-and-comment period. The administration has simply moved, and the companies have complied. That compliance, understandable as it is commercially and politically, should not be mistaken for legitimacy.

## What This Means for US Competitiveness

The administration's evident theory is that restricting foreign access to the most capable American AI models advances national security. This is not an unreasonable intuition. The concern that adversaries — particularly China — could exploit frontier model capabilities for intelligence, cyberattack, or weapons development is legitimate and well-documented.

But the implementation raises serious competitiveness questions that the national security framing tends to obscure.

First, the approved-customer bottleneck slows deployment. With roughly 20 companies cleared for GPT-5.6 access, OpenAI is operating at a fraction of the commercial velocity it would achieve in an open market. Every week of restricted deployment is revenue foregone, talent competition disadvantaged, and feedback loops from real-world use interrupted. The models that win are the ones with the most users, the most edge cases surfaced, the fastest iteration cycles. Gatekeeping access slows all of that.

Second, it creates a two-tier AI ecosystem: companies with government relationships and companies without. The approved customers presumably include defense contractors, large financial institutions, and well-connected technology firms. Startups, academic researchers, and smaller enterprises are not in that cohort. That is not a competitive market — it is a politically-allocated one, and politically-allocated markets tend to calcify.

Third, it may accelerate the development of alternatives. If American frontier models become difficult to access, foreign companies — and foreign-government-backed AI programs — have a stronger incentive to close the capability gap. The strategic logic of denial assumes the gap is durable. History suggests it is not.

## The Customer Vetting Model as Precedent

Perhaps the most consequential question is not whether this specific action was justified, but whether the customer vetting model becomes the new baseline for frontier AI deployment in the United States.

That is the genuine inflection point here. If GPT-5.6's launch architecture — government approval required per customer, access tiered by security clearance and relationship — becomes the template for GPT-6, Claude 4, and whatever comes next, then the structure of the American AI industry will have been fundamentally reorganized without a single congressional vote.

There is a version of this that is tolerable: narrowly scoped to foreign access, limited to a small number of genuinely exceptional capabilities, administered through transparent criteria, and subject to judicial review. There is a version that is not: open-ended domestic access control, criteria determined by executive discretion, no rulemaking, no appeals process, no sunset.

The current arrangement looks more like the latter than the former.

The administration is right that frontier AI capabilities have national security implications. It is wrong to assume that administrative control is the appropriate tool for managing them. What is needed is a transparent legal framework, developed with congressional input, that specifies the narrow conditions under which access restrictions are warranted — and that builds in the procedural protections necessary to prevent emergency authority from hardening into permanent industrial policy.

We do not have that framework. We have executive fiat. And the precedent being set right now will be available to every future administration, regardless of its ideological orientation.

That ought to concentrate minds.
