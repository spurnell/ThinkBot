Voluntary programs run on trust in their own durability. Take that away, and the program collapses even if nothing about its substance has changed. That is precisely what has happened to the Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act of 2015 (CISA 2015) over the past ten months — not because the law stopped working, but because Congress has let it lapse, twice, and nearly lapse a third time, purely as collateral damage in fights that have nothing to do with cybersecurity.

CISA 2015 expired on September 30, 2025 when the government shut down. It was revived on November 12, 2025, as part of the deal that reopened the government, but only through January 30, 2026. It lapsed again — briefly — before Congress extended it a second time, through September 30, 2026, in the February 3, 2026 funding bill. Three brushes with expiration in under a year is not evidence that the underlying law is broken. It's evidence that Congress has turned a foundational piece of cyber-defense infrastructure into a bargaining chip in disputes about something else entirely, and that habit is now doing more damage to information sharing than any gap in the statute's substance ever could.

## What CISA 2015 Actually Does — and Why the Model Is Right

CISA 2015 does not mandate that any company share anything. It creates no new regulator, imposes no reporting deadlines, and sets no technical standards. What it does is narrower and, precisely because of that, more effective: it removes the legal risk that would otherwise deter companies from voluntarily sharing cyber threat indicators and defensive measures with the federal government, with each other, and with information sharing and analysis organizations (ISAOs) and centers (ISACs). Specifically, the law provides liability protection against private lawsuits arising from sharing threat data, an exemption from Freedom of Information Act disclosure, protection against antitrust liability for competitors coordinating on threat information, and safeguards against waiver of legal privilege — all as summarized in the [Congressional Research Service's primer on the law's expiring provisions](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF12959).

That is a deliberately light-touch design, and it is the right one. The alternative — a mandatory reporting regime with a new compliance apparatus — already exists in a different statute (CIRCIA, still working through its own rulemaking fights), and mandate-based models carry real costs: they turn threat sharing into a compliance exercise defined by regulators rather than a defense practice driven by security teams, and they invite scope creep into data retention, use restrictions, and enforcement discretion that voluntary frameworks avoid by design. CISA 2015's contribution isn't that it requires more sharing — it's that it clears away the reason companies' general counsels would otherwise tell security teams to say less. As the House Homeland Security Committee's own account of the law makes clear, the entire theory of the 2015 bargain was that clearing legal risk, not creating legal obligation, would produce better real-time threat visibility across sectors — see [Nossaman's overview of the House's cybersecurity reauthorization push](https://www.nossaman.com/newsroom-insights-house-advances-key-cybersecurity-measures-ahead-of-september-deadline). Ten years of operation validated that theory. What's now undermining it isn't the design — it's Congress's inability to leave the design alone.

## The Lapse Timeline Is the Real Policy Failure

Walk through what actually happened. When the government shut down on October 1, 2025, CISA 2015's protections lapsed along with it, stripping companies of liability cover, FOIA exemption, and privilege protection for any sharing that occurred during the gap — a dynamic [Goodwin's client alert](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2025/10/alerts-practices-dpc-cisa-2015-lapses-with-reauthorization-uncertain) and [Davis Wright Tremaine's analysis](https://www.dwt.com/blogs/privacy--security-law-blog/2025/10/cybersecurity-information-sharing-act-expires) both flagged as immediately consequential, not theoretical. Congress restored the protections on November 12, 2025 as part of the shutdown-ending funding package, but only through January 30, 2026 — a stopgap patch, not a fix, as [Federal News Network reported at the time](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/cybersecurity/2025/11/congress-extends-cisa-2015-but-path-to-long-term-reauthorization-remains-murky/). The law then lapsed again at the end of January before Congress passed a second extension in the February 3, 2026 appropriations bill, pushing the sunset to September 30, 2026 — again, per [Davis Wright Tremaine's follow-up post](https://www.dwt.com/blogs/privacy--security-law-blog/2026/02/congress-extends-cisa-2015-thru-september) and [Wiley's own analysis of the extension](https://www.wileyconnect.com/congress-reauthorizes-cisa-2015-through-september-but-then-what), which bluntly notes that a durable long-term fix remains nowhere close to enactment despite the reprieve.

Three near-misses in one year for a law industry treats as settled infrastructure is a distinct failure from anything in the statute's text. Nothing about CISA 2015's liability shield, FOIA exemption, or privilege protection has been shown to need revision after a decade of use. What has changed is that Congress now treats its expiration date as a lever in shutdown brinkmanship — attaching it to continuing resolutions where it has no substantive connection to the underlying spending dispute, simply because it happens to share a fiscal-year clock. That is a process failure, and it is one Congress is inflicting entirely on itself.

## The Chilling Effect Is Not Hypothetical

Supporters of muddling through might argue that since Congress always restored the law within weeks, no real harm resulted. The evidence says otherwise. During the September–November 2025 lapse, reduced willingness on both sides of the sharing relationship became visible in practice: Health-ISAC's chief security officer reported private-sector companies pulling back on what they were willing to share with the federal government, while less threat intelligence flowed back from FBI, DHS, and CISA partners in return — a two-way chilling effect [documented by Health-ISAC](https://health-isac.org/cisa-2015-receives-extension-offering-brief-relief-for-cyber-information-sharing/). A senior director at the Business Software Alliance separately reported that legal counsel became more involved in every sharing decision during the gap, exactly the dynamic the 2015 law was designed to eliminate. Utilities reported similar effects: incident response and reporting timelines that normally run in the 30-minute range stretched out dramatically once the legal cover disappeared, as [Utility Dive reported](https://www.utilitydive.com/news/as-cyber-threats-grow-utilities-say-lapsed-information-sharing-law-stymies/803189/).

That is the mechanism working exactly as legal risk theory predicts: general counsels don't wait to see whether a lapse turns out to matter in practice. The moment the statutory shield is gone, the rational instinct is to assume liability exposure is live and route sharing decisions through legal review — which means less gets shared, more slowly, for the duration of every gap, regardless of how briefly it lasts. This is happening while ransomware activity against critical infrastructure and nation-state intrusion campaigns remain elevated — precisely the conditions under which fast, low-friction threat sharing matters most, and precisely the wrong moment for the legal architecture underneath it to be uncertain even intermittently.

## Decouple the Law From the Shutdown Fight

The fix is not complicated, even if the politics are. Congress should pass a clean reauthorization — ideally permanent, or at minimum a ten-year window matching the original 2015 authorization horizon — and pass it on a vehicle detached from any future appropriations or shutdown fight. Bipartisan groundwork already exists: Senators Gary Peters and Mike Rounds introduced S. 1337, the Cybersecurity Information Sharing Extension Act (also referred to as the PAACT Act), a clean ten-year extension with no substantive policy changes, while the House Homeland Security Committee advanced its own ten-year extension led by Chairman Andrew Garbarino, reported by [GovCIO Media](https://govciomedia.com/congress-seeks-10-year-renewal-of-cyber-threat-sharing-law/). Both efforts recognize the core point of this piece: the law's substance is not what's in dispute.

What's blocking action is not disagreement about whether information sharing works — it's Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee Chairman Rand Paul's insistence that any reauthorization address his objections to CISA (the agency)'s past work on election misinformation, a fight [Punchbowl News](https://punchbowl.news/article/tech/paul-cisa/) and [POLITICO](https://subscriber.politicopro.com/article/2025/06/rand-paul-2015-cisa-reauthorization-disinformation-00390060) have both covered in detail. Paul's draft alternative reportedly would strip the FOIA exemption and change core definitions — changes that would reopen the entire 2015 bargain rather than preserve it. Whatever the merits of the separate disinformation-policy dispute, it belongs in its own legislative vehicle, addressed on its own timeline, adjudicated in ordinary committee process — not used as leverage against a threat-sharing liability shield with no connection to that fight. Holding CISA 2015 hostage to an unrelated dispute has already cost the private sector two documented periods of degraded information sharing; a third lapse at the September 30, 2026 deadline would cost it a third.

## Permanence Is the Deregulatory Position

There is a version of this argument that treats sunset clauses as accountability — a chance for Congress to check in periodically and decide whether a program still deserves to exist. That logic has real force for programs with unresolved harms or open empirical questions. It has none here. CISA 2015 has a decade of operational history, and the questions worth asking about it — is the liability shield too broad, is FOIA exemption appropriate, is DHS's indicator-scrubbing adequate — are questions for ordinary oversight hearings, not questions that require the statute's legal footing to lapse before Congress will engage them. Making the law permanent doesn't insulate it from scrutiny; it just stops using scrutiny as an excuse to let a working liability shield go dark on a recurring, unpredictable schedule.

This is also, notably, the case where "leave it alone" is the pro-market, deregulatory position rather than the status quo. A prescriptive alternative — new mandatory-sharing rules, an expanded federal retention and use regime for shared data, a new compliance body — would mean more government machinery layered onto a program that currently works because it has none. Permanence means less recurring legislative intervention, not more federal footprint: a decade or more where CISOs and general counsels can build sharing programs around a legal foundation they can actually rely on, instead of recalculating risk exposure every time a continuing resolution comes up for a vote. Congress has the sponsors, the House committee vote, and the September 30 deadline to make that case concrete. It should use them — and it should resist every temptation, from any direction, to use this bill as the vehicle for a fight that belongs somewhere else.
