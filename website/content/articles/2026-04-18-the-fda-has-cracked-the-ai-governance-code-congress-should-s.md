# The FDA Has Cracked the AI Governance Code — Congress Should Study It Before Legislating

In January 2026, the Food and Drug Administration did something remarkable and almost entirely unreported in the AI governance conversation: it [announced a significant pullback from oversight of AI-enabled clinical decision support software and low-risk wearables](https://www.statnews.com/2026/01/06/fda-pulls-back-oversight-ai-enabled-devices-wearables/), explicitly calibrating its regulatory footprint to actual demonstrated risk. At the same moment, the agency was operating the most sophisticated tiered AI governance architecture in the federal government — one built on decades of clinical evidence standards, robust post-market surveillance infrastructure, and a novel clearance pathway called the Predetermined Change Control Plan that allows AI products to improve iteratively without running the full regulatory gauntlet each time a model updates.

Meanwhile, on the other end of Pennsylvania Avenue, Congress is drafting horizontal AI mandates that would apply uniform rules to healthcare AI and entertainment AI alike, as though the risk of a misdiagnosed cancer and the risk of a bad Netflix recommendation belong to the same regulatory category.

The juxtaposition should be embarrassing. The FDA has spent a decade developing hard-won institutional knowledge about how to govern AI systems in a domain where errors kill people, and Congress is preparing to legislate over the top of it with blunt, sector-agnostic instruments. Before any member of Congress votes on the [AI Foundation Model Transparency Act](https://beyer.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=9013) or the [AI LEAD Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2937/text), they should spend an afternoon studying the FDA's playbook. The lessons are directly transferable — if legislators are willing to learn them.

## The FDA's Tiered Architecture: What It Actually Does

The FDA regulates AI-enabled medical devices under its existing device framework, but it has spent the past several years refining that framework into something far more sophisticated than most AI governance discussions acknowledge. The core insight is deceptively simple: not all AI is equally dangerous, and regulatory burden should scale with risk.

The January 2026 announcement made this explicit. The FDA signaled that it would ease its oversight posture on software-based clinical decision support tools and low-risk wearables — devices that provide information to clinicians or patients but leave decision-making authority in human hands. This is risk-calibrated oversight, not an abdication of regulatory responsibility. The FDA wasn't retreating; it was reallocating. By pulling back from lower-stakes software, the agency signals clearly that it intends to concentrate its limited review bandwidth on the devices that actually need it: autonomous diagnostic AI, computer-aided detection tools for radiology, AI-driven surgical guidance systems — systems where a software error directly produces patient harm.

The [Bipartisan Policy Center's analysis of FDA health AI oversight](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/issue-brief/fda-oversight-understanding-the-regulation-of-health-ai-tools/) documents how this tiered structure works in practice: Class I devices with minimal risk face general controls only; Class II devices clear through the 510(k) pathway requiring demonstrated substantial equivalence; Class III devices — the highest-risk category — must navigate full premarket approval with rigorous clinical evidence. AI-enabled devices slot into this taxonomy based on their intended use and risk profile, not based on the technology powering them.

This architecture accomplishes something that every proposed horizontal AI bill fails to achieve: it embeds a coherent theory of harm calibration. The question the FDA asks is not "is this AI?" but "what goes wrong if this AI fails, and how bad is it?" That is the right question. It is, notably, not the question the AI Foundation Model Transparency Act is asking.

## PCCPs: The Innovation-Compatible Solution Congress Has Never Heard Of

The most consequential innovation in the FDA's AI governance framework isn't getting nearly enough attention outside specialized legal and regulatory circles. In December 2024, the FDA finalized its [Predetermined Change Control Plan guidance](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2024/12/fda-finalizes-guidance-on-predetermined-change-control-plans-for-ai-enabled-device-software), the capstone of a multi-year rulemaking that also encompasses [AI-enabled device software lifecycle management guidance](https://www.hoganlovells.com/en/publications/fda-device-guidance-agenda-what-to-watch-in-2026) addressing how manufacturers should continuously validate and monitor AI systems post-clearance. PCCPs solve a problem that anyone who has thought seriously about AI regulation has had to confront: AI systems improve continuously, and a regulatory framework that requires full clearance every time a model updates is one that either freezes AI at the moment of approval or drives manufacturers to forgo improvements they could legally market.

The PCCP framework cuts this knot elegantly. Rather than requiring manufacturers to re-run full clearance every time an AI algorithm updates, the FDA allows manufacturers to pre-specify a change methodology — what kinds of changes they plan to make, under what conditions, using what validation protocols — and get that methodology cleared upfront. Once the change protocol is approved, the manufacturer can execute improvements within those bounds without triggering a new premarket submission.

This is architecturally brilliant. It shifts regulatory review from individual model updates to the governance framework governing those updates. The FDA is asking: does this manufacturer have a rigorous, validated process for making changes responsibly? If yes, approve the process and trust the manufacturer to execute within it. If they deviate from the approved methodology, that is a compliance failure. If the methodology itself proves inadequate, post-market surveillance catches it.

The policy implication is direct: the "frozen at clearance" problem that critics of AI regulation correctly identify as a major innovation cost is not a fundamental feature of AI governance — it is a feature of badly designed AI governance. PCCPs demonstrate that rigorous pre-market review and continuous AI improvement can coexist. You just need a framework sophisticated enough to distinguish between approving a product and approving a process.

No pending horizontal AI bill comes anywhere near this level of sophistication. The [AI Foundation Model Transparency Act](https://fedscoop.com/ftc-enforcement-ai-foundation-models-bill/), introduced in March 2026 by Representatives Beyer, Lawler, and Jacobs, focuses on disclosure requirements for foundation model training data and capabilities. The [AI LEAD Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2937/text) creates a federal products liability framework for AI-caused harms. Neither proposal contains anything resembling a PCCP analog — no mechanism for pre-approving a change methodology that allows AI systems to improve without regulatory paralysis. They impose documentation burdens or liability exposure without solving the underlying governance problem the FDA has already solved.

## Sectoral Expertise Is Not Replicable on a Legislative Timeline

There is a deeper problem with horizontal AI mandates that disclosure requirements and liability frameworks cannot address: they require regulatory agencies, courts, and regulated entities to develop substantive competence they do not have, in domains they do not understand, on timelines that bear no relationship to the complexity of the underlying technology.

The FDA has been regulating medical devices since 1976. It built post-market surveillance infrastructure over decades. It has real-world evidence standards refined through hundreds of product cycles. It employs biostatisticians, clinical scientists, and software engineers who understand the difference between a radiology AI trained on a demographically narrow dataset and one validated across diverse patient populations. When the FDA evaluates a clinical decision support tool, it can ask: does this training set reflect the patient population likely to use this device? What are the failure modes under distribution shift? How does performance degrade in low-resource clinical settings?

A horizontal mandate cannot ask these questions competently — not because the FTC lacks capable staff, but because clinical evidence evaluation, medical device safety, and health outcomes research are not the FTC's institutional knowledge base. Similarly, [SR 11-7 model risk management guidance](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr1107.htm) — the Federal Reserve and OCC framework governing how banks validate quantitative models — represents decades of supervisory learning developed through hundreds of examination cycles. No horizontal AI bill can transfer that accumulated expertise by statute.

This is not a knock on any particular agency. It is a structural point: institutional knowledge takes decades to build, it lives in agencies with specific mandates, and it is not transferable by legislation. A bill that says "the FTC shall regulate AI across all sectors" does not give the FTC clinical knowledge, financial modeling capacity, or aviation safety expertise. It gives the FTC jurisdiction — a very different thing.

The [FDA's tiered device framework](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/issue-brief/fda-oversight-understanding-the-regulation-of-health-ai-tools/) represents the output of 50 years of institutional learning about how to evaluate safety evidence for complex medical technologies. The proposal to layer a horizontal AI overlay on top of that framework does not supplement that expertise — it implicitly tells the FDA its judgment is insufficient and routes authority to entities without comparable experience. That is not a recipe for better patient outcomes.

## What the Horizontal Bills Get Wrong

The [AI Foundation Model Transparency Act](https://beyer.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=9013) reflects a genuine and understandable concern: as foundation models become infrastructure for high-stakes decisions across sectors, the opacity of their training, capabilities, and limitations creates real accountability gaps. The sponsors are right that disclosure matters. The problem is that the bill proposes a single disclosure framework administered by a single agency — the FTC — for AI deployed across healthcare, finance, entertainment, transportation, and national security. These are domains with wildly different risk profiles, evidence standards, and regulatory traditions.

A radiology AI used to flag potential tumors and a large language model used to recommend streaming content are both "AI-enabled systems." They share essentially nothing else relevant to their governance. The training data disclosure requirements that make sense for one are irrelevant to the other. The clinical validation standards that matter for the first have no analog for the second. A one-size-fits-all disclosure mandate optimized for neither use case will generate compliance documentation that satisfies a checkbox without improving safety or accountability for either.

The [AI LEAD Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2937/text) takes a different but equally horizontal approach: it would classify AI systems as products subject to federal liability standards, creating a cause of action for design defects, failure to warn, and unreasonably dangerous outputs — applying that framework uniformly across healthcare diagnostics, financial services, transportation, and consumer entertainment. Accountability through liability has intuitive appeal; it does not require building a new regulatory apparatus. But uniform products liability rules face the same domain-expertise problem from a different angle. A court adjudicating whether a diagnostic AI was defective must apply clinical evidence standards — the same standards the FDA has spent decades developing the institutional capacity to apply. Federal courts cannot substitute for that expertise, and a liability statute cannot conjure it.

The FTC's institutional limits compound the problem for the Foundation Model Transparency Act specifically. The FTC is well-positioned to address deceptive AI practices in consumer markets — that is squarely within its existing expertise. It is not positioned to second-guess FDA clinical evidence standards for diagnostic AI, [OCC model risk management guidance](https://www.federalreserve.gov/supervisionreg/srletters/sr1107.htm) for credit underwriting algorithms, or FAA software safety requirements for aviation AI. Vesting disclosure authority in the FTC does not transfer domain expertise; it creates a new jurisdictional layer that sector regulators and regulated entities will spend years litigating.

The category error underlying both proposals is the same: treating AI as a sector rather than a technology. Electricity is a technology. We don't have a single "electricity regulator" — we have FERC for wholesale power markets, state utility commissions for retail distribution, OSHA for workplace electrical safety, and the National Electrical Code for building standards. The right governance architecture for a technology that pervades every sector of the economy is sector-specific expertise coordinated at the margins, not a single horizontal overlay that discards the knowledge already embedded in domain regulators.

## The Right Policy Prescription: Replicate the FDA Model, Don't Override It

Congress's instinct to legislate on AI is not wrong — but the form that legislation should take is different from what is currently being drafted. The FDA's framework suggests a set of principles that would actually work:

**First, fund sector regulators' AI capacity.** The FDA, the OCC, the FAA, the FTC, and the CFPB are all encountering AI in their regulatory domains with budgets and staff capacity that predate the current AI capabilities inflection. Congress should appropriate dedicated resources for AI evaluation expertise at each agency — not to build new offices, but to deepen existing ones. The FDA's [AI-enabled device software lifecycle guidance](https://www.hoganlovells.com/en/publications/fda-device-guidance-agenda-what-to-watch-in-2026) required years of development and significant clinical expertise to produce. Other agencies need comparable runway and investment.

**Second, mandate NIST coordination without mandating NIST uniformity.** The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) provides a vocabulary and process structure that sector regulators can adapt to their domains without imposing uniform substantive requirements. Congress could require that agencies with AI-related regulatory responsibilities develop sector-specific AI governance frameworks consistent with the NIST RMF — ensuring coordination and shared concepts without overriding domain expertise.

**Third, adopt the PCCP model as a statutory principle.** Congress could instruct sector regulators to develop iterative clearance pathways — analogous to the FDA's PCCPs — for AI systems in their domains. This would be a technology-policy mandate about governance architecture, not about specific disclosure requirements or liability standards. It would reduce the innovation tax of regulatory friction while preserving rigorous pre-market review for high-risk applications.

**Fourth, resist the new-agency temptation.** Every major technology policy debate produces calls for a new dedicated regulator. For AI, the answer is the same as it was for the internet, for financial technology, and for biotechnology: domain knowledge already exists in functioning agencies. The question is whether to fund and direct that knowledge, or to route around it. Routing around it produces worse governance and wastes the institutional investment already made.

## Conclusion

The FDA's evolving framework for AI-enabled medical devices is the federal government's most coherent answer to a genuinely hard problem: how do you govern rapidly improving AI systems in high-stakes domains without either freezing innovation or abdicating safety responsibility? Its answers — tiered risk stratification, risk-calibrated oversight, predetermined change control, post-market surveillance, and deep domain expertise — are not perfect, but they are serious. They represent the output of real institutional learning applied to real safety challenges.

Congress is on the verge of legislating past that experience in favor of horizontal mandates built on the assumption that AI risk is uniform across radically different applications. It is not. The FDA knows this. The OCC knows this. The FAA knows this. The question is whether Congress is willing to listen to the regulators who have already done this work before it tries to override them.

The lesson for Capitol Hill is not complicated: learn from the FDA model, fund it, extend it to other agencies, and coordinate through NIST. What the moment does not call for is a parallel architecture built from scratch, administered by agencies without the expertise to run it, covering domains where the answers are already being worked out by the people who know the territory.

That is not regulatory sophistication. That is regulatory theater — and patients, consumers, and innovators alike will pay the price.
