The comment period on the Trump Department of Labor's proposed independent contractor rule [closed on April 28](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/whd/flsa/misclassification/2026rulemaking). The agency is now sifting through tens of thousands of submissions and drafting a final rule that will define how tens of millions of Americans who work independently relate to federal labor law. This is the inflection point.

The proposal — [published in the Federal Register on February 27](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/02/27/2026-03962/employee-or-independent-contractor-status-under-the-fair-labor-standards-act-family-and-medical) as RIN 1235-AA46 — would replace the Biden administration's six-factor totality-of-the-circumstances test with a framework that centers classification on two core factors: the nature and degree of control over the work, and the worker's opportunity for profit or loss based on initiative and investment. Three secondary guideposts — skill required, permanence of the relationship, and integration into the hiring entity's operations — apply when the core factors don't resolve the question. This is not a rollback of worker protections. It is an alignment of regulatory standards with the economic reality test that federal courts have applied for nearly eight decades.

The Biden rule was regulatory mission creep — a totality-of-the-circumstances test engineered to funnel platform workers into employee status regardless of the actual working relationship. The DOL should finalize the two-factor framework promptly. And Congress should pair it with a federal preemption floor before state-level ABC tests fragment a national labor market that 72.9 million Americans depend on for income, autonomy, and flexibility.

## A Six-Factor Test Designed to Reclassify

The Biden administration's 2024 independent contractor rule was not neutral analysis dressed in regulatory language. Its six-factor totality-of-the-circumstances test — which held explicitly that no single factor carried greater weight than any other — was engineered to maximize enforcement discretion and minimize the likelihood that any gig worker would be classified as an independent contractor. The framing was deliberate: if control and profit/loss opportunity (the two factors courts have historically treated as dispositive) couldn't be decisive, then classification outcomes were always subject to regulatory override.

The practical consequences were immediate. [At least five lawsuits were filed](https://www.uschamber.com/employment-law/u-s-chamber-praises-pause-on-enforcement-of-biden-era-independent-contractor-rule) against the rule before it could take full effect, including a challenge by the U.S. Chamber of Commerce. Small platforms — unable to absorb the cost of federal litigation — faced a stark choice: shoulder the compliance costs of treating contractors as employees, or exit the market. Large incumbents with legal departments and billion-dollar balance sheets could weather the uncertainty; the smaller platforms most likely to drive competition and innovation could not. This is the anticompetitive logic of outcome-driven rulemaking: it concentrates the market in favor of incumbents who can bear its costs.

The DOL's issuance of [Field Assistance Bulletin 2025-1](https://www.jacksonlewis.com/insights/businesses-get-break-dol-wont-enforce-2024-independent-contractor-rule) — announcing non-enforcement of the Biden rule — acknowledged the rule's problems, but it left the Biden rule technically in force for purposes of private litigation. That unstable state of affairs imposed costs without resolving the underlying legal uncertainty.

The six-factor framework was a departure from judicial precedent, not a restoration of it. Since [*United States v. Silk*](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/331/704/) (1947), federal courts have applied an economic reality test that asks whether a worker is economically dependent on the hiring entity or genuinely in business for themselves. Control and opportunity for profit or loss have been the load-bearing factors in that analysis across every circuit. The Biden rule didn't add factors to capture economic complexity — it added factors to dilute the two that courts have consistently treated as most important.

## Courts Have Always Known Which Factors Matter

The 2026 NPRM's two-factor framework is not a novel invention. It restores the approach codified in the 2021 rule, which recognized that control and profit/loss opportunity are the "core factors" in economic dependence analysis — the ones whose alignment generally determines whether a worker is in business for themselves or economically dependent on the hiring entity.

Critically, the [current proposal retains three secondary guideposts](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/02/27/2026-03962/employee-or-independent-contractor-status-under-the-fair-labor-standards-act-family-and-medical) — skill required, permanence of the relationship, and integration into the hiring entity's operations — that courts consider when the core factors don't point to a clear answer. This is not a two-question test that ignores complexity. It is a structured hierarchy that reflects how economic reality analysis actually works in practice: start with what matters most, apply secondary analysis when necessary.

Every circuit that has grappled with FLSA independent contractor classification has reached the same conclusion about which factors matter most. Simplifying the regulatory framework to reflect that conclusion is not a retreat from rigor. It removes the pretense that regulators can achieve through rulemaking what courts have consistently declined to do through adjudication — and it restores the predictability that small businesses and independent workers need to structure their relationships with confidence.

## 72.9 Million Americans Have Made Their Preference Clear

The argument that forced reclassification helps workers runs directly into the evidence on worker preference. The [MBO Partners 2025 State of Independence report](https://www.mbopartners.com/state-of-independence/) — the most comprehensive annual survey of the independent workforce — found that 72.9 million Americans worked independently in 2025. Of those, 86% of full-time independents say they are *happier* working on their own, and a record 5.6 million reported annual earnings above $100,000, a 19% increase from the year before. A majority also report feeling more financially secure as independents than they did as traditional employees.

Upwork's [2025 study of the U.S. skilled knowledge workforce](https://investors.upwork.com/news-releases/news-release-details/upwork-study-finds-1-4-us-skilled-knowledge-workers-now-work) sharpens the picture. One in four skilled knowledge workers — roughly 20 million people — now work independently, generating $1.5 trillion in annual earnings. The median income for full-time freelancers ($85,000) exceeds the median for traditional employees ($80,000). Critically, **79% of full-time freelancers chose independent work by preference, not necessity**, and 84% cite schedule flexibility as the primary driver of that choice.

These are not workers trapped in the platform economy for lack of options. These are workers who have structured their professional lives around the autonomy that independent contracting provides. Mandatory reclassification would not empower them. It would eliminate the scheduling freedom, multi-client income diversification, and career autonomy they have deliberately chosen — replacing those arrangements with a traditional employment relationship they could already obtain if they wanted it. The regulatory intervention is paternalistic by design.

The DOL's projected [$2.31 billion in 10-year compliance savings](https://www.dol.gov/newsroom/releases/whd/whd20260226) — $329 million annualized at a 7% discount rate — flow disproportionately to the small businesses and solo operators who bore the heaviest burden under the Biden rule's classification uncertainty. These savings are not accounting tricks. They represent real resources freed from compliance bureaucracy and litigation risk, available to hire more contractors, invest in better tools, or lower prices for the consumers they serve.

## AB5 Is the Cautionary Tale — And It's Still Spreading

Even a sound federal rule doesn't solve the problem alone. The more pressing structural threat is state-level fragmentation, and California's AB5 is the template.

AB5 (2019) replaced the economic reality standard with an "ABC test" that creates a near-irrebuttable presumption of employee status for anyone who performs work that falls within the usual course of a hiring entity's business — a standard so broad that it swept up not just platform workers but freelance journalists, translators, and musicians. The legislative response to that overreach was telling: California was forced to carve out [dozens of professional categories](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billTextClient.xhtml?bill_id=201920200AB5) because the ABC test was too blunt even by its own proponents' standards. Rideshare and delivery platforms obtained a separate exemption through a 2020 ballot initiative (Proposition 22). More than three dozen exemptions now exist in the law — a patchwork that testifies to the original test's overreach.

What did AB5 actually accomplish for the workers it was designed to protect? Research published in [*Information Systems Research*](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/isre.2023.0007) found that employers responded to the rising cost of hiring California-based gig workers by lowering hourly rates — and workers responded by extending their working hours to sustain total income. The law that was supposed to improve working conditions measurably made workers work more for less per hour. This is the economic logic of rigid classification mandates: costs imposed on platforms don't disappear; they shift, and they typically shift downward onto the workers the regulation claimed to help.

The AB5 dynamic is not contained to California. As of 2026, [more than 27 states](https://www.aoshearman.com/en/insights/recent-developments-in-us-worker-classification-rules) use some version of the ABC test for unemployment insurance or labor law purposes. With federal classification standards in flux, state legislative sessions in full swing, and labor advocacy organizations actively shopping AB5-style bills to receptive legislatures, the risk of further spread is real. A sound federal rule matters — but it cannot, by itself, preempt the state-level patchwork from which this fragmentation flows.

This fragmentation dynamic recurs across technology policy: a national economy cannot function coherently under 50 different classification standards, particularly when some of those standards are structured as de facto prohibitions on the platform business model.

## Platform Infrastructure Is Consumer Infrastructure

The standard cost-benefit frame for worker classification debates focuses on compliance costs and labor protections. It systematically underweights a third category of harm: the downstream effects on consumers and communities that depend on platform-mediated services.

Platform-mediated work is not merely a labor market arrangement — it is an infrastructure layer. Rideshare, food and grocery delivery, on-demand home services, and related platform categories represent access channels that are particularly critical in markets where traditional service providers have thin coverage: rural communities, suburban areas without dense public transit, and lower-income urban neighborhoods underserved by legacy industries. As [Brookings has documented](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/to-enhance-community-services-and-empower-workers-local-governments-are-building-their-own-gig-work-platforms/), even local governments have begun building platform-based service delivery mechanisms because of the access advantages the model provides.

Forced reclassification restructures the unit economics of these platforms in predictable ways. Employee status requires payroll taxes, benefits, guaranteed hours, and overtime compliance — costs that translate directly into higher service prices, reduced geographic coverage, and market exits in the thin-margin service areas that can least absorb them. The consumers who lose access to a grocery delivery platform or rideshare service are not, for the most part, wealthy urban professionals with abundant alternatives. They are the communities regulators claim to be protecting.

The DOL's regulatory impact analysis accounts for direct compliance savings. It does not fully account for the consumer welfare losses associated with reclassification-driven service contraction. A more complete analysis would show that the true cost of over-regulation falls most heavily on the communities that depend most on platform access — and the true benefit of a clear, predictable classification standard accrues to those same communities through sustained service availability.

## What the DOL and Congress Should Do

**Finalize the two-factor framework without restoring Biden-rule elements through the back door.** The comment period is closed. The DOL should issue a final rule that maintains the core-factors structure and provides maximum predictability. Regulatory clarity is the goal; stability of the standard is the precondition for achieving it. Any temptation to re-introduce Biden-rule factors as "illustrative considerations" in regulatory guidance would recreate the enforcement discretion problem under a different label.

**Congress should pass a federal preemption floor.** A sound DOL rule is necessary but not sufficient. Without a federal standard that prevents states from applying classification tests more restrictive than the FLSA framework, California's ABC test will continue to spread — and the gains from federal regulatory clarity will be eroded one state legislature at a time. The [Cassidy-Scott-Paul legislative package](https://www.help.senate.gov/rep/newsroom/press/chair-cassidy-scott-paul-release-legislative-package-empowering-independent-workers-to-access-portable-benefits) — including the Modern Worker Empowerment Act, which would preempt stricter state classification standards — represents exactly this kind of federal floor. It should move alongside, or immediately after, the DOL finalizes its rule.

**Expand the regulatory impact analysis to capture downstream consumer effects.** The DOL should quantify the consumer welfare effects of forced reclassification scenarios in the final rule's RIA, including service price increases and geographic coverage contraction in rural and underserved markets. A complete cost-benefit analysis strengthens the rule's durability against legal challenge — and it produces a more honest accounting of who actually bears the cost of regulatory overreach.

**Pursue statutory codification.** Executive-branch rulemaking is reversible by the next administration. The regulatory pendulum that has made independent contractor classification a moving target across four administrations cannot be stopped by another executive rule alone. Congress should codify the two-factor economic reality standard in the FLSA itself. Statutory durability is the only reliable protection against future mission creep.

## Conclusion

The DOL is now doing exactly what it should: weighing the record, evaluating evidence, and preparing a final rule grounded in judicial precedent and economic reality. The two-factor framework is the right answer — not because it favors any particular industry, but because it reflects how federal courts have analyzed economic dependence for nearly 80 years, because the workers most affected prefer the arrangements it protects, and because the compliance savings it unlocks flow to the small businesses and independent workers who bore the heaviest burden under the Biden rule's manufactured uncertainty.

The harder problem is state-level fragmentation — and it requires a Congressional solution. The Cassidy-Scott-Paul package is the vehicle. The moment, with the DOL actively writing a final rule and state legislative sessions in progress, is now. Without statutory codification alongside federal preemption, the classification whipsaw will resume the next time the White House changes hands.

The 72.9 million Americans who work independently should be able to keep the arrangements they chose. The DOL's proposed rule gets the economics and the law right. Congress needs to make it stick.
