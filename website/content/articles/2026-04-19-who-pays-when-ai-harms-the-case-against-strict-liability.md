# Who Pays When AI Harms? The Case Against Strict Liability

The AI liability debate has arrived faster than Congress expected, and on two fronts simultaneously. In federal court, a May 2025 ruling in [*Garcia v. Character Technologies*](https://www.transparencycoalition.ai/news/important-early-ruling-in-characterai-case-this-chatbot-is-a-product-not-speech) treated a large language model's outputs as a defective "product" rather than an interactive service — denying Character.AI First Amendment protection and allowing strict product liability claims to proceed toward trial. On Capitol Hill, Sen. Marsha Blackburn's [Trump America AI Act discussion draft](https://www.blackburn.senate.gov/2026/3/technology/blackburn-releases-discussion-draft-of-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence) would codify that logic in statute, establishing a no-fault federal cause of action for harms caused by AI systems before courts have had the opportunity to develop workable standards.

The liability standard America chooses in the next eighteen months will shape the AI industry for a generation. Get it wrong in the direction of strict liability, and the industry concentrates at the hyperscaler level, startups are priced out of deployment, and the competitive pressure that drives model quality evaporates. Get it wrong in the direction of zero accountability, and injured parties have no meaningful recourse and developers face no incentive to exercise care.

Neither extreme is right. The appropriate standard is **sector-calibrated negligence**: a reasonable-care test tuned to deployment context and risk severity, paired with mandatory incident disclosure in high-stakes domains so that plaintiffs have the evidentiary access they need to prove claims. That framework is rigorous, proven, and compatible with a competitive AI market. Strict liability is none of those things.

## What *Garcia* Actually Held — and What It Got Wrong

The facts of *Garcia* are not in dispute, and they are genuinely tragic. Sewell Setzer III, a fourteen-year-old, developed an obsessive relationship with an AI persona on Character.AI's platform and died by suicide in 2024. His mother, Megan Garcia, sued Character Technologies on multiple theories, including product liability. In May 2025, U.S. District Judge Anne Conway denied the defendants' motion to dismiss, [ruling that the Character.AI application constitutes a "product" for product liability purposes](https://naturalandartificiallaw.com/garcia-v-character-ai-update/) — so long as the alleged defect arises from the application's design rather than any specific expression within it — and that the chatbot's outputs do not qualify as constitutionally protected speech that would shield the company from liability.

The ruling's narrow holding — that a motion to dismiss should not be granted — is defensible. The broader doctrinal import, however, is significant and troubling. By classifying a generative AI system as a product for strict liability purposes, the court imported a body of doctrine — strict products liability as descended from *[MacPherson v. Buick Motor Co.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MacPherson_v._Buick_Motor_Co.)* and *[Greenman v. Yuba Power Products, Inc.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenman_v._Yuba_Power_Products,_Inc.)* — that was designed for manufactured goods with fixed, discoverable defect profiles.

That analogy breaks down on first contact with how large language models actually work. A defective automobile wheel has a physical flaw that exists prior to and independent of any particular user's interaction with it. An LLM's output is a probabilistic function of an enormous parameter space interacting with a specific user prompt in a specific context. The same model that returns a helpful response to 99.999 percent of inputs may, in an edge case shaped by an unusual prompt sequence, return something harmful. This is not analogous to a manufacturing defect; it is closer to [the inherent unpredictability of a professional judgment](https://www.winston.com/en/blogs-and-podcasts/product-liability-and-mass-torts-digest/is-an-ai-chatbot-a-product) made under uncertainty.

Strict liability doctrine was never designed to reach that kind of harm, and for good reason. Making developers insurers of statistically improbable autonomous-system behaviors they cannot fully predict would impose a cost structure that the law has historically reserved for abnormally dangerous activities — dynamite blasting, nuclear facilities — not information services.

## Strict Liability Would Fortify the Hyperscaler Moat

The most politically potent argument against strict AI liability is also the most counterintuitive: **strict liability is an incumbent shield dressed up as consumer accountability.**

Here is the mechanism. If developers face no-fault liability for all downstream harms caused by AI systems they deploy — regardless of whether they exercised reasonable care — the cost of that exposure must be internalized before deployment. For a large frontier AI company with a legal department, compliance infrastructure, and balance sheet to self-insure, that cost is material but manageable. For a startup or academic research group, it is prohibitive. [A law-and-economics analysis in *Computer Law & Security Review*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/article/pii/S0267364923000055) reaches the same conclusion: strict liability "will have a particularly deleterious effect on startups where much AI innovation comes, as they cannot self-insure against the risks that strict liability imposes, and it places a regulatory moat protecting the concentration of AI in a few big tech actors."

The venture capital data makes the concentration problem concrete. As of Q1 2026, AI startups captured an [estimated 81 percent of all global venture capital](https://evermx.com/case/q1-2026-venture-capital-record-300-billion-ai-dominance) — but that headline figure obscures extreme internal concentration. [Crunchbase data from 2025](https://news.crunchbase.com/ai/big-funding-trends-charts-eoy-2025/) shows that 60 percent of global AI venture funding went to rounds of $100 million or larger, and the [number of seed-stage AI deals declined by 30 percent](https://news.crunchbase.com/venture/state-of-startups-q3-2025-ai-megarounds-charts-data/) even as megafund rounds ballooned. The AI startup ecosystem is already concentrating. Strict no-fault liability would accelerate that consolidation by making early-stage deployment economically irrational.

There is a painful irony here. The firms most loudly advocating for "AI accountability" often benefit from regulatory frameworks that their smaller competitors cannot absorb. Strict liability, in practice, would further entrench the very hyperscalers that critics of AI concentration purport to check. The beneficiaries are not injured plaintiffs; they are the incumbents whose compliance infrastructure converts liability exposure into a barrier to entry.

## Negligence Already Works — Courts Know How to Calibrate

The alternative to strict liability is not impunity. It is the standard that has governed complex technological liability for over a century: **negligence**.

Negligence asks whether the defendant exercised the care that a reasonable actor in their position would exercise, given the foreseeable risks of their activity. Unlike strict liability, it incorporates context: the severity of foreseeable harm, the feasibility of precautions, the nature of the deployment environment, and the sophistication of the users involved. Courts have successfully adapted this standard to pharmaceuticals (via the learned-intermediary rule, which calibrates the duty to warn based on whether a professional intermediary stands between manufacturer and end user), to autonomous vehicles, and to medical device software — all domains characterized by complex probabilistic systems and hard-to-foresee failure modes.

Iowa law professor Mihailis Diamantis, writing in the [*Vanderbilt Law Review*](https://scholarship.law.vanderbilt.edu/vlr/vol78/iss2/4/), proposes a workable calibration: AI systems should be deemed negligent when they cause injury more frequently than the combined incident rate for all actors — human and AI — engaged in the same type of conduct. That framework preserves the negligence standard's core virtue (proportionality to actual behavior) while providing a deployable benchmark that doesn't require the impossible task of assessing AI against itself in isolation.

The negligence standard also vindicates the intuition behind accountability concerns without overreaching. In *Garcia*, the negligence theory already survives alongside the strict liability theory — meaning that if Character.AI failed to exercise reasonable care in designing its application for a vulnerable adolescent user population, it faces liability regardless of whether strict liability also applies. The negligence standard gets the job done. The strict liability overlay is not a refinement; it is an overextension.

## The Trump America AI Act's Self-Defeating Liability Provisions

The doctrinal problems with strict AI liability compound when Congress attempts to codify them in statute. Sen. Blackburn's Trump America AI Act discussion draft, [released in March 2026](https://www.blackburn.senate.gov/2026/3/technology/blackburn-releases-discussion-draft-of-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence), establishes federal strict liability causes of action for AI harms — covering defective design, failure to warn, and products deemed "unreasonably dangerous" — while also empowering the FTC, state attorneys general, and private plaintiffs to enforce that standard.

The bill's stated purpose is federal preemption: replacing the patchwork of state AI regulations with a single national standard. The executive order it seeks to codify explicitly frames state AI laws as "cumbersome," "onerous," and obstacles to a "minimally burdensome national standard."

The contradiction is glaring, and [legal analysts have noted it](https://natlawreview.com/article/trump-america-ai-act-federal-preemption-meets-comprehensive-regulation). The [Jones Walker analysis](https://www.joneswalker.com/en/insights/blogs/ai-law-blog/the-trump-america-ai-act-federal-preemption-meets-comprehensive-regulation.html) observes that the gap between the bill's "stated deregulatory purpose and its actual regulatory density" is the legislation's most striking feature. A bill premised on clearing regulatory underbrush cannot coherently plant a thicket of federal no-fault liability in its place.

This internal contradiction should be disqualifying on the bill's own terms. The strict liability provisions are analytically separable from the rest of the legislation — there are provisions related to preemption of state AI laws and AI in government procurement that merit separate analysis. But the liability provisions are not a drafting ambiguity to be resolved in rulemaking. They represent a categorical mistake in liability doctrine, and should be removed from the discussion draft before it advances further.

## A Workable Path: Sector-Calibrated Negligence with Disclosure Floors

If strict liability is the wrong answer, what is the right one? The framework is not complicated, but it requires disaggregating the AI liability question by domain rather than applying a single horizontal rule.

**In low-stakes, general-purpose consumer applications**, standard negligence doctrine applies without modification. Existing tort law is sufficient; courts can develop the standard through ordinary common-law evolution. The risk of harm is bounded, users can exercise independent judgment, and the appropriate learned-intermediary analogue exists where professional advisors stand between the AI system and lay users.

**In high-stakes deployment domains** — healthcare decision support, employment screening, criminal risk assessment, credit underwriting — a heightened duty of care applies. Developers deploying AI in these contexts should be held to the standard of reasonable actors who know their systems will influence consequential decisions about identifiable individuals. This is not strict liability; it is the negligence standard with a higher baseline of expected care, calibrated to the severity and foreseeability of harm. Courts can build this body of doctrine sector by sector, informed by sector-specific regulators: HHS and FDA for healthcare, the EEOC for employment, the CFPB for credit. This is precisely what [sector-specific regulatory authority](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/03/white-house-releases-a-national-policy-framework-for-artificial) was designed for.

**The evidence-access problem is real, but it is solved by disclosure — not by strict liability.** The strongest objection to the negligence standard is evidentiary: plaintiffs injured by AI systems often cannot access the technical record they need to prove that a defendant failed to exercise reasonable care. Model architecture, training data provenance, red-teaming results, and incident logs are typically proprietary. The answer is mandatory incident disclosure in high-stakes domains — requiring AI developers to maintain and, in litigation, produce records of known failure modes, safety evaluations, and material incidents. That gives plaintiffs the evidentiary access they need without distorting the liability standard itself. The [*Garcia* litigation](https://mccarthystudentarticles.com/2026/04/01/case-analysis-megan-garcia-v-character-technologies-inc-and-the-first-amendment-rights-of-artificial-intelligence-large-language-model-output/) will test how discovery works in this context; Congress should watch that process before preemptively legislating.

## Conclusion

The AI liability question admits of two easy wrong answers: zero accountability, which is both indefensible and politically impossible, and strict no-fault liability, which is doctrinally misguided, economically devastating for smaller developers, and ultimately a gift to the hyperscalers it purports to check.

The right answer is less satisfying to advocates on either side: negligence, properly calibrated to deployment context, with disclosure floors to ensure plaintiffs can access the evidentiary record they need. Courts applying existing tort doctrine are already feeling their way toward that standard. The lesson of *Garcia* is not that product liability doctrine must be extended to generative AI — it is that negligence law is flexible enough to handle these cases, and that Congress should be cautious about codifying doctrine that the common law hasn't finished developing.

Sewell Setzer's death was real, and the accountability question it raises is legitimate. Answering it by converting AI developers into no-fault insurers of autonomous-system behaviors they cannot fully predict would not vindicate that harm. It would simply ensure that only the largest incumbents can afford to remain in the market — leaving consumers with fewer options, less competition, and a liability framework that protects no one it claims to protect.
