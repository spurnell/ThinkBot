There is a question the United States should be asking with some urgency right now — not "why are researchers leaving?" but "who is ready to receive them?"

The answer is embarrassingly clear.

[A poll published in *Nature*](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-00938-y) found that 75 percent of US-based scientists are actively considering leaving the country. Applications from US-based researchers to the European Research Council [nearly tripled in just two years](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00362-w) — from 60 for the 2024 grant call to 169 for the 2026 call. International student enrollment at American universities [fell 17 percent in fall 2025](https://www.intereconomics.eu/contents/year/2026/number/2/article/america-s-self-inflicted-brain-drain.html), the largest single-year decline outside the pandemic. That last number is not a trailing indicator — it is a leading one. The undergraduates and graduate students who aren't enrolling today are the principal investigators and frontier AI researchers America will not have in 2035.

This is a strategic competition failure. It should be treated as one.

## The Pipeline Is Fracturing at the Source

The US AI advantage has always rested on a structural fact: the world's best researchers disproportionately trained here, stayed here, and built here. The numbers behind that fact are stark. According to [MacroPolo's Global AI Talent Tracker](https://archivemacropolo.org/americas-got-ai-talent-us-big-lead-in-ai-research-is-built-on-importing-researchers/), approximately 67 percent of the top-cited AI researchers working in the United States trained abroad. [Fifty-nine percent of AI-relevant PhDs](https://bidenwhitehouse.archives.gov/cea/written-materials/2025/01/14/ai-talent-report/) awarded at American universities go to international students. America did not grow this talent — it attracted it. That is not a criticism; it is the model. Attracted talent built American AI dominance. The model is now running in reverse.

The fracture is happening at every stage of the pipeline simultaneously. Research funding unpredictability is a push factor at the senior researcher level. Visa processing uncertainty — months-long waits, status ambiguity, the grinding anxiety of renewal cycles — is a push factor at the postdoc and early-career level. And the collapse in international graduate enrollment means the next cohort of frontier researchers is starting to route around the US entirely, choosing programs in Canada, the UK, and continental Europe before they ever arrive on an American campus.

The compounding effect matters: AI model performance is not fungible across mediocre contributors. It scales with the concentration of exceptional ones. Diffusing the world's top AI researchers across five competing jurisdictions doesn't spread progress proportionally — it fractures the concentration effect that made US AI dominance possible in the first place. The math on talent concentration works the same way it works on compute clustering. Fragmentation is loss.

## The Receiving Countries Are Not Waiting

It would be one thing if the researchers being pushed out were landing in a disorganized global labor market. They are not. They are being caught by purpose-built, well-resourced counter-recruitment operations that are explicitly targeting the cohort US talent friction is producing.

[Canada's Global Talent Stream](https://www.niskanencenter.org/the-global-race-for-talent/) processes high-skill tech visas in under two weeks. Not two months. Not two years. Two weeks — with a guaranteed service standard. The program was designed to move faster than market opportunities evaporate, and it does. Canadian universities and research institutions have spent the past year marketing directly to US-based researchers, with messaging that is not subtle about the contrast.

The United Kingdom [committed £2.5 billion to secure its position as a global AI leader](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/ai-to-power-national-renewal-as-government-announces-billions-of-additional-investment-and-new-plans-to-boost-uk-businesses-jobs-and-innovation) and paired that commitment with the [High Potential Individual visa](https://www.fragomen.com/insights/uk-widens-access-to-the-high-potential-individual-visa-to-attract-the-worlds-top-graduate-talent-or-mobilityminute.html) — a pathway specifically designed for graduates of top global universities, streamlining the route for exactly the researchers US institutions train and US policy then makes uncertain. Singapore, [ranked first globally in government AI readiness](https://www.fragomen.com/insights/demographics-ai-and-global-mobility-in-2026-a-global-outlook-on-workforce-strategy-and-immigration-policy.html), has constructed AI research incentive schemes that make the cost of locating there, for a researcher weighing options, genuinely competitive with staying in the United States.

This is not passive brain drain — researchers reluctantly packing up because circumstances got difficult. This is active counter-recruitment: structured programs, dedicated funding, and explicit marketing aimed at a specific, identifiable cohort that US policy is producing on a predictable schedule. [The Niskanen Center's comparative analysis of high-skill immigration processing times](https://www.niskanencenter.org/the-global-race-for-talent/) makes the gap concrete: the US does not have a competitive talent attraction offering. It has a talent processing system that treats frontier researchers the same as every other visa applicant, on timelines calibrated to bureaucratic capacity rather than to the pace of scientific careers or AI development.

## This Is Not a Visa Bureaucracy Complaint

The framing matters enormously here. The standard version of this argument becomes an H-1B processing-time complaint — a legitimate grievance, but one that lands as an immigration policy dispute and gets routed into a political debate where it dies. That framing undersells the stakes.

The correct frame is strategic competition. Human capital is the upstream input to everything that matters in the AI race: frontier models, defense AI applications, semiconductor design, biotech, the entire technology stack on which US economic and security advantages depend. Export controls on advanced chips matter. Compute clusters matter. But the researchers who understand how to use those chips and clusters — who are building the next generation of capabilities — matter more. You cannot sanction your way to retaining talent that has already chosen to locate in Toronto or London.

Washington is comfortable thinking about the AI competition in terms of hardware and data. It is less comfortable thinking about it in terms of the human capital pipeline — perhaps because the latter requires confronting the role that domestic policy choices are playing in accelerating the disadvantage. The *Nature* data and the ERC statistics are not abstractions. They are a measurement of a policy own-goal accumulating in real time.

## What an Affirmative Response Looks Like

The answer is not simply removing friction — though removing friction is necessary. Canada does not win on processing times alone; it wins on affirmation. It has constructed a national brand around being a place that wants frontier talent, moves fast to get it, and offers researchers the stability to do serious work. The US has no equivalent posture.

Three concrete priorities follow from this:

**Fast-track green cards for frontier AI researchers.** Not expanded H-1B quotas — a purpose-built pathway, scoped to demonstrably frontier-level credentials, that processes in weeks and provides permanent status rather than renewable uncertainty. The policy case is narrow and defensible: these are not job-for-job substitutes for domestic workers. They build research ecosystems that generate domestic employment downstream. The concession required from immigration restrictionists is small; the strategic return is large.

**Research funding predictability, not just funding levels.** The push factor for senior researchers is not primarily the absolute level of NSF and NIH support — it is the uncertainty. Multi-year grant cycles that face mid-stream disruption, funding freezes, and shifting priorities make long-horizon research impossible to plan. Predictability is a retention tool independent of budget size.

**A competitive brain attraction brand.** This is the part that has no existing bureaucratic home in Washington, which is why it doesn't happen. Canada built Global Talent Stream as a branded program with a guaranteed service standard and a marketing operation. The US needs an equivalent — a named, fast-track pathway for frontier AI researchers with a processing commitment that is competitive with what other countries are offering, and a deliberate outreach strategy aimed at the researchers currently weighing their options.

The window for this is not infinite. Talent pipelines have inertia. A researcher who builds a lab in Toronto, trains graduate students in London, and establishes collaborations in Singapore is not simply waiting for US policy to improve before returning. Careers compound in place. The researchers being pushed out now are establishing the institutional foundations — the labs, the PhD programs, the research networks — that will define where the next generation of frontier AI talent trains.

America built its AI advantage by being the place everyone wanted to come. The question is whether it intends to be that place going forward, or whether it will continue generating a steady supply of frontier talent for competitors who have already answered the question for themselves.

They are ready. The researchers are at the door. The only question is whose door they walk through.
