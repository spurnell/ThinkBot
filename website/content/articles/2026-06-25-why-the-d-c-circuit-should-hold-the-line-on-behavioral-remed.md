When [Google filed its 111-page opening brief](https://www.courthousenews.com/google-urges-dc-circuit-to-overturn-search-monopoly-remedies/) at the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit on May 22, 2026, it set the stage for what may be the most consequential antitrust appellate argument since *United States v. Microsoft*. The brief challenges Judge Amit Mehta's liability finding directly — calling it "as basic an error of antitrust law as a court can make" — and separately attacks the data-sharing regime and technical committee oversight that Mehta imposed as remedies.

The DOJ and [35 state attorneys general filed their cross-appeal on February 3, 2026](https://www.pymnts.com/google/2026/justice-department-to-appeal-ruling-in-google-search-antitrust-case/), pressing in the opposite direction. They characterize Mehta's behavioral remedies as ["a slap on the wrist for a recidivist monopolist"](https://searchengineland.com/doj-states-appeal-google-search-antitrust-remedies-ruling-468230) and demand the forced divestiture of Chrome or Android that the district court explicitly and correctly declined to order. Oral arguments before a three-judge D.C. Circuit panel are expected in late 2026 or early 2027. Whatever the panel decides will set the template for how courts remediate platform monopoly power for a generation.

The D.C. Circuit should reject both positions at their extremes. Google's challenge to the liability finding is an uphill argument against a factually dense record and a deferential standard of review — courts retain broad discretion to fit decrees to the special needs of the individual case, and Mehta's monopoly finding rested on extensive trial evidence. But the government's push for structural divestiture is worse: a remedy without a properly calibrated theory of harm, dressed up in rhetorical outrage. The behavioral framework deserves affirmance on fundamentals. The data-sharing mandates, however, warrant scrutiny — not because data portability is wrong in principle, but because the mandates as drafted risk compelling trade-secret disclosure that exceeds what the competitive harm requires and what the Constitution permits.
