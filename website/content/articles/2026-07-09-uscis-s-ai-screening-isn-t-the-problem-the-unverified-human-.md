Since early 2026, immigration lawyers have been reporting a pattern that should worry anyone who cares about due process and anyone who cares about smart government IT alike: Requests for Evidence, Notices of Intent to Deny, and outright denials are climbing across visa categories, and the documents driving them are frequently ones applicants already submitted. [Cozen O'Connor's April 2026 practitioner alert](https://www.cozen.com/news-resources/publications/2026/growing-use-of-artificial-intelligence-in-u-s-immigration-adjudications-is-driving-higher-rfe-and-denial-rates) traces the pattern to a specific cause: U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services' growing reliance on machine-learning tools to triage the evidence officers see. [Greenberg Traurig's analysis](https://www.gtlaw.com/en/insights/2026/2/published-articles/the-us-governments-expanding-use-of-ai-in-immigration-adjudications-considerations-for-employers-and-investors) reaches the same conclusion from the employer and investor side of the bar.

The centerpiece is USCIS's ELIS Evidence Classifier, a machine-learning tagging tool built into the agency's Electronic Immigration System. It has processed more than [24 million page-scrolls](https://www.cozen.com/news-resources/publications/2026/growing-use-of-artificial-intelligence-in-u-s-immigration-adjudications-is-driving-higher-rfe-and-denial-rates) since deployment, automatically labeling uploaded evidence — pay stubs, degree certificates, tax transcripts, employer letters — so adjudicators can jump to the page that matters instead of paging through a 200-page PDF by hand. It sits alongside dozens of other AI use cases DHS has now disclosed: [USCIS alone lists roughly 29 in its January 2026 inventory update](https://www.dhs.gov/ai/use-case-inventory/uscis), covering everything from fraud-pattern detection to naturalization-eligibility prediction. On the consular side, the State Department's ["Catch and Revoke" initiative](https://www.axios.com/2025/03/06/state-department-ai-revoke-foreign-student-visas-hamas) uses AI to scan visa holders' social media and public records for grounds to flag a case, a program that has already drawn a federal court finding — in [*AAUP v. Rubio*](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/us-ai-driven-catch-and-revoke-initiative-threatens-first-amendment-rights) — that its enforcement was viewpoint-discriminatory and unlawful under the First Amendment and the APA.

USCIS's own high-impact classification of the Evidence Classifier has separately drawn scrutiny this week, with critics flagging a pattern of the agency narrowing which of its own systems count as high-impact in the first place. That classification fight matters, but it is not this piece's subject — the argument here holds even if DHS's classification stands exactly as it is. The tool still requires a working human check, and right now nobody outside the agency can verify one exists.

None of this is a story about an agency going rogue. It's a story about an agency doing something worth encouraging in principle — using AI to attack a paperwork backlog — and getting the accountability half of the job wrong. That distinction matters, because the two constituencies now shouting past each other on this issue are both making a category error. USCIS and its contractors are treating "we deployed the tool responsibly" as synonymous with "an officer signed off," which isn't true. And a growing immigration-advocacy coalition — echoing a [2024 letter from more than 140 civil-rights, labor, and privacy organizations](https://www.theadvocatesforhumanrights.org/News/A/Index?id=513) calling on DHS to suspend AI in adjudication and enforcement outright — is treating "this specific oversight mechanism is broken" as proof that "AI has no legitimate role in immigration decisions." Both conclusions skip past the actual, fixable problem.

## Triage-assist is not the same thing as AI-driven denial

USCIS's evidence backlog is real, and it is not a manufactured pretext for automation. The agency has spent years buried in cases where officers must locate the three or four dispositive pages inside a scanned, sometimes mis-ordered, often non-English-language filing. A classifier that tags "this is the I-140 wage attestation" or "this is the marriage certificate" and routes the officer there directly is not an adjudicative decision — it's a search-and-retrieval improvement, the digital equivalent of a well-organized case file. Used this way, it is a legitimate, pro-innovation response to a backlog that hurts applicants and taxpayers alike: every day a case sits unadjudicated because an officer is manually paging through a PDF is a day USCIS's fee-funded budget is being spent on clerical work instead of decisions.

The problem practitioners are documenting is different in kind, not degree. [Cozen O'Connor's alert](https://www.cozen.com/news-resources/publications/2026/growing-use-of-artificial-intelligence-in-u-s-immigration-adjudications-is-driving-higher-rfe-and-denial-rates) describes cases where the classifier mis-tags or fails to surface a document that was, in fact, submitted — and the resulting RFE or NOID cites the document as missing. Reported failure modes include cross-document mismatch flags triggered by trivial discrepancies in job titles, dates, or signatures, and "ghost text" false positives, where the tool reads a hidden PDF text layer and treats it as evidence of tampering. The same alert documents more than 1,200 international students who lost status after automated database terminations — a wave large enough to trigger nationwide litigation and a government reversal. In each case, the AI's tag is not surfacing evidence for a human to weigh — it is generating the substantive basis for the adverse action, with the officer's sign-off attached after the fact rather than functioning as an independent check. USCIS has not published error-rate data for the classifier, so there is no way for practitioners, Congress, or the agency's own leadership to know how often this happens. That silence is the actual scandal — not the existence of a tagging tool.

## The rule already exists. It just isn't being checked.

This is where the case for a moratorium breaks down, because the accountability standard immigration advocates are demanding largely already exists on paper. [OMB Memorandum M-25-21](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-21-Accelerating-Federal-Use-of-AI-through-Innovation-Governance-and-Public-Trust.pdf), which took effect in April 2025 to replace the Biden-era M-24-10, is explicitly framed around accelerating federal AI adoption — it is not a precautionary document. But it classifies immigration-adjudication tools as "high-impact AI" and, for that category, requires agencies to maintain testing and monitoring records, access controls, and — critically — to "offer consistent remedies or appeals," meaning affected individuals should have access to a timely human review and remediation process. As of the April 2026 reporting cycle, [56 agencies had submitted inventories covering 3,611 AI use cases, 445 of them flagged high-impact](https://www.nextgov.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/04/agencies-report-over-3000-ai-use-cases-2025/412898/). The memo gives agencies a rolling [365 days from the date a use case is designated high-impact](https://www.wiley.law/alert-Trump-Administration-Revamps-Guidance-on-Federal-Use-and-Procurement-of-AI) to document that it meets the minimum practices or be discontinued — meaning that for tools like the ELIS Evidence Classifier, already high-impact when the memo took effect, that window has already closed.

In other words: the question of whether USCIS is allowed to use a classifier to triage evidence has already been answered, correctly, in the affirmative, with conditions attached. What hasn't been answered is whether anyone is checking that the conditions are being met. "Meaningful human review" is satisfiable by an officer clicking "concur" on an AI-generated flag in under a minute, with no requirement that the file show independent judgment, and no public metric that would let an outside observer tell the difference between real review and rubber-stamping. That is a verification gap, not a missing rule. USCIS doesn't need Congress to write a new statute defining human review — it needs to be made to prove, with data it already has the technical capacity to produce, that human review is not theater.

## What a moratorium would actually cost

The advocacy position — suspend or cancel AI use in immigration adjudication and enforcement — is understandable given the stakes: immigration decisions carry consequences (family separation, loss of status, deportation) that are more severe and less reversible than almost any other administrative action AI touches. But a blanket prohibition solves the wrong problem. It would strip USCIS of a document-retrieval tool that, used as designed, makes cases move faster without touching the substance of who gets approved. It would not, by itself, guarantee that whatever process replaces the classifier is any more transparent — an officer manually reviewing an unlabeled 200-page PDF under backlog pressure is not obviously a more accountable decision-maker than one working from a well-labeled one, if neither process is audited. And it forecloses the tool in exactly the cases where it's least controversial — an evidence tagger — because of failures documented in the cases where the AI's role is closest to dispositive. That's a mismatch between the harm and the remedy.

It is also worth being precise about what happened with the State Department's social-media screening program, because it's frequently cited in the same breath as the ELIS Classifier and it is a genuinely different animal. *AAUP v. Rubio* did not find that AI-assisted screening is inherently unlawful; it found that named officials used enforcement authority in a viewpoint-discriminatory way, a First Amendment and APA problem that exists independent of whether AI or a human analyst did the flagging. Collapsing that case into a general indictment of "AI in visa screening" obscures the more precise lesson: automation makes it easier to apply a facially neutral criterion at scale, but it does not create — and does not cure — the underlying legal defect in how the criterion is chosen and enforced. Congressional oversight of Catch and Revoke should focus on the enforcement standard, not on whether a computer helped identify candidate cases.

## Recommendations

USCIS and State should not need new legislation to fix this; they need to make M-25-21's existing "meaningful human review" and remedies requirement auditable rather than aspirational.

**Publish override and reversal-rate statistics.** For any high-impact AI use case in immigration adjudication, agencies should report, at minimum quarterly, what share of AI-generated flags (a classifier tag driving an RFE, a mismatch flag driving a NOID) are affirmed versus overturned on human review, and what share are overturned on appeal. If officer concurrence with AI flags runs close to 100%, that is either evidence the tool is highly accurate or evidence review is not meaningfully independent — and the only way to tell the difference is to also track outcomes when officers are prompted to review a random sample of flagged cases blind to the AI's tag, as a calibration check.

**Require applicant-facing disclosure when an AI flag is dispositive.** An applicant who receives an RFE or NOID substantially driven by an automated evidence-classification or cross-document-mismatch flag should be told that, specifically enough to identify and rebut the underlying error — the same principle behind adverse-action notices in credit decisions under the Fair Credit Reporting Act. This is due-process infrastructure, not a constraint on how the AI functions.

**Build an expedited human-appeal pathway for AI-attributed denials.** Where an applicant can show a flagged document was in fact timely submitted, correction should not require refiling an entire case or waiting through the standard motion-to-reopen queue.

None of this requires Congress to legislate a new AI statute, empower a new regulator, or impose a moratorium that forfeits a real backlog-reduction tool. It requires OMB and DHS to enforce the accountability standard M-25-21 already sets — and to let outside observers verify that they are doing so. That is the version of AI governance that actually matches the stakes here: not banning the algorithm, and not trusting it silently, but making sure the human in the loop is really there.
