On March 17, 2026, the SEC and CFTC did something the crypto industry had been demanding for nearly a decade: they issued [a joint interpretive framework](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2026-30-sec-clarifies-application-federal-securities-laws-crypto-assets) clarifying how federal securities law applies to digital assets, establishing a formal token taxonomy covering digital commodities, digital collectibles, digital tools, stablecoins, and digital securities. A companion [SEC staff FAQ issued February 19](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/speeches-statements/peirce-stablecoin-021926-cutting-two-would-do) extended near-cash capital treatment to qualifying payment stablecoins, allowing broker-dealers a 2% haircut rather than the 100% haircut previously imposed.

This is what proportionate, rules-based governance of a nascent technology sector looks like. Washington should take note — and then make sure it lasts.

The joint framework is not a gift to the crypto industry. It is a correction of a policy failure. For five years, the SEC's primary regulatory tool was the lawsuit, used as a substitute for rulemaking. That regime drove innovation offshore, failed to prevent the largest crypto fraud in US history, and left the United States holding the bill for the jurisdictional vacuum it created. The agencies have now pivoted to rules-first governance. The question — urgent, underdiscussed, and genuinely time-limited — is whether Congress will codify these categories before the next administration reverses them with a commission memo.

## The Enforcement-by-Lawsuit Era Was a Tax on US Innovation

Between 2021 and early 2025, the SEC under Chair Gary Gensler pursued what critics across the political spectrum called ["regulation by enforcement."](https://theblockchainassociation.org/chair-genslers-sec-regulation-by-enforcement-regime-cost-crypto-industry-more-than-400-million-dollars-and-counting/) Rather than issuing clear rules defining which digital assets qualified as securities, the agency sued projects into compliance — or out of the US market entirely. Based on self-reported legal costs from a sample of its member firms, the Blockchain Association [tallied more than **$400 million**](https://theblockchainassociation.org/chair-genslers-sec-regulation-by-enforcement-regime-cost-crypto-industry-more-than-400-million-dollars-and-counting/) extracted from the crypto industry under that regime — without producing a single durable regulatory standard.

The market responded rationally. Founders, investors, and legal counsel concluded that US domicile was a liability rather than an asset. [Dubai drew hundreds of new crypto businesses](https://fortune.com/crypto/2024/10/30/dubai-blockchain-vara-difc-binance-crypto-hub/) citing the UAE's clear and commercially friendly regulatory environment. The Cayman Islands emerged as the preferred jurisdiction for legal structuring, DAO governance, and crypto fund domiciles — [hosting thousands of blockchain foundations](https://beincrypto.com/cayman-islands-crypto-hub/) by 2025. Singapore and Hong Kong competed for operational headquarters. A [2023 CoinDesk report](https://www.coindesk.com/consensus-magazine/2023/03/27/crypto-leaving-us) found US crypto founders actively evaluating overseas moves to escape regulatory uncertainty, with lawyers describing clients fleeing due to what one called simply "fear."

This is what legal ambiguity actually costs. It is not neutral. Regulatory uncertainty systematically advantages incumbents — who can afford large legal teams to navigate undefined rules — over startups. It advantages offshore actors — who face no US jurisdiction — over US-domiciled firms. When agencies decline to define the rules, they do not create a level playing field; they tilt it against domestic entrants.

The cruelest part of the enforcement era's legacy is that it failed even on its own terms. The SEC's aggressive posture was justified in part by investor protection concerns. And yet the largest crypto fraud in American history — [FTX's $8 billion collapse](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2022-219) in November 2022 — occurred not because the SEC lacked authority, but because FTX was domiciled in the Bahamas, largely outside its reach. Enforcement-by-lawsuit didn't stop Sam Bankman-Fried. It didn't stop Genesis, Celsius, or Voyager. What it did was chase compliant US operators offshore while serial fraudsters operated in the jurisdictional gaps the SEC left behind.

## The Token Taxonomy Is the Right Framework

The [joint interpretive framework](https://www.jonesday.com/en/insights/2026/03/a-longawaited-step-sec-and-cftc-provide-interpretation-for-crypto-asset-taxonomy) does what scholars, industry participants, and bipartisan congressional proposals had converged on for years: it classifies digital assets by economic function rather than superficial form.

The taxonomy establishes five categories:

- **Digital commodities** — assets with functional utility on a sufficiently decentralized network (Bitcoin, Ethereum, and at least 18 other named cryptocurrencies fall here, under CFTC jurisdiction)
- **Digital collectibles** — non-fungible tokens with primarily collectible or artistic character
- **Digital tools** — utility tokens tied to specific platform services
- **Stablecoins** — payment instruments with defined reserve and disclosure requirements
- **Digital securities** — assets that retain the economic character of traditional securities (investment contracts, equity-like structures), subject to full SEC jurisdiction

The analytical move here is significant. The prior enforcement regime tried to collapse most of these categories into the last one — treating nearly every token issuance as a potential securities offering subject to Howey Test analysis and SEC registration requirements. The joint framework rejects that approach in favor of economic-function analysis: what does this asset *do* in the market, who bears what kind of risk, and what regulatory framework is actually proportionate to those risks?

This is the right methodology. A Bitcoin transaction does not carry the same risk profile as an initial coin offering for a startup's equity-equivalent token. Treating them identically is not caution — it is category error. The framework corrects that error.

The institutional backing is notable. The framework was issued jointly by [SEC Chairman Paul Atkins and CFTC Chairman Michael Selig](https://www.forvismazars.us/forsights/2026/03/sec-cftc-issue-historic-crypto-asset-framework-what-to-know) following the [SEC-CFTC Memorandum of Understanding](https://www.sullcrom.com/insights/memo/2026/March/SEC-Clarifies-Application-Securities-Laws-Crypto-Assets) signed March 11, 2026, establishing ongoing coordination on shared regulatory concerns. For the first time, both principal US financial regulators are reading from the same page — and that page makes sense.

## The Stablecoin Capital Treatment Signals Market Maturation

The [February 19 SEC staff FAQ](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/sec-sets-2-haircut-for-stablecoin-positions-but-no-netting-sting/) addressed a specific but consequential question: how should broker-dealers treat proprietary positions in payment stablecoins under the net capital rule?

Under the previous regime, any digital asset with unresolved securities status received a 100% haircut — meaning it contributed nothing to a firm's net capital and had to be fully offset by liquid assets. The practical effect was to make stablecoin holdings prohibitively expensive for regulated broker-dealers, which in turn kept institutional capital on the sidelines of the digital asset market.

The new guidance allows broker-dealers to apply a **2% haircut** on qualifying payment stablecoins — defined as USD-denominated instruments issued by state-regulated money transmitters or national trust banks, backed by US dollars, short-term Treasuries, and similar instruments, with enumerated disclosure and valuation requirements. The 2% haircut aligns with the treatment of registered money market funds, which hold materially similar reserve assets.

This is regulatory calibration, not deregulation. The haircut applies only to instruments that satisfy specific reserve and disclosure criteria — it is not a blanket exemption for stablecoins broadly. As [SEC Commissioner Peirce noted](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/speeches-statements/peirce-stablecoin-021926-cutting-two-would-do), a 100% haircut on instruments backed by short-term Treasuries was "unnecessarily punitive given the underlying reserve assets." The correction reflects economic reality.

The downstream consequences matter for US capital markets. Institutional participation in digital asset markets has been constrained by regulatory cost structures that bore no relationship to actual risk. Normalizing the capital treatment of qualifying stablecoins opens the door for the kind of institutional adoption — custody, settlement, collateral — that would deepen US digital asset market infrastructure. [CoinDesk observed](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/02/20/sec-makes-quiet-shift-to-brokers-stablecoin-holdings-that-may-pack-big-results) that while the change was made quietly, its market implications could be substantial.

## Guidance Is Fragile. Statute Is the Only Fix.

Here is the problem: everything described above is interpretive guidance. It can be reversed by a new commission memo. It has no statutory foundation. The next chair of the SEC — appointed by a different president with different political priorities — can rescind the token taxonomy before noon on his or her first day in office.

The history of crypto regulation in the United States is a history of administrative reversals. The enforcement-first regime that preceded this framework was itself a departure from earlier SEC staff letters that had taken a more permissive view of certain digital assets. The industry has survived multiple regulatory pivots. It should not have to survive another.

Congress has been close to a solution before. The [Financial Innovation and Technology for the 21st Century Act (FIT21)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/house-bill/4763/text), passed by the House in May 2024 with [bipartisan support of 279–136](https://financialservices.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=409277), established a statutory token taxonomy dividing jurisdiction between the SEC and CFTC along lines substantially similar to the joint framework issued in March 2026. The House vote demonstrated that a legislative coalition for crypto market structure legislation exists — the question has always been Senate action.

The joint framework changes the political calculus. It creates an administrative baseline that congressional action could codify, extend, and lock in. The window is open: both chambers are operating under a crypto-friendly political environment that carries no permanence guarantee. Allowing the framework to remain guidance-only is not a safe middle path. It is an invitation to the next regulatory pendulum swing.

The ask from Congress is not complex: pass legislation that (1) ratifies the five-category token taxonomy as a statutory matter, (2) resolves the SEC-CFTC jurisdictional boundary with explicit language on decentralization tests, and (3) establishes a safe harbor for good-faith compliance with the framework pending final rulemaking. This is not a heavy lift given the groundwork already laid by FIT21.

## The US-EU Divergence Is a Prize Worth Protecting

One underappreciated dimension of the joint framework is its competitive significance. While the United States was litigating the basic question of whether Bitcoin is a security, the European Union was constructing the [Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA)](https://www.esma.europa.eu/esmas-activities/digital-finance-and-innovation/markets-crypto-assets-regulation-mica) — a comprehensive, prescriptive regime that took full effect in 2024.

MiCA is not without virtues. It provides legal certainty across 27 member states. But the compliance costs are substantial. [Annual MiCA compliance costs for large crypto exchanges run between €500,000 and €2,000,000](https://coinlaw.io/mica-regulations-impact-on-crypto-businesses-statistics/), covering licensing, auditing, and regulatory reporting. Small crypto startups face estimated annual compliance costs of €250,000 to €500,000 to satisfy minimum capital and legal requirements. [A significant share of non-EU firms report plans to reduce or exit EU operations](https://coinlaw.io/mica-regulations-compliance-requirements-statistics/) rather than absorb those costs.

The US function-based framework is lighter-touch and proportionate by design. Digital commodities and stablecoins face obligations calibrated to their actual risk profiles, not the full securities-disclosure apparatus. This is not a race to the bottom — it is a recognition that compliance overhead imposes real costs on real markets, and that proportionate oversight better serves both innovation and investor protection.

If Washington can hold this line, it has a genuine competitive differentiation opportunity. The entrepreneurs and capital that fled to Dubai and Singapore in the Gensler years are looking for a reason to return. A stable, predictable, function-based regulatory framework — codified in statute — gives them one. A guidance document that may not survive the next election cycle does not.

## What Comes Next

The SEC and CFTC deserve credit for the March 17 framework. The methodological shift from enforcement-as-rulemaking to rules-based governance is the correct move, and the token taxonomy reflects the intellectual consensus that had been building in academic, industry, and congressional circles for years. The stablecoin capital treatment is a calibrated, overdue correction. Both actions together represent the most consequential US crypto regulatory moment since the DAO Report in 2017.

But the praise is conditional on what happens next. Good guidance, quickly reversed, is worse than no guidance at all — it creates reliance interests and then destroys them. The framework's value is entirely a function of its durability.

Congress should treat the March 17 release as a legislative gift: a ready-made regulatory architecture, bipartisan policy convergence, and a functional window to act. The financial services committees have the text of FIT21 as a starting point and the joint framework as the administrative confirmation that the approach works. What they need is the will to move.

The enforcement-by-lawsuit era exported innovation and failed to stop fraud. The rules-based era is a better path — and it cannot be allowed to exist only in a PDF on SEC.gov.
