The Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act of 2022 was built on a sound premise: when critical infrastructure gets hit, the federal government should know fast, and threat intelligence should flow to defenders across the economy. Nobody serious disputes that goal. The question is whether CISA's proposed rule — now racing toward a [May 2026 finalization date](https://www.dwt.com/blogs/privacy--security-law-blog/2025/09/cisa-delays-cyber-incident-reporting-rules-2026) after missing its statutory October 2025 deadline — actually achieves it. On current trajectory, the answer is no.

CISA has produced a rule that sweeps [more than 316,000 entities](https://www.cisa.gov/topics/cyber-threats-and-advisories/information-sharing/cyber-incident-reporting-critical-infrastructure-act-2022-circia) across all 16 critical infrastructure sectors under a rigid 72-hour mandatory reporting obligation — a compliance apparatus that will divert security budgets to paperwork, stack obligations atop the 54 existing federal cyber disclosure regimes, and punish smaller covered entities that lack the legal and compliance infrastructure to absorb yet another federal mandate. Before the ink dries on the final rule, CISA should hear what its own public comments are telling it: right-size this rule.

The intervention window is closing. CISA recently concluded a [series of virtual town halls on "scope and burden"](https://www.mondaq.com/unitedstates/new-technology/1753882/cisa-announces-new-circia-town-halls-focused-on-scope-and-burden-a-sign-the-final-rule-could-narrow) — a notable signal that the agency is receptive to structural adjustments before finalization. The path forward is not to gut CIRCIA. It is to implement it intelligently: with risk-tiered applicability, mandatory deconfliction with parallel federal regimes, and safe harbors for entities that have demonstrably invested in resilience. That combination would deliver more actual security per regulatory dollar than what CISA has proposed.

## How CIRCIA Got Overcalibrated

Congress passed CIRCIA in 2022 targeting operationally significant nodes in critical infrastructure — the facilities, networks, and systems whose disruption would ripple across the economy or national security. The statutory text directed CISA to issue rules covering entities in critical infrastructure sectors, but the practical question of what that means was left to rulemaking. CISA's answer, reflected in the [April 2024 Notice of Proposed Rulemaking](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/04/04/2024-06526/cyber-incident-reporting-for-critical-infrastructure-act-circia-reporting-requirements), has been sweeping: any organization within one of the 16 designated sectors that clears Small Business Administration size thresholds becomes a "covered entity" subject to the full reporting regime. The result is a definition broad enough to capture mid-sized regional hospitals, water districts serving a few thousand residents, small manufacturers with industrial control systems, and community colleges — entities Congress likely never had in mind when it used the phrase "critical infrastructure."

The proposed definition does not calibrate to operational significance. It does not ask whether the entity's disruption would affect the broader system, or whether the entity is deeply interdependent with others in ways that make its incident data nationally valuable. It asks only: do you operate in one of these sectors and exceed the SBA threshold? That is the wrong question, and it produced the wrong number: [316,000 covered entities](https://www.conference-board.org/research/ced-policy-backgrounders/cisa-proposed-rule-on-cyber-reporting) that CISA will need to process reports from, many of which will contribute little signal value to the national cyber threat picture.

The [Congressional Research Service's analysis](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48025) of the NPRM flagged this tension directly. Industry commenters — including the [Information Technology Industry Council](https://www.itic.org/documents/cybersecurity/ITICIRCIANPRMResponseFINAL.pdf) — pressed CISA to anchor the "covered entity" definition to entities whose disruption would produce meaningful consequences, not merely their sector membership. That is a reasonable ask, and it aligns with congressional intent more faithfully than the proposed rule does.

## The Regulatory Stacking Problem

The harmonization problem is CIRCIA's most concrete and least addressed flaw. Covered entities do not operate in a regulatory vacuum — they already navigate a patchwork of federal cyber incident reporting obligations that has grown substantially in recent years. The Department of Homeland Security's own [Harmonization of Cyber Incident Reporting report](https://www.dhs.gov/sites/default/files/2023-09/Harmonization%20of%20Cyber%20Incident%20Reporting%20to%20the%20Federal%20Government.pdf) identified 54 distinct federal cyber reporting regimes on the books. That count has only grown since.

Consider what a significant cyber incident looks like for a publicly traded healthcare company that operates pipelines or water treatment systems: the SEC's 72-hour material-incident disclosure rule triggers. HIPAA breach notification triggers. TSA's pipeline security directive triggers. EPA water sector guidance triggers. State breach notification statutes in multiple jurisdictions trigger. And now CIRCIA triggers — with its own definitions of "covered cyber incident," its own reporting deadlines, its own designated recipient agency, and its own required disclosures. As [Cybersecurity Dive has reported](https://www.cybersecuritydive.com/news/critical-infrastructure-changes-circia/720703/), critical infrastructure operators have been pressing CISA specifically on this stacking problem, warning that competing federal requirements create practical impossibilities during an active incident.

CIRCIA as drafted deconflicts with none of these regimes. It adds to them. A covered entity facing a major breach must manage multiple simultaneous regulatory timelines — each with different definitions of reportable incidents, different information requirements, and different recipient agencies — while its security team is trying to contain the damage. For a healthcare organization without a dedicated federal compliance function, this stack is not a bureaucratic inconvenience. It is an active impediment to effective incident response.

The fix is straightforward in concept, if harder in execution: mandatory regulatory deconfliction provisions that allow entities to satisfy CIRCIA obligations through joint reports filed with multiple agencies, harmonized definitions of "covered cyber incident" aligned with SEC materiality standards and HIPAA breach triggers, and explicit provisions that a report filed with one federal regulator satisfies the CIRCIA obligation. Congress called for this in CIRCIA's text; [EDUCAUSE](https://er.educause.edu/articles/2026/3/cisa-announces-town-hall-meetings-on-circia-regulations) and dozens of other commenters have demanded it in public comments. CISA should deliver it.

## The 72-Hour Clock Can Make Incidents Worse

There is a deeper problem with mandatory 72-hour reporting that CISA has underweighted: in many breach scenarios, forced early disclosure actively degrades the quality of the federal threat picture and creates perverse incentives for covered entities.

When a significant intrusion is discovered, the first 72 hours are typically spent on forensics — identifying the threat actor, tracing the attack vector, scoping the affected systems, and beginning containment. Mature incident response is investigative before it is communicative. Organizations given time to develop a complete picture produce actionable threat intelligence. Organizations forced to file a formal regulatory report at hour 72 — when they may not yet know whether the incident was ransomware or an advanced persistent threat, how far the lateral movement extended, or what data was exfiltrated — produce noise dressed up as signal.

The perverse incentive is real: a covered entity that files an incomplete report and subsequently discovers more damaging facts faces regulatory exposure for what could be characterized as an inaccurate submission. That creates pressure to under-investigate before reporting, not to investigate thoroughly and update the record. The result is worse intelligence for CISA and worse security outcomes for the entity.

This is not an argument against reporting. It is an argument for a reporting framework calibrated to the actual intelligence value of what organizations can responsibly disclose at different stages of an incident. A tiered timeline — an initial notification within 72 hours confirming that an incident has occurred, followed by a substantive report within 30 days with forensic detail that actually enables federal threat analysis — would produce better intelligence than the current all-or-nothing approach. The financial sector provides a tested model: initial notifications to bank regulators are brief, and substantive reports follow on a longer timeline, preserving the space for real investigation before formal disclosure.

## Safe Harbors for CSF Adopters Would Deliver More Security Per Dollar

CISA has for years promoted voluntary adoption of the [NIST Cybersecurity Framework](https://www.nist.gov/cyberframework) as the gold standard for critical infrastructure security posture. That promotion has had real effect: CSF adoption has spread broadly across financial services, healthcare, energy, and manufacturing — the sectors most heavily represented in the CIRCIA covered-entity population. CISA's own [Cybersecurity Performance Goals Adoption Report](https://www.cisa.gov/sites/default/files/2025-01/CPG%20Adoption%20Report.pdf), released in January 2025, documented improving uptake of CSF-aligned practices across critical infrastructure sectors.

CIRCIA's proposed rule treats this investment as irrelevant. An entity that has spent years building audited, CSF-aligned security programs — with mature detection capabilities, robust incident response procedures, and documented governance — faces the identical compliance burden as an entity that has done none of that. That is the wrong signal. If CISA wants critical infrastructure operators to invest in resilience, the rule should reward resilience investment, not treat it as orthogonal to compliance status.

The mechanism is a reporting safe harbor: entities that can demonstrate audited adherence to the NIST Cybersecurity Framework at the "Tier 3" or "Tier 4" maturity level should receive extended reporting timelines and reduced mandatory disclosure scope. This is not a voluntary alternative to CIRCIA compliance — it is a structured incentive, with audit requirements and verification, that rewards the security behavior CISA has been promoting for over a decade. The entity gets regulatory relief; CISA gets a sectoral population that is genuinely more resilient, not merely more compliant.

As the [R Street Institute has argued](https://www.rstreet.org/commentary/cybersecurity-incident-and-breach-reporting-requirements/), compliance-first cybersecurity policy tends to produce checkbox behavior — entities that meet the letter of a disclosure mandate while doing little to improve actual security posture. A CSF safe harbor would break that pattern by tying regulatory benefit to substantive security investment.

## Risk-Tiering Is the Right Model

The most structurally important reform CISA should make is risk-tiering. Not all critical infrastructure operators are equivalent risk nodes, and treating them as such produces a rule that is simultaneously too strict for smaller entities and insufficiently rigorous for the most systemically significant ones.

Financial regulation offers the right model. The [Federal Financial Institutions Examination Council](https://www.ffiec.gov/resources/cybersecurity-awareness) calibrates examination intensity, capital requirements, and regulatory burden to the systemic significance of individual institutions — a community bank faces different obligations than a globally systemically important bank, for good reason. That logic applies directly to critical infrastructure cyber reporting. A nuclear facility, a major financial market utility, or a large electric grid operator is a systemically significant node whose incident data is nationally valuable and whose reporting burden is justified. A water district serving 5,000 people is not in the same risk category and should not face the same compliance regime.

A workable tiering structure would classify covered entities on two axes: sector criticality (based on cascading disruption potential) and organizational scale (based on operational footprint, not merely revenue thresholds). The 72-hour and 24-hour ransomware-payment clocks would apply to Tier 1 entities — the nodes whose disruption triggers systemic consequences. Mid-tier entities would face an initial notification within 72 hours and a full report within 30 days. The smallest covered entities would operate under a notice-and-investigation framework with no hard reporting deadlines, trading speed for analytical depth.

This structure is achievable without statutory amendment — CISA has sufficient rulemaking latitude to define reporting tiers within the CIRCIA framework — and it would be more effective than the current approach. CISA's threat intelligence function would receive higher-quality, actionable reports from the entities most likely to be targeted by sophisticated threat actors, rather than a flood of pro forma filings from organizations that lack the forensic capacity to produce meaningful incident reports within 72 hours.

## The Path Forward

CISA built CIRCIA on legitimate authority for a legitimate purpose. The agency does not need to be told it can issue these rules; it needs to be told it can do better with them. The public comment record — hundreds of submissions from critical infrastructure operators, industry associations, and peer institutions, culminating in a [town hall series explicitly scoped to "scope and burden"](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/02/13/2026-02948/cyber-incident-reporting-for-critical-infrastructure-act-circia-rulemaking-town-hall-meetings) — has delivered a clear verdict: the rule as drafted is too broad, too duplicative, and insufficiently calibrated to operational risk.

The May 2026 final rule is CISA's opportunity to respond to that verdict constructively. Three reforms would transform CIRCIA from a compliance tax into a genuine security tool.

**First**, anchor the "covered entity" definition to operational criticality, not sector membership and size thresholds. Entities whose disruption would produce meaningful cascading consequences should be covered; entities that merely exist in a covered sector should not be swept in by default.

**Second**, build mandatory deconfliction into the rule from day one. A joint filing mechanism, harmonized incident definitions, and explicit satisfaction provisions would eliminate the regulatory stacking problem without undermining CIRCIA's intelligence objectives.

**Third**, establish a reporting safe harbor for entities with verified, audited NIST Cybersecurity Framework adherence, and implement a risk-tiered reporting timeline that calibrates mandatory deadlines to the systemic significance of the covered entity.

CIRCIA can work — but only if CISA finalizes a rule that reflects how security actually operates, not how compliance departments do. The goal is faster, better cyber defense, and that goal is not served by mandating that security teams file regulatory paperwork while they are still fighting a breach.
