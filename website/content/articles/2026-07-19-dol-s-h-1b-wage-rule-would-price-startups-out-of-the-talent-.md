The comment period closed on May 26, 2026. The Department of Labor is now reviewing more than three million labor condition applications' worth of data, and legal trade press this month reports the agency must finish that review before it can issue a final rule — a process advisors estimate will take [several months](https://bizlegalservices.com/2026/07/16/additional-h-1b-employment-wage-protections-proposed/), not years. Once it publishes, the four-tier wage structure that has governed H-1B and PERM sponsorship for two decades will look fundamentally different — and the window for a narrower alternative to reach the rulemaking record will have closed with it.

DOL's [March 27, 2026 proposed rule](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/03/27/2026-06017/improving-wage-protections-for-the-temporary-and-permanent-employment-of-certain-foreign-nationals) is billed as closing a wage-suppression loophole: employers, the agency argues, have learned to classify foreign hires at artificially low skill levels to avoid paying what comparably qualified U.S. workers earn. That is a real and legitimate concern, and ThinkBot has no quarrel with enforcing it. The problem is the instrument DOL chose. Instead of auditing the subset of employers who misuse Level I and Level II classifications for work that is actually senior, the rule raises the wage floor for every employer, at every level, uniformly and immediately. That is a blunt across-the-board floor hike dressed up as targeted enforcement — and it lands hardest on exactly the employers who were never gaming the system in the first place: startups, research labs, and university-adjacent employers hiring recent STEM PhDs into genuinely entry-level roles.

## What the rule actually does

The proposal replaces the current four-tier percentile benchmarks — drawn from the Bureau of Labor Statistics' Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS) survey — with substantially higher ones. Level I, the entry-level tier, moves from the 17th percentile of the relevant occupational wage distribution to the 34th. Level II moves from the 34th to the 52nd. Level III moves from the 50th to the 70th. Level IV, the tier used for senior specialists, moves from the 67th to the 88th. Every tier shifts by roughly the same amount, but because wage distributions compress at the bottom and stretch at the top, the entry-level jump is the largest in percentage terms — Level I compensation requirements rise by more than 30 percent, the steepest increase of the four tiers, according to legal-industry summaries of the rule (see, e.g., [KPMG's Flash Alert](https://kpmg.com/xx/en/our-insights/gms-flash-alert/2026/flash-alert-2026-089.html) and [Holland & Knight's summary](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/03/dol-targets-prevailing-wages-sweeping-increases-proposed-for-h1b-h1b1)).

DOL's own regulatory analysis, drawn from a comparison of historical and proposed prevailing-wage data covering FY2020 through FY2024, estimates the rule would raise the average certified prevailing wage by roughly $14,000 per worker per year, according to [Ogletree Deakins' summary of the proposal](https://ogletree.com/insights-resources/blog-posts/dol-proposed-rule-would-increase-wage-levels-for-h-1b-visas-perm-labor-certifications/). The [Cato Institute's independent modeling](https://www.cato.org/blog/dols-h-1b-wage-rule-massively-understates-wage-increases-26) puts the true figure closer to $26,000 — arguing DOL understated the increase by as much as 26 percent because it treated the average wage across the entire top decile as equivalent to the 95th percentile, an assumption skewed by a small number of extreme outlier earners in that top decile. Even taking DOL's more conservative number at face value, a $14,000 mandatory increase is not a marginal compliance cost. For a large technology employer sponsoring hundreds of visas a year against a payroll in the billions, it is a rounding error. For a 12-person startup or a university lab sponsoring one or two postdoc-adjacent researchers, it can be the difference between making an offer and not.

## The rationale doesn't match the mechanism

DOL's stated justification rests on two premises: that H-1B workers are paid, on average, roughly $19,000 less than comparable U.S. workers in the same occupation and location — a gap DOL derived from its analysis of more than [three million LCA filings from FY2020 through FY2025](https://ogletree.com/insights-resources/blog-posts/dol-proposed-rule-would-increase-wage-levels-for-h-1b-visas-perm-labor-certifications/) — and that [Presidential Proclamation 10973](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/09/restriction-on-entry-of-certain-nonimmigrant-workers/), issued September 19, 2025, directed the agency to revise wage levels after finding the H-1B program had been "deliberately exploited to replace, rather than supplement, U.S. workers with lower-paid, lower-skilled labor." Both premises can be true — and the fix DOL chose can still be wrong. The misuse DOL is worried about is a classification problem: some employers assign Level I or Level II designations to positions that, in substance, require the judgment and experience of a Level III or IV worker, and then pay the corresponding lower wage. That is a fraud-and-audit problem. It calls for tighter occupational classification standards, more rigorous review of level assignments at the LCA certification stage, and enforcement resources aimed at the employers who are actually doing it.

Instead, DOL raised the floor under every posting at every level, for every employer, regardless of whether that employer has ever misclassified anything. This is the same category of error ThinkBot has flagged in other regulatory contexts — a precision mismatch between a narrow, real harm and a broad, prescriptive remedy applied to the entire regulated population. It is the wage-rule equivalent of banning a chemical industry-wide because a handful of plants mishandled it, rather than inspecting the plants.

Cato's own analysis of the current baseline cuts against DOL's framing further. Using American Community Survey data, [Cato estimates that actual entry-level wages](https://www.cato.org/blog/dol-inflates-h-1b-wages-exclude-skilled-foreign-workers) in H-1B-heavy occupations, among workers who hold the bachelor's degree or equivalent experience an H-1B position requires, sit around the 19th percentile — "slightly above the 17th" percentile that Level I currently requires, and within Cato's own stated margin of error for that estimate. In other words, the *existing* Level I benchmark is not obviously suppressing wages relative to what entry-level workers in these fields actually earn; it is roughly where the labor market already prices those jobs. If that's right, the case for nearly doubling the percentile threshold for entry-level roles specifically — as opposed to tightening enforcement against the subset of employers who misassign levels — becomes much harder to sustain on the evidence DOL itself is relying on.

[Cato also reports](https://www.cato.org/blog/dol-inflates-h-1b-wages-exclude-skilled-foreign-workers) that more than 80 percent of H-1B wage offers filed in the first half of FY2026 would fail to meet the proposed thresholds, and that H-1B workers today already earn roughly a $10,191 premium over the *current* prevailing wage requirement — evidence, Cato argues, that competitive market forces are already pushing sponsor wages above the regulatory floor without DOL's intervention. ThinkBot cites this as independent corroboration of the mismatch, not as a substitute for DOL's own numbers, which tell much the same story: the agency's own stated $19,000 average gap and its $14,000 projected annual cost per worker are themselves a description of how uniformly, rather than selectively, the rule bites.

## Why this is an innovation-competitiveness problem, not just a compliance one

Entry-level H-1B sponsorship is disproportionately how startups, research-adjacent employers, and university tech-transfer offices access specialized STEM talent that the domestic labor supply doesn't fill in sufficient volume — recent PhDs in fields like semiconductor physics, computational biology, and applied machine learning, where U.S. graduate programs produce a meaningful share of foreign nationals who would otherwise have to leave the country immediately after finishing their degree. Large, established technology firms can absorb a $14,000–$26,000 per-worker wage floor increase across a sponsorship pipeline numbering in the thousands. A well-capitalized incumbent competing for the same specialized talent barely notices the shift.

A small employer cannot. Early-stage companies operate on tight burn-rate budgets where every hire is underwritten against a fixed runway; university labs and tech-transfer offices work within grant budgets set years in advance and rarely have discretionary room to absorb a sudden 30-percent-plus jump in the cost of hiring a research associate. When the wage floor for entry-level roles rises uniformly, it does not equalize competition for talent — it entrenches the employers who already have scale, and prices out the smaller, higher-growth-potential employers who depend on entry-level foreign STEM hiring precisely because they cannot yet compete with incumbents on salary for domestic talent either. That outcome runs directly counter to the innovation-diffusion goal the rule's proponents presumably share: keeping the pipeline of specialized talent flowing to the labs and startups where the next generation of applied research gets commercialized, not just to the handful of firms large enough to treat a wage floor as a line item.

## The procedural window is closing

This is not a hypothetical concern about a rule still in early drafting. The comment period closed May 26, 2026, and DOL is now in the review-and-finalize phase that typically precedes OMB clearance and Federal Register publication of a final rule. Once that happens, the percentile structure is locked in absent a subsequent rulemaking or litigation — a multi-year process with no guarantee of success. The record that DOL, OMB, and any reviewing court will consult is being built now. If a narrower alternative is going to shape the final rule, or provide the basis for a future amendment, it needs to be in that record before finalization — not raised for the first time in litigation after the rule takes effect.

## Recommendations

DOL should not abandon the goal of closing genuine wage-suppression loopholes. It should abandon the uniform-percentile-shift mechanism in favor of instruments that actually target the misuse it has identified:

1. **Audit level assignments, not just wage floors.** Direct enforcement resources at reviewing whether Level I/II designations match the actual duties and requirements of the certified position — the specific behavior DOL says it wants to stop — rather than raising the floor for every posting regardless of classification accuracy.

2. **Exempt or phase in Level I separately from Levels II–IV.** If DOL retains a percentile increase for the upper tiers, it should treat entry-level positions tied to STEM-shortage occupations and research/postdoc-adjacent roles differently — either holding Level I closer to its current benchmark or phasing in any increase over several years rather than applying the full shift at once.

3. **Index future adjustments rather than jump them.** A gradual, OEWS-data-indexed adjustment mechanism would let wage floors track real occupational earnings growth over time without imposing a one-time compliance shock that disproportionately burdens employers with the least ability to absorb it.

4. **Publish classification-audit data, not just aggregate wage-gap estimates.** If DOL wants the rulemaking record — and the public — to understand where the misuse is concentrated, it should report findings by employer size and industry segment, which would make clear whether the wage-suppression problem is in fact concentrated among a subset of large filers rather than distributed evenly across the employer population DOL is about to regulate uniformly.

None of this requires DOL to concede that prevailing-wage enforcement is unnecessary, or that H-1B wages should float free of any floor. It requires DOL to match the instrument to the harm. A rule aimed at classification fraud should audit classifications. A rule that instead raises every floor for every employer, immediately and uniformly, is an innovation tax on entry-level hiring dressed up as a fix for a much narrower problem — and it is about to become permanent.
