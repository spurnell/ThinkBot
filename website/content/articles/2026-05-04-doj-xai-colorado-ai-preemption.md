On April 24, 2026, the Department of Justice intervened in *X.AI LLC v. Weiser* — the [first time the federal government has formally taken sides](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-intervenes-xai-lawsuit-challenging-colorados-algorithmic-discrimination) in a constitutional challenge to a state AI law. The move puts the full weight of the executive branch behind an effort to block [Colorado's Senate Bill 24-205](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/sb24-205) — the country's most comprehensive algorithmic discrimination statute — before its June 30 effective date.

The instinct is understandable. Colorado's law is genuinely burdensome, the state-by-state patchwork is genuinely dangerous, and the Trump Administration has been consistent in its view that fragmented state regulation threatens U.S. AI leadership. The [White House's National Policy Framework for AI](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/20/trump-ai-policy-framework.html), released March 20, called explicitly for preempting state AI laws that impose "undue burdens" on developers.

But the instinct and the instrument are two different things. The DOJ's legal theory rests on untested constitutional ground. The legislative consensus needed to sustain any durable preemption regime doesn't exist — a fact Congress demonstrated when it voted [99-to-1](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/big-beautiful-bill-ai-moratorium-ted-cruz-pass-vote-rcna215111) to strip AI preemption language from the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. And using executive litigation to override state law Congress chose not to preempt sets a rule-of-law precedent that should alarm anyone who cares about constitutional order beyond the next election cycle.

The governance vacuum is real. The executive branch's chosen tool for filling it is not.

## Colorado's Law Is Burdensome. DOJ's Legal Theory Is Another Matter.

[SB24-205](https://content.leg.colorado.gov/sites/default/files/2024a_205_signed.pdf), signed into law in May 2024 and delayed twice since, imposes a duty of reasonable care on developers and deployers of "high-risk" AI systems to protect consumers from algorithmic discrimination. High-risk systems — those making consequential decisions in education, employment, finance, healthcare, housing, insurance, and legal services — must complete impact assessments, maintain documentation, notify consumers of AI-driven outcomes, and provide mechanisms to correct errors. Developers must publish public-facing statements about their systems' known limitations and risks of discriminatory impact. All of it is enforceable by the Colorado Attorney General.

The compliance complexity is real. The law's key operative terms — "high-risk artificial intelligence system," "algorithmic discrimination," "historical discrimination" — are partially defined in the statute but leave significant scope to subsequent rulemaking. A company deploying a hiring algorithm, a credit-scoring model, and a healthcare triage tool simultaneously faces a layered set of obligations, assessment timelines, and disclosure requirements, all specific to Colorado and all different from what Texas, New York, and a dozen other states are building.

xAI's [complaint in federal court](https://ourtake.bakerbotts.com/post/102mpre/xai-sues-to-enjoin-colorados-ai-act-before-june-30-effective-date) raises four constitutional challenges: that the law compels viewpoint-based speech in violation of the First Amendment; that it impermissibly regulates out-of-state conduct under the Dormant Commerce Clause; that its undefined terms render it unconstitutionally vague; and that its exemption for diversity-motivated differential treatment violates the Equal Protection Clause.

DOJ has chosen to litigate only the Equal Protection claim. Its argument, rooted in [Executive Order 14365](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/), is that Colorado's law compels AI developers to discriminate based on race, sex, and other protected characteristics by requiring them to prevent disparate outcomes, while simultaneously exempting "intentional differential treatment" taken to advance diversity or redress historical discrimination. In the DOJ's framing, the law forces disparate-impact remediation on one hand and blesses race-conscious correction on the other — an Equal Protection violation under *Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard*.

The argument is not frivolous. But it is novel, untested, and strategically risky. Disparate-impact liability frameworks have survived constitutional scrutiny in employment and housing law for decades under *Griggs v. Duke Power* and its successors. Applying equal-protection analysis to state AI statutes — where the discrimination in question is algorithmic, probabilistic, and deeply contested as a technical matter — asks courts to do something they have never done before. If DOJ loses, it doesn't just leave Colorado's law intact: it creates binding precedent that state AI statutes can survive Equal Protection scrutiny, making every future challenge harder. Failed litigation doesn't merely fail — it actively entrenches the patchwork.

Colorado's algorithmic-discrimination concerns, moreover, are not illegitimate. [NIST's research on AI bias](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/SpecialPublications/NIST.SP.1270.pdf) and a substantial academic literature document that AI systems used in hiring, lending, and healthcare can produce disparate outcomes along racial and gender lines — the question is whether Colorado's compliance architecture is the right response, not whether the underlying concern is real. ThinkBot's position is that the [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework), applied through a federal safe harbor, is a better vehicle for addressing those harms than a patchwork of 50 state algorithmic-impact regimes. But that argument belongs in Congress, not a district court brief.

## Congress Has Walked Away. Twice.

The most important context for the DOJ's litigation gambit is the legislative record — specifically what Congress has decided *not* to do.

The reconciliation bill known as the One Big Beautiful Bill Act initially included a ten-year moratorium on state AI regulation, a provision championed by Sen. Ted Cruz. Cruz and Blackburn negotiated a compromise that would have reduced the moratorium to five years with carve-outs for child safety laws — but the deal collapsed. Blackburn joined a bipartisan coalition including Sen. Maria Cantwell, Sen. Ed Markey, and Sen. Susan Collins to offer an amendment stripping the moratorium entirely. The Senate passed it [99 to 1](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/big-beautiful-bill-ai-moratorium-ted-cruz-pass-vote-rcna215111); only Sen. Thom Tillis voted no. The bill was signed into law on July 4 without any federal restriction on state AI legislation. Earlier in the same Congress, [AI preemption language had been stripped from the FY 2026 NDAA](https://thehill.com/policy/technology/5639209-ndaa-ai-preemption-chip-exports/) following the same bipartisan resistance.

This is not an oversight. It is not the result of insufficient lobbying or inadequate industry coordination. It reflects a genuine bipartisan reluctance — across states-rights conservatives, progressive consumer-protection advocates, and moderate Democrats skeptical of federal override — to hand Washington authority over algorithmic discrimination standards before the technology, the harms, and the appropriate regulatory architecture are well understood.

The executive branch cannot manufacture by litigation the legislative consensus Congress declined to reach. Preemption, in the constitutional sense, requires federal law. DOJ can argue that Colorado's statute conflicts with the Fourteenth Amendment — and that argument will be tested in court — but there is no federal AI statute to preempt Colorado's law on conflict-preemption or field-preemption grounds. The executive branch is attempting to produce a preemptive *effect* through constitutional litigation that it could not achieve through the legislative process. That is not a feature of the rule of law. It is a workaround of it.

## The Patchwork Is Calcifying in Real Time.

While Congress deliberates and DOJ litigates, the state-by-state regulatory architecture is hardening.

[Texas's Responsible AI Governance Act (TRAIGA)](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/texas-signs-responsible-ai-governance-act-into-law) took effect January 1, 2026. It prohibits behavioral manipulation and discriminatory AI deployment, establishes civil penalties ranging from $10,000 to $200,000 per violation, and includes a regulatory sandbox with a 36-month testing window. Crucially, it provides an affirmative defense for entities that substantially comply with the NIST AI RMF — a model worth noting. [New York's RAISE Act](https://www.cooley.com/news/insight/2026/2026-04-24-state-ai-laws-where-are-they-now), signed in December 2025 and amended in March 2026, reaches for civil penalties up to $3 million per subsequent violation. [California's suite of AI transparency laws](https://www.cooley.com/news/insight/2024/2024-10-16-californias-new-ai-laws-focus-on-training-data-content-transparency) — covering training data disclosure, AI-generated content labeling, and platform algorithmic curation — are phasing in through 2028. Fifteen or more additional state legislatures have [active high-risk AI governance bills](https://iapp.org/resources/article/us-state-ai-governance-legislation-tracker) in various stages of consideration.

Each of these regimes has different definitions of "high-risk," different compliance timelines, different enforcement mechanisms, and different carve-outs. A company deploying an AI-assisted underwriting model nationally faces the prospect of 50 separate impact-assessment obligations, 50 separate disclosure requirements, and 50 separate attorney general enforcement regimes — many of which will conflict on the margins.

The window for harmonization is narrowing. Colorado's June 30 effective date means that even if DOJ secures a preliminary injunction, the legal battle will extend well past the date by which compliance infrastructure would need to be built. Every month Congress fails to act, more state laws take effect, more compliance programs are built to state-specific specifications, and the cost of harmonizing to a single federal standard rises. Patchwork calcification is not a metaphor — it describes a process in which each new state enactment raises the switching cost of federal coordination.

## Litigation as Regulation Is a Rule-of-Law Problem.

DOJ's intervention stems directly from [Executive Order 14365](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/12/eliminating-state-law-obstruction-of-national-artificial-intelligence-policy/), signed in December 2025, which instructed DOJ to establish an AI Litigation Task Force charged with challenging state AI laws on Commerce Clause, preemption, or other constitutional grounds. The task force is now operationalized.

The theory of change here is that executive litigation can substitute for legislative preemption — that a sufficiently aggressive DOJ can knock out state AI laws faster than Congress can build a federal floor, effectively creating a de facto preemption regime through judicial attrition. That theory should be rejected regardless of the substantive outcome in *X.AI v. Weiser*.

ThinkBot has argued consistently — including in our analysis of the Pentagon's AI vendor blacklist — that using executive power to override outcomes Congress chose not to produce is constitutionally corrosive regardless of which party governs. The symmetry matters. If it is wrong for an administration to use DOJ to suppress state regulation it dislikes in contexts where federal statutory authority is absent, that principle does not become right when the substantive policy goal is deregulation rather than regulation. The administrative state is not less dangerous when it acts to shrink itself by fiat rather than to expand itself by fiat.

There is also a strategic irony worth naming. If DOJ prevails on the Equal Protection theory, it wins one injunction against one Colorado statute. If DOJ loses — or if courts cabin the ruling narrowly — it produces precedent that Colorado's approach survives constitutional scrutiny, emboldening the 15+ other states considering similar legislation. The asymmetry of outcomes argues strongly for a legislative solution: congressional action can create a durable federal floor in one statute; litigation can win one battle while losing the war.

## What a Federal Floor Actually Looks Like.

The answer is not to abandon federal preemption as a goal. It is to build it correctly — through Congress, with proper statutory authority, and with architecture designed to harmonize rather than merely displace.

A sensible federal floor has three components.

**First, a NIST AI RMF safe harbor.** The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/ai/nist.ai.100-1.pdf) is the most credible, technically grounded, and broadly accepted framework for managing AI risk in the United States. Texas already recognized this by building a NIST-compliance defense into TRAIGA. A federal safe harbor for developers and deployers that substantially comply with the AI RMF — including the Generative AI Profile — would create a single compliance standard that satisfies algorithmic-accountability concerns without a new prescriptive federal mandate. Entities that meet the NIST standard would be shielded from state-law algorithmic-discrimination liability. This is not a deregulatory giveaway; it is a consumer-protection floor that happens to be uniform.

**Second, targeted preemption of duplicative high-risk AI mandates.** The federal legislation should expressly preempt state laws that impose algorithmic impact assessment, disclosure, or bias-audit requirements on the same categories of AI systems addressed by the federal safe harbor — but only those requirements. States retain authority over consumer protection laws of general applicability, fraud, deceptive trade practices, and civil rights enforcement. The preemption is not field preemption of all AI regulation; it is conflict preemption of the specific overlapping mandates that create the compliance patchwork.

**Third, state carve-outs for state government AI use.** The White House's March framework correctly identified this: states should retain full authority over how they themselves deploy AI in government services. The preemption regime targets private-sector AI deployment obligations; it does not touch state procurement standards, state contracting requirements, or state agency governance rules.

Legislative vehicles exist. The [TRUMP AMERICA AI Act](https://www.blackburn.senate.gov/2026/4/ai/what-they-are-saying-blackburn-announces-growing-momentum-for-trump-america-ai-act), Sen. Blackburn's discussion draft, and the White House framework all provide starting points — though all would need significant narrowing and refinement to avoid becoming the kind of horizontal AI mandate ThinkBot has consistently opposed. The [GUARDRAILS Act](https://beyer.house.gov/news/documentsingle.aspx?DocumentID=9009), sponsored by Rep. Don Beyer and 34 cosponsors, moves in the opposite direction: it would repeal the executive framework entirely and freeze the status quo. Neither extreme is correct. The architecture above threads the needle.

## The Clock Is Running.

Colorado's June 30 effective date is eight weeks away. The DOJ's preliminary injunction motion, if filed, will move quickly — but federal litigation timelines are not eight-week timelines. And even a successful injunction in *X.AI v. Weiser* resolves only one law in one state, leaving Texas TRAIGA, New York's RAISE Act, California's phased statutes, and the 15+ pending state bills entirely unaffected.

The executive branch reached for the tool it had. The tool is legally fragile, strategically limited, and precedentially dangerous. It is not a substitute for the tool Congress has so far declined to forge.

The governance vacuum will not stay empty. It will fill, state by state, until the cost of harmonization becomes prohibitive. Congress has until the calcification is complete to act. That window is shorter than most members of Congress appear to realize — and no number of DOJ interventions will extend it.

A narrow federal floor is achievable. It requires accepting that states have legitimate interests, that the NIST AI RMF is a credible accountability mechanism, and that preemption is only politically sustainable if it replaces state regimes with something real rather than simply removing them. The Senate's 99-to-1 vote on the moratorium was not a vote for the patchwork. It was a vote against a blunt instrument. Congress should take the hint and build a precise one.
