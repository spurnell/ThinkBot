On April 22, 2026, Rep. John Joyce (R-PA), working alongside House Energy and Commerce Committee Chairman Brett Guthrie (R-KY), introduced [HR 8413](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/8413/text) — the Securing and Establishing Consumer Uniform Rights and Enforcement Over Data Act, or the SECURE Data Act — the House's leading comprehensive consumer privacy bill in the 119th Congress. The bill grants consumers rights to access, correct, delete, and port their personal data; expands children's privacy protections to teenagers under 16; bars the sale of personal data without explicit consent; and establishes FTC and state attorney general enforcement as the exclusive remedy.

Its most consequential provision is also its most contested: a "relates to" preemption standard that would nullify any state law covering data practices that "relates to" the Act's provisions. That phrase covers [an expansive range of state law](https://iapp.org/news/a/secure-data-act-analysis-of-the-new-federal-privacy-bill) — from California's CPRA and Washington's My Health My Data Act to Illinois' Biometric Information Privacy Act and more than a dozen other comprehensive state regimes. The California Privacy Protection Agency has already [fired back](https://privacy.ca.gov/2026/04/california-privacy-protection-agency-releases-letter-opposing-the-secure-data-act/), warning that the bill would strip 40 million Californians of their DELETE Request and Opt-Out Platform and undermine consent rights that "tens of millions of people already depend on."

The CPPA is wrong — but not entirely wrong, and the distinction matters enormously for whether this bill lives or dies.

The preemption principle embedded in the SECURE Data Act is strategically correct. The fragmented, state-by-state approach to consumer data privacy has produced a compliance labyrinth that mainly benefits entrenched hyperscalers and the plaintiff's bar — not consumers. Congress should defend the preemption principle, narrow the "relates to" language to survive constitutional scrutiny, add a surgical savings clause for discrete non-conflicting sectoral laws, and hold the line on no private right of action. That is the path to a durable national privacy framework.

## The Patchwork Is the Moat

Privacy advocates who celebrate the proliferation of state privacy law as a substitute for federal action have the politics right and the economics wrong. As of January 2026, [19 states have enacted comprehensive privacy legislation](https://itif.org/publications/2026/05/15/state-privacy-laws-show-the-secure-data-acts-merits-and-political-appeal/), with nine more amending existing laws during 2025 alone. Each regime differs on consent standards, covered entities, exemptions, enforcement mechanisms, and consumer rights scope. California requires opt-in for sensitive data and empowers an independent enforcement agency. Virginia and Kentucky run on opt-out models with attorney general enforcement. Illinois BIPA applies statutory damages of $1,000 to $5,000 per violation — per scan — for biometric data collection, a figure so punishing it has [generated hundreds of class action lawsuits](https://www.privacyworld.blog/2025/12/2025-year-in-review-biometric-privacy-litigation/) and nine-figure settlements.

The compliance burden this layered system imposes falls hardest on the firms least equipped to bear it. A startup deploying a mobile application in 2026 must map its data practices against 19 different opt-out regimes, at least three sensitive-data frameworks with different thresholds, multiple breach notification schemas, and Illinois biometric requirements that apply regardless of whether the company is incorporated, headquartered, or operating primarily in that state. The [Information Technology and Innovation Foundation estimated in 2022](https://itif.org/publications/2022/01/24/looming-cost-patchwork-state-privacy-laws/) that a 50-state patchwork could cost more than $1 trillion over 10 years relative to a single federal standard — with California's law alone imposing approximately $78 billion in annual compliance costs, $32 billion of which falls on out-of-state businesses with no California nexus.

Large technology companies with mature legal departments, established compliance infrastructure, and economies of scale absorb these costs as routine overhead. Startups and mid-size firms cannot. The practical effect of regulatory fragmentation is to raise fixed compliance costs in ways that advantage scale — transforming the privacy regime into a regulatory moat that entrenched incumbents can cross but new entrants cannot. Privacy advocates who champion state-level action as the progressive alternative should reckon with whom it primarily benefits.

The [CPPA's objection letter](https://privacy.ca.gov/wp-content/uploads/sites/357/2026/04/CalPrivacy-Letter-HR-8413-SECURE-Data-Act.pdf) frames preemption as "stripping rights from tens of millions of people." That is institutional turf defense dressed as consumer welfare. Californians have rights under the CPRA today; they will have rights under the SECURE Data Act if it passes. The question is whether those rights will be exercised against a coherent national standard or an ever-thickening matrix of inconsistent state regimes — and whether entrepreneurs outside Silicon Valley will be able to build privacy-respecting products without a compliance budget rivaling their engineering budget.

## Preemption Has Earned Its Precedent

Preemption of state privacy and data law by federal statute is not a novel Republican gambit — it is the established architecture of American information law. [HIPAA preempts contrary state health privacy requirements](https://www.hhs.gov/hipaa/for-professionals/faq/399/does-hipaa-preempt-state-laws/index.html), establishing federal standards for protected health information across all 50 states. The Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act preempts inconsistent state financial privacy rules. CAN-SPAM expressly preempts state spam laws "except to the extent that such laws prohibit falsity or deception." COPPA preempts contradictory state requirements governing children's data.

In each case, Congress concluded that a national market for services — healthcare, financial products, email, children's software — requires national rules. The alternative is regulatory arbitrage: businesses structure operations to comply with the least restrictive state, consumers in stricter states cannot access services that refuse to comply with their rules, and the market fragments into state-specific versions of products that should function uniformly across the country. That is the equilibrium the SECURE Data Act correctly seeks to escape.

The [Congressional Research Service's August 2025 analysis of privacy preemption](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48667) confirms this pattern: Congress has repeatedly chosen preemption when federal interests in uniform interstate commerce outweigh state interests in experimentation. The digital economy — characterized by instantaneous cross-border data flows, cloud-based processing, and products designed for national and global scale — is precisely the context where uniformity concerns are strongest. A privacy law that treats data protection as a national standard is not a threat to federalism; it is federalism working as designed.

## Where the Bill Gets It Wrong: The "Relates To" Problem

The SECURE Data Act's drafters chose their preemption language unwisely. The "relates to" standard — prohibiting any state law that "relates to" the Act's provisions — is the broadest preemption formulation in federal statutory law. It is the language Congress used in ERISA, and the Supreme Court spent four decades trying to cabin [its sweeping implications](https://scholarship.law.unc.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=6839&context=nclr). As the [Future of Privacy Forum's analysis notes](https://fpf.org/blog/contextualizing-the-proposed-secure-data-act-in-the-state-privacy-landscape/), even the SECURE Data Act's own exempted data categories — employee data, B2B data, applicant data — create immediate legal uncertainty about which state provisions actually conflict.

Contrast this with HIPAA's formulation: that statute preempts state laws that are "contrary to" its provisions, defined as requirements that make compliance with both state and federal law impossible or that "stand as an obstacle" to federal objectives. COPPA similarly preempts only "inconsistent" state requirements. These narrower standards have proven judicially manageable and politically stable. "Relates to" has not.

The practical litigation risk is significant. If the SECURE Data Act passes with its current "relates to" language, California's Privacy Protection Agency and a coalition of blue-state attorneys general will immediately seek injunctive relief arguing the preemption clause is unconstitutionally overbroad. Federal courts will face pressure to either dramatically narrow the clause — undermining the uniformity the bill seeks — or strike it and leave the patchwork intact. Neither outcome serves the legislation's goals.

Critically, the bill's savings clause is already partial: it preserves HIPAA, GLBA, COPPA, FCRA, and FERPA — while notably [repealing the Video Privacy Protection Act](https://fpf.org/blog/contextualizing-the-proposed-secure-data-act-in-the-state-privacy-landscape/). The architecture of selective preservation already exists; the question is whether the bill's drafters will extend it to state sectoral laws that genuinely operate in non-conflicting domains.

The necessary fix is a savings clause covering two categories of state law: first, state biometric privacy statutes like Illinois BIPA, to the extent they apply to collection practices not addressed by the federal bill's sensitive data provisions; and second, state data breach notification laws, which operate in a domain the SECURE Data Act does not comprehensively address. These are not concessions to the states-rights argument — they are surgical refinements that make preemption durable by eliminating the constitutional challenges that would otherwise unravel it.

## Hold the Line on No Private Right of Action

The SECURE Data Act's decision to vest enforcement exclusively in the FTC and state attorneys general, rather than creating a private right of action, is correct and should not be weakened.

The Illinois BIPA experience offers a controlled experiment in what happens when privacy statutes include statutory damages and private enforcement rights. The result: [at least 100 class actions filed in 2025 alone](https://www.privacyworld.blog/2025/12/2025-year-in-review-biometric-privacy-litigation/), a $51.75 million settlement against Clearview AI, [a $47.5 million settlement against Motorola Solutions](https://natlawreview.com/article/2025-year-review-biometric-privacy-litigation) for its FaceSearch facial recognition system, and an $8.75 million settlement with Google over student biometric data. Illinois eventually [amended BIPA in 2024](https://www.gtlaw.com/en/insights/2024/8/bipa-update-illinois-limits-liability-and-clarifies-electronic-consent-for-biometric-data-collection) to cap violations at one per plaintiff rather than one per scan — a tacit admission that the original statutory damage structure generated litigation disproportionate to any genuine privacy harm. The plaintiff's bar built an industry around BIPA's private right; defendants settled regardless of merit to avoid catastrophic per-violation exposure.

A federal private right of action would replicate this dynamic at national scale. Privacy class actions would proliferate not because they vindicate consumer interests but because they represent highly lucrative fee opportunities for plaintiff's firms. Product design decisions would be distorted by litigation risk rather than consumer preference. The result would be less privacy innovation, not more.

By contrast, 21 states have already enacted privacy legislation relying on FTC-style enforcement through state attorneys general — [demonstrating that the enforcement model has achieved its own bipartisan consensus](https://itif.org/publications/2026/05/15/state-privacy-laws-show-the-secure-data-acts-merits-and-political-appeal/). FTC enforcement produces consistent standards, institutional expertise, and enforcement priorities accountable to public interest rather than plaintiff fee calculations. The SECURE Data Act should strengthen that enforcement regime — including an adequate funding authorization for the FTC's privacy division — rather than supplement it with private litigation.

## The Political Path to 60 Votes

The SECURE Data Act faces the same Senate arithmetic that killed the American Privacy Rights Act in 2024. APRA — which had bipartisan backing from House Energy & Commerce leadership and Senate Commerce Chair Cantwell — ultimately stalled because California Democrats objected to its preemption of state law and House Republicans in safe seats faced pressure from the same coalition. The SECURE Data Act, introduced without Democratic co-sponsors, starts in a weaker position.

The path to 60 Senate votes runs through three adjustments:

**First, replace "relates to" with "directly conflicts with."** Adopt the HIPAA model: federal law preempts state requirements that make compliance with both impossible or that stand as obstacles to the Act's objectives. This preserves uniformity where it matters while eliminating the constitutional vulnerability that would invite immediate litigation.

**Second, add a targeted savings clause for state biometric and breach notification law.** Illinois BIPA addresses collection practices and consent requirements for biometric identifiers; the SECURE Data Act's sensitive data provisions for biometrics provide some overlap but are not comprehensive. A savings clause that preserves BIPA-style collection consent requirements — while preempting BIPA's catastrophic per-violation damages structure — threads the needle between consumer protection and liability rationality. Similarly, preserving state breach notification requirements in the absence of a robust federal framework removes a legitimate objection without compromising preemption's core purpose.

**Third, strengthen the federal floor.** The bill's [data minimization language "lacks teeth"](https://www.osano.com/articles/secure-data-act-federal-data-privacy-law) by the assessment of most privacy practitioners. If the federal standard is going to serve as the national ceiling, it must be substantive enough to command Democratic support as a genuine consumer protection. Minimum consent requirements for sensitive data collection, clear data minimization obligations with FTC rulemaking authority to specify sector-specific standards, and a mandatory security rule are the baseline asks. A stronger floor makes preemption defensible on the merits — and makes it harder for California Democrats to argue the federal bill simply erases rights.

The [Consumer Data Privacy and Security Act of 2026](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/4211/text) — S. 4211, introduced in March and currently pending in the Senate Commerce Committee — signals that appetite for privacy legislation exists this Congress. Alignment between the House and Senate Commerce Committees, if it can be achieved, is the necessary condition for any floor vote.

## Recommendations

The House Energy & Commerce Committee markup window is open now. The moment to shape the preemption debate is before positions harden, not after.

**For the bill's sponsors**: Replace "relates to" preemption with a "directly conflicts with" standard modeled on HIPAA. Add a savings clause covering state biometric collection consent requirements and state breach notification laws. Authorize the FTC to issue data minimization rules within 24 months of enactment. Retain exclusive public enforcement.

**For Senate Commerce members**: Engage early. Signal openness to the preemption principle while demanding a stronger floor. The goal is a bill that can genuinely displace 20 state regimes — not one that passes the House only to collapse in committee over constitutional objections.

**For compliance officers and legal teams**: Begin gap analysis now against the SECURE Data Act's baseline. The bill's substantive provisions — access, correction, deletion, portability, opt-out of targeted advertising — are largely consistent with the Virginia and Kentucky frameworks many mid-market companies already map against. The variable is preemption scope; plan for two scenarios (narrow HIPAA-style preemption and broad ERISA-style preemption) to bracket your transition risk.

**For startup founders**: The SECURE Data Act, if enacted in roughly its current form, is likely the most favorable federal privacy environment you will see for years. The bill's compliance requirements are real but manageable — and they replace 19 different compliance regimes with one. Engage your trade association now. The window to shape the final text is weeks, not months.

The California Privacy Protection Agency wants you to believe that preemption means less privacy. The evidence says otherwise: the patchwork that preemption would replace has delivered fragmented enforcement, a litigation-industrial-complex liability regime, and compliance costs that the biggest players absorb and smaller competitors cannot. Federal preemption with a strong floor is not a rollback of privacy rights. It is the only path to a national privacy standard that actually works.
