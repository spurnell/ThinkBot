# The FTC's Anti-DSA Order: Washington Puts Big Tech Between Brussels and a Hard Place

Washington has handed Silicon Valley a poison chalice. FTC Chairman Andrew Ferguson's order directing thirteen of America's largest technology companies — including Google, Meta, Apple, Amazon, and Microsoft — to reject European Union Digital Services Act compliance requirements has triggered a transatlantic regulatory crisis that will reverberate through boardrooms, legal departments, and trade delegations for months to come.

The underlying instinct is understandable. The EU's Digital Services Act is a sprawling content moderation mandate that imposes genuine speech and privacy risks on American platforms. Ferguson's letter invokes Section 5 of the FTC Act, framing DSA compliance as a potential act of consumer deception — warning that companies that "censor Americans or weaken privacy and communications security at the request of a foreign power" will face enforcement action.

But good instincts, badly executed, produce bad policy. And this order is a textbook case of regulatory overreach colliding with geopolitical reality to produce something that satisfies no one, fixes nothing, and creates enormous new risks for the very companies it purports to defend.

## Two Regulators, One Compliance Team, Zero Good Options

Let's be precise about what Ferguson's order actually does. It doesn't just *criticize* the DSA. It tells companies to *reject* foreign regulatory requirements on pain of US enforcement — and threatens them with consequences if they comply with European law. The EU, in turn, can fine DSA-designated "very large online platforms" up to [6% of global annual revenue](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX%3A32022R2065) for non-compliance. For a company like Meta, that's a potential liability measured in billions of dollars — per violation.

The companies that received Ferguson's letter are now caught in a genuine legal trap. Comply with the DSA and face FTC enforcement. Defy the DSA and face the European Commission. Hire more lawyers and do nothing, and face both.

This is what happens when two regulatory superpowers conduct foreign policy through compliance mandates directed at the same set of private actors. It is not a coherent strategy. It is a compliance catastrophe — and the cost gets passed on to innovation.

## The DSA's Real Problems Don't Justify This Response

ThinkBot has consistently opposed the Digital Services Act's most aggressive provisions. The law's content moderation mandates — which require large platforms to remove "illegal content" under vague EU standards, conduct algorithmic risk assessments, and submit to expansive audit regimes — genuinely threaten to export European speech norms to American platforms serving global audiences. The concern that DSA compliance could force platforms to moderate legal American speech, or weaken end-to-end encryption in response to [EU pressure on messaging apps](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2023/06/eu-chat-control-vote-postponed), is not paranoid. It is a real and documented regulatory risk.

But acknowledging those risks does not validate the FTC's response. The question isn't whether the DSA is problematic — it is. The question is whether ordering American companies to violate EU law is a coherent answer to that problem. It isn't.

Consider what "compliance" with Ferguson's order actually requires. Companies must now assess, jurisdiction by jurisdiction, which DSA provisions the FTC has decided cross the line — a standard nowhere defined in the order. They must then make operational decisions about their European products and services that could expose them to EU enforcement, court proceedings, and potential exclusion from the European market. And they must do all of this without statutory authority from Congress, without USTR engagement, and without any bilateral framework for resolving the underlying conflict.

That's not protecting American platforms. That's weaponizing them.

## The FTC Is Not a Trade Negotiator

The institutional problem here is as serious as the policy problem. The FTC is a consumer protection and competition agency. It has real and important authority over deceptive practices, unfair competition, and consumer data. It does not have authority over US foreign policy, transatlantic trade relationships, or the compliance choices American companies make under the laws of sovereign foreign governments.

That authority belongs to the [United States Trade Representative](https://ustr.gov/), working through bilateral trade frameworks, WTO dispute resolution mechanisms, and the kind of sustained diplomatic engagement that produced the USMCA's digital trade chapter and the ongoing Indo-Pacific Economic Framework negotiations. When the EU imposes regulations that threaten American digital trade interests, the correct response is to raise those concerns through [USTR's formal trade barrier process](https://ustr.gov/about-us/policy-offices/press-office/reports-and-publications/2025/2025-national-trade-estimate-report-foreign-trade-barriers), negotiate bilateral frameworks for regulatory recognition, and — where necessary — pursue formal dispute resolution.

None of those tools are fast. All of them are more legitimate, more durable, and less likely to blow up in everyone's faces than an FTC enforcement letter.

The principle at stake extends beyond this specific order. If Washington can instruct companies to defy Brussels' regulations, Brussels can instruct European companies to defy Washington's. China already does this routinely. Extraterritorial regulatory weaponization is a game the United States — with its globally exposed tech sector — has the most to lose from playing. ThinkBot opposes this logic regardless of which government deploys it.

## Congress Needs to Act — Before the Courts Do

The deeper problem is that American platforms have been operating in a regulatory vacuum on these questions for years. There is no federal framework governing how US companies should handle conflicting foreign regulatory demands. There is no statutory safe harbor for platforms that make good-faith compliance decisions under irreconcilable legal requirements. And there is no congressional guidance on how to weigh DSA-style content mandates against First Amendment values and American data security interests.

That vacuum is what gave Ferguson the opening to issue this order in the first place — and what will give the next administration, or the next FTC chair, the opening to issue a contradictory one.

Congress should pass legislation that does three things. First, establish a clear legal safe harbor for platforms operating under conflicting US and foreign regulatory requirements, protecting companies from domestic liability when acting in good-faith compliance with foreign law. Second, direct USTR to formally designate DSA provisions that conflict with US trade commitments and pursue resolution through appropriate bilateral and multilateral channels. Third, set statutory standards for when and how US regulators may weigh in on foreign compliance questions — keeping that authority with trade agencies, not consumer protection commissions.

Until Congress acts, companies will continue to navigate this transatlantic minefield with no map and maximum exposure. That is bad for investment, bad for innovation, and bad for the American digital economy.

## The Bottom Line

The FTC's anti-DSA order mistakes regulatory theater for trade policy. It takes a real problem — the DSA's overreaching speech and content mandates — and responds with a tool that creates legal chaos rather than resolving it. Thirteen of America's most globally significant technology companies are now caught between two of the world's most powerful regulatory regimes, with no statutory guidance, no diplomatic framework, and no safe harbor.

ThinkBot's position is consistent: we oppose the DSA's heaviest content moderation mandates on the merits. We also oppose using the FTC as an instrument of transatlantic regulatory war. Both positions follow from the same principle — that legal predictability and institutional legitimacy are prerequisites for the innovation economy, and that agencies fighting proxy battles through Silicon Valley compliance teams serves neither American platforms nor American consumers.

The proper venue for this fight is a negotiating table in Geneva or Brussels, not an enforcement letter from 600 Pennsylvania Avenue. Washington should send its trade negotiators to Europe — not its compliance cops.
