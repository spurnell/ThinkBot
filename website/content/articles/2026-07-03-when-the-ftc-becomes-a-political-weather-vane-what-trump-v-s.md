On June 29, 2026, the Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in [*Trump v. Slaughter*](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/25pdf/25-332_qn12.pdf) that FTC commissioners may be fired by the president without cause, formally overruling [*Humphrey's Executor v. United States*](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/295/602/) (1935) after 91 years. Commissioner Rebecca Slaughter was told her "continued service on the FTC [was] inconsistent with [the] Administration's priorities" and [removed on that basis alone](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/29/supreme-court-trump-slaughter-ftc.html); Chief Justice Roberts's majority held that was constitutionally sufficient. ThinkBot [covered the accountability dimension of this ruling last week](/articles/2026-06-30-the-ftc-s-accountability-reckoning-why-trump-v-slaughter-is-) — whether it corrects a genuine democratic-legitimacy problem or hands future presidents a bigger lever than they should have. That debate matters, but it isn't the one that should occupy the antitrust bar, the M&A community, and congressional staff this month. The operational question is narrower and more urgent: what does at-will removal do to the *predictability* of merger review and Big Tech enforcement — the variable dealmakers actually price?

## The holding, briefly

*Humphrey's Executor* held that Congress could shield certain multimember agencies from at-will presidential removal because their commissioners exercised "quasi-legislative" and "quasi-judicial" functions distinct from purely executive power. *Slaughter* rejects that distinction outright, extending the logic the Court had already applied to single-director agencies in [*Seila Law v. CFPB*](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/591/19-7/) (2020): if a commission executes the laws, its members answer to the president, full stop. A companion ruling preserved for-cause protection for Federal Reserve governors on separate historical grounds, but the FTC, the NLRB, and [roughly two dozen other multimember agencies](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/29/supreme-court-trump-slaughter-ftc.html) — including, [reportedly](https://natlawreview.com/article/what-supreme-courts-slaughter-decision-means-nlrb), the FCC and SEC — now operate under a different removal regime. This piece does not relitigate that constitutional question; it asks what the ruling means for the volume and character of dealmaking.

## The whiplash was already here — now it compounds

Commissioners with fixed seven-year terms and for-cause protection could, in theory, hold an enforcement line across a change of administration. That insulation was already imperfect — presidents fill vacancies as terms expire, and a determined White House could eventually reconstitute a majority. But it built in lag. *Slaughter* removes the lag. An FTC chair can now be replaced, and a commission majority reconstituted, in the time it takes to sign a memo.

The pre-*Slaughter* world already showed how far enforcement theory can swing between administrations. The FTC's 2015 Statement of Enforcement Principles limiting Section 5 "unfair methods of competition" claims to rule-of-reason analysis was rescinded on a party-line vote in 2021; the [2022 replacement policy statement](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2022/11/ftc-restores-rigorous-enforcement-law-banning-unfair-methods-competition) discarded that limit — the theory later deployed against Amazon's "Project Nessie" pricing algorithm. The Khan-Kanter FTC and DOJ then rewrote the 2010 Horizontal and 2020 Vertical Merger Guidelines into a single [2023 document](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2023/12/federal-trade-commission-justice-department-release-2023-merger-guidelines), replacing the HHI-threshold approach that had governed since 1982 with one built around trend analysis and platform effects. That agenda went 0-for-2 in the two cases dealmakers remember best: a federal judge denied the FTC's bid to enjoin [Meta's acquisition of Within](https://techcrunch.com/2023/02/01/report-meta-wins-ruling-against-ftc-to-move-forward-with-purchase-of-vr-startup-within/) in February 2023, and the FTC [later dropped the case entirely](https://www.axios.com/2023/02/24/ftc-meta-within-case-dismissed); a different judge denied the FTC's preliminary injunction against [Microsoft's acquisition of Activision Blizzard](https://www.cnbc.com/amp/2023/07/11/microsoft-activision-deal-moves-closer-as-judge-denies-ftc-injunction.html) that July, the [Ninth Circuit affirmed](https://www.davispolk.com/insights/client-update/district-court-denies-ftcs-bid-halt-microsoftactivision-deal) days later, and the FTC abandoned the case in May 2025. Courts, not commissioners, supplied the check the guidelines invited.

That whiplash occurred *with* the for-cause removal shield in place. What changes after *Slaughter* is the clock speed: a commission majority no longer needs to wait out fixed terms to reverse course; it can be replaced wholesale whenever an election changes who occupies the White House. Notably, the incoming Trump FTC's own chair, Andrew Ferguson, chose *not* to exploit that new latitude: his February 2025 memo announced the FTC and DOJ would [retain the 2023 Merger Guidelines](https://www.wilmerhale.com/en/insights/client-alerts/20250228-here-to-stay-ftc-and-doj-announce-that-2023-merger-guidelines-remain-in-effect) rather than rewrite them, reasoning that "no business can plan for the future on the basis of guidelines they know are one election away from rescission, and no court will rely on guidance that is so obviously partisan." That is, quite precisely, ThinkBot's argument — made by the man who now runs the agency. But it was a discretionary choice, not a constraint, and the next commission is free under *Slaughter* to make the opposite one with no institutional friction to stop it.

## Volatility is a tax dealmakers already pay

None of this is theoretical to the people who structure transactions. [Bonaime, Gulen, and Ion](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2813466), in a widely cited *Journal of Financial Economics* study, show that political and regulatory uncertainty is strongly negatively associated with deal volume at both the macro and firm level — and that deal premiums, termination fees, and MAC clauses all move in ways consistent with uncertainty shifting negotiating leverage toward targets, who demand compensation for bearing regulatory risk. A [2023 practitioner survey in the ABA's *Business Lawyer*](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/business_law/resources/business-lawyer/2023-fall/antitrust-mergers-regulatory-uncertainty/) found antitrust counsel reporting that shifting enforcement agendas changed how clients structured deals and timed announcements. The Mercatus Center's Gregory Werden makes the rule-of-law case directly: [predictable guidelines let "persons... know the consequences of their actions"](https://www.mercatus.org/research/policy-briefs/rule-law-and-draft-merger-guidelines); guidelines that cannot signal which deals will be challenged "instill fear" rather than provide workable boundaries.

That fear is not evenly distributed. Large incumbents can staff a standing antitrust function, absorb an extra six months of second-request delay, or walk from a deal that goes sideways. The buyer who cannot afford that optionality is the startup acquirer, the mid-market strategic, or the founder whose exit justifies years of runway risk. Enforcement volatility that reads as a minor cost of doing business to Alphabet or Amazon reads as a dealbreaker to everyone one tier down — which is exactly the market entry and exit activity ThinkBot wants policy to enable, not price out.

## Durable-by-design, not insulation

The obvious rejoinder — restore for-cause removal — is both constitutionally foreclosed for now and not the right lever even if it weren't. Insulation never eliminated whiplash; it only slowed it, and it did so by making the FTC less accountable to anyone, including voters who might reasonably want enforcement priorities to track election outcomes. The better fix constrains *discretion*, not removability, so it survives regardless of who can fire whom. Three mechanisms would do real work.

First, Congress should give the [merger guidelines](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11138) — currently non-binding agency guidance, per the Congressional Research Service — statutory backing through a notice-and-comment rulemaking requirement with a supermajority or delay threshold for wholesale rewrites, so a new commission can refine but not instantly replace the framework industry has planned around. Ferguson's voluntary restraint proves the argument can be made from inside the building; it should not depend on which chair happens to agree with it.

Second, novel enforcement theories — a new Section 5 standalone claim, an unprecedented market definition — should carry a sunset or reauthorization requirement rather than standing indefinitely as agency policy statements subject to reversal at commission whim.

Third, Congress should resolve the [jurisdictional overlap between the FTC and DOJ Antitrust Division](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-23-105790) by transferring competition authority to a single agency — an idea a [House Judiciary proposal floated in April 2025](https://news.bgov.com/antitrust/house-proposal-would-transfer-ftc-antitrust-authorities-to-doj) before the committee withdrew it weeks later amid bipartisan pushback. That withdrawal reflects how hard the politics are, not that the underlying diagnosis is wrong: two enforcers with overlapping mandates and now-independent political reset clocks double the surface area for the next administration to relitigate settled ground. *Slaughter* is already nudging the agencies toward convergence on its own — Ferguson's move to litigate mergers exclusively in federal court rather than through FTC administrative proceedings leaves the [two agencies looking more alike than they have in decades](https://www.mlex.com/mlex/antitrust/articles/2495846) — a trend Congress could finish rather than leave to the next chair's preference.

None of this requires taking a side on whether *Humphrey's Executor* deserved to survive, and none of it revives an insulation shield the Court has now closed off. It treats the actual problem — that antitrust's substantive rules can flip with less friction than at any point in the agency's history — as a design defect to be fixed with durable statutes, not a battle to be refought at the Supreme Court. Predictable rules are pro-competition infrastructure. The FTC just lost its ability to provide that predictability on its own; Congress should stop assuming it ever will.
