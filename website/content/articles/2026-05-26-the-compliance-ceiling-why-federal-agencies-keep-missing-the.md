A year ago, the Office of Management and Budget issued [M-25-21](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-21-Accelerating-Federal-Use-of-AI-through-Standardization.pdf), directing agencies to accelerate AI adoption, designate Chief AI Officers (CAIOs), and stand up governance boards within 60 to 90 days. The memo represented the most ambitious federal AI deployment push in the government's history — an explicit declaration that agencies had spent too long on cautious evaluation and not enough time on actual use.

One year in, [a Nextgov/FCW analysis](https://www.nextgov.com/ideas/2026/05/why-access-ai-does-not-equal-federal-mission-capability/413552/) cuts through the optimistic progress reports with an inconvenient distinction: **access to AI does not equal federal mission capability.** Agencies have procured tools. They have standing governance boards. They have CAIOs. What they often lack is operational permission for front-line civil servants to deploy approved tools in actual workflows — without navigating a serial approval chain that was never designed for the speed of AI deployment.

This is the compliance ceiling. It is newly documented, structurally predictable, and getting worse as workforce reductions at agencies like GSA — [which has lost roughly 40 percent of its total staff since FY2024](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/workforce/2026/03/after-deep-staffing-cuts-agencies-seek-mix-of-hiring-and-ai-tools-to-rebuild-capacity/) — transform AI productivity from a policy aspiration into a genuine mission-survival question. The government bought the tools. The governance apparatus is preventing the return on that investment.

## What the Compliance Ceiling Actually Is

The compliance ceiling is not a procurement failure. It is a deployment failure — and the distinction matters enormously for diagnosis and remedy.

M-25-21's 60-day CAIO designation requirement and 90-day governance board mandate created institutional infrastructure at speed. That infrastructure was necessary. But it came with a design flaw: it optimized for the establishment of oversight bodies, not for the authorization of use. Agencies read the memo's governance requirements as a license to layer — adding agency-level acceptable-use policies (AUPs) on top of already-approved enterprise tools, attaching mandatory training queues with no clear completion timelines, and leaving override-authority gaps that make supervisors uncertain whether approving an AI-assisted work product exposes them to accountability risk.

[A Federal News Network commentary on building an AI-ready government workforce](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/commentary/2026/05/compliance-and-capability-building-an-ai-ready-government-workforce/) puts the operational picture plainly: compliance infrastructure and capability deployment are not the same thing, and agencies that have invested heavily in the former have systematically underinvested in the latter. Training completion rates and risk-register submissions become the measurable output. Whether a procurement officer, a benefits specialist, or a contracting official can actually use an AI tool to do their job faster is harder to measure — and so it goes unmeasured.

[A separate Nextgov analysis published in April](https://www.nextgov.com/ideas/2026/04/government-buying-ai-faster-it-assigning-authority/412985/) frames the structural problem precisely: the government is buying AI faster than it is assigning authority to use it. Procurement velocity has outpaced authorization clarity. The result is a growing inventory of approved tools sitting beneath a ceiling of undefined permissions.

The [CDT's one-year retrospective on M-25-21 implementation](https://cdt.org/insights/one-year-retrospective-on-the-federal-governments-implementation-of-updated-ai-guidance-accelerating-usage-with-incomplete-safeguards/) describes the pattern charitably as "accelerating usage with incomplete safeguards" — but that framing gets the causation backwards for many agencies. The problem is not that usage is racing ahead of safeguards. It is that safeguards have been designed to generate paperwork rather than to enable safe and effective use. Process theater has displaced mission capability as the visible deliverable.

## The Workforce-Cut Multiplier

This governance design flaw would matter in any environment. In the current federal workforce context, it is a mission-failure risk.

Federal agencies have absorbed significant workforce reductions in the past 18 months. GSA is the most documented case — down approximately 40 percent of total staff — but it is not alone. Agencies across the civilian executive branch are executing statutory missions with materially fewer people than they had in FY2024. In that environment, AI productivity gains are not a nice-to-have. They are the operational bridge between what an agency is legally required to do and what it can actually accomplish with the staff it has.

A civil servant who cannot effectively use an approved AI tool in a depleted agency is not a success story of cautious governance. It is a mission-failure risk with a known and preventable cause. The compliance ceiling converts the government's AI investment into shelf-ware precisely when the need for that investment to perform is highest.

[FedScoop's reporting on OMB's follow-on M-26-05 guidance](https://fedscoop.com/omb-memo-m-26-05-agencies-ai-adoption/) confirms that the White House has recognized the deployment gap — issuing additional guidance calling for quicker adoption. That political will is real. But political will expressed through additional memos does not dissolve a compliance ceiling built by the same memo-issuing apparatus. The implementation problem requires an implementation solution, not an implementation memo about the implementation memo.

## GSA's Sledgehammer: A Cautionary Model

The most instructive current illustration of compliance-ceiling thinking is not an agency AUP — it is a proposed acquisition clause. GSA's draft AI contract provision, analyzed in [a Lawfare piece aptly titled "Governance by Sledgehammer,"](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/the-gsa-s-draft-ai-clause-is-governance-by-sledgehammer) would impose sweeping vendor obligations — transparency requirements, human-oversight mandates, audit rights — across AI procurements without meaningful risk differentiation.

The clause, as drafted, would apply substantially identical requirements to a low-risk drafting assistant and a high-stakes automated decision system. That is not governance. It is regulatory laziness with a governance aesthetic. It treats the category "AI" as a monolithic risk object when the actual risk landscape runs from trivially low (AI-assisted document summarization) to genuinely elevated (AI-assisted benefits eligibility determinations or enforcement actions).

The practical consequences are predictable. Vendors calibrate compliance posture to the highest-friction jurisdiction. Compliance costs flow into procurement prices. Agencies face longer time-to-deployment even for low-risk tools. And the agencies that most need to move quickly — those with depleted workforces and statutory obligations — bear the heaviest burden from blanket compliance overhead designed for edge cases they are not running.

The GSA clause is not an isolated policy design error. It mirrors the broader compliance-ceiling dynamic: governance architecture that cannot distinguish between a drafting tool and an adjudication system will impose high-stakes requirements uniformly, and neither category will be governed well.

## A Permissionless-Innovation Standard for Government

The innovation policy literature offers a useful reframe. The permissionless-innovation principle — articulated clearly by Adam Thierer in [*Permissionless Innovation: The Continuing Case for Comprehensive Technological Freedom*](https://www.mercatus.org/research/books/permissionless-innovation-continuing-case-comprehensive-technological-freedom) — holds that the default posture should favor experimentation, with the burden of proof falling on those who would restrict it. Applied to AI tools that have already cleared federal procurement vetting, the principle suggests a clean operational rule: **an approved tool in a low-risk workflow should carry a presumption of authorized use.**

This is not a deregulatory argument. Procurement vetting exists, and it matters. The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) provides a rigorous, voluntary architecture for risk identification and mitigation that agencies can and should apply — especially for high-stakes applications. The argument is proportionality: once a tool has passed vetting, the additional layer of agency AUPs, training prerequisites, and override-authority uncertainty should not function as a second veto point for low-risk use.

A workable two-tier framework follows naturally from this logic.

**Tier One — Low-risk tools in approved workflows**: AI systems supporting drafting, summarization, research, scheduling, internal communications, and similar administrative functions in non-adjudicative contexts. These tools should carry a presumption of use with lightweight logging requirements. No mandatory pre-use training queue. No supervisor sign-off for individual outputs. Accountability is outcome-based: agencies track task offload, time savings, and error rates in aggregate — not individual sessions.

**Tier Two — High-stakes automated decision contexts**: AI systems involved in benefits determinations, enforcement actions, HR decisions affecting individual employees, or any context where AI output directly influences an adverse action against a person. These applications warrant elevated ex-ante review, meaningful human-oversight requirements, and clear override-authority documentation. The [NIST AI RMF's](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE functions are appropriate governance anchors here.

The failure of current implementation is not that Tier Two is governed — it should be. The failure is that Tier One tools are governed as if they were Tier Two, producing compliance overhead with no commensurate risk reduction and a significant mission-capability cost.

CAIOs are not the problem here; they are products of a governance architecture that gives them inadequate tools to make the Tier One / Tier Two distinction operationally meaningful. M-25-21's 90-day governance board requirement offered no guidance on structuring acceptable-use policies in ways that preserve deployment velocity for low-risk tools. CAIOs filled that vacuum with caution — the rational response when accountability flows to compliance theater rather than capability outcomes.

## Policy Recommendations

The fix does not require new horizontal AI legislation. It requires targeted OMB implementation guidance and a reorientation of CAIO accountability metrics.

**Congress should direct OMB — via appropriations language or an AI policy authorization provision — to issue M-25-21 implementation guidance that accomplishes three things.**

First, require agencies to publish formal AUP waiver procedures for low-risk AI tool use. Agencies may maintain AUPs; they may not maintain them without a documented waiver pathway for tools that have completed procurement vetting and are deployed in Tier One workflows. The waiver standard should be affirmative justification for restriction, not affirmative justification for use.

Second, require agencies to formally distinguish between acceptable-use governance for AI tools and decision-system governance for automated adjudication. These are not the same problem and should not be governed by the same policy instrument. An agency AUP that treats them identically is a design failure, not a conservative precaution.

Third, direct OMB to reorient CAIO performance metrics. Compliance checkbox completion — governance board standing, AUP adoption rate, training completion percentage — should be supplemented by capability metrics: tasks offloaded per FTE, time-to-deployment for approved tools, and workflow integration rate. CAIOs who demonstrate capability deployment at scale should receive the same institutional recognition currently reserved for those who build compliance infrastructure.

**GSA should revise its draft AI acquisition clause to incorporate risk tiering before finalization.** A blanket clause imposing identical obligations on drafting tools and autonomous decision systems is not a defensible governance model — it is a procurement friction generator with a governance rationale. [The Lawfare critique](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/the-gsa-s-draft-ai-clause-is-governance-by-sledgehammer) identifies the specific provisions most in need of calibration; GSA should engage those arguments rather than absorb them into a comment-response record and proceed unchanged.

## Conclusion

M-25-21 was directionally correct. The federal government should aggressively adopt AI. The workforce context — with agencies like GSA executing statutory missions on a fraction of prior headcount — makes that directive not just sensible policy but operational necessity. The question is not whether to adopt but whether the governance architecture is enabling adoption or impeding it.

The evidence from a year of implementation is uncomfortable but clear: the compliance ceiling is real. Agencies have tools they cannot effectively deploy, CAIOs who are optimizing for the wrong metrics, and an acquisition framework that is about to layer blanket risk requirements across the entire AI vendor community regardless of actual risk level.

The solution is proportionality. Low-risk tools that have cleared procurement vetting should carry a presumption of authorized use. High-stakes automated decision contexts should be governed rigorously, with accountability to outcomes rather than process completion. CAIOs should be evaluated on whether federal employees can actually use AI in their work — not on whether they have sat through the training module and signed the acknowledgment form.

The government spent the last year building the governance infrastructure. It is time to ask whether that infrastructure is serving the mission — and to redesign the parts that are not.
