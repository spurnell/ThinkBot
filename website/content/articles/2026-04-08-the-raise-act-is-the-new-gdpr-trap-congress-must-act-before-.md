# The RAISE Act Is the New GDPR Trap — Congress Must Act Before the Patchwork Calcifies

On March 27, Governor Kathy Hochul [signed the RAISE Act into law](https://www.governor.ny.gov/news/governor-hochul-signs-nation-leading-legislation-require-ai-frameworks-ai-frontier-models) — making New York the first state to enact a frontier-model safety statute that survived a full legislative gauntlet. The law targets models trained on more than [10²⁶ floating-point operations](https://www.nysenate.gov/legislation/bills/2025/A6453/amendment/A) with compute costs exceeding $100 million, developed by companies with over $500 million in annual revenue. It imposes a 72-hour incident reporting obligation to New York's Department of Financial Services, a 24-hour law enforcement notification for incidents posing imminent risk of death or serious physical injury, and civil penalties of up to $1 million for first violations and $3 million for repeat offenses. It takes effect January 1, 2027.

The safety intentions behind the RAISE Act are not the problem. The problem is structural: New York has just handed a single state regulator de facto authority over the governance of the most consequential technology in a generation. And [California has already moved to align with it](https://www.cooley.com/news/insight/2026/2026-03-31-new-yorks-frontier-ai-law-gets-a-california-makeover-with-some-key-differences). The window for Congress to define the alternative is closing fast.

## Twenty-Five Laws in One Quarter

The RAISE Act did not appear in a vacuum. [Since mid-March, the number of new AI laws enacted in 2026 has surged from six to twenty-five](https://pluralpolicy.com/blog/the-ai-governance-watch-april-2026-nineteen-new-ai-bills-passed-into-law/), with Utah, Washington, Tennessee, Idaho, Colorado, Oregon, and now New York all adding to the ledger. The [IAPP's state AI governance tracker](https://iapp.org/resources/article/us-state-ai-governance-legislation-tracker) counted 1,561 AI-related bills introduced in 45 states as of March 2026. [More than 100 AI bills were signed into law in 2025 alone](https://fpf.org/blog/the-state-of-state-ai-legislative-approaches-to-ai-in-2025/). This is not a handful of outlier states experimenting at the margins. [State AI enforcement is accelerating precisely because federal policy has stalled](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2026/04/ai-enforcement-accelerates-as-federal-policy-stalls-and-states-step-in).

The result is a 50-state compliance nightmare forming in real time. Each state imposes slightly different thresholds, reporting timelines, liability regimes, and enforcement mechanisms. The labs that must navigate this are, by definition, the ones building the most capable systems — the ones the United States most needs to remain competitive.

## Competitiveness Asymmetry Is the Structural Flaw

The innovation tax falls on a specific, identifiable group. The RAISE Act's $500 million revenue threshold and 10²⁶ FLOP compute floor will, in practice, apply to a handful of American frontier labs. Chinese frontier developers — operating under a [state-centric model that deploys agentic AI at scale without democratic deliberation](https://www.cfr.org/articles/how-2026-could-decide-future-artificial-intelligence) — face precisely zero exposure to RAISE Act civil penalties, zero DFS reporting obligations, and zero compliance overhead. The [US-China Economic and Security Review Commission's March 2026 report](https://www.uscc.gov/research/two-loops-how-chinas-open-ai-strategy-reinforces-its-industrial-dominance) documents how China's open AI strategy functions as a tool of industrial dominance. Imposing a multi-state compliance architecture exclusively on American developers accelerates that divergence.

This is not a regulatory burden argument. It is a national competitiveness argument. Every dollar an American frontier lab spends on state-by-state compliance is a dollar not spent on safety research, model capability, or talent. The RAISE Act's authors surely did not intend to design a competitive subsidy for Beijing. But the structural effect is the same.

The GDPR parallel is instructive. [PwC survey data shows that 88 percent of global companies spend more than $1 million annually on GDPR compliance](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/services/consulting/cybersecurity-risk-regulatory/library/privacy-reset.html), with 40 percent exceeding $10 million. GDPR was a single coherent regime. The emerging American AI compliance landscape is GDPR's worst feature — mandatory overhead for covered entities — without GDPR's lone redeeming virtue: uniformity.

## Litigation Is Not a Governance Strategy

The [DOJ AI Litigation Task Force, announced in January and led by Attorney General Pam Bondi](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/doj-creates-task-force-to-challenge-state-ai-regulations/), has a mandate to challenge state AI laws as unconstitutionally regulating interstate commerce or preempted by federal law. On paper, this is the executive branch doing its job. In practice, it is the slowest of all available options.

Commerce Clause litigation against state AI laws will take years. Courts have shown no consistent appetite for striking down state economic regulation, and without a federal analog statute on the books, implied preemption arguments face steep doctrinal headwinds. [The DOJ has yet to file suit, and the state laws remain in effect](https://www.kslaw.com/news-and-insights/new-state-ai-laws-are-effective-on-january-1-2026-but-a-new-executive-order-signals-disruption). In the meantime, frontier labs must either build compliance infrastructure for laws they believe are unconstitutional, or absorb the legal and reputational risk of noncompliance while litigation winds through the courts.

That is litigation limbo — a holding pattern that chills investment without resolving the underlying policy question. Congress can resolve this cleanly; the courts cannot, and not on a timeline that matters.

## A Federal Floor Is Not Federal Expansion

The White House [National Policy Framework for AI, released March 20, 2026](https://rollcall.com/2026/03/20/white-house-ai-framework-calls-for-preemption-of-state-laws/), calls on Congress to preempt state AI laws imposing "undue burdens." That is the right instinct. But calling for preemption without specifying what replaces the preempted rules is a political non-starter — it looks like Washington stripping states of authority with nothing offered in return.

The answer is a narrow federal floor: standardized incident reporting requirements, model card disclosure obligations for frontier systems, and a federal third-party audit framework for models above a defined compute threshold. Not a horizontal AI safety statute. Not a new federal agency. A minimal standard that occupies the field and gives states a clear boundary — protecting companies from proliferation without inviting federal expansion.

[Ropes & Gray's analysis of the White House framework](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/03/the-white-house-legislative-recommendations-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence-an) notes that Congress has already declined to enact comprehensive preemption in multiple legislative vehicles, including the One Big Beautiful Bill Act and the NDAA. That reluctance is politically understandable and substantively wrongheaded. The cost of inaction is not the status quo — it is patchwork calcification.

## California Is the Inflection Point

[New York and California have now aligned their frontier AI frameworks](https://carnegieendowment.org/emissary/2026/02/ai-state-law-new-york-raise-act-california-sb53) in the most significant convergence of state AI governance to date. When two states accounting for roughly a third of US GDP and a majority of American frontier AI development adopt compatible but not identical compliance regimes, the compliance architecture stops being theoretical and starts being load-bearing. Labs must now build systems, processes, and legal infrastructure — and those investments, once made, become sunk costs that entrench the patchwork rather than resist it.

With a second major state now aligned, the argument that "Congress will eventually act" is becoming indistinguishable from an argument for accepting the patchwork as permanent.

Congress's choice is not between federal regulation and state regulation. It is between a coherent federal floor defined in Washington by lawmakers who understand the national competitiveness stakes, and fifty competing floors defined in fifty state capitals by legislators who, however well-intentioned, are making national frontier AI policy one bill at a time. The RAISE Act is not a reason to despair. It is a deadline. Treat it like one.
