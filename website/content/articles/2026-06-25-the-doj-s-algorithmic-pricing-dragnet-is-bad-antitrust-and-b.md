On May 14, 2026, Daniel Glad, the Antitrust Division's Acting Deputy Assistant Attorney General for Criminal Enforcement, [delivered a message that should concern every software company in the revenue management space](https://www.justice.gov/opa/speech/acting-deputy-assistant-attorney-general-criminal-enforcement-daniel-gladd-delivers): licensing the same pricing algorithm as your competitors is no longer merely a civil antitrust risk — it could make you a federal criminal defendant. "Software cannot launder collusion," Glad told attendees at the Antitrust West Coast Conference, framing algorithmic coordination as simply "an old crime in new code."

The rhetoric is memorable. The legal theory is strained.

The DOJ's position arrives on the heels of its [March 2026 RealPage consent decree](https://www.justice.gov/opa/pr/justice-department-requires-realpage-end-sharing-competitively-sensitive-information-and), which settled the agency's civil case against the residential pricing software company by mandating detailed restrictions on how RealPage's algorithms may be architected — prescriptions governing data age, geographic aggregation, and runtime behavior. Meanwhile, state legislatures have piled on: over 50 algorithmic pricing bills have been introduced across at least [24 states in 2026 alone](https://www.insideprivacy.com/artificial-intelligence/state-lawmakers-introduce-new-wave-of-personalized-algorithmic-pricing-bills/), already exceeding the prior year's total. The enforcement wave has arrived.

The problem is that it rests on a legal theory federal courts have already declined to sustain, economic analysis that runs backward, and a consent decree that quietly rewrites regulatory policy without the benefit of notice-and-comment rulemaking. Courts and Congress should resist the impulse to treat the parallel adoption of efficient software tools as a per se criminal conspiracy.

## The Legal Theory Is Strained

Section 1 of the Sherman Act prohibits "every contract, combination...or conspiracy, in restraint of trade." The operative word is *concerted action* — there must be an agreement among horizontal competitors, not merely parallel behavior. This is not a technicality. The Supreme Court in *Bell Atlantic Corp. v. Twombly*, 550 U.S. 544 (2007), held explicitly that parallel conduct, even consciously parallel conduct, does not alone state a Section 1 claim. There must be "plus factors" indicating an actual agreement beyond the parallelism itself.

The DOJ's [hub-and-spoke criminal theory](https://www.hoganlovells.com/en/publications/doj-antitrust-official-lays-out-criminal-enforcement-playbook-for-algorithmic-pricing-cases) attempts to bridge this gap by treating the pricing software vendor as the "hub," each subscribing competitor as a "spoke," and their combined use of the platform's pricing recommendations as the "rim" of a horizontal conspiracy. The theory has surface appeal. But it has a critical defect: the rim of a hub-and-spoke conspiracy requires an agreement among the spokes themselves — a mutual understanding that each competitor will abide by the hub's outputs precisely because the others are doing the same. Without that rim, what you have is multiple independent vertical agreements with a common vendor, not a horizontal price-fixing conspiracy.

Federal courts have already grappled with exactly this framework. In [*Gibson v. Cendyn Group*, No. 24-3576 (9th Cir. Aug. 15, 2025)](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca9/24-3576/24-3576-2025-08-15.html), the Ninth Circuit affirmed dismissal of antitrust claims against Las Vegas hotel operators who had independently licensed revenue management software from Cendyn. On appeal, plaintiffs abandoned their explicit hub-and-spoke conspiracy theory, leaving the court to address the narrower question of whether independent licensing of common software, without more, could sustain a Section 1 claim. It could not. The court rejected the proposition that competitors independently choosing the same service provider — even when followed by higher prices — is sufficient to require antitrust scrutiny under Section 1. The critical analytical question, the court found, was whether the vendor had actually shared confidential competitor data among the licensees to generate pricing recommendations — a form of mutual disclosure that *Gibson* found absent.

The DOJ's theory in the RealPage context depends heavily on the allegation that RealPage's algorithm *did* pool nonpublic data across subscribing landlords to generate pricing recommendations. If true, that makes the RealPage fact pattern materially distinguishable from *Gibson* and potentially more defensible on the merits. [But DOJ is now signaling criminal enforcement as the posture for the entire category of AI-powered pricing tools](https://www.aoshearman.com/en/insights/ao-shearman-on-investigations/us-doj-signals-criminal-risk-for-ai-powered-pricing-tools) — not just vendors who commingle nonpublic competitor data in real-time recommendations. The *Gibson* court's analytical framework strongly suggests that the broader claim — independent licensing of common software, full stop — cannot sustain a Section 1 charge. Federal courts are going to be skeptical of the leap from "we all used the same software" to "we conspired to fix prices."

This matters enormously for criminal enforcement, where the stakes include up to ten years in prison for individuals and fines up to $100 million per offense. The per se rule in price-fixing cases exists precisely because per se prohibited conduct is so obviously anticompetitive that no efficiency defense could redeem it. Extending per se criminal liability to conduct that federal appellate courts have found insufficient to state even a civil Section 1 claim is not incremental enforcement — it is a category error.

## The Economics Cut Against Enforcement

The DOJ's framing assumes that the primary effect of shared algorithmic pricing tools is to elevate prices through coordination. The empirical record is considerably more complicated.

Dynamic and algorithmic pricing routinely produces [substantial consumer benefits](https://www.mercatus.org/research/policy-briefs/case-algorithmic-pricing-consumer-welfare-market-efficiency-and-policy): lower prices during off-peak periods, faster price adjustment to supply-and-demand conditions, reduced search costs as prices more accurately reflect real-time market clearing, and greater inventory availability when yield management allows variable rather than uniform pricing. Airlines that use revenue management algorithms fill planes more efficiently. Ride-share surge pricing attracts drivers when demand spikes. Hotel revenue management systems allow smaller properties to compete with chain brands by setting sophisticated prices without large internal analytics teams.

[A 2024 NBER analysis of algorithmic pricing's regulatory implications](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32540) underscores why the consumer-harm story is more complicated than enforcement rhetoric suggests: personalized pricing can shift surplus from consumers to producers in aggregate, but distributional effects vary substantially by market and pricing method — with many individual consumers receiving lower prices than they would under uniform pricing. Per se condemnation forecloses this analysis entirely, treating markets where algorithm-driven pricing harms consumers identically to markets where it does not. That is precisely the kind of analytical overcorrection the Sherman Act's rule-of-reason framework was designed to prevent.

More fundamentally, information sharing among competitors — even sharing that improves pricing accuracy — has historically been evaluated under the rule of reason, not per se condemnation. The Supreme Court made this point in *United States v. United States Gypsum Co.*, 438 U.S. 422 (1978), holding that verification of prices with competitors, while potentially anticompetitive, is not per se illegal and must be assessed for its actual competitive effects. The DOJ is now attempting to criminalize a form of indirect information aggregation — independent data inputs to a shared analytical model — without the rule of reason analysis that category of conduct has traditionally required.

[Legal challenges to algorithmic pricing, when not carefully calibrated, may discourage companies from using pooled data at all](https://truthonthemarket.com/2025/12/08/legal-challenges-to-algorithmic-pricing-may-undermine-market-process-improvements/), reverting markets to less efficient pricing mechanisms and ultimately harming the consumers the Sherman Act is meant to protect. That is the canonical false positive: condemning efficiency as if it were collusion.

## The RealPage Decree Is Regulatory Policymaking by Another Name

Even if one accepts that RealPage's specific data-pooling practices warranted legal challenge, the consent decree entered by a North Carolina federal court on March 26, 2026 should concern anyone who cares about institutional process.

The decree does not simply prohibit anticompetitive conduct. It mandates [detailed architectural requirements for how RealPage's algorithms must be designed](https://www.wsgr.com/en/insights/doj-settles-its-algorithmic-price-fixing-case-against-realpage.html): competitor data cannot be used in real-time pricing recommendations at all; model training must rely only on nonpublic data aged at least twelve months; geographic effects may not be modeled below the state level; features that historically limited price decreases must be removed or redesigned. The [Federal Register's May 2026 response to public comments on the proposed final judgment](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/05/08/2026-09147/united-states-et-al-v-realpage-inc-et-al-response-to-public-comments) reveals the level of technical specificity with which DOJ is engaging the product's AI architecture.

This is not a remedy that prohibits conduct. It is a product design mandate — an algorithm architecture rule with the force of a federal court order, issued through negotiated settlement without any notice-and-comment rulemaking, without any regulatory impact analysis, and without any opportunity for affected industries to develop alternative compliance frameworks.

The Tunney Act requires courts to determine that DOJ consent decrees are "in the public interest," but that standard has never been understood as a substitute for the Administrative Procedure Act's procedural requirements. When the federal government wants to establish rules governing how software products must be designed — what data they can ingest, at what temporal lag, aggregated to what geographic level — that is regulatory activity that should flow through the rulemaking process, not be embedded in a bilateral litigation settlement that binds only one company but signals design requirements for an entire industry.

The [May 2026 Agri Stats settlement](https://www.wilmerhale.com/en/insights/client-alerts/20260512-doj-states-reach-significant-settlement-in-agri-stats-information-sharing-antitrust-litigation), requiring an overhaul of how benchmarking data is collected and distributed to the meat processing industry, follows the same pattern. DOJ is writing information-sharing rules for entire sectors through consent decrees, not rulemaking. That process gives industry no meaningful opportunity to propose alternative architectures that might achieve competitive objectives with less innovation cost, and it gives courts no analytical framework for evaluating whether the mandated architecture is actually optimal or merely convenient.

## The Criminal Threat Is the Enforcement Tool

Glad's May 2026 remarks are best understood not as a preview of imminent prosecution but as a coercive signal — an attempt to reshape industry behavior through the credible threat of criminal indictment rather than the adjudicated finding of criminal liability.

This is a rational enforcement strategy in a narrow sense: the DOJ has not yet brought a criminal charge against a pricing algorithm vendor, and the legal theory has never been tested in a criminal context. But the mechanism is troubling. Under the Sherman Act, criminal indictment alone — independent of conviction — imposes catastrophic consequences: reputational damage, inability to bid on government contracts, stock price collapse, and executive careers destroyed while litigation proceeds for years. For a SaaS company offering revenue management software, the threat of criminal prosecution is not an incentive to stop anticompetitive conduct; it is an incentive to stop offering the product entirely.

[Every company offering AI-powered pricing tools now operates under the shadow of this threat](https://www.afslaw.com/perspectives/investigations-blog/doj-warns-ai-and-algorithmic-pricing-tools-can-trigger-criminal): hospital systems using algorithmic charge-master optimization, airlines deploying revenue management systems that model competitor fare data (legally obtained from public booking systems), retailers using price optimization software that trains on market-basket data, SaaS companies serving the hospitality, commercial real estate, and logistics sectors. The enforcement posture does not distinguish between these categories. Glad's framework triggers criminal scrutiny when competitors "share proprietary, competitively sensitive information with a common algorithmic platform" and "know that their data will be used to inform competitors' pricing decisions" — a description that encompasses nearly every multi-vendor data platform in existence.

The error costs here are asymmetric. A false positive in criminal antitrust enforcement — wrongly threatening or charging pro-competitive conduct — produces investment chilling, product withdrawal, and market inefficiency that persists long after the error is corrected. As the Supreme Court held in *Verizon Communications Inc. v. Law Offices of Curtis V. Trinko*, 540 U.S. 398 (2004), "the cost of false positives counsels against an expansive definition of" prohibited conduct, because "mistaken inferences...are especially costly, because they chill the very conduct the antitrust laws are designed to protect." That warning applies with considerably greater force in the criminal context than in the civil context where *Trinko* itself arose.

## State Legislation Compounds the Problem

Fifty-plus algorithmic pricing bills introduced across 24 states in the current legislative cycle add a compliance morass on top of the federal enforcement risk. [New York's Algorithmic Pricing Disclosure Act](https://www.skadden.com/insights/publications/2026/01/new-york-algorithmic-pricing-law), enacted in late 2025, imposes disclosure requirements on companies using algorithms to set prices based on consumer personal data. [California's SB 295](https://calmatters.digitaldemocracy.org/bills/ca_202520260sb295) targets common pricing algorithms more directly, prohibiting the distribution of pricing tools that process competitors' nonpublic data. Other states are pursuing per se bans on specific data-sharing practices. The [S.232 Preventing Algorithmic Collusion Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/232) — reintroduced in the Senate by Sen. Amy Klobuchar in January 2025 — would make it categorically unlawful to "use or distribute any pricing algorithm that uses, incorporates, or was trained with nonpublic competitor data," a prohibition so broad it would eliminate most serious revenue management software from the market.

This patchwork does not protect competition. It creates compliance uncertainty so severe that rational software vendors will either exit the market or move toward the most restrictive common denominator — which means algorithms that use only public, self-generated data, producing recommendations that are less accurate, less useful, and less responsive to market conditions. The consumers who benefit from dynamic pricing efficiency will bear the cost.

## What Congress Should Actually Do

The DOJ has placed Congress in an unusual posture: the Antitrust Division is writing industry-specific algorithm design rules through consent decrees, threatening criminal liability under a legal theory federal appellate courts have found insufficient to state even a civil claim, and doing so in the absence of any legislative instruction about how algorithmic pricing should be treated under the Sherman Act.

Congress should step in — not to validate DOJ's current approach, but to establish a coherent statutory framework. The right framework has three elements. First, proof of an actual agreement: to sustain a price-fixing claim based on algorithmic pricing, the government should be required to demonstrate that competitors entered into an explicit arrangement to feed data into a common system with the mutual understanding that the system's outputs would coordinate their pricing — not merely that they independently licensed common software from the same vendor. Second, proof of effect: parallel pricing, standing alone, is not harm to competition; enforcers should be required to demonstrate actual price effects beyond those attributable to common cost inputs and market conditions. Third, a civil default: first-instance algorithmic pricing violations should be subject to civil enforcement and injunctive relief, with criminal prosecution reserved for cases involving explicit communication between competitors about pricing intentions, consistent with the historical scope of per se criminal antitrust.

Congress should also preempt the state-level patchwork. A fragmented regulatory landscape with fifty-plus incompatible disclosure, prohibition, and architecture mandates does not serve competition; it serves the compliance industry. A single federal standard, arrived at through proper legislative process with economic input, would provide both the predictability that innovating companies need and the competitive protection that consumers deserve.

## Conclusion

The DOJ's algorithmic pricing criminal theory is ambitious, aggressive, and — if tested — likely to be found wanting by the same federal courts that have declined to sustain hub-and-spoke theories in analogous civil contexts. The RealPage consent decree, whatever its merits as an individual remedy, should not be mistaken for a legislatively endorsed framework governing how an entire category of software products must be designed. And the criminal enforcement signal Glad sent in May 2026 — deployed before a single criminal indictment has been brought, tested, or sustained — is precisely the kind of indictment-as-coercion that produces chilling effects without adjudicated findings of harm.

Antitrust law at its best is a scalpel: it identifies specific anticompetitive agreements that harm consumers and excises them while leaving efficient conduct undisturbed. What DOJ is deploying against AI-powered pricing is something closer to a dragnet — broad enough to capture genuine coordination at the edges, but broad enough also to sweep in pro-competitive efficiency that consumers depend on. The distinction between harm to competitors and harm to competition has never mattered more.
