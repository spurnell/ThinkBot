Federal research agencies have spent the past year building an elaborate enforcement apparatus around one kind of AI use in grantmaking — the kind researchers do. They have built almost nothing around the other kind — the kind agencies do to researchers' proposals. That asymmetry is about to get more dangerous, not less, because the human reviewers who exist to catch agency-side error just lost the job protections that made it safe for them to push back.

## The double standard, in black and white

In July 2025, NIH issued [NOT-OD-25-132](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/notice-files/NOT-OD-25-132.html), a notice declaring that grant applications "substantially developed by AI" will not be treated as the applicant's original work. NIH has never defined "substantially developed" by word count, percentage, or section — reviewers are left to know it when they see it. The consequences for guessing wrong are severe: a [May 14, 2026 reminder jointly issued by NIH and HHS's Office of Research Integrity](https://grants.nih.gov/news-events/nih-extramural-nexus-news/2026/05/helpful-reminders-to-ensure-integrity-of-nih-supported-research-when-using-artificial-intelligence) makes clear that presenting AI-generated content as original scientific work, without disclosure, can trigger an ORI referral, suspension, termination, or withholding of an award — the federal research-integrity system's version of a criminal referral. Applicants may still use AI for grammar and formatting, but generating hypotheses, aims, or background material with AI is expressly out of bounds, enforced after the fact by an agency empowered to end a career over it.

Now compare that to what agencies are doing on their own side of the desk. NSF's [notice to the research community on AI](https://www.nsf.gov/news/notice-to-the-research-community-on-ai) bars reviewers from uploading proposal content to unapproved commercial AI tools — a sensible confidentiality safeguard — but merely "encourages" applicants to disclose their own AI use, with no penalty regime attached. NSF has, to its credit, so far declined to formally authorize AI tools inside its own peer-review process, though [Science reported](https://www.science.org/content/article/science-funding-agencies-say-no-using-ai-peer-review) the agency has formed an internal working group to study what "guardrails" might allow limited use down the road. The line is already moving elsewhere in the federal research enterprise. In May 2026, [ARPA-H launched its Intelligent Generator of Research (IGoR) program](https://arpa-h.gov/news-and-events/arpa-h-launches-new-program-deliver-rigorous-gold-standard-research-faster), and with it, one of the first major federal biomedical solicitations to explicitly build AI into the [proposal-review pipeline](https://arpa-h.gov/explore-funding/programs/igor/statement): secure large language models that "organize, summarize, and surface key information" from the initial solution summaries agencies use to decide who advances. ARPA-H insists "AI will not replace human judgment," and to its credit publishes that assurance on a public statement page — a disclosure NIH and NSF do not match for their own internal review workflows. But the statement itself is silent on what recourse, if any, exists for a proposal that gets triaged out on the strength of an AI-generated summary a human reviewer never independently re-derived.

That is the double standard this piece is about. NIH will refer a researcher to an integrity office for using AI to help draft a background section. No agency has yet published a comparable disclosure-and-accountability regime for using AI to help decide who gets funded. Meanwhile, NSF officials describe artificial intelligence as increasingly central to what the agency funds — more than $500 million committed across [30-plus AI Research Institutes](https://www.nsf.gov/focus-areas/artificial-intelligence) in over 40 states, with new 2026 vehicles like TechAccess: AI-Ready America and ExpandAI extending the footprint further. An agency that treats AI as the organizing logic of its research portfolio should be expected to hold its own evaluation processes to at least the transparency standard it imposes on applicants. Right now it does not.

## The humans meant to catch the errors just lost their shield

This gap would be a normal, correctable oversight — regulatory infrastructure lagging technology adoption is the rule, not the exception — except for a second policy shift landing at the same moment. On February 5, 2026, the Office of Personnel Management finalized the [Schedule Policy/Career rule](https://www.opm.gov/news/news-releases/opm-finalizes-schedule-policycareer-rule-to-strengthen-accountability/), which took effect roughly a month later. The rule creates a new excepted-service category for federal employees in "policy-influencing" positions, converting them from traditional civil servants into at-will employees. Reclassified staff lose Title 5 removal protections that have governed the civil service since the Pendleton Act, lose appeal rights to the Merit Systems Protection Board, and lose direct access to the Office of Special Counsel for whistleblower complaints — those now route through agency general counsel instead. OPM estimates roughly 50,000 positions government-wide are eligible for reclassification; as of June 2026, [close to 8,000 positions had already been converted](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/workforce/2026/06/trump-moves-about-8000-federal-positions-to-schedule-policy-career/), with agencies continuing to designate more.

Critically, in written responses to public comments on the rule, [OPM itself named the exact roles this piece is concerned with](https://www.statnews.com/2026/02/06/nih-grant-reviewers-schedule-f-designation-politicizes-science/) as eligible for reclassification: "reviewers, program officers, advisory councils, and leadership" at science-funding agencies — squarely covering NIH's grant reviewers, the program officers who manage portfolios and recommend funding decisions, its National Advisory Council members, and institute leadership. NIH program officer Jenna Norton put the practical stakes in concrete terms while the rule was still a proposal, telling *STAT*: if program officers were moved onto the new schedule, "it will become even harder for us to push back." That is precisely the risk this piece is concerned with, now realized. These are the people institutionally positioned to notice when an AI-assisted triage tool has mis-ranked a proposal, flagged the wrong section, or systematically undervalued a research area outside its training distribution — and to say so. Program officers already exercise enormous, difficult-to-audit discretion over which scored proposals actually get funded; that discretion is the informal check that has kept algorithmic tools like NIH's decades-old bibliometric scoring aids or NSF's merit-review analytics from becoming the whole story. Removing for-cause removal protections from the people who exercise that discretion does not eliminate their judgment, but it changes its price. A program officer weighing whether to overrule an AI-influenced score, or to flag a triage tool's error to a superior, now does so knowing that disagreement with a politically appointed reclassification decision-maker carries a risk it did not carry eighteen months ago. Nothing in the rule requires this chilling effect to occur in any individual case. But civil-service protections exist precisely because institutional designers have long understood that independent judgment is safer to exercise when the person exercising it cannot be fired at will for the wrong reason — and that logic does not become less true because the judgment in question concerns a machine-generated score rather than a policy dispute.

## The chain is compounding, not coincidental

A third, live development makes the timing worse. On May 29, 2026, OMB published a [proposed rewrite of the government-wide Uniform Guidance](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/06/omb-proposed-revisions-to-the-uniform-guidance-key-takeaways-for-award-recipient-organizations), the regulation governing how virtually every federal discretionary grant is issued, monitored, and terminated. Comments closed July 13, with OMB targeting a final rule by October 1. Among its most contested provisions: mandatory pre-issuance review of discretionary awards by senior political appointees, who must certify that awards "advance the President's policy priorities," plus expanded discretionary authority to terminate grants that fall out of alignment. At a June 30 House Appropriations hearing on the proposal, [OMB Director Russ Vought confirmed](https://fedscoop.com/white-house-ai-tool-grants-trump-agenda-russell-vought/) the administration is "definitely working on technology, including AI" to get "a wider-angle lens" on federal spending, while insisting such tools would supplement rather than replace human officials' determinations — a promise with no enforceable definition attached. Rep. Rosa DeLauro and other Democratic members [pressed Vought at the same hearing](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/congress/2026/07/house-senate-dems-ringing-alarm-bells-over-draft-grants-rewrite/) on the argument that shifting, appointee-driven priorities without stable standards leave grantees unable to predict outcomes — an objection that applies with equal force to an AI-scored, politically reviewable grant pipeline staffed by at-will civil servants. NSF, for its part, has already [deferred its planned FY2026 PAPPG update](https://osr.ucsf.edu/news/nsf-26-200-supplement-released-to-pappg-24-1) to align with whatever the Uniform Guidance rewrite ultimately requires — meaning the rules governing agency conduct in this exact space are unsettled right now, at the same moment agencies are adopting AI tools inside review pipelines and stripping protections from the officials meant to check them.

Put the three pieces together and the shape of the risk is clear. AI-assisted triage is entering agency review workflows (ARPA-H today, plausibly NIH and NSF next, given NSF's own working group is already studying deployment rather than ruling it out). The program officers and scientific review officers positioned to catch a bad AI call have just lost the due-process protections that made overruling a superior, or an algorithm, a safe professional choice. And a pending rule would add a layer of political pre-issuance review with its own AI-assisted monitoring, atop a review process already thinner on independent civil-service judgment than it was a year ago. No single piece of this is a scandal. Together, they describe a grantmaking pipeline with more automation and less institutional friction at every stage where a human being might have said "wait."

## Neither ban nor shrug: a disclosure-plus-appeal framework

The answer is not to prohibit AI from federal grant evaluation. Used as ARPA-H describes — summarizing lengthy submissions, surfacing claims that deserve scrutiny, helping an overloaded reviewer corps move faster through a growing volume of proposals — AI triage tools can make peer review more consistent and more scalable, not less rigorous. NIH's own justification for tightening its applicant-side rules cited a flood of low-quality, AI-assisted submissions; agencies facing that volume problem have a legitimate efficiency case for AI-assisted review support. A blanket ban would also do nothing to address the underlying accountability gap — it would just push agency AI adoption further from public view, the way NSF's 2023 reviewer restrictions arguably have, absent any parallel transparency requirement for the agency's own tools.

Nor is the status quo — punitive rules for applicants, discretionary silence for agencies — defensible. It inverts the accountability agencies should demand of themselves relative to the people they regulate, and it does so at the exact moment the human check on agency-side error has been weakened.

The better model already exists inside the federal grantmaking system: NSF's applicant-facing approach, which asks for disclosure rather than threatening ORI referral, and which pairs restriction (no uploading confidential proposals to commercial tools) with a defined, narrow rationale (protecting reviewer confidentiality) rather than an undefined "substantially developed" standard enforced after the fact. NIH, NSF, and mission agencies like ARPA-H should adopt a version of that same logic applied reflexively, to themselves:

1. **Mandatory, specific disclosure of agency-side AI use.** Every solicitation should state, in the way ARPA-H's IGoR statement already gestures toward, which AI tools are used at which stage of review, what they do (summarize, score, flag, rank) and do not do (make final funding decisions), and whether the tool is agency-controlled or vendor-hosted. This is a transparency floor, not a technical restriction — it costs agencies nothing to publish and gives applicants, oversight committees, and GAO auditors a fixed target to evaluate rather than an assumption to litigate.
2. **A defined human-only re-review path.** Any applicant whose proposal was triaged, scored, or ranked with AI assistance should have a clearly published, time-bound right to request re-review by a human panel without AI involvement, before a final funding decision is made. This does not require agencies to abandon AI tools; it requires them to guarantee that no proposal's fate is sealed by an AI-assisted process the applicant cannot contest.
3. **Explicit reaffirmation that program officers and scientific review officers retain protected discretion to flag AI-influenced errors**, regardless of Schedule Policy/Career reclassification status — through statute, appropriations report language, or, short of that, binding OMB guidance clarifying that reclassification does not diminish an official's existing authority or expectation to document disagreement with an automated score. Congress cannot easily reverse OPM's rule through appropriations riders alone, but it can raise the political cost of using reclassification to punish a program officer for flagging a bad AI call, and it can require agencies to report annually on override rates for AI-influenced triage decisions — a cheap transparency mechanism that makes chilling effects visible if they occur.
4. **OMB should fold agency-side AI disclosure into the Uniform Guidance rewrite now, while the rule is still open.** The current proposal focuses political-appointee review and AI-assisted spending monitoring on downstream compliance; it says nothing about disclosure standards for AI used in the upstream merit-review process that determines which grants exist to be reviewed. With comments closed July 13 and a final rule targeted for October, this is a live and closing window for Congress and OSTP to press OMB to add exactly this requirement before it hardens into binding regulation.

None of this requires a new AI statute, a moratorium, or a licensing regime — tools that are generally premature and poorly matched to how fast the underlying technology moves. It requires agencies to apply to themselves the same disclosure logic NSF already applies, imperfectly, to applicants, and to make sure the humans meant to catch the machine's mistakes are not simultaneously being told their jobs depend on not making waves. Good AI governance in this instance is not about restricting a model. It is about building the transparency and appeal infrastructure that lets a legitimately useful tool remain a tool — checked, contestable, and accountable — rather than becoming the decision by default.
