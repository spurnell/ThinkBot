The spectrum pipeline is live — and for the first time in years, that phrase carries operational meaning rather than aspirational hope. On December 19, 2025, the FCC formally scheduled bidding for [Auction 113](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2025/12/23/2025-23785/auction-of-advanced-wireless-services-aws-3-licenses-filing-requirements-minimum-opening-bids) — the reauction of 200 Advanced Wireless Services licenses from the FCC's spectrum inventory — to commence on **June 2, 2026**. That date lands three weeks before the [June 23, 2026 statutory deadline](https://www.fcc.gov/auction/113) established by the *One Big Beautiful Bill Act* (OBBBA), the legislation President Trump signed on July 4, 2025 that restored the FCC's lapsed auction authority and erected the first genuine spectrum pipeline in American wireless history.

For now, the Commission is executing. Short-form applications closed February 11; upfront payments were due April 8. The pipeline is not just authorized — it is loaded. Whether the warm-up succeeds matters enormously: the June 23 AWS-3 deadline is less about 200 individual licenses and more about whether American wireless infrastructure governance is capable of the far larger and harder upper C-band proceeding that follows.

## How the Pipeline Lapsed — and How Congress Rebuilt It

The FCC's spectrum auction authority expired in March 2023 after Congress failed to reauthorize it, leaving the agency unable to conduct competitive bidding for new spectrum for more than two years. The lapse was not a technical regulatory hiccup — it was a self-inflicted governance failure that handed spectrum policy limbo to federal incumbents, legacy licensees, and the lobbying coalitions that benefit from delay. No competitive auction means no pressure to clear, no proceeds to appropriate, and no timeline forcing federal agencies to move out of spectrum they have hoarded for decades.

The OBBBA corrected the structural defect. The law [restores general auction authority through September 30, 2034](https://www.wiley.law/alert-One-Big-Beautiful-Bill-Act-Passes-Restoring-FCC-Auction-Authority-and-Establishing-Spectrum-Pipeline) and establishes an 800-megahertz pipeline through two complementary tracks: the FCC is directed to auction at least 300 MHz of commercial spectrum — including at least 100 MHz from the upper C-band (3.98–4.2 GHz) within two years — while NTIA is directed to identify 500 MHz of federal spectrum for eventual FCC auction, with 200 MHz identified within four years and an additional 300 MHz within six. A $50 million appropriation funds NTIA's analysis of the 2.7–2.9 GHz, 4.4–4.9 GHz, and 7.25–7.4 GHz bands. Hard statutory dates rather than aspirational timetables are the OBBBA's most important innovation. Congress correctly recognized that "we're working on it" is not a spectrum policy.

## The AWS-3 Test: Institutional Muscle, Not Just Licenses

The 200 licenses in Auction 113 occupy three AWS-3 band segments: 1695–1710 MHz, 1755–1780 MHz, and 2155–2180 MHz. The bulk of the inventory traces to DISH-affiliated entities that won over 700 licenses in [Auction 97 — the landmark 2015 proceeding that generated $44.9 billion in gross bids](https://www.fcc.gov/auction/97), then a record for any FCC auction — before defaulting on 197 of them after years of legal dispute. The reauction is the cleanup. Proceeds are [earmarked for the FCC's Secure Networks Act Reimbursement Program](https://www.khlaw.com/insights/communications-and-technology-alert-fcc-partial-suspension-fy-2026-spectrum-auctions), funding the "rip and replace" of insecure Huawei and ZTE equipment from domestic networks — a useful alignment of spectrum proceeds with national security priorities.

The procedural execution — scheduled start, filed applications, cleared payment deadlines — is worth recognizing as a concrete institutional win after two years without competitive bidding. But the real question Auction 113 must answer is not whether the licenses sell. They will. The question is whether the FCC's coordination machinery — the auction bureau, NTIA, the legal and technical processes that clear spectrum and prepare it for bidding — is ready to run at the scale and speed the upper C-band will require.

That is a different and harder test. The upper C-band auction will involve not a few hundred returned licenses in known, already-cleared bands, but the active relocation of incumbent satellite operators from 220 MHz of spectrum they currently use for commercial service. It will require technical clearing rules, interference coordination mechanisms, and federal-commercial coexistence standards that Auction 113 does not. The June 2 launch date is not the finish line — it is the warmup lap.

## The Main Event: 100 MHz, 180 MHz, or the Full 220?

The more consequential near-term decision sits inside [FCC 25-78](https://www.fcc.gov/document/fcc-proposes-upper-c-band-rules-2027-auction-0), the upper C-band NPRM adopted November 20, 2025. The proceeding covers the 3.98–4.2 GHz band — a 220 MHz slice that sits adjacent to the lower C-band spectrum auctioned in 2020 for $81 billion and now constituting the core mid-band layer for U.S. 5G deployments. The FCC proposed making up to 180 MHz of this band available for terrestrial wireless flexible use via competitive bidding, targeting a 2027 auction. The OBBBA statutory floor is 100 MHz.

CTIA and the major carriers filed comments asking for [220 MHz — the entire band](https://broadbandbreakfast.com/carriers-push-fcc-for-extra-upper-c-band-spectrum/), arguing that the upper C-band should replicate the C-band's success and deliver the full capacity investment the transition to next-generation services demands. Satellite incumbents Intelsat and SES currently operate in the band and have pushed back on aggressive relocation timelines. The FCC's proposed ceiling of 180 MHz is, in effect, the zone of compromise the Commission has mapped around that conflict.

ThinkBot's position is clear: the FCC should adopt **180 MHz**, not 100 MHz, in the final order.

The argument for defaulting to the floor runs like this: 100 MHz satisfies the statutory mandate, protects satellite incumbents from an overly aggressive transition timeline, and gives the Commission room to expand later if needed. This argument is wrong on every count.

First, the statutory minimum is a floor on what Congress requires — not a ceiling on sound spectrum policy. The Commission has authority and obligation to maximize the productive use of the spectrum under its stewardship. Choosing the minimum because it is the minimum is regulatory learned helplessness, not deliberate policy.

Second, satellite incumbents are not threatened by the upper C-band terrestrial auction — they are subject to the same performance-based coexistence framework that [the FCC's April 2026 satellite spectrum modernization order](https://thinkbot.ai/articles/2026-04-26-fcc-satellite-spectrum-modernization) rightly established as the model going forward. That order correctly replaced rigid EPFD limits with engineering-based interference thresholds. The same principle applies here: performance-based coexistence standards can protect satellite operations without capping terrestrial spectrum abundance. These are different technical questions answered by different regulatory tools, not a zero-sum fight between satellite and terrestrial users.

Third, the 6G transition does not reward incrementalism. [5G Americas research](https://www.5gamericas.org/from-spectrum-scarcity-to-scale-why-the-hunt-for-mid-band-spectrum-is-defining-the-5g-to-6g-transition/) projects typical 6G channel bandwidths of 200 to 400 MHz — more than double today's 5G channels of 60 to 100 MHz — requiring spectrum in the upper mid-band and FR3 (7–15 GHz) regions. The upper C-band auction is not the last move in the 6G pipeline; it is the next move. But undershooting now forecloses the contiguous mid-band depth that operators need to build the dense cell architectures 6G requires. The [Brattle Group has estimated](https://www.aei.org/technology-and-innovation/the-crucial-role-of-mid-band-spectrum-for-5g-and-6g-networks/) that U.S. carriers need an additional 400 MHz of full-power licensed mid-band spectrum within five years just to meet projected demand. The upper C-band contributes 100 to 220 MHz of that need. Choosing 100 MHz over 180 MHz at this juncture is a decision to extend the mid-band scarcity problem, not solve it.

## The China Factor Is Not Abstract

The competitive context for this decision is not hypothetical. China has allocated mid-band spectrum for 5G across multiple bands — 2.6 GHz, 3.5 GHz, and 4.9 GHz — totaling well over 350 MHz of licensed mid-band 5G capacity in active commercial deployment, atop which it [designated an additional 700 MHz in the 6 GHz band](https://www.rcrwireless.com/20230629/spectrum/china-allocates-6-ghz-spectrum-band-for-5g-6g-services) specifically for next-generation 5G and 6G services beginning in July 2023. [China's 5G network now comprises nearly 5 million base stations](https://news.cgtn.com/news/2026-04-21/China-s-5G-base-stations-top-4-958-million-at-end-of-March-2026-1Mw8gqf5GEw/p.html) — the largest mobile infrastructure build in history — and [surpassed 1 billion 5G subscriptions in December 2024](https://english.www.gov.cn/archive/statistics/202412/24/content_WS676a16aec6d0868f4e8ee3ce.html), becoming the first nation to do so. Laboratory trials for 6G technologies are already underway in the upper mid-band and sub-THz ranges, with Beijing aiming to complete initial standardization work by 2028–2029 and [achieve commercial launch by 2030](https://www.rcrwireless.com/20231213/featured/china-aims-6g-commercialization-2030-report).

None of this is inscrutable Chinese industrial planning that Americans should feel helpless before. It is a supply-side spectrum policy that recognized, a decade ago, that mid-band abundance was the defining variable in mobile infrastructure competitiveness — and acted accordingly. The United States, by contrast, [assigns seven times more spectrum to unlicensed Wi-Fi applications than to commercial licensed networks](https://www.aei.org/technology-and-innovation/the-crucial-role-of-mid-band-spectrum-for-5g-and-6g-networks/), a policy choice that reflects a longstanding prioritization of consumer applications over infrastructure investment.

The OBBBA corrected part of this imbalance. American dominance in international standards bodies means little if domestic operators lack the licensed spectrum to deploy the networks those standards describe. An upper C-band order that allocates 100 MHz — barely a rounding error against China's 700 MHz 6 GHz haul — does not close that gap. It signals to network equipment vendors, standards bodies, and spectrum-dependent industries that the United States is still rationing its way through a mid-band crunch that Beijing solved years ago.

## Four Things the FCC Must Do Before Summer

**First**, the FCC should publicly confirm AWS-3 auction readiness before June 1. The scheduled June 2 start date is encouraging. But the Commission should affirmatively communicate to industry and to Congress that Auction 113's milestones — applications, payments, system testing, award processes — are proceeding on schedule. Transparency is the check on the institutional drift that two years without auction authority can produce. A public readiness confirmation costs nothing and demonstrates that the auction bureau can function at speed.

**Second**, the FCC should adopt 180 MHz in the upper C-band final order, not the floor. The Commission's proposed ceiling acknowledged that the policy question is not whether to exceed 100 MHz but by how much. Carriers, equipment manufacturers, and spectrum economists all point in the same direction: supply-side abundance, not incremental additions. The incumbent accommodation question is a technical challenge that performance-based interference standards have already solved in analogous proceedings. It is not a reason to leave mid-band spectrum on the shelf.

**Third**, the FCC must work with NTIA to set aggressive, milestoned federal incumbent relocation timelines. The 2015 AWS-3 auction — the very proceeding whose licenses are being rerun in Auction 113 — demonstrated that NTIA relocation with [appropriated funding and hard milestones produces results](https://www.ntia.gov/category/aws-3-transition). Complexity is not an excuse for delay; it is a reason to plan rigorously and fund the relocation process adequately. The OBBBA's $50 million NTIA appropriation for band analysis is a start. Relocation funding and milestone accountability must follow.

**Fourth**, the FCC should initiate a 6 GHz upper-band proceeding in parallel to keep the pipeline flowing beyond 2027. The upper C-band auction is the OBBBA's centerpiece, but it cannot be the endpoint. The transition to 6G will require spectrum in the 7–15 GHz FR3 range, and what portions of those bands can be made available for licensed, full-power terrestrial use should be in an active FCC proceeding by the time the upper C-band auction closes. Permissionless pipeline flow — a continuous sequence of spectrum proceedings, not episodic one-off auctions — is the governance model that matches the pace of wireless technology development.

## The Pipeline Has to Deliver

Congress got the pipeline structure right. The OBBBA's auction authority restoration, hard statutory deadlines, and 800 MHz mandate represent the most coherent spectrum policy framework the United States has assembled in a generation. The question now is whether the FCC can execute.

On AWS-3, the early evidence is positive. The June 2 bidding start date puts the Commission on pace to meet the June 23 statutory deadline with room to spare. That is not a small thing: after two years without the institutional muscle of competitive bidding, demonstrating that the FCC's auction processes still work is itself a signal — to carriers planning capital deployment, to standards bodies evaluating where to locate network development, and to allied nations watching whether American wireless governance is reliable enough to build on.

But the warm-up is not the main event. The upper C-band order, expected in 2026 ahead of the 2027 auction, is where the 6G race is won or lost at the regulatory level. An order that defaults to 100 MHz because 100 MHz is the minimum the statute requires is not spectrum policy — it is spectrum rationing dressed up as compliance. The pipeline Congress built is sound. The FCC's job is to fill it.
