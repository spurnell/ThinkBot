# The Commerce Department's AI Preemption Map Is a Win for Innovation. Now Act On It.

Yesterday, the Commerce Department delivered what may be the most consequential federal AI governance action since President Trump signed his [December 2025 Executive Order on AI dominance](https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/march-2026-federal-deadlines-that-will-9297133/) directing agencies to pursue a "minimally burdensome national policy framework." The department's evaluation of state AI laws — mandated within 90 days of that order — names Colorado's AI Act, California's SB 53, California's AB 2013, and New York's RAISE Act as targets for potential DOJ litigation, and conditions $21 billion in remaining BEAD broadband funding on state compliance.

This is the right move. But the diagnosis is only the beginning. The administration now faces a harder challenge: translating a preemption map into coherent federal policy before the compliance limbo it has created becomes its own innovation tax.

## The Patchwork Problem Is Real

Absent federal action, the United States was sleepwalking into a fragmented, 50-state AI compliance regime — assembled not by deliberate national policy, but by a cascade of state-level reactions to AI anxiety, often written by legislators who have never looked under the hood of a foundation model.

Colorado's AI Act, [the only state law explicitly named in the original Executive Order](https://www.kslaw.com/news-and-insights/new-state-ai-laws-are-effective-on-january-1-2026-but-a-new-executive-order-signals-disruption), requires developers and deployers to "use reasonable care to protect consumers from any known or reasonably foreseeable risks of algorithmic discrimination." That sounds sensible in a press release. In practice, applying a tort-style negligence standard to probabilistic model outputs — where "discrimination" can mean anything from differential error rates to disparate impact across protected classes — creates compliance obligations so vague as to be effectively unlimited. Legal teams, not engineers, end up driving product decisions.

New York's RAISE Act and California's SB 53 layer on additional requirements: mandatory impact assessments, developer liability regimes, and disclosure mandates that in some readings would require companies to expose proprietary architectural details. Multiply these obligations across jurisdictions with conflicting definitions and inconsistent enforcement timelines, and you have not a safety regime — you have an innovation tax collected in lawyer fees.

The EO flagged something else worth attention: state laws that "compel disclosures or alter model outputs in ways that may raise constitutional concerns." Several state proposals would effectively require AI systems to produce outputs shaped by state-mandated viewpoint criteria — a form of compelled speech applied to probabilistic systems that no jurisdiction has the technical infrastructure to actually audit. The Commerce evaluation is right to treat these provisions as presumptively problematic.

## What Federal Preemption Is — and Isn't

Preemption skeptics will frame this as the federal government protecting Big Tech from accountability. That framing is wrong.

The goal of preemption is not to immunize AI developers from all responsibility — it is to ensure that AI accountability frameworks are set through deliberate national policy rather than whichever state legislature moves fastest. The choice is not between state accountability and no accountability. It is between coherent national governance and a regulatory patchwork that primarily benefits compliance consultants.

The BEAD leverage is a legitimate exercise of federal spending power. The [Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment program](https://broadbandusa.ntia.doc.gov/programs-services/broadband-equity-access-and-deployment-bead-program) is a federal investment in national digital infrastructure. Conditioning those dollars on states not maintaining AI regulations that conflict with federal innovation policy is constitutionally sound and strategically sensible. States retain full authority to set their own AI policy — but they cannot expect federal dollars underwriting that choice.

The EU's experience is instructive as a cautionary tale. The [EU AI Act](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/regulatory-framework-ai), now under implementation, is producing exactly the dysfunction the U.S. should avoid: member states interpreting high-risk categories inconsistently, enforcement authorities standing up at different speeds, and compliance obligations accumulating before a single enforcement action has produced meaningful safety outcomes. American policymakers should not recreate that mess domestically, with fifty states playing the role of the member states.

## The DOJ Task Force Must Move

Here is where the administration's approach requires direct criticism: the [DOJ AI Litigation Task Force](https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/march-2026-federal-deadlines-that-will-9297133/), established January 9, 2026, has yet to file a single lawsuit. Meanwhile, Colorado's AI Act takes effect June 30, 2026. California's SB 53 is already on the books. Companies are making compliance decisions right now — dual-tracking legal teams, adjusting product roadmaps, rerouting deployment decisions — based on laws the federal government has formally identified as conflicting with national policy but has not yet challenged.

Compliance limbo is itself a burden. Uncertainty forces industry into the worst of all positions: spend resources complying with laws that may be preempted, or gamble that preemption arrives before enforcement. Neither outcome reflects competent governance.

The Commerce report is a necessary first step, but a preemption map without litigation is a map without a territory. The DOJ Task Force must prioritize Colorado and New York — the most structurally problematic laws, with the nearest enforcement dates — and file before summer. Not as a favor to any particular company, but as an affirmation that the federal government means what its Executive Order says.

## What a Federal Floor Should Look Like

Federal preemption of state AI overreach does not mean replacing it with federal AI overreach. The administration's stated goal — a "minimally burdensome national policy framework" — is the right framing. The appropriate federal role is establishing a floor, not a ceiling.

That floor should include:

- **Voluntary standards via NIST**: The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) already provides sector-agnostic guidance that industry can adopt at appropriate risk levels. It should be the baseline reference for any federal policy — not a mandatory compliance regime.
- **Sector-specific enforcement via existing authority**: Healthcare AI through FDA and HHS. Financial AI through the SEC and CFPB. Hiring AI through the EEOC. Existing regulators with domain expertise are better positioned to calibrate AI risk than any horizontal federal mandate.
- **A clear preemption doctrine**: Congress should codify that federal sector-specific AI standards preempt conflicting state requirements, providing the legal certainty that executive action alone cannot fully deliver.

## The Bottom Line

The Commerce Department's evaluation is a serious, correct response to a genuine threat to U.S. AI competitiveness. A 50-state compliance maze does not make AI safer — it makes American AI development slower, more expensive, and more legally uncertain than development in jurisdictions unburdened by these mandates. The beneficiary of that dynamic is not consumers. It is Beijing.

The administration has drawn the map. Now it needs to move the pieces.
