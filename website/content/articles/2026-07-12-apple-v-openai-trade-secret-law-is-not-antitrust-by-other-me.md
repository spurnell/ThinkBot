Apple filed suit against OpenAI this week alleging systematic trade secret theft by OpenAI's hardware division, including the recruitment of Apple's former Chief Hardware Officer Tang Tan and systems engineer Chang Liu. The complaint claims OpenAI's hardware team extracted confidential Apple chip and device technology to build a competing consumer AI device — arriving just as OpenAI, via its acquisition of Jony Ive's io Products, positions itself as a serious entrant into hardware. The story has dominated coverage at [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-10/apple-sues-openai-for-trade-secret-theft-in-blockbuster-case), the [Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2026/07/10/apple-sues-openai-alleging-ai-company-stole-trade-secrets/), [CNN](https://www.cnn.com/2026/07/10/tech/apple-openai-devices-lawsuit), [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/10/apple-openai-lawsuit-trade-secrets.html), and [Axios](https://www.axios.com/2026/07/10/apple-sues-openai-trade-secret-theft), and the instant commentary has settled on a familiar narrative: an entrenched incumbent is deploying intellectual property law to keep a disruptive new entrant out of its market.

That narrative is intuitive. It is also, on the available facts, unsupported — and it reflects a conflation that antitrust analysts should resist.

## Two different questions, two different bodies of law

Trade secret litigation and antitrust law ask fundamentally different questions. Antitrust asks whether conduct harms *competition* — output, price, quality, innovation across a market. Trade secret law asks whether a specific party misappropriated specific confidential information. A firm can be dead right on the second question and have zero bearing on the first, and vice versa.

Apple's complaint, as reported, is a garden-variety (if high-profile) trade secret claim: it alleges that identified individuals took identified confidential technical material and used it in a competing product. That is precisely the kind of *ex-post*, evidence-particularized claim our antitrust framework should welcome relative to *ex-ante* regulation — it requires Apple to prove a specific harm in front of a court, not to invoke market share or firm size as a proxy for wrongdoing.

The Supreme Court settled the underlying policy tension between trade secret protection and free competition decades ago in *[Kewanee Oil Co. v. Bicron Corp.](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/416/470/)*, holding that trade secret law and competitive markets are complementary, not in conflict — secrecy protection encourages the kind of costly R&D investment that produces the innovations competition policy wants more of. If Apple cannot protect confidential chip and device engineering from being carried out the door by departing employees, its incentive to invest in that engineering falls. That logic doesn't disappear because the alleged beneficiary happens to be a well-funded AI lab rather than a garage startup.

## Talent mobility is not the same as theft

None of this means every lawsuit alleging misappropriation is meritorious, and it does not mean employee mobility itself is suspect. Engineers move between firms constantly, and that mobility is a genuine engine of diffusion and competition — courts and legislatures have generally protected it (most states, following California's lead, sharply limit noncompete enforcement for exactly this reason).

The relevant legal question in *Apple v. OpenAI* is not "did Apple's former Chief Hardware Officer go work for a competitor" — that's unremarkable — but whether he or others carried specific confidential material with them, which is the conduct the Defend Trade Secrets Act and California's Uniform Trade Secrets Act actually reach. Apple will have to prove that in discovery and at trial. It has not yet done so; it has filed a complaint. Commentary that treats the filing itself as proof of anticompetitive gatekeeping is doing the plaintiff's work for it without the plaintiff's evidence.

## Resist the urge to route this through antitrust or ex-ante hardware rules

The more consequential error would be to treat this dispute as evidence that regulators need to step in — either by scrutinizing Apple's hardware position under antitrust theories or by crafting prophylactic rules to insulate AI entrants from IP claims by incumbents. Neither is warranted on what's public.

There is no allegation, and no evidence, that Apple is using litigation strategically to raise rivals' costs across a hardware market where it holds durable power; smartphone and consumer device markets remain intensely contested, and OpenAI's hardware ambitions — however serious — do not yet constitute market entry that any incumbent could plausibly "block" through a single lawsuit. If Apple's claims fail on the merits, OpenAI's hardware unit proceeds unimpeded. If they succeed, that is the trade secret regime working as designed: punishing misappropriation after the fact, not preventing entry in advance. Either outcome is compatible with, not a threat to, dynamic competition in AI hardware.

This is also the right moment to note what would make this a genuine competition concern, so the distinction doesn't collapse the next time a similar suit is filed: evidence that an incumbent systematically deploys IP litigation — not as adjudication of specific theft, but as a pattern of harassment against every credible entrant regardless of merit — would raise real foreclosure questions, potentially under sham-litigation doctrines the courts have recognized since *[Professional Real Estate Investors v. Columbia Pictures](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/508/49/)*. Nothing in the current complaint, or in how it has been reported, suggests that pattern. One lawsuit against one company, built on named individuals and named allegedly-misappropriated technology, is not a strategy of exclusion; it's a lawsuit.

## The bottom line

Apple's suit against OpenAI belongs in federal court, not in an antitrust complaint or a new ex-ante hardware-market rulebook. The counterfactual matters here: a world in which incumbents cannot enforce trade secrets against departing employees is a world with less incentive to invest in the hard engineering that makes hardware innovation possible in the first place — including, eventually, OpenAI's own. Let the case proceed on its merits. If the facts show theft, Apple should win. If they don't, OpenAI should. Antitrust law has no dog in this fight, and it shouldn't be drafted into one on the strength of a headline.
