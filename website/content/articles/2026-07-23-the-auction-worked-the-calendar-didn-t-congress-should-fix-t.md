On July 22, 2026, the FCC [adopted rules](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DOC-422694A1.pdf) to auction 160 megahertz of Upper C-Band spectrum (3.98–4.14 GHz) — 60 percent more than the 100 MHz floor Congress set in last year's spectrum-pipeline mandate under the [One Big Beautiful Bill Act](https://www.wiley.law/alert-One-Big-Beautiful-Bill-Act-Passes-Restoring-FCC-Auction-Authority-and-Establishing-Spectrum-Pipeline). Paired with the adjacent Lower C-Band, the order creates a harmonized 440 MHz "super band" spanning 3.70–4.14 GHz — [more contiguous licensed mid-band spectrum than any other industrialized country holds](https://www.newscaststudio.com/2026/07/22/fcc-adopts-rules-for-upper-c-band-auction-sets-stage-for-440-mhz-super-band/). [ITIF's same-day analysis](https://itif.org/publications/2026/07/22/the-fccs-upper-c-band-auction-rules-will-strengthen-americas-wireless-leadership/) rightly praised the aviation-safety coordination built into the order. That coordination is real, but it's also where the story most people will tell about this order stops — and where the more consequential one starts.

Here is the number that should worry Congressional Commerce Committee staff more than anything in the order itself: winning bidders will spend billions on licenses in an auction concluding by July 2027, and then wait until [**December 2030**](https://www.dwt.com/blogs/broadband-advisor/2026/07/fcc-upper-c-band-draft-transition-order) — for the top 75 markets — before they can turn any of that spectrum on. Smaller markets wait until July 2031. Four years between "the government sold you the airwaves" and "you may use the airwaves." That gap is not a flaw in the auction. It's a flaw in what happens after the auction, and it deserves to be treated as a distinct policy problem.

## The auction is the hero here

Competitive bidding for scarce mid-band spectrum is about as close to textbook market allocation as spectrum policy gets, and this auction cleared that bar comfortably. The FCC didn't just hit the statutory floor — it structured a 3,248-license offering [60 percent above the 100 MHz Congress mandated](https://phillipslytle.com/fcc-advances-upper-c-band-auction-under-tight-statutory-timeline-july-4-2027/), and did it inside the [tight July 2027 statutory deadline](https://phillipslytle.com/fcc-advances-upper-c-band-auction-under-tight-statutory-timeline-july-4-2027/) OBBBA imposed. Congress deserves credit for restoring FCC auction authority and setting an aggressive pipeline — [800 MHz of commercial spectrum by 2034](https://www.wiley.law/alert-One-Big-Beautiful-Bill-Act-Passes-Restoring-FCC-Auction-Authority-and-Establishing-Spectrum-Pipeline), on top of the Upper C-Band tranche; the FCC deserves credit for using that authority to maximize the offering rather than satisfice at the floor. None of what follows is an argument that auctions need to slow down, add more upfront "coordination" steps, or defer to interagency committees before licenses go out the door. The mechanism worked. The question is what happens to the asset once someone has legitimately purchased it.

## An unaccountable calendar, not an illegitimate concern

The reason usable service waits until 2030 is not engineering uncertainty about whether Upper C-Band signals interfere with aircraft radio altimeters — the FAA's underlying safety concern is legitimate and well-documented. The FAA's 2023 altimeter rule addressed *Lower* C-Band interference; the agency has since determined those altimeter specifications are [insufficient for the Upper C-Band](https://www.flightglobal.com/safety/faa-proposes-additional-radio-altimeter-standards-amid-5g-expansion/165850.article), and issued a [new proposed rule in January 2026](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/01/07/2026-00051/requirements-for-interference-tolerant-radio-altimeter-systems) requiring a fresh retrofit — at an estimated [$4.5 billion cost to airlines](https://aerospaceglobalnews.com/news/faa-5g-radio-altimeters-airlines-retrofit-interference/). Nobody should want the FCC or Congress overriding that safety judgment on a shot clock.

What nobody should accept, either, is that the *retrofit schedule* itself carries no comparable urgency. The FAA's own estimate puts initial implementation "likely" between 2029 and 2032 depending on aircraft class — a four-year window with no statutory anchor, no interim milestones, and no consequence for slippage. Part 121 carriers and larger foreign operators face the earliest deadline, tied loosely to whenever the FCC authorizes service; smaller aircraft get two additional years on top of that. Nothing in that structure creates pressure to compress the calendar — only pressure, if anything, to extend it. That is the distinction this piece is drawing: the *retrofit requirement* is not the problem; the *absence of an accountable, incentivized retrofit deadline* is.

## This has happened before — and Congress already knows the fix

The 2021 C-Band auction offers a useful counter-example, because Congress and the FCC solved almost exactly this problem once already. Satellite operators occupying the Lower C-Band were originally on a track to clear their spectrum by December 2025 — roughly five years after the January 2021 auction concluded. Instead, the FCC structured [accelerated relocation payments of $9.7 billion](https://www.telecompetitor.com/satellite-operators-agree-to-accelerate-c-band-relocation-sets-stage-for-historic-5g-auction/), funded by the winning bidders themselves, conditioned on satellite operators hitting compressed deadlines — December 2021 for the first tranche of markets, December 2023 nationwide. All five eligible operators took the deal. The clearing timeline was cut by roughly two years not through a mandate on the operators but through a *priced incentive tied to a hard date*. CBRS tells a similar, messier story on the front end: [Spectrum Access System certification and government testing delays](https://www.lightreading.com/mobile/5g/government-testing-adds-5-month-delay-%28and-counting%29-to-cbrs/d/d-id/750266) pushed that band's commercial launch roughly two years behind the FCC's original schedule before a single Priority Access License went to market. The pattern across three consecutive mid-band bands — CBRS, C-Band, now Upper C-Band — is that the auction moves fast and the adjacent clearing or retrofit process, run by a different agency or a different set of incumbents, does not, absent an explicit deadline with teeth.

## What Congress should actually legislate

The next spectrum pipeline tranches are already due — up to [500 MHz of additional federal and shared spectrum](https://www.wiley.law/alert-One-Big-Beautiful-Bill-Act-Passes-Restoring-FCC-Auction-Authority-and-Establishing-Spectrum-Pipeline), with 200 MHz to be identified by July 2027 and auctioned by July 2029, and the balance by July 2033 — which gives Congress a live vehicle to fix the sequencing defect rather than repeat it. That means writing a statutory completion deadline into the FAA's retrofit rule itself, or structuring an accelerated-completion incentive analogous to the 2021 satellite relocation payments — funded, as before, by the license winners who benefit from earlier access, not by taxpayers. Either approach converts an open-ended agency calendar into an accountable one, without touching the FAA's authority to set the underlying safety standard.

Two camps will resist this framing. Spectrum-auction skeptics will point to the 2030 date as proof that auctions are slow and "coordination" needs to happen earlier and more thoroughly — but the auction was oversubscribed, fast, and delivered above the statutory floor; the four-year lag sits entirely downstream of it, in a different agency's hardware calendar. FAA-aligned voices, meanwhile, may argue the retrofit timeline needs *more* room, not a deadline — but an accountable schedule and a safe one aren't in tension; the ask here is a firm date, not a rushed one.

The stakes are not abstract. Competing markets building out mid-band 5G are not waiting on a four-year, cross-agency hardware-certification calendar to light up spectrum their operators have already paid for. Every year the United States lets an interagency scheduling gap sit between a purchased license and a usable network is a self-inflicted cost against that competition — not a cost the market or the auction mechanism created, but one Congress can remove by legislating the deadline the calendar currently lacks.
