The AI competition with China is often described in terms of model architecture, chip design, and talent supply. Those dimensions matter. But the binding constraint on American AI capacity in 2026 is none of them. It is the time it takes to connect a building to the power grid.

FERC interconnection queues now stretch four to seven years. Roughly half of all planned US data center projects face delay or outright cancellation — not because of capital constraints, not because of regulatory ambiguity about AI, but because they cannot get power on a timeline that makes investment rational. Eleven states have introduced construction moratorium legislation. Local governments in New Orleans and Chandler, Arizona have already imposed construction halts. And in Congress, Senator Bernie Sanders and Representative Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez have introduced the [Artificial Intelligence Data Center Moratorium Act](https://www.sanders.senate.gov/press-releases/news-sanders-ocasio-cortez-announce-ai-data-center-moratorium-act/), which would halt all new data center construction pending what they call "comprehensive AI regulation."

Meanwhile, a genuine legislative window has opened on the other side of that debate — Senator Tom Cotton's DATA Act, [bipartisan calls for permitting reform](https://www.nextgov.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/02/lawmakers-both-parties-back-data-center-permitting-reform/411700/) championed by Senator Ted Budd and Representative Jake Auchincloss, a DOE Secretary directive ordering FERC to fast-track large-load interconnection rules, and DOE's federal land site-selection program for data center and energy co-location. That window will not stay open indefinitely. The moratorium faction is building a counter-narrative, and without a forceful response, it may gain the momentum needed to stall the reform agenda.

Washington should not wait. Grid permitting reform is AI policy — and the case for treating it that way, urgently, is overwhelming.

## The Bottleneck Is Real and Already Binding

This is not a projection problem. It is an active constraint.

The [International Energy Agency projects global data center electricity consumption will hit 1,100 TWh in 2026](https://www.iea.org/reports/energy-and-ai/energy-demand-from-ai) — an 18 percent upward revision from estimates published just months earlier in December 2025. That revision was not driven by changed assumptions about model efficiency; it was driven by the pace of deployment. AI inference, in particular, has proven far more electricity-intensive than prior planning cycles assumed, as workloads once run in batch are increasingly run in real time.

Meeting that demand on the US side requires grid additions at a scale the existing interconnection queue apparatus is structurally incapable of delivering. The queue itself has become the problem: as of early 2026, [approximately half of planned US data center projects](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/us-senator-proposes-bill-permitting-ai-data-centers-to-bypass-federal-power-rules-via-off-grid-energy-infrastructure-development/) face delays or cancellations attributable to power access failures, not market conditions.

Critics sometimes argue that AI data centers' power demands are overstated, and [ITIF has offered four reasons why new AI data centers won't overwhelm the electricity grid](https://itif.org/publications/2026/04/07/four-reasons-new-ai-data-centers-wont-overwhelm-the-electricity-grid/) given sufficient investment in supply-side expansion. That argument is correct — but it is also the argument for urgency, not complacency. The grid can handle AI's power demands if interconnection queues are cleared and new capacity comes online. The alternative — rationing access to existing grid capacity through procedural delay — is not a sustainable equilibrium. It is a slow-motion infrastructure failure.

What makes the current moment distinct is that the bottleneck is no longer primarily about physical generation. The US has abundant pipeline capacity in solar, wind, and natural gas peakers. The constraint is the administrative process for connecting that generation, and connected loads, to the transmission system — a process that FERC has allowed to calcify into a multi-year backlog through procedural design that has not kept pace with demand.

## The Federal Vacuum Is Filling with State Patchwork

When the federal government fails to provide a clear framework for a major economic sector, states and localities move in. That dynamic is well understood in AI governance debates; it is equally operative in AI infrastructure.

Absent federal leadership on data center permitting and interconnection, [eleven states have introduced moratorium bills](https://stateline.org/2026/03/06/temporarily-banning-data-centers-draws-more-interest-from-state-local-officials/) as of April 2026. Local governments, facing constituent concerns about water use, noise, and visual impact, have imposed construction halts in communities like New Orleans and Chandler, Arizona. The pattern is familiar: a legitimate gap in federal policy creates a regulatory vacuum, and the vacuum fills with the most locally convenient answer — which is usually a ban.

The same federalism logic that animates calls for federal AI preemption applies here with equal force. If the federal government does not establish a coherent framework for large-load interconnection and data center siting — one that balances legitimate community concerns against national infrastructure imperatives — it will get fifty frameworks, designed by fifty state legislatures with fifty different risk tolerances and fifty different political pressures. The result will be investment uncertainty compounding on itself: developers who cannot predict the regulatory environment in any given state will shift capacity offshore or defer projects indefinitely.

[Bipartisan Policy Center analysis](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/explainer/strategic-federal-actions-aim-to-strengthen-ai-and-energy-infrastructure/) has documented this dynamic, noting that strategic federal action on AI and energy infrastructure is increasingly understood as a prerequisite for maintaining US competitive position — not merely a favor to the industry, but a structural necessity for the entire AI ecosystem, including startups, academic researchers, and national security applications who depend on available compute capacity without the market power to navigate multi-year queue delays on their own.

## The Right Tools: DATA Act and FERC Rulemaking

Two legislative and regulatory instruments are currently in play. Both deserve serious engagement — on their merits and their limits.

**DOE Secretary Wright's FERC Directive**

The more structurally significant intervention is [DOE Secretary Wright's directive to FERC to propose new rules accelerating large-load interconnection](https://www.energy.gov/articles/secretary-wright-acts-unleash-american-industry-and-innovation-newly-proposed-rules). The rulemaking is currently in comment period. Its target is the interconnection queue process itself — the backlog of generator and load interconnection applications that, under current FERC procedures, can sit for four to seven years before a project receives a final interconnection agreement.

FERC rulemaking is the right structural lever because it addresses the root cause rather than routing around it. A reformed queue process — with binding timelines, deposit requirements to deter speculative applications that clog the queue, and cluster studies that process related projects simultaneously — would benefit every load class, not only large data centers. Transmission investment would accelerate. Industrial manufacturers, EV charging networks, and clean energy projects would all see faster connection times. The sectoral spillovers are substantial.

The limits are real: FERC rulemaking moves slowly, legal challenges are predictable, and implementation requires utility cooperation that is not guaranteed. This is the scalable solution, but it is not the fast solution.

**The DATA Act**

Senator Cotton's [DATA Act proposes a more immediate workaround](https://www.utilitydive.com/news/senate-bill-exempts-fully-isolated-large-loads-from-ferc-doe-regulation/809330/): allow AI data centers to establish off-grid power arrangements exempt from Federal Power Act rate regulation. Under this structure, a data center operator could contract directly with generators, build or finance dedicated transmission, and bypass the interconnection queue entirely — operating as a self-contained power system rather than a grid customer.

The innovation is real. For projects where the economics support dedicated generation — and where the scale of the load makes such investment viable — the DATA Act offers a path around a queue that is not going to clear on its own in time to matter. [Bipartisan support exists for this general approach](https://www.nextgov.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/02/lawmakers-both-parties-back-data-center-permitting-reform/411700/), reflecting recognition across party lines that the status quo is untenable.

The limits are also real. [As AEI's analysis of the DATA Act notes](https://www.aei.org/articles/the-upsides-and-downsides-of-senator-cottons-data-act/), off-grid solutions are economically viable only at very large scale — a threshold that smaller operators, research institutions, and startup cloud users cannot meet. The DATA Act may relieve pressure for the largest loads while leaving mid-tier and small-scale users still trapped in the queue. It should be understood as a pressure-relief valve and a proof-of-concept for regulatory flexibility, not a comprehensive solution. Structural FERC reform remains necessary.

DOE's announcement of [sixteen federal sites for AI data center and energy co-location](https://www.energy.gov/articles/doe-identifies-16-federal-sites-across-country-data-center-and-ai-infrastructure) represents a third instrument: using the federal land estate to create pre-permitted, pre-sited locations where both generation and load can co-locate, bypassing the most contentious elements of the local siting and interconnection process. This is worth scaling aggressively.

Together, these instruments — FERC reform for structural change, the DATA Act for immediate pressure relief, federal land co-location for the next cohort of projects — form a coherent package. None is sufficient alone. All three are superior to the alternative.

## Reframing the Debate: This Is Competitiveness Policy

The US debate frames data center power access as an energy policy question — efficiency standards, grid stability, utility rate design. That framing is analytically correct but politically misaligned.

China does not frame its data center buildout as energy policy. It frames it as a national security and economic sovereignty imperative. The Chinese government's AI infrastructure investments are coordinated across energy, land, telecommunications, and industrial policy precisely because the government understands that compute infrastructure is not a sector — it is the substrate for every other sector. Manufacturing optimization, drug discovery, autonomous logistics, defense systems: none of these applications are available to a country that cannot build and power the computing infrastructure to run them.

Washington should adopt the same framing — not because government direction of capital allocation is wise as a general rule, but because the question of whether AI infrastructure gets built in the United States is a question about whether the United States retains the capacity to develop, deploy, iterate on, and govern AI systems. If the physical infrastructure is built in other jurisdictions — because US permitting is too slow, too uncertain, or too fragmented — the policy debate about AI safety, labor market impacts, and national security applications becomes increasingly theoretical. You cannot govern systems you cannot run.

This reframing matters for the legislative conversation. Permitting reform is not a favor to large technology companies. Compute access is the foundational resource for the entire innovation ecosystem: for the startup that cannot afford its own data center and depends on cloud pricing that reflects available supply; for the university research lab that needs affordable access to large-scale inference; for the national laboratory running classified workloads that require domestic, high-security infrastructure; for the small manufacturer trying to integrate AI-assisted quality control into a production line. Compute scarcity is a regressive policy outcome — it most disadvantages the actors with the least market power to navigate multi-year queue delays.

## The Moratorium Argument Gets the Causation Backwards

The Sanders/AOC moratorium proposal deserves a direct answer, because it will attract media attention and will be presented as a responsible, precautionary approach to AI governance. It is neither.

The argument for a data center construction moratorium pending "comprehensive AI regulation" inverts the causal relationship between infrastructure and governance. Evidence-based AI regulation — the kind that reflects actual system capabilities, actual deployment patterns, and actual observed harms — requires operational AI infrastructure. You cannot study, regulate, or govern systems you cannot run.

A moratorium does not create AI safety. It creates compute scarcity and offshores the development that Washington most needs to observe and govern. If US data center construction halts for the several years that "comprehensive AI regulation" would plausibly require — no serious regulatory framework has been developed and implemented quickly — AI development continues at scale in jurisdictions that impose no moratorium. The systems that shape global commerce, security, and information flow get built in China, in the Gulf, in countries with no interest in US safety standards. The US then faces the task of governing AI systems built elsewhere, on infrastructure it does not control, with limited visibility into training data, model architecture, or deployment context.

This is not a hypothetical. It is the predictable consequence of a construction halt applied unilaterally by one country in a competition that does not pause for its regulatory processes.

The honest version of the moratorium argument is that AI development is moving too fast for democratic governance to keep pace. That concern is legitimate and deserves engagement. The answer is to accelerate governance development — not to constrain infrastructure in ways that offshore development while preserving the appearance of precaution.

## Recommendations

**For Congress:**

1. Move the DATA Act through markup and to the floor with amendments that address mid-tier access — either by lowering the scale threshold for off-grid utility status or by creating a shared infrastructure pathway for smaller operators.
2. Advance bipartisan permitting reform legislation as a complement, not a substitute, for FERC rulemaking. Congressional authorization strengthens FERC's legal position on new rules and provides clearer statutory direction.
3. Reject the Sanders/AOC moratorium on substantive grounds: moratoriums offshore AI development without producing governance, and governance without observational infrastructure is guesswork.

**For FERC:**

1. Finalize the large-load interconnection rulemaking on the timeline Secretary Wright's directive contemplates. Prioritize binding timelines over procedural elegance — a rule with teeth and a two-year implementation horizon is more valuable than a comprehensive rule that arrives in 2029.
2. Adopt deposit requirements and withdrawal penalties that reduce speculative queue clogging without creating new barriers for legitimate projects.
3. Work with DOE to create a federal land interconnection pathway that leverages DOE's site-selection program.

**For DOE:**

1. Expand the federal land co-location program aggressively. Federal land with pre-cleared interconnection rights and streamlined environmental review represents the fastest path to new AI infrastructure capacity in the near term.
2. Publish data on the cost of queue delay to the US economy — the cost of deferred investment, lost tax revenue, and foregone innovation — to reframe the permitting debate in terms policymakers can act on.

**For state policymakers:**

1. Coordinate with the federal framework rather than preempting it. State moratoriums create investment uncertainty that compounds without producing safety outcomes. States have legitimate interests in water use, land use, and community impact; those interests can be addressed through disclosure, mitigation requirements, and local negotiation — not blanket construction bans.

## Conclusion

The AI arms race has a physical chokepoint that no executive order can fix with a signature and no talent pipeline can route around. The models exist. The capital exists. The engineers exist. What does not exist, in sufficient quantity, is the power capacity to run AI at the scale the moment demands — because the administrative process for connecting new capacity to the grid has not been meaningfully reformed in decades, and the political will to reform it has only recently arrived.

That political will is here now, in the form of the DATA Act, bipartisan calls for permitting reform, a DOE Secretary directive, and federal land site selection across sixteen sites. The counter-pressure — in the form of state moratoriums and a congressional construction halt — is building on the other side.

Washington has a choice: treat grid permitting reform as an AI competitiveness imperative and act on the legislative window that currently exists, or treat it as an energy policy debate and watch the window close. The first choice leads to a US AI ecosystem that can build, deploy, and iterate. The second leads to an infrastructure gap that no amount of model architecture excellence will overcome.

The bottleneck is concrete. The policy tools exist. The urgency is real.
