The [National Quantum Initiative Reauthorization Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/3597/text) has cleared both the Senate Commerce Committee — unanimously, [on World Quantum Day](https://www.commerce.senate.gov/press/dem/release/led-by-cantwell-and-young-national-quantum-initiative-reauthorization-act-unanimously-passes-commerce-committee-on-world-quantum-day) — and the House Science, Space, and Technology Committee. Floor votes are imminent. Led by Senators Maria Cantwell (D-Wash.) and Todd Young (R-Ind.), the bill extends the original 2018 NQI through 2034, creates five new NSF Multidisciplinary Centers for Quantum Research, launches new NIST quantum engineering centers, grants NASA its first explicit quantum R&D authority, and mandates the Commerce Department to address the structural failure that has bedeviled American quantum policy since the first law passed: the yawning gap between laboratory breakthrough and commercial product.

This is the most consequential quantum policy action since the original law, and it arrives in a legislative environment where bipartisan agreement on anything is rare. Congress deserves credit. The investment posture is right, the commercialization architecture is sound, and reauthorization is overdue. But the bill carries an unresolved tension that deserves honest scrutiny before floor debate begins.

The scientific openness that produced America's quantum lead depends on a global research community that an increasingly hawkish export control apparatus is treating as a strategic liability rather than an asset. Congress needs to resolve that tension explicitly — not leave it to the Bureau of Industry and Security to work out through rulemaking. The NQI Reauthorization gets the investment right. The openness question demands a sharper answer.

## The 2018 Law Worked — and Letting It Lapse Was a Policy Own-Goal

The 2018 National Quantum Initiative Act was, by any reasonable measure, a policy success. Federal investment in quantum information science was already growing before the law's passage, but the NQI formalized it into a whole-of-government framework, established the National Quantum Coordination Office, and catalyzed the private sector co-investment that government funding alone rarely generates.

The results are visible in the data. Between enactment and 2024, the federal government invested [$2.5 billion in quantum programs](https://www.quantum.gov/) through NSF, DOE, NIST, and DARPA. American university patent filings in quantum technology reached a record 1,668 patent families in 2023. Private venture capital has followed public investment: U.S.-based quantum companies raised approximately [$1.7 billion of the $2.6 billion](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/tech-forward/quantum-technology-investment-hits-a-magic-moment) invested globally in quantum startups in 2024 alone — a 58 percent increase over 2023. PsiQuantum and Quantinuum together captured nearly half of all 2024 venture capital, a sign of maturing late-stage investment concentration that reflects genuine commercial expectations, not speculative enthusiasm.

Allowing the NQI authorization to lapse was, to put it plainly, a policy own-goal. The funding streams Congress authorized did not disappear — NSF, the DOE national labs, and NIST continued operating — but the coordinating architecture weakened, the political signal to industry was muddied, and the reauthorization debate opened a period of legislative uncertainty that no technology sector benefits from. The 2026 bill is not a pivot; it is a recovery.

## The Valley of Death Provision Is the Most Consequential Clause in the Bill

The most important policy problem the NQI Reauthorization attempts to solve is not raw funding levels. It is the structural failure of American quantum policy to translate world-class scientific output into commercially available hardware and software before competitors do.

The United States remains the global leader in high-impact quantum research. American groups at IBM, Google, and a constellation of university labs have produced the most significant advances in superconducting qubit coherence times, quantum error correction, and fault-tolerant computing architectures. [According to ITIF's September 2024 analysis](https://itif.org/publications/2024/09/09/how-innovative-is-china-in-quantum/), the U.S. maintains a commanding advantage in quantum computing hardware sophistication and algorithm development — areas where China openly acknowledges its lag.

But China has built the commercialization infrastructure the United States has not. The Hefei "Quantum Avenue" model — a state-directed industrial hub that converts research output into market-ready products — is exactly the kind of research-to-application bridge that American labs cannot replicate through peer-reviewed publications and federal grants alone. [China's 15th Five-Year Plan (2026–2030)](https://www.china-briefing.com/news/chinas-quantum-technology-15th-fyp-commercialization/) names quantum technology the first of seven designated "future industries." [China claims over $15 billion in cumulative public quantum investment](https://www.csis.org/analysis/understanding-chinas-quest-quantum-advancement) versus the U.S. government's approximately $3.8 billion — and while American private capital dramatically outpaces Chinese venture investment, it cannot close the commercialization gap if the basic infrastructure to test, validate, and manufacture quantum hardware does not exist.

The NQI Reauthorization's mandate for the Commerce Department to map quantum supply chain vulnerabilities and propose a commercialization bridge directly targets this structural failure. The five new NSF Multidisciplinary Centers for Quantum Research and Education, new NIST quantum acceleration centers, and expanded quantum testbeds represent a deliberate attempt to build the shared infrastructure — fabrication facilities, testing environments, industry-university partnerships — that closes the lab-to-market gap.

This is the right architecture. Federal testbeds modeled on the Manufacturing USA institute network and university centers with explicit industry partnership requirements have a better track record than either pure basic research grants or directed commercial subsidies. The NQI Reauthorization is correctly building the connective tissue, not trying to pick quantum computing winners.

## Workforce: The Binding Constraint the Bill Partially Addresses

Congress cannot fully solve the quantum workforce problem through authorization bills alone, but the NQI Reauthorization takes meaningful steps. The five new NSF centers and a quantum workforce coordination hub will create new PhD training pipelines and, critically, new master's-level programs that produce the applied scientists industry actually needs.

The numbers make the urgency plain. [McKinsey research finds](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/mckinsey-digital/our-insights/five-lessons-from-ai-on-closing-quantums-talent-gap-before-its-too-late) only one qualified candidate for every three specialized quantum positions globally — a ratio that has held even as demand accelerates. U.S. quantum-related job postings have tripled since 2011. McKinsey estimates that more than 250,000 new quantum professionals will be needed globally by 2030, a supply gap that American doctoral programs alone cannot fill.

Here the bill's limits become visible. NSF centers and training grants are supply-side interventions operating on a five-to-ten-year horizon. The near-term workforce gap requires two policy tools the reauthorization cannot deliver: robust high-skilled immigration pathways and a funded national quantum education pipeline from undergraduate through professional master's programs.

On the immigration front, administrative uncertainty around H-1B wage rule enforcement and processing backlogs has created a talent bottleneck that is entirely self-inflicted. The United States attracted global quantum talent because it combined world-class research institutions with a credible immigration pathway. Undermining that pathway does not protect American quantum jobs — it pushes global talent to Canada, the United Kingdom, and Germany, each of which has made high-skilled immigration a deliberate component of its quantum strategy. [China's quantum workforce strategy](https://itif.org/publications/2024/09/09/how-innovative-is-china-in-quantum/) draws on a relatively insular domestic pipeline; the U.S. advantage has always been the aggregation of global talent. That advantage is not self-sustaining under restrictive immigration conditions.

Congress should treat the NQI Reauthorization's workforce provisions as a floor, not a ceiling, and pair them with explicit immigration language that signals to the global quantum research community that American doors remain open.

## The Openness Paradox: Where the Bill's Tension Lives

The bill's most consequential unresolved difficulty lives here.

Quantum information science is inherently collaborative and inherently global. Quantum error correction — the field that separates near-term noisy intermediate-scale quantum devices from fault-tolerant computers — is a theoretical and experimental problem being worked in parallel by researchers at MIT, Caltech, the University of Waterloo, ETH Zürich, Delft, and Tokyo. [Approximately 50 percent of U.S. quantum research publications involve international co-authorship](https://itif.org/publications/2024/09/09/how-innovative-is-china-in-quantum/), compared to only 19–29 percent for Chinese quantum research across computing, communications, and sensing. American scientific velocity is, in measurable part, a product of its openness to the global research community. China's relative isolation is a genuine structural disadvantage, despite its larger public investment figures.

The [September 2024 BIS interim final rule](https://www.bis.gov/press-release/department-commerce-implements-controls-quantum-computing-other-advanced-technologies-alongside) on quantum computing export controls added 18 new Export Control Classification Numbers covering quantum computers, components, materials, software, and technology, subjecting them to export licensing requirements on national security and regional stability grounds. The rule includes a narrow License Exception for countries with "equivalent technical national controls" — Australia, Canada, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, Spain, and the United Kingdom — but the administrative burden of licensing and the legal uncertainty around "deemed exports" (the transfer of controlled technology to foreign nationals on U.S. soil) has already begun to chill research collaboration in ways the exception was designed to prevent.

More troubling is what happened after the controls took effect. A [Royal United Services Institute analysis](https://www.rusi.org/explore-our-research/publications/commentary/export-controls-accelerate-chinas-quantum-supply-chain) found that the export restrictions have paradoxically *accelerated* China's domestic quantum supply chain development. By eliminating the option of purchasing established foreign suppliers' hardware, the restrictions forced Chinese quantum laboratories to rapidly iterate with domestic manufacturers — suppliers that China's government had been cultivating since 2018, precisely in anticipation of U.S. controls. Chinese manufacturers are now producing microwave interconnects, dilution refrigerators, and quantum control electronics domestically. The RUSI analysis concludes it is "only a matter of time" before Chinese quantum hardware begins exporting to European markets.

The semiconductor export control playbook provides the cautionary precedent. When the United States restricted advanced lithography equipment exports — including controls affecting ASML, a Dutch company — the short-term effect was to slow Chinese progress in cutting-edge chip manufacturing. The medium-term effect was to accelerate Chinese investment in domestic alternatives while simultaneously damaging allied companies and research partnerships that U.S. competitiveness depends on. The controls were defensible in targeting China but miscalibrated in their breadth and in the diplomatic damage they caused before a Wassenaar-like multilateral framework could be assembled. Quantum controls risk replicating the same overreach.

## The Framework Congress Should Adopt: Ally-Permissive, Adversary-Restrictive

Targeted controls on the transfer of advanced quantum hardware and manufacturing know-how to the People's Republic of China and other adversary states are defensible on national security grounds and consistent with a pro-innovation framework that takes technology competition seriously.

The objection is to blanket securitization that fails to distinguish between adversary access and allied collaboration. The NQI Reauthorization creates an opportunity to embed that distinction in statute rather than leaving it to BIS discretion — where mission creep is a documented feature, not a hypothetical risk.

Congress should include three provisions in the final bill:

**First, a statutory definition of "ally-permissive quantum collaboration."** The bill should define a category of quantum research activities — including joint publications, shared access to NSF-funded testbeds, and hardware component transfers — that are explicitly permitted with Five Eyes partners, EU allies, Japan, South Korea, and Israel without individual export licenses. The BIS License Exception IEC is a start; it needs statutory reinforcement and explicit coverage of academic research collaboration that the current deemed-export framework threatens.

**Second, an annual review requirement for quantum control classification breadth.** BIS classification decisions on quantum items should be subject to annual interagency review that includes NSF, OSTP, and representatives from the quantum research community — not just Commerce and national security agencies. The current framework allows controls to expand at BIS discretion without any mechanism for correcting overbreadth once it begins creating research chilling effects.

**Third, a statutory mandate for the National Quantum Coordination Office to track and report on international collaboration metrics.** Congress should require NQCO to report annually on the volume and origin of quantum research co-authorship, the number of international researchers participating in NQI-funded programs, and any documented instances of collaboration chilling attributable to export control enforcement. Without a transparency requirement, controls will expand by bureaucratic default — and the research community will have no mechanism to document the damage.

The goal is not to abandon controls — it is to prevent the BIS apparatus from gradually walling off the global research community whose participation is a core source of American scientific velocity. Scientific openness is not a vulnerability to be mitigated. It is a strategic asset that the NQI exists to leverage.

## What's Missing: A Commercialization Tax Incentive

The NQI Reauthorization is heavy on public R&D spending and light on incentivizing private risk capital to close the valley of death. The bill's testbeds and NSF centers are the right connective tissue, but federal facilities alone cannot replicate what market capital does: allocate resources to the most commercially promising hardware architectures, fund the manufacturing scale-up that transforms laboratory demonstrations into producible systems, and accept the commercial risk that government grants are structurally incapable of bearing.

The CHIPS and Science Act demonstrated how a targeted investment tax credit can crowd in private capital at scale. The [advanced manufacturing investment credit — a 25 percent tax credit](https://www.irs.gov/credits-deductions/advanced-manufacturing-investment-credit) for qualifying semiconductor manufacturing investments — has helped catalyze more than $540 billion in private semiconductor supply chain investments across more than 100 projects in 28 states. Private capital followed public incentive at a ratio that direct appropriations could not have achieved.

A targeted quantum manufacturing investment tax credit, modeled on the CHIPS credit, would do more to close the lab-to-market gap than federal testbeds alone. The credit should apply to qualifying investments in quantum hardware fabrication, cryogenic systems manufacturing, and quantum control electronics — precisely the supply chain nodes where Chinese domestic development is accelerating and American commercial production remains thin. Unlike direct grants, a tax credit rewards demonstrated private investment rather than grant-writing capability, and its cost scales with actual capital mobilization rather than appropriated funding levels.

The political window for adding such a provision to the NQI Reauthorization is now, before floor votes crystallize the bill's structure. Congressional staff on the Senate Commerce and House Science committees should examine whether the bill's commercialization mandate can be extended with a tax credit rider — or whether the provision belongs in accompanying tax legislation.

## The Case for Urgency

The NQI Reauthorization is arriving at a moment when the quantum technology competition is moving from the research phase into the applications phase. IBM's roadmap targets utility-scale quantum advantage in specific chemistry and materials simulation applications. Quantinuum and IonQ are executing commercial contracts. DARPA has launched dedicated quantum sensing programs. NASA's new authority under the reauthorization bill will open quantum satellite communications as an application domain with direct defense relevance.

The window for the United States to establish a durable commercialization infrastructure before competitors close the gap is not open indefinitely. China's 15th Five-Year Plan designates quantum as a priority industry. The EU Quantum Flagship has committed €1 billion over a decade with explicit commercialization milestones. South Korea, Japan, and Canada are all executing national quantum programs with industry partnership requirements that American programs have historically underweighted.

The NQI Reauthorization is the right response to this competitive moment. It should pass — quickly, cleanly, and with the three provisions identified above added to the floor text: a statutory ally-permissive framework for international collaboration, a private capital tax incentive for quantum hardware manufacturing, and an annual interagency review mechanism for export control breadth. These are not conditions; they are improvements that would make a good bill genuinely excellent.

Congress has bipartisan agreement on the core investment. That is rarer and more valuable than it may appear in the current environment. The question is whether the floor debate will sharpen the bill's openness framework or leave it soft — and whether the commercialization provision will get the private-capital lever it needs to actually close the gap the bill correctly identifies.

The votes are coming. Staff reading this brief should treat these provisions as live amendment targets, not aspirational addenda. The quantum technology competition is not waiting for the next legislative vehicle.
