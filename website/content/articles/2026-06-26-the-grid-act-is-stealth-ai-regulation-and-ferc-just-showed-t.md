On June 18, 2026, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission did something rare in Washington: it got the policy right. FERC issued tailored [Section 206 orders](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-launches-aggressive-targeted-action-speed-large-load-integration) to the nation's six regional grid operators — PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, ISO-NE, and NYISO — compelling each to justify or revise its pricing structures within 60 days and submit reliability reports on large-load interconnection capacity. The goal: dramatically accelerate the process by which AI data centers, manufacturing plants, and other large energy users connect to the electric grid. Rather than launching a multi-year rulemaking, FERC is forcing grid operators to act within months — compressing a review cycle that currently stretches beyond five years for interconnection applicants.

That same week, the [Hawley-Blumenthal GRID Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/3852/text) (S. 3852) continues advancing in the Senate, threatening to force every hyperscale data center off the grid or impose punishing financial penalties that critics estimate in the hundreds of billions of dollars in aggregate compliance costs. These two visions of data center energy policy are now on a collision course. One is market-oriented, supply-focused, and pro-growth. The other is an off-grid mandate dressed up as ratepayer protection — and it may be the most consequential anti-AI infrastructure legislation before Congress.

## The Correct Diagnosis: Interconnection, Not Prohibition

The rate pressure behind the GRID Act is real. [Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory](https://newscenter.lbl.gov/2025/01/15/berkeley-lab-report-evaluates-increase-in-electricity-demand-from-data-centers/) projects that data center electricity demand will grow from 176 TWh in 2023 — roughly 4.4% of total U.S. consumption — to as much as 580 TWh by 2028, or 12% of total U.S. consumption. Interconnection queues are backlogged with [nearly 2,300 gigawatts](https://emp.lbl.gov/publications/queued-2025-edition-characteristics) of generation and storage capacity stuck waiting for grid access, more than the country's entire installed generating capacity. That congestion creates real costs for existing ratepayers.

But the GRID Act misdiagnoses both the problem and its cure. Residential rate increases attributable to large-load interconnection are not driven by the mere fact that data centers use grid power. They are driven by inadequate transmission capacity and opaque cost-allocation rules that socialize upgrade costs across existing ratepayers rather than assigning them to the new load that triggered them. FERC's June 18 orders go directly at both failures: compel grid operators to justify their pricing structures, prevent cost shifts, and accelerate study processes — with cost-causation principles intact. Sunlight and speed. That is the correct intervention.

The GRID Act's response is to ban the load. That is not ratepayer protection. It is supply suppression.

## What the GRID Act Actually Does

The [Guaranteeing Rate Insulation from Data Centers Act](https://www.hawley.senate.gov/hawley-blumenthal-introduce-bill-to-prevent-data-centers-from-increasing-electricity-costs-for-americans/) targets new data centers with 20 MW or more of power demand, requiring them to either derive all energy from on-site generation or captive power plants, or pay "rate effect credits" offsetting any measured increase in residential rates attributable to their grid presence. Existing facilities face a 10-year compliance treadmill with escalating credit obligations. Violations carry civil penalties of up to $1 million per day.

The bill's architecture does not refine cost allocation; it penalizes grid connection itself. The [Center for Data Innovation](https://datainnovation.org/2026/02/the-grid-act-is-the-wrong-way-to-protect-consumers-from-price-spikes/) correctly identifies the mechanism's flaw: by treating the grid-connected data center as the problem rather than fixing the cost-allocation rules that allow upgrade expenses to be socialized, the GRID Act eliminates supply to solve a distribution problem. That is not the same as protecting ratepayers — it is an infrastructure tax masquerading as consumer protection.

## The DATA Act Shows a Better Path

The alternative is already drafted. Senator Cotton's [Decentralized Access to Technology Alternatives (DATA) Act](https://www.cotton.senate.gov/news/press-releases/cotton-introduces-bill-to-lower-energy-costs-for-arkansans/) creates a new category of "consumer-regulated electric utility" — a self-contained, grid-isolated power system built exclusively to serve new large loads. The DATA Act mandates nothing. It creates a voluntary regulatory pathway for facilities that want to go off-grid, freeing them from Federal Power Act provisions not designed for self-contained, on-site power systems. A House companion bill is already in preparation.

[AEI's analysis](https://www.aei.org/articles/the-upsides-and-downsides-of-senator-cottons-data-act/) of the DATA Act correctly identifies the key virtue: the voluntary opt-in framework respects legitimate differences in facility type, business model, and energy strategy, rather than imposing a one-size-fits-all mandate on infrastructure that took decades to build. The contrast with the GRID Act could not be sharper. One creates a compliance treadmill for existing, grid-integrated facilities with no viable transition path. The other creates a genuine on-ramp for the off-grid model — without coercing anyone onto it.

Opt-in beats mandate. Congress should prefer the DATA Act's voluntary pathway.

## AI Compute Is Strategic Infrastructure

Senators Hawley and Blumenthal have framed the GRID Act as protecting ordinary Americans from Big Tech's electricity bills. But the framing obscures the strategic stakes. The four hyperscale leaders — [Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft](https://about.bnef.com/insights/commodities/ai-data-center-build-advances-at-full-speed-five-things-to-know/) — are collectively investing more than $650 billion in 2026 alone to expand AI compute capacity, and [nearly three-quarters of new global data center capacity](https://about.bnef.com/insights/data-centers/ai-data-center-build-advances-at-full-speed-five-things-to-know/) under construction is being built in the United States. That concentration reflects American advantages in frontier models, advanced chips, private capital, and existing grid infrastructure. It is not self-sustaining under regulatory headwinds.

China understands what is at stake. Beijing is drafting a [$295 billion plan](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/china-drafts-295-billion-plan-to-build-a-national-ai-data-center-grid-running-on-80-percent-domestic-chips) for a nationwide AI computing grid running on 80% domestic chips, a direct challenge to American compute dominance at the infrastructure layer. If the GRID Act's off-grid mandate makes domestic data center development economically nonviable and pushes investment to jurisdictions without such constraints, it does not protect ratepayers. It exports American AI infrastructure — and the jobs, tax revenue, and strategic advantage that come with it — to adversaries and allies who impose no such burden.

Treating AI compute as a ratepayer liability rather than strategic infrastructure comparable to the Interstate Highway System or the broadband buildout is a category error with national security consequences. [ITIF's April 2026 analysis](https://itif.org/publications/2026/04/07/four-reasons-new-ai-data-centers-wont-overwhelm-the-electricity-grid/) argues compellingly that the grid challenge is manageable — a supply problem, not a load problem. The GRID Act responds to a supply problem by eliminating demand. That is backwards.

## The Case for Federal Preemption

The urgency extends beyond the GRID Act itself. More than 300 data center bills are active across 30 states in 2026, and the patchwork is calcifying fast. In Maine, the legislature passed a construction moratorium before Governor Mills [vetoed it](https://www.maine.gov/governor/mills/news/governor-mills-announces-decision-ld-307-2026-04-24) in April — but [ballot measure campaigns](https://www.multistate.us/insider/2026/5/7/voters-target-data-centers-with-local-and-statewide-ballot-measures) targeting data center development are already in motion, extending the threat through the 2027 election cycle. Across the country, incompatible state mandates are emerging on siting, power sourcing, cost contribution, and community benefit requirements.

This is a rare case where federal preemption serves innovation rather than constraining it. A FERC-led uniform national framework — one that enforces cost-causation principles, accelerates interconnection timelines, and sets a baseline for large-load cost allocation — would replace the emerging 50-state compliance maze with a single coherent set of rules. The [Ratepayer Protection Pledge](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/03/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-advances-energy-affordability-with-the-ratepayer-protection-pledge/), signed in March 2026 by Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft, OpenAI, Oracle, and xAI, demonstrates that hyperscalers are willing to make voluntary commitments on cost-causation. A FERC framework that builds on those commitments — with real enforcement teeth — is the logical next step.

## What Congress Should Do

FERC's 60-day clock is already running. The first major grid operator responses are due in mid-August, which means FERC's market-transparency intervention is about to produce actionable data on pricing structures and interconnection capacity. Congress should let that process work before legislating a solution to a problem the agency is actively addressing. In the meantime:

**Reject the GRID Act.** The off-grid mandate solves a cost problem by eliminating the supply — an infrastructure tax masquerading as consumer protection. The architecture is wrong, not merely the calibration.

**Pass the DATA Act.** The voluntary opt-in pathway respects business model diversity, creates genuine regulatory incentives for off-grid development, and does not coerce existing grid-integrated facilities into a decade-long compliance treadmill.

**Support FERC preemption.** A uniform federal framework on large-load cost allocation and interconnection acceleration will prevent the 300-bill state patchwork from calcifying into incompatible mandates that make domestic AI buildout impossible.

American AI leadership is not inevitable. It depends on the physical infrastructure — the data centers, transmission lines, and generation capacity — that turns model advances into deployed intelligence. That infrastructure is built on grid access, and its future depends on whether Washington treats it as a strategic asset or a ratepayer problem to be regulated away.

FERC answered that question correctly on June 18. Congress should follow its lead.
