On July 1, [Arizona became the first state](https://www.azfamily.com/2025/06/05/new-arizona-law-prevents-ai-making-health-insurance-denials/) to require that a licensed medical director personally review and sign every health insurance denial touching a medical-necessity determination, and [may not rely solely on recommendations from any other source](https://www.azleg.gov/legtext/57leg/1R/bills/HB2175H.pdf) — including an AI system. Indiana's near-identical mandate, [House Bill 1271](https://iga.in.gov/legislative/2026/bills/house/1271), enacted in March, takes effect the same day and bars insurers from using any automated tool as the sole basis to downcode a claim — reassign it to a lower-paying billing code — absent prior human review of the medical record. Louisiana's [Senate Bill 246](https://www.sheppard.com/insights/blogs/state-legislatures-consider-oversight-of-artificial-intelligence-in-health-insurance-decisions) would have gone further still, requiring a licensed physician to review all medical records behind any adverse determination; instead, it was [withdrawn from the Senate floor on May 28](https://www.legis.la.gov/legis/BillInfo.aspx?s=26RS&b=SB246&sbi=y) after clearing committee. Florida's companion bill, [HB 527](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2026/527), passed the House 108–0 before dying in the Senate Rules Committee in March. Two dead bills in two states are a reminder that the industry pushback this legislative template draws is real, even as the underlying model keeps spreading to new statehouses.

That's the pattern worth examining: not one state's law, but a legislative template now moving through a half-dozen capitols in a single session. And it rests on a theory of accountability that doesn't hold up.

## Two theories of accountability, one that actually bites

There are two distinct ways to make insurers answer for wrongful denials touching AI. The first is a **process mandate**: require a credentialed human to review and sign off before a denial goes out, on the theory that a person in the loop will catch what an algorithm gets wrong. The second is **outcome accountability**: let plaintiffs sue under existing bad-faith and unfair-claims-practices law when a denial turns out to have been unreasonable, and let discovery expose whether AI substituted for judgment.

Only the second actually tests what regulators say they care about — whether the denial was correct. A signature answers a different question: did a licensed person put their name on the file? Those are not the same question, and the gap between them is exactly where compliance theater lives.

This isn't hypothetical. A federal court in Minnesota recently [allowed discovery into UnitedHealth Group's use of an AI system called nH Predict](https://www.hunton.com/hunton-insurance-recovery-blog/court-allows-discovery-into-insurers-use-of-ai-to-deny-claims) to evaluate post-acute care claims, over the insurer's objection, in a bad-faith suit brought by [the estate of a deceased policyholder](https://natlawreview.com/article/court-allows-discovery-insurers-use-ai-deny-claims). The plaintiffs weren't asking a regulator to certify that a human had signed something. They were asking whether the AI system was, in the court's words, designed to supplant physician decision-making — and a jury may now get to see the answer. That is accountability tied to an actual outcome, tested through an adversarial process with real evidentiary stakes. It is the system working as designed, under law that predates any of these AI-specific statutes and needs no amendment to reach AI-assisted denials.

## The rubber-stamp problem

A "personally review and sign" requirement is trivially satisfiable without changing a single outcome if the reviewing physician faces the same caseload pressure and the same employer relationship that produced the disputed denial in the first place. A medical director reviewing hundreds of AI-flagged files a week, employed by the insurer whose loss ratio — the share of premium dollars paid out in claims — depends on denial volume, has every incentive to affirm the machine's recommendation and little practical capacity to second-guess it file by file.

That isn't a hypothetical failure mode — it's the identical dynamic that critics of "meaningful human review" requirements in other automated-decision contexts, from hiring algorithms to immigration adjudication, have pointed to for years: the mandate specifies that a human must act, not that the human must be positioned, incentivized, or resourced to actually catch errors. A signature requirement measures the presence of a name on a form. It says nothing about whether that name reflects independent judgment or a rubber stamp applied under caseload pressure.

None of this means AI-assisted claims processing is inaccurate at scale — that's an open empirical question, not one these statutes answer, and it's precisely why case-by-case litigation testing actual outcomes is the better instrument than a paperwork requirement applied uniformly regardless of whether any given system is working well or badly.

## Who actually pays the compliance cost

Large national carriers already run bench strength in licensed medical directors — that staffing model predates AI and exists to handle utilization review and appeals under decades-old regulatory regimes. For an incumbent processing millions of claims, adding an AI-touched-denial signature requirement is a modest incremental cost layered onto infrastructure that already exists.

For AI-native and digital-first insurers built around automated claims processing as their core value proposition, the same requirement is not incremental — it's a fixed per-claim compliance cost that scales directly against the volume advantage automation was supposed to provide. A challenger whose entire model is faster, cheaper claims handling through automation now has to staff physician sign-off at a rate proportional to the automation itself, which erodes precisely the efficiency gain that let it compete against incumbents on price. That is a barrier to entry dressed up as consumer protection, in a market where lowering entry costs — not raising them — is what disciplines incumbent insurers on price and service.

## What states should do instead

The NAIC's [Model Bulletin on the Use of Artificial Intelligence Systems by Insurers](https://content.naic.org/insurance-topics/artificial-intelligence), now adopted by [24 states and the District of Columbia](https://www.quarles.com/newsroom/publications/nearly-half-of-states-have-now-adopted-naic-model-bulletin-on-insurers-use-of-ai), already points toward the better model: governance documentation, bias testing, and explainability tied to market-conduct examination of actual outcomes, rather than a per-claim signature requirement. States weighing an Arizona-style bill this session don't need to invent something new — they need to direct resources toward enforcing the bad-faith and unfair-claims-practices standards already on their books, and toward examiner capacity to audit denial outcomes rather than denial paperwork. A signature scales with headcount. An outcome audit scales with risk. Legislators chasing a visible, easy-to-explain fix in an election year should recognize which one actually protects claimants — and which one just gives incumbents a new moat.
