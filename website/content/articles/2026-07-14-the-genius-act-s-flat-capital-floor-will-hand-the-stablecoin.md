Six federal agencies have four days left. Under the GENIUS Act's one-year rulemaking clock, the [OCC, Federal Reserve, FDIC, NCUA, and Treasury must finalize implementing rules for payment stablecoin issuers by July 18, 2026](https://www.sullcrom.com/insights/memo/2026/March/OCC-Proposes-Regulations-Implement-GENIUS-Act) — one year to the day after the statute's enactment, with FinCEN and OFAC's companion AML and sanctions rules due on the same clock. The OCC's proposal sets a [$5 million minimum capital floor for newly chartered stablecoin issuers during an initial three-year "de novo" period](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/occ-issues-proposal-to-implement-the-genius-act), alongside a requirement to hold 10 percent of reserves in same-day liquidity. The [FDIC has confirmed that deposits backing a stablecoin will be insured only to the issuer as a corporate depositor — not passed through to individual token holders](https://www.fdic.gov/news/press-releases/2026/fdic-approves-proposal-implement-genius-act-requirements-and-standards).

Both of those design choices are correct, and ThinkBot has said so before. A stablecoin issuer is not a bank; it should not get a bank's implicit government backstop. Full reserve backing, redemption rights, and the absence of an FDIC-style deposit-insurance subsidy are the prudential core the GENIUS Act was built to establish, and agencies should hold that line against the inevitable pressure — from consumer groups and eventually from issuers themselves — to treat stablecoin balances as insured deposits by another name. That would reintroduce exactly the moral hazard the statute was designed to avoid.

The problem is narrower and more fixable than "stablecoins are being over-regulated." It's that the OCC has translated a statute demanding *risk-calibrated* capital into a *flat-dollar floor* that falls hardest on the issuers who can least absorb it — and the agencies still have four days to fix the calibration, not the concept. This is the second time that exact mismatch has shown up in GENIUS Act implementation: ThinkBot flagged the same uniform-standard-over-risk-tiering problem in the FinCEN/OCC customer-identification rule on [July 8](/articles/2026-07-08-genius-act-kyc-rulemaking-stablecoin-oligopoly). Here it's the capital rule, not the KYC rule, but the pattern — a hard statutory deadline pushing agencies toward the simplest, most bank-like, most incumbent-favoring version of a rule — is becoming the through-line of this entire rulemaking cycle.

## The statute asked for risk-tailoring. The rule delivers a floor.

This is not a case of agencies inventing a capital requirement out of thin air. The GENIUS Act's text explicitly directs regulators to tailor capital standards "to the business model and risk profile" of each issuer, and gives primary federal regulators authority to differentiate among issuers "by category, taking into consideration their capital structure, riskiness, complexity, financial activities, size, and any other risk-related factors" they deem appropriate — language documented in the [Congressional Research Service's summary of the Act](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IN12553). The OCC's proposal even describes itself as "individualized" and case-by-case.

But baked into that individualized process is [an absolute floor of $5 million for any newly chartered issuer during its first three years](https://www.lw.com/en/insights/occ-issues-proposal-to-implement-the-genius-act), regardless of how small its issuance or how conservative its reserve composition. A startup issuer holding $10 million in outstanding stablecoins backed entirely by Treasury bills and central bank deposits — the lowest-risk reserve mix the statute permits — faces the same nominal capital requirement as one many times its size. That's not risk-tailoring; it's a minimum ticket price for entering the business at all, and it applies with equal force to the issuer whose risk profile the statute says should drive a *lower* number.

## Fixed costs are regressive by construction

The floor is the visible part of a larger compliance-cost structure that scales with the *fact* of being a regulated issuer, not with issuance volume. Treasury's BSA/AML rules for permitted payment stablecoin issuers require dedicated compliance officers, transaction-monitoring systems calibrated to on-chain flows, SAR filing infrastructure, and the technical capacity to freeze or block specific blockchain transactions — the same infrastructure, in substance, that costs the smallest community banks [11 to 15.5 percent of payroll and 16.5 to 22 percent of their budgets on compliance and data processing, roughly double the share the largest banks spend](https://www.csbs.org/too-small-scale-what-10-years-data-say-about-community-bank-compliance-costs), according to a decade of Conference of State Bank Supervisors survey data. Those costs don't scale down for a smaller issuer; one with $500 million outstanding bears roughly the same compliance overhead as one with $50 billion, on a fraction of the revenue to spread it across.

That math is exit-inducing for exactly the tier of issuer US stablecoin policy should want to keep in the market: mid-sized fintechs building payment products around stablecoin rails rather than running the settlement layer itself. Reporting on the pre-deadline compliance scramble already points to the mechanism in motion — [non-bank payment platforms facing a choice between raising dedicated capital to charter a stablecoin bank or exiting the space](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/319654/20260703/stablecoin-compliance-costs-land-july-18-mid-market-issuers-face-existential-math.htm), with acquisition activity among stablecoin-adjacent fintechs expected to pick up through Q3 2026. Issuers able to absorb bank-scale compliance costs — Circle, at roughly $75 billion in outstanding issuance, and Tether, at roughly $187 billion — are positioned to treat the new floor as a rounding error. The mid-tier issuers the market needs for genuine price and product competition are not.

This is a familiar pattern with a documented precedent, not a hypothetical one. [US commercial banks fell from roughly 14,500 institutions in the early 1980s to about 4,100 today](https://usafacts.org/articles/whats-behind-the-decline-in-us-banks/), a decline FDIC data show was driven overwhelmingly by mergers rather than failures after the early-1990s crisis passed. Regulators themselves have linked that consolidation to compliance costs that fall disproportionately on smaller institutions: the same CSBS analysis cited above concludes that "regulation is not the only culprit, but these data suggest it is a significant contributor" to shrinking bank ranks and near-vanished de novo formation. A capital and compliance regime modeled on bank regulation, applied to a market regulators want to keep competitive rather than concentrated, should expect the same result unless it's deliberately designed not to.

## Fewer issuers is a bad outcome on the merits

The GENIUS Act's premise is that a properly regulated stablecoin market is a *competitive alternative* to bank-intermediated payments — faster settlement, lower transaction costs, more experimentation in product design, all disciplined by redemption rights and reserve transparency rather than by a handful of incumbents' internal risk committees. A market that consolidates to two or three issuers by regulatory design defeats that premise before it's tested. Less issuer competition means less pressure on transaction fees and slower iteration on redemption mechanics — a dollar-stablecoin market that behaves less like an innovation showcase and more like the correspondent-banking oligopoly it was supposed to improve on.

## The floor is exportable — and other regimes are undercutting it

Dollar-denominated stablecoin issuance is not geographically fixed, and neither is the Treasury-bill demand that full reserve-backing requirements generate. Singapore's Monetary Authority requires base capital of the *greater* of S$1 million or 50 percent of annual operating expenses — a figure that scales with the issuer's actual size rather than sitting as a fixed floor. The UK is building a structurally tiered regime, with the [Bank of England overseeing systemically significant stablecoins and the FCA handling non-systemic issuers](https://www.wolterskluwer.com/en/expert-insights/the-genius-act-era) under a lighter-touch standard. Hong Kong's Stablecoins Ordinance sets a paid-up capital floor of [HK$25 million, roughly $3.2 million, for non-bank applicants](https://www.reedsmith.com/en/perspectives/2025/09/stablecoin-regime-in-full-swing-bite-sized-faqs) — meaningfully below the OCC's $5 million — with the Hong Kong Monetary Authority retaining discretion to scale requirements up for higher-risk issuers rather than imposing that ceiling on everyone up front.

None of these are deregulatory free-for-alls; all require licensing, reserve backing, and redemption guarantees. They simply calibrate the entry cost to issuer scale instead of setting one number for everyone. A challenger issuer priced out of the US market by a flat floor has somewhere else to go — and every dollar of stablecoin issuance that migrates is a dollar of Treasury-bill demand that migrates with it.

## The fix agencies can still make

Nothing here requires reopening the GENIUS Act or relitigating its reserve, redemption, or deposit-insurance provisions. It requires the OCC, in the four days before finalization, to replace the flat $5 million de novo floor with a tiered structure the statute already authorizes: capital requirements scaled to outstanding issuance volume and reserve asset composition, with a genuinely lower entry tier for issuers holding the lowest-risk reserve mix — cash, insured deposits, and short-dated Treasuries — at modest scale. That preserves every prudential guardrail this piece endorses while keeping the on-ramp open to more than the two or three issuers who can treat $5 million, and the compliance infrastructure behind it, as a rounding error. The statute asked for risk-based capital. The agencies still have time to deliver it.
