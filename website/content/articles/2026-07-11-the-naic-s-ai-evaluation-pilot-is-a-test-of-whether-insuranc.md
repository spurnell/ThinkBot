Insurance regulation runs on the market conduct exam — the periodic, often years-long process by which state examiners crawl through an insurer's files, procedures, and decision logic to check whether the company is treating policyholders fairly. It is slow, paper-heavy, and until recently had almost nothing to say about the machine learning models increasingly doing the underwriting, pricing, and claims-triage work inside the companies being examined. That gap is closing right now, in real time, in twelve states most people have never heard are running a live pilot.

Since January 2026, the National Association of Insurance Commissioners' Big Data and Artificial Intelligence (H) Working Group has been piloting an **AI Systems Evaluation Tool** — a standardized rubric that gives market-conduct and financial examiners a common method for reviewing how insurers govern their AI systems. The pilot expanded to its current twelve-state roster — California, Colorado, Connecticut, Florida, Iowa, Louisiana, Maryland, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Vermont, Virginia, and Wisconsin — in March 2026, and runs through September, after which the tool will be revised based on examiner feedback, re-exposed for comment, and — the NAIC expects — formally adopted at the Fall National Meeting in November ([Fenwick](https://www.fenwick.com/insights/publications/naic-expands-ai-systems-evaluation-tool-pilot-program-to-12-states-key-updates-for-insurers-and-ai-vendors-supporting-insurers)).

This has received almost no policy attention outside insurance-compliance trade press, which is a mistake. It is the first serious test of whether the industry's leading AI governance framework — the [NAIC Model Bulletin on the Use of Artificial Intelligence Systems by Insurers](https://content.naic.org/sites/default/files/cmte-h-big-data-artificial-intelligence-wg-ai-model-bulletin.pdf.pdf), adopted in December 2023 and now in force in some form in at least 24 states plus DC as of the NAIC's Spring 2026 National Meeting ([Mayer Brown](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2026/04/us-naic-spring-2026-national-meeting-highlights-innovation-cybersecurity-and-technology-h-committee-update)) — actually works when examiners sit down across the table from a carrier's compliance officer. Statutes and bulletins are cheap to write with restraint. Exam methodology is where restraint gets tested.

## What the Evaluation Tool actually does

The Model Bulletin was always principles-based: insurers using AI systems in decisions affecting consumers should maintain a written AI governance program, document the design and testing of models (including third-party and vendor models), conduct bias and outcome testing, and maintain human accountability over automated decisions. It did not specify algorithms, mandate pre-approval, or require disclosure of model weights. That restraint was the right call, and ThinkBot said so when the bulletin was first adopted.

The problem the NAIC now faces is translation: a principles-based bulletin is only as good as the examiner who has to operationalize it. Forty-some states adopting compatible language does not guarantee forty-some examiners applying it the same way — one examiner's "show me your governance documentation" can become another's open-ended fishing expedition through proprietary model internals. The Evaluation Tool is the NAIC's answer to that inconsistency risk. It is not a new rule; it is a rubric — four structured exhibits examiners use during existing market conduct, financial analysis, or financial exams ([Fenwick](https://www.fenwick.com/insights/publications/naic-expands-ai-systems-evaluation-tool-pilot-program-to-12-states-key-updates-for-insurers-and-ai-vendors-supporting-insurers)):

- **Exhibit A** quantifies where and how extensively AI is used across the organization's lines of business.
- **Exhibit B** is a governance risk-assessment framework — the heart of the tool, asking about board and executive oversight, documented risk management policies, and accountability structures.
- **Exhibit C** drills into systems the insurer itself has flagged or the examiner identifies as higher-risk, asking for more detail on design, testing, and monitoring.
- **Exhibit D** covers the data inputs feeding AI systems, including a newly added field on "reasonable accommodations or policy modifications" tied to consumer outcomes.

That structure is, in design, a governance-process audit: it asks whether a documented oversight structure exists, whether bias testing happened and what it found, whether a human is accountable for the decision loop — not whether the underwriting model itself produces outputs an examiner personally finds defensible. This is the correct instrument. A market-conduct exam is built to assess whether a company did what it said it would do and treated consumers fairly in the process; it was never built to certify the statistical merits of a proprietary actuarial model, and turning it into that kind of body would put examiners in the position of approving or rejecting risk-scoring methodology without the actuarial expertise, transparency into training data, or statutory mandate to do so.

## Why process-audit beats model-approval — and why it matters for who gets to compete

The alternative models were on the table when the NAIC first started work on AI oversight in 2023: algorithm disclosure regimes modeled loosely on parts of the EU's approach, or a pre-deployment sign-off requirement under which a regulator effectively certifies a model before it reaches the market. Both would functionally convert underwriting AI into a licensed-product category, with all the delay and ossification that implies. An underwriting model that has to clear a regulatory review before every material update is a model that gets updated less often, which is a strange thing to want from a technology whose main value proposition is that it can incorporate new loss data and correct for miscalibration faster than a traditional actuarial refiling cycle.

The compliance-cost asymmetry is the part state regulators should sit with longest. A national incumbent carrier already has a compliance department, a model risk management function built for Sarbanes-Oxley-adjacent financial exams, and outside counsel on retainer who have seen a dozen of these exams before. An insurtech entrant or a regional mutual writing auto or homeowners' business in a handful of states does not. If the Evaluation Tool stays a documentation-and-process audit — do you have a governance policy, did you test for bias, is there a human in the loop, can you show your work — that is a burden every insurer of reasonable size can meet with a competent compliance hire and a written policy. If examiner practice under the pilot drifts toward demanding granular disclosure of model architecture, training data provenance down to the feature level, or an implicit expectation that the examiner "approves" a model before the carrier can keep using it, the marginal cost of that drift falls almost entirely on the companies without existing infrastructure to absorb it. That is not a hypothetical concern about paperwork; it is a concrete channel by which examination methodology, not statute, ends up protecting incumbents from exactly the kind of AI-native competition that has begun putting real pricing pressure on legacy carriers in auto and homeowners' lines.

The industry's own trade associations are already flagging a version of this anxiety, if not in these exact terms. In a joint letter sent to the NAIC in December 2025, a coalition representing life, health, property-casualty, mutual, and reinsurance insurers objected that the pilot is "one-sided, voluntary for regulators while compulsory for companies," has no defined end point, and could expose participating carriers to enforcement risk based on "negative" findings gathered during what NAIC has described as a data-collection exercise rather than an exam ([InsuranceNewsNet](https://insurancenewsnet.com/innarticle/naic-regulators-prep-ai-evaluation-tool-for-use-in-2026-as-industry-balks)). That is a narrower complaint than a general documentation-burden argument, but it points at the same underlying risk: a pilot that lacks hard guardrails on how findings get used, or how long the "pilot" label lasts, is a pilot that can harden into de facto pre-clearance without anyone voting on it. The open question the NAIC needs to answer before adoption is whether the final tool builds in those guardrails, or leaves the question of enforcement use to whichever examiner happens to be running a given company's exam.

Nothing about preserving that line requires examiners to have zero discretion. Some judgment is inherent to any exam — an examiner has to decide whether documentation is adequate, whether testing was rigorous enough, whether a governance structure is real or performative. The risk this brief is flagging is narrower and more specific: discretion calcifying, informally and without public articulation, into an unwritten norm that carriers must clear an examiner's comfort level with a model's internal logic before continuing to use it. That is pre-clearance in substance, however the exam report labels it, and it is exactly the regime the industry feared when the Big Data Working Group first started drafting AI rules.

## Convergence is the story worth protecting — not fragmentation

It's tempting to read 24-plus states adopting variants of the same bulletin as the familiar state-patchwork problem that shows up in nearly every other tech policy fight. That framing undersells what actually happened here. Insurance regulation is state-based by design under the McCarran-Ferguson Act, and the realistic alternative to state adoption of a shared model bulletin was never a single federal AI-insurance statute — it was fifty independent, uncoordinated state approaches. Getting half the country's insurance regulators to converge on one text, coordinated through an NAIC working group, is closer to a policy success story than a fragmentation story. Some genuine variation exists at the edges — Colorado's [SB 21-169-linked quantitative testing requirements](https://doi.colorado.gov/for-consumers/sb21-169-protecting-consumers-from-unfair-discrimination-in-insurance-practices) for life insurers and New York's [DFS Circular Letter No. 7 (2024)](https://www.dfs.ny.gov/industry-guidance/circular-letters/cl2024-07) both layer additional specifics on top of the baseline bulletin language — but the core governance architecture is shared, and that convergence is worth protecting rather than treating as background noise.

The reason this matters for the pilot specifically: convergence at the level of adopted text does nothing to guarantee convergence at the level of examiner practice. Twelve states are about to generate twelve independent sets of examiner experience applying the same four exhibits. If those experiences diverge sharply — some states treating Exhibit C's high-risk-system questions as an invitation to demand proprietary detail, others keeping it at the governance-documentation level the bulletin's text supports — the pilot will have quietly re-fragmented outcomes even while every state points to the same adopted bulletin. The NAIC's own monthly coordination calls among pilot states are the right mechanism to catch this before it hardens ([Fenwick](https://www.fenwick.com/insights/publications/naic-expands-ai-systems-evaluation-tool-pilot-program-to-12-states-key-updates-for-insurers-and-ai-vendors-supporting-insurers)); whether they actually converge examiner judgment, or just compare notes without correcting outliers, is the thing to watch between now and September.

## The line regulators already have authority to enforce

None of this counsels ignoring bias. Regulators have long-standing, uncontroversial authority to police proxy discrimination and actuarial unfairness — pricing or underwriting variables that function as an illegal protected-class proxy, or risk classifications that aren't actuarially justified. That authority predates AI and doesn't need the Evaluation Tool to exist; it needs to be enforced well, which the bulletin's bias-testing documentation requirement is meant to support by giving examiners a paper trail to check against. A finding that a company skipped bias testing altogether, or that its testing surfaced a disparate-impact signal it did nothing about, is squarely inside the governance-process audit this tool is built for.

What examiners should not do, and what the pilot needs explicit guardrails against, is treating a legitimate underwriting variable an examiner is simply uncomfortable with — a variable with a defensible actuarial basis that happens to correlate with a protected characteristic through ordinary risk factors like geography or credit-linked behavior — as equivalent to a governance failure. Those are two different findings requiring two different remedies, and one health-insurance data point worth taking seriously here: the NAIC's own Big Data and Artificial Intelligence Working Group has found that nearly a third of health insurers still don't regularly test their models for bias at all ([Fenwick](https://www.fenwick.com/insights/publications/tracking-the-evolution-of-ai-insurance-regulation)) — a governance-process gap the tool is well-suited to catch, distinct from a dispute over whether a specific variable belongs in a rating plan.

## Recommendation

The NAIC and the twelve pilot states should publish pilot findings in a form that makes this distinction explicit and auditable — separating, in whatever summary report follows the September close-out, findings characterized as "governance process was inadequate" (missing documentation, no bias testing, no accountable human function) from anything that amounts to "we didn't like this underwriting variable or model output." The first category is the Evaluation Tool doing its job. The second, if it appears in exam findings without separate statutory basis, is the tell that examiner discretion is metastasizing past what the Model Bulletin authorizes — and state legislators watching this pilot before drafting their own AI-in-insurance bills should treat that distinction, not the state-adoption count, as the metric that tells them whether the principles-based model is holding.
