On April 7, a major commercial AI portfolio cleared its [FedRAMP Authorization](https://newsroom.ibm.com/2026-04-01-IBM-Expands-FedRAMP-Portfolio-with-Authorization-of-11-Software-Solutions,-Including-watsonx) for federal use — a genuine milestone in the government's effort to bring capable AI systems into agency operations. One day later, the comment period on a GSA draft contract clause remained open. Read together, those two facts describe a policy contradiction the administration has not yet resolved.

[GSAR 552.239-7001](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/03/gsas-proposed-ai-clause-a-deep-dive), the General Services Administration's proposed standard contract clause for AI procurements, would impose government data ownership over AI inputs and outputs, bar contractors from using government data to improve their models, and require affirmative certifications that create meaningful [False Claims Act](https://www.justice.gov/civil/false-claims-act) exposure. Industry groups from SIIA to NetChoice have [filed formal warnings](https://www.nextgov.com/acquisition/2026/04/trade-and-industry-groups-warn-risks-gsas-draft-ai-procurement-guidance/412614/). The comment record is building. The clause, as drafted, would accomplish the opposite of its stated goal.

The administration has spent the past year insisting that AI modernization across the federal enterprise is a strategic imperative. [OMB Memorandum M-25-21](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-21-Accelerating-Federal-Use-of-AI-through-Innovation-Governance-and-Public-Trust.pdf) directed every agency to maximize use of commercially available AI. [M-25-22](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-22-Driving-Efficient-Acquisition-of-Artificial-Intelligence-in-Government.pdf) followed with a companion memo on efficient AI acquisition. [Executive Order 14179](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/01/removing-barriers-to-american-leadership-in-artificial-intelligence/) declared the removal of barriers to American AI leadership a national priority. GSAR 552.239-7001, if finalized as written, would erect exactly the kind of barrier those documents were designed to tear down — only this one would live inside government contracting vehicles, invisible to most AI policy observers, quietly poisoning the vendor ecosystem the government depends on.

Procurement design is AI policy. The GSA clause, if it calcifies into the default template, is a de facto veto on capable AI participation in the federal market.

## What the Clause Actually Does

GSAR 552.239-7001 contains five provisions that, in combination, alter the commercial calculus for AI vendors in ways that legacy software clauses never did.

**Government data ownership.** The clause asserts government ownership not merely over data the government provides to contractors — a defensible position — but over AI outputs and "Custom Developments" derived from that data. For traditional software, this maps cleanly: the government pays for code, it owns the code. For AI systems, the distinction between input, training signal, model behavior, and output is neither clean nor static. A language model that processes government documents does not produce government-owned outputs in any technically meaningful sense; it produces probabilistic inferences shaped by a training regime built over years. Claiming ownership of those outputs without distinguishing them from the underlying model is not a data-protection measure — it is a structural IP acquisition dressed in procurement language.

**Blanket prohibition on model improvement.** The clause bars contractors from using government data to improve their AI models. The policy intuition is understandable: agencies have legitimate interests in preventing sensitive government information from leaking into commercial training pipelines. But the clause applies this restriction as a blanket prohibition, without any tie to data classification, sensitivity level, or contractual data category. The effect is to prohibit responsible use of non-sensitive, unclassified data alongside genuinely sensitive material — an overreach that bears no relationship to the government data-protection interest the restriction is meant to serve.

**The "Custom Developments" IP grab.** The clause's definition of "Custom Developments" is broad enough to capture model fine-tuning, workflow integrations, retrieval systems, and other AI configurations that vendors build across multiple clients and treat as core commercial infrastructure. If those configurations become government property whenever they touch a federal contract, the commercial model that makes frontier AI investment possible collapses. [Legal analysts](https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/ai-for-government-7-days-for-contractor-comments-on-gsa-proposed-contract-clause-for-ai-systems) have flagged this as among the clause's most significant drafting problems.

**Foreign-AI prohibition.** The clause imposes restrictions on AI systems of foreign origin that go beyond existing security screening frameworks. The government has legitimate national security interests in AI procurement — but [FedRAMP Authorization](https://www.fedramp.gov/) and FISMA-based security requirements (the Federal Information Security Management Act governs how agencies assess and authorize federal IT systems) already screen for exactly those risks. A blanket foreign-origin prohibition that operates independently of security assessment is not a security measure; it is protectionism that forecloses commercial options without a corresponding security rationale.

**The "any lawful government purpose" use right.** The clause grants the government a use right covering any lawful government purpose, without scope limitation. For outputs produced under a discrete contract, that right is difficult to operationalize and potentially boundless in its downstream implications. Vendors entering federal contracts cannot model their exposure under a rights grant with no defined boundary.

## The Vendor Deterrence Problem

Consider the position of any commercially capable AI provider evaluating a federal contract under GSAR 552.239-7001 as drafted. The provider must: accept government ownership over outputs and Custom Developments; agree to a blanket prohibition on using government data for model improvement, regardless of classification; grant a boundless use right to the government; and make affirmative certifications about AI use that, as discussed below, create litigation exposure the provider cannot quantify.

The alternative is to exit the federal market.

For frontier AI providers — the vendors whose capabilities the administration most wants in federal operations — this calculus is not abstract. These are companies with commercial products, large non-government customer bases, and boards that can identify IP and legal risk. If federal contracts require accepting government data ownership and a training ban while commercial contracts do not, the federal market becomes systematically less attractive to the most capable providers. The government retains vendors willing to accept those terms, which, in a competitive AI market, means the government retains second-tier solutions.

The April 7 FedRAMP clearance is a useful data point — not because of the specific vendor involved, but because of what it signals about market timing. [FedRAMP Authorization](https://marketplace.fedramp.gov/) is a demanding, expensive process that commercial AI providers undertake specifically to qualify for government contracts. Vendors completing that process now are making multi-year bets on federal market participation. If GSAR 552.239-7001 stands as drafted, those bets hit a wall at the contracting stage — and the government's investment in expanding the pool of FedRAMP-qualified AI providers yields nothing.

This is the contradiction at the center of the administration's AI posture. [M-25-22](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-22-Driving-Efficient-Acquisition-of-Artificial-Intelligence-in-Government.pdf) directs agencies to acquire AI efficiently and leverage commercial capabilities. GSAR 552.239-7001 would require those same agencies to offer contract terms that commercially capable vendors cannot accept.

## The False Claims Act Trap

The clause's certification requirements create a second, distinct problem that has received less attention than the IP provisions but may prove more consequential for day-to-day AI adoption.

The [False Claims Act](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title31-section3729&num=0&edition=prelim) imposes liability on contractors who submit false claims or make false certifications to the federal government. It is enforced through civil penalties, treble damages, and qui tam provisions that allow private parties to sue on the government's behalf. For established product categories — software licenses, hardware specifications, service-level agreements — contractors know what they are certifying and can verify compliance before submitting claims. The FCA functions reasonably well in that environment.

AI systems are not established product categories. The definition of "AI use" in a federal contract is not settled. The boundary between a vendor's AI systems and conventional software components is contested. What constitutes a "Custom Development" subject to government ownership is, as noted above, genuinely ambiguous. GSAR 552.239-7001 requires contractors to make affirmative certifications about AI use in an environment where the underlying technical and legal definitions are unresolved.

The result is predictable: contractors uncertain whether their AI deployments fit within the clause's definitions will either over-certify (accepting liability they cannot bound) or under-certify (avoiding AI deployment to minimize exposure). [Industry observers](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/acquisition-policy/2026/03/gsas-new-ai-clause-drives-contractors-to-sound-the-alarm/) have flagged this dynamic explicitly in their comments on the clause. The certification architecture, as drafted, does not accelerate AI adoption — it gives agencies' own contractors a structural incentive to avoid AI tools entirely.

This is not an argument against accountability in federal AI contracting. Clear performance standards, audit rights, and incident reporting obligations are legitimate governance tools. The problem is the specific mechanism: FCA-backed certification mandates applied to novel technical questions where no established compliance standard exists. Accountability frameworks work when the underlying obligations are legible. They backfire when compliance is genuinely unknowable at signing.

## What Good AI Procurement Policy Looks Like

The government has legitimate interests in AI procurement. It needs to protect sensitive data. It needs operational continuity when vendors exit or change products. It needs usable rights to the outputs it funds. None of those interests requires the structural IP acquisition approach embedded in GSAR 552.239-7001.

A better framework would do four things differently.

**Perpetual license to outputs, not ownership.** The government's operational interest is the right to use outputs it commissions — indefinitely, across agencies, without vendor permission. That interest is fully served by a perpetual, irrevocable, government-wide license. Asserting ownership is not merely redundant with that interest; it extends into territory — training capacity, model improvement, IP encumbrance — that has no operational justification and significant commercial deterrence effects.

**Data-use restrictions tied to sensitivity categories.** Prohibitions on using government data for model improvement should track the government's actual data-protection interests. Classified information, controlled unclassified information, personal data, and law enforcement data each carry distinct sensitivity profiles with distinct statutory frameworks governing their use. A blanket prohibition that treats all government data identically applies the most restrictive rule universally, regardless of whether the underlying data warrants it. Targeted restrictions by classification category accomplish the government's security objective without penalizing responsible use of non-sensitive material.

**Audit rights and clear performance standards instead of FCA-triggering certifications.** Vendor accountability in AI procurement can be achieved through inspection rights, technical standards for system documentation and explainability, incident reporting obligations, and performance benchmarks. These tools provide meaningful oversight without creating litigation exposure for novel definitional questions. The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) offers a vocabulary for those standards that has already achieved broad industry adoption and could anchor procurement accountability requirements without the FCA exposure problem.

**FedRAMP and FISMA security standards as the foreign-AI screening mechanism.** The government's national security interest in AI procurement is real. FedRAMP exists precisely to assess whether cloud-based products — including AI systems — meet federal security requirements. Layering a foreign-origin prohibition on top of those frameworks does not add security; it adds supply-chain restriction by national origin independent of actual security risk. The [FedRAMP Marketplace](https://marketplace.fedramp.gov/) is the appropriate screening mechanism. Vendors that pass it should be eligible to compete.

## The Template Problem

Federal procurement clauses are not one-off determinations. When GSA establishes a standard clause for a product category, it becomes the default template across agencies, incorporated by reference into thousands of contracts, interpreted and applied by contracting officers who lack the technical background to evaluate whether a given clause makes sense for AI systems specifically. The GSAR clause, if finalized as drafted, does not merely affect the contracts into which it is directly inserted — it establishes the IP and data-rights framework for federal AI procurement for years or decades.

[SIIA](https://www.siia.net/siia-comments-on-gsa-proposed-ai-clause-gsar-552-239-7001/) and [NetChoice](https://netchoice.org/netchoice-comments-for-the-record-cautioning-the-gsa-against-onerous-ai-procurement-rules/), among others, have made this point in their formal comments: the clause reflects legacy software contracting logic that maps poorly onto AI systems, and if it hardens into standard practice before the comment record corrects it, the government will be operating under a procurement framework mismatched to its technology environment for a generation.

The comment period is still open. The GSA should hear from the full range of stakeholders — federal technology policy staff, agency CIOs, legal counsel, and AI developers — that the clause's structural IP acquisition approach, blanket training prohibition, and ambiguous certification requirements represent the wrong design pattern for federal AI. The government's legitimate data-protection and operational continuity interests can be achieved through targeted license frameworks, classification-sensitive data-use restrictions, and accountability standards grounded in clear technical definitions.

The administration's AI modernization agenda depends on capable vendors participating in the federal market. Procurement design is not a downstream implementation detail — it is the mechanism by which that agenda either succeeds or fails at scale. Getting the contract clause right is, in the most direct sense, AI policy.
