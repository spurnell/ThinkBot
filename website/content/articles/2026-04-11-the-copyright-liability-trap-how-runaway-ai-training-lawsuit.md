On March 18, 2026, music rights company [BMG filed suit against Anthropic](https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-news/bmg-anthropic-copyright-lawsuit-justin-bieber-bruno-mars-1235533474/), alleging infringement of 493 copyrighted works — lyrics from Bruno Mars, the Rolling Stones, Ariana Grande, and dozens of others — used in training its Claude models. BMG is seeking up to $150,000 per work in statutory damages, putting maximum theoretical exposure above $73 million from this suit alone. The filing came weeks after Anthropic received preliminary court approval for [*Bartz v. Anthropic*](https://legalblogs.wolterskluwer.com/copyright-blog/the-bartz-v-anthropic-settlement-understanding-americas-largest-copyright-settlement/), a $1.5 billion class action settlement — the largest copyright settlement in American legal history — covering nearly half a million books allegedly drawn from pirated shadow libraries. Six additional opt-out suits remain active. According to [Norton Rose Fulbright's 2026 AI copyright litigation tracker](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/ce8eaa5f/ai-in-litigation-series-an-update-on-ai-copyright-cases-in-2026), the docket now includes major actions against OpenAI, Meta, and Midjourney, with diverging rulings accumulating across circuits.

The conventional framing of this litigation wave is straightforward: AI companies built billion-dollar businesses on other people's creative work without permission, and creators deserve their cut. That framing has genuine moral force. But it conceals a structural problem policymakers have not adequately confronted. The copyright liability regime emerging from this litigation is calibrated not against creator harm but against market access. If it hardens into settled law, it will not redistribute value from AI companies to artists. It will consolidate the AI market in the hands of companies large enough to survive it — and functionally ban open-source AI development in the process.

That is not copyright justice. It is regulatory capture via litigation.

## The Settlement as Competitive Moat

The $1.5 billion Bartz settlement is painful for Anthropic. It is not existential. A company valued at [$61.5 billion](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-22/anthropic-raises-4-billion-from-amazon-at-61-5-billion-valuation) can absorb the cost, embed it as a line item, negotiate blanket licensing deals for future training data, and continue building frontier models. The same liability structure applied to a Series A startup or an open-source project is a death sentence.

This asymmetry is the central structural fact of the AI copyright debate, and it is almost entirely absent from public discourse. When commentators say big AI companies "should just pay" for the content they used, they are implicitly endorsing a world where only companies large enough to pay can legally operate. That is a tollbooth, not a marketplace. The transaction cost of negotiating per-work licensing at scale is prohibitive — not slightly inconvenient, but categorically prohibitive — for any organization without a legal department, a licensing budget, and an established relationship with the major rights clearinghouses.

BMG's demand structure illustrates this precisely. Statutory damages of [$150,000 per work](https://www.billboard.com/pro/bmg-ai-song-copyright-lawsuit-sues-claude-maker-anthropic/) are not calibrated to actual harm — they are a litigation weapon. The question is not whether BMG can demonstrate $73 million in demonstrable economic damage from Claude's training process. It cannot. The question is whether Anthropic can afford to litigate through trial. This is how barriers to entry get built in courtrooms rather than through regulatory text: a sufficiently credible damages demand makes settlement cheaper than adjudication, regardless of the underlying legal merits.

## Training Is Not Reproduction: The Fair Use Analysis Courts Should Apply

The core legal question is whether AI training on copyrighted material constitutes infringement at all. Under a faithful application of existing doctrine, the answer should be no — or at minimum, yes-but-with-transformative-fair-use as a complete defense.

The fair use framework established in [*Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music* (1994)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/510/569/) asks whether a use transforms the original work — whether it adds "new expression, meaning, or message" — rather than merely superseding it. The Court was explicit that transformative uses "lie at the heart of the fair use doctrine's guarantee of breathing space." AI training is a paradigmatic case. A language model does not store lyrics; it extracts statistical relationships across a vast corpus of text and encodes them as billions of floating-point weights in a neural network. The expressive content of Bruno Mars's lyrics is no more preserved in Claude's parameters than the plot of a novel is preserved in the neural weights of a human who read it. The output of a model trained on lyrics is a probability distribution, not a lyric.

The Second Circuit applied exactly this logic to a comparably large-scale use in [*Authors Guild v. Google* (2015)](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca2/13-4829/13-4829-2015-10-16.html). Google had digitized tens of millions of books without permission and made them searchable, displaying snippets in results. The court found this to be fair use: the purpose was highly transformative, the display was limited, and the digitization did not function as a market substitute for the originals. The court explicitly rejected the argument that Google's commercial nature foreclosed the analysis. If mass digitization of books for a search index is fair use, the case for AI training — which does not even display the underlying text — is considerably stronger.

Yet courts are not converging on this analysis. A [Reed Smith assessment](https://www.reedsmith.com/en/perspectives/2025/07/a-new-look-fair-use-anthropic-meta-copyright-ai-training) of the Bartz and *Kadrey v. Meta* rulings found that the two courts took "vastly different approaches" to evaluating market harm from AI training — the most contested of the four fair use factors. Judge Alsup in the Meta case was skeptical of market substitution concerns; Judge Chhabria in the Anthropic case called the risk of LLM-generated substitutes "unprecedented." These are not minor doctrinal nuances. They are foundational disagreements about what harm copyright is designed to prevent.

This divergence matters enormously because of how statutory damages work. BMG's suit requires no showing of actual harm — only a showing of infringement, after which the court may award up to $150,000 per work. Whether courts accept the transformative use argument determines not just whether training is legal, but whether the entire damages structure is theoretically applicable.

## Circuit Divergence Creates Dangerous Uncertainty

The AI copyright litigation ecosystem is generating irreconcilable precedents across district courts, with no appellate resolution in sight. [Norton Rose Fulbright's review of six key 2026 rulings](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/ce8eaa5f/ai-in-litigation-series-an-update-on-ai-copyright-cases-in-2026) found that the central questions — whether training constitutes infringement, what market harm is cognizable, whether use of pirated training data changes the analysis — are being decided inconsistently by courts that are making AI policy from the bench.

This does not produce legal clarity. It produces legal paralysis. Faced with uncertain exposure across jurisdictions, AI developers make conservative legal bets: restrict training data to licensed or public-domain sources, avoid open datasets with uncertain provenance, decline to release model weights that could expose them to secondary liability claims, and limit fine-tuning options for downstream users. These are not hypothetical concerns — they are the predictable response to litigation risk. And the developers with the greatest incentive to make those conservative bets are not the large incumbents, who can buy their way out of uncertainty through licensing, but mid-tier labs, university researchers, and open-source developers who cannot.

The result is a landscape in which legal certainty is a resource distributed according to ability to pay. That is the inverse of what copyright law is supposed to accomplish.

## Open-Source AI Is the Real Casualty

The stakes of this litigation extend far beyond any individual company. The [Hugging Face open-source model ecosystem](https://huggingface.co/blog/huggingface/state-of-os-hf-spring-2026) now hosts hundreds of thousands of model variants, and independent developers have grown from 17% to 39% of all model downloads between 2022 and 2025, at times accounting for more than half of total usage. Open-weight models from Meta, Mistral, and others — and the vast community of fine-tuned derivatives built on top of them — have democratized AI capability in ways that closed API-only systems cannot replicate. Researchers, nonprofits, governments, and small developers depend on open models to build AI applications without being subject to the pricing, terms of service, and access restrictions of proprietary providers.

A legal regime requiring per-work licensing for training data does not inconvenience this ecosystem. It eliminates it. Open-weight models cannot monetize their data relationships. There is no revenue stream to fund a licensing deal, no legal department to negotiate it, and no corporate counterparty to sign it. A finding that training requires licensing is functionally a ban on open-source frontier AI development — not because courts intend this result, but because the economics of compliance are categorically incompatible with the open-source model.

The geopolitical dimension reinforces the urgency. Chinese open-source models — including Alibaba's Qwen family, which [reached approximately 700 million downloads as of January 2026](https://english.news.cn/20260113/004b0522f987475cbf83ffc3a8d009aa/c.html) and surpassed Meta's Llama in cumulative downloads — are developed under a legal framework that imposes no equivalent constraints. A US copyright ruling that makes domestic open-source AI legally untenable would not eliminate open-weight frontier models; it would shift their development to jurisdictions beyond the reach of US copyright law. American researchers and developers would lose access to the most capable open models. The beneficiaries would not be American creators — they would be Chinese AI labs.

## The Legislative Fix: A Training-Data Safe Harbor

Courts are not well-positioned to resolve this policy question. The four-factor fair use test was not designed to analyze the systemic effects of a new technology on an innovation ecosystem; it was designed to adjudicate specific disputes about specific uses. The doctrinal divergence accumulating in district courts reflects genuine ambiguity in how existing law maps onto AI training — not bad judging. Congress needs to act before that ambiguity resolves into precedent that locks in the wrong answer.

The appropriate legislative response is a narrow statutory safe harbor for non-expressive, transformative uses of copyrighted works in AI training — modeled on [DMCA Section 512](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/512), which created conditional immunity for online service providers tied to takedown compliance and transparency obligations. Section 512 is an imperfect analogy — its notice-and-takedown mechanism does not map cleanly onto training pipelines — but its structural logic applies directly: conditional immunity paired with transparency requirements threads the needle between creator interests and innovation access.

A training-data safe harbor should include, at minimum:

1. **A non-expressive use standard.** The safe harbor should apply to uses that do not reproduce expressive content — that is, uses where the copyrighted work serves as a training signal rather than an output. This excludes cases where a model is specifically designed to reproduce protected works verbatim.

2. **Training data provenance disclosure.** As a condition of eligibility, developers should be required to maintain and make available a description of the datasets used to train production models, at a level of specificity sufficient for rights holders to assess whether their works were included. This does not require disclosing proprietary data — it requires transparency about categories and sources.

3. **An opt-out mechanism with prospective effect.** Rights holders who wish to exclude their works from future training should have a technically practicable mechanism to do so, with safe harbor protection conditional on honoring documented opt-outs going forward. Crucially, this opt-out should operate prospectively — not as a retroactive licensing requirement that would render past training retroactively infringing.

4. **No compulsory licensing mandate.** The White House's [National AI Policy Framework](https://www.sullcrom.com/insights/memo/2026/March/White-House-Releases-National-Policy-Framework-AI) suggests Congress consider enabling voluntary collective licensing frameworks. Voluntary collective licensing — modeled on the music industry's ASCAP/BMI structure — could provide an efficient mechanism for rights holders who wish to be compensated. But participation should be voluntary, not a precondition for legally operating. Compulsory licensing converts a copyright question into a tax on AI development, with rates set by litigation rather than markets.

Congressional proposals currently in circulation — including a [bipartisan bill by Representatives Dean and Moran](https://dean.house.gov/2026/1/ean-moran-introduce-bipartisan-bill-to-protect-creators-from-unauthorized-ai-training) and the [TRAIN Act](https://iapp.org/news/a/us-sen-blackburn-proposes-ai-framework-to-protect-children-copyrights) — focus primarily on disclosure and creator protection rather than safe harbor creation. These are useful starting points, but disclosure without immunity merely enables litigation; it does not resolve the structural problem. The right legislative package pairs transparency requirements with conditional safe harbor protection.

## The Stakes

The AI copyright question will be largely settled by the courts before most legislators finish reading the briefs. Rulings in the remaining active cases — *In Re OpenAI*, *Disney v. Midjourney*, and the new BMG action — could arrive in Q2-Q3 2026. If those decisions converge on the wrong answer — that AI training requires licensing, that per-work statutory damages are available, that pirated training data poisons the entire fair use analysis — the window for legislative correction will narrow significantly. Precedent has inertia.

There is a version of this story where AI companies pay their fair share, creators are compensated, and the market remains open. That version requires voluntary licensing frameworks, transparent training data practices, and a fair use doctrine that accurately reflects the transformative nature of the training process. It does not require a liability regime that converts copyright into a barrier accessible only to companies that can afford billion-dollar settlements.

The version currently emerging from the courts is the other one: a tollbooth at the entry to the AI market, staffed by the companies large enough to have paid to build it. Congress should recognize that outcome for what it is — and act before it becomes settled law.
