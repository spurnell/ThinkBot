On July 6, 2026, Illinois Gov. JB Pritzker signed [SB 315, the AI Safety Measures Act](https://ilga.gov/Legislation/BillStatus/FullText?DocName=10400SB0315enr&DocNum=315&DocTypeID=SB&GAID=18&LegDocId=197587&LegID=157797&Session=&SessionID=114&SpecSess=), making Illinois the first state to require [annual third-party audits of frontier AI developers](https://capitolnewsillinois.com/news/pritzker-signs-landmark-ai-regulation-bill-that-aims-to-mitigate-risks/). The mandate applies to any company that trains a model using more than 10²⁶ floating-point operations, with the audit requirement itself falling on "large frontier developers" — those clearing $500 million in annual revenue. It is, by design, a compliance regime: disclosure of safety frameworks, mandatory incident reporting, and an independent audit, all imposed on the basis of what a model *could* do, not what it has been shown to do in the field.

Halfway across the country, Utah has been running the opposite experiment for far longer than Illinois has had a bill. The state's Office of AI Policy signed its first supervised-deployment agreement in December 2024 — [a mental-wellness chatbot for public-school students](https://commerce.utah.gov/2024/12/02/news-release-utah-department-of-commerces-office-of-artificial-intelligence-announces-first-regulatory-mitigation-agreement/). Since January 2026 it has [added an AI service that renews prescriptions for chronic-condition patients](https://commerce.utah.gov/2026/01/06/news-release-utah-and-doctronic-announce-groundbreaking-partnership-for-ai-prescription-medication-renewals/), an AI-assisted psychiatric medication-renewal pilot, and an AI-assisted dental diagnostic tool to its supervised roster, and in February 2026 it stood up a task force whose explicit job is to turn what regulators observed in those pilots into future rules. Nobody in Utah had to prove anything before deploying. Nobody in Illinois will be allowed to deploy anything meaningfully differently than they already do — the law just adds a compliance layer on top, whether or not the underlying system has generated any evidence of harm.

Both states are now several months into live, comparable natural experiments in AI governance design. It is worth taking that comparison seriously, because most of the 2026 state AI legislative wave has treated "regulate now, learn later" and "learn first, regulate from what you learn" as interchangeable postures. They are not, and the two states' track records so far show why.

## Two live models, concretely contrasted

Illinois' approach is blanket and ex-ante. SB 315's core mechanics apply uniformly to every developer that clears its compute and revenue thresholds, regardless of what any specific model deployment has actually done: an annual third-party audit, [72-hour incident reporting and Attorney General enforcement carrying civil penalties of up to $1 million for a first offense and $3 million for repeat violations](https://capitolnewsillinois.com/news/pritzker-signs-landmark-ai-regulation-bill-that-aims-to-mitigate-risks/), plus [whistleblower protections for employees who raise safety concerns](https://gov-pritzker-newsroom.prezly.com/gov-pritzker-signs-nation-leading-artificial-intelligence-safety-law). The compute and revenue thresholds are a reasonable attempt to target the regime at the largest players rather than every startup, and Illinois deserves credit for that scoping choice relative to more sweeping proposals elsewhere. But within the regulated population, the obligation is undifferentiated: a frontier lab whose only deployed product is a coding assistant faces the same audit and disclosure regime as one running the system in a hospital. Risk is assumed from size and compute, not measured from deployment. Notably, [the audit and transparency-reporting obligations themselves don't bite until January 1, 2028](https://capitolnewsillinois.com/news/pritzker-signs-landmark-ai-regulation-bill-that-aims-to-mitigate-risks/) — meaning Illinois has locked in the *architecture* of the mandate now, well before any of the evidence that architecture will govern has been generated.

Utah's [AI Policy Act](https://law.justia.com/codes/utah/title-13/chapter-72/part-3/section-301/), first enacted in 2024, works the other way. The Office of AI Policy's Learning Laboratory Program lets a company apply for a [time-limited "regulatory mitigation agreement" — in force for no more than 12 months initially, with one 12-month extension available on request](https://law.justia.com/codes/utah/title-13/chapter-72/part-3/section-302/) — tied to a specific product and a specific regulatory obstacle. The [Doctronic agreement](https://www.fiercehealthcare.com/ai-and-machine-learning/doctronic-partners-utah-ai-sandbox), for example, waives Utah's requirement that a licensed physician issue prescription renewals, but only for a defined list of 191 low-risk medications, only for renewals (not new prescriptions), and — critically — waives nothing else. Health-data privacy and cybersecurity law continue to apply in full, and [participants must immediately report to the office any incident involving consumer harm, a privacy breach, or unauthorized data use](https://law.justia.com/codes/utah/title-13/chapter-72/part-3/section-302/), on pain of removal from the program. That is a narrower, more precisely targeted intervention than "every frontier developer above a revenue line gets audited every year," because the relief is scoped to the exact rule blocking the exact deployment being tested — not a blanket compliance obligation layered on top of a company's entire product line.

## The evidentiary payoff — and its limits

The point of the sandbox model is that it produces data the audit model cannot generate by construction, because the audit model never requires anyone to deploy anything new before complying. Six-plus months into the Doctronic pilot, [Telehealth.org reports the program remains in Phase I](https://telehealth.org/news/utah-expands-ai-prescription-pilots-as-early-data-shows-no-safety-issues/): every AI-generated renewal is still reviewed and authorized by a licensed physician before it reaches a pharmacy, because no drug category has yet completed the 250 renewals required to advance to lighter, pharmacist-facing oversight. Officials say they have not observed any notable safety incidents so far.

That is genuinely useful evidence. It is not proof of anything at scale. Six months of physician-reviewed renewals within a bounded, 191-drug formulary is a small-n, closely supervised sample — and a deliberately conservative one, since no drug category has advanced past the strictest tier of review the agreement allows. Utah's own medical licensing board pushed back regardless: in a March 2026 letter, [eleven board members called for the program to be halted](https://www.cnn.com/2026/07/07/health/ai-prescription-refills-utah), citing the risk of automatically renewing medications that can carry side effects or dangerous drug interactions. The state declined to halt the program, and Doctronic's physician-review requirement has remained in place throughout. That tension is worth sitting with rather than smoothing over: it shows the sandbox model doesn't eliminate disagreement about acceptable risk, it relocates that disagreement to regulators who are watching live deployment data instead of legislators debating hypotheticals. Illinois-style ex-ante audit regimes cannot produce even this contested, imperfect evidentiary record, because they never require a company to put a system in front of real patients under real supervision before the compliance obligation attaches. A no-incident report over six months in one drug-renewal pilot is a data point, not a verdict — but it is a data point Illinois' architecture is structurally incapable of generating before 2028, if ever.

## Scaling beyond one pilot

The relevant test of a sandbox model is not whether one deployment worked but whether the state is willing to extend the same evidentiary discipline into progressively higher-stakes domains. Utah has done that. In March 2026, the Office of AI Policy signed a mitigation agreement with [Legion Health](https://commerce.utah.gov/ai/agreements/ai-legion-health/), an AI-native psychiatric clinic, to pilot AI-assisted renewal of maintenance psychiatric medications — a narrower and, if anything, higher-stakes cousin of the Doctronic model, limited to a formulary of non-controlled drugs and gated by its own volume thresholds: physician review of the first 250 renewals, then intensive retrospective review of the next 1,000 before the program can advance to routine sampling. Two months later, the office authorized [Dentacor](https://commerce.utah.gov/ai/regulatory-relief/authorized-ai-pilots/dentacor/) to test an AI-assisted diagnostic radiograph tool aimed at expanding dental-care capacity for underserved patients. Each of those sits in a materially more sensitive risk category than renewing statins or blood-pressure medication for stabilized chronic patients — psychiatric medication management carries higher stakes for misjudgment, and diagnostic imaging carries its own error profile. That the state extended the model into those domains, rather than treating one uncontroversial pilot as the sandbox's ceiling, is the more meaningful evidence that this is a scalable governance approach rather than a one-off accommodation. It is also not a new posture for Utah: the office's original mitigation agreement, with the mental-wellness chatbot ElizaChat in December 2024, predates Illinois' bill by a year and a half.

Utah paired that expansion with an institutional commitment to actually use the data: the [Pro-Human AI Task Force, announced in February 2026](https://business.utah.gov/news/utah-announces-pro-human-ai-task-force-members/), is explicitly charged with converting what the Learning Laboratory observes into durable governance frameworks — regulation-from-evidence rather than regulation-from-precaution. Whether that task force's eventual output is more or less permissive than Illinois' framework is an open question the pilots have not yet answered. But the sequencing is the point: Utah is positioned to write rules informed by measured outcomes across four deployment contexts, while Illinois wrote its rule before any state-supervised deployment generated outcomes at all.

## Sandboxes are conditional access, not deregulation

The standard objection to regulatory sandboxes is that they let companies buy their way out of accountability — testing on real patients or consumers under looser rules than everyone else has to follow. That objection deserves a direct answer rather than a dismissal, because it is not baseless as a general critique of poorly designed sandboxes.

But it does not describe what Utah's statute actually does. Mitigation agreements are time-bound (12 months, one renewal), scoped to a specific rule and a specific product rather than a blanket exemption from state law, and revocable — the statute requires immediate incident reporting and permits removal from the program for consumer harm, privacy breaches, or unauthorized data use. The state retains full enforcement authority over everything the agreement doesn't waive, which in Doctronic's case meant privacy and cybersecurity law stayed fully in force throughout. This is closer to conditional market access — "you may operate under supervision, subject to disclosure obligations and immediate revocation, while we and you both learn whether this works" — than it is to deregulation. The Utah medical board's public objection, and the state's public rejection of its call for suspension, is itself evidence the mechanism has teeth: dissenting regulators had a channel to register concern in the middle of a live deployment — a channel Illinois' pre-deployment audit framework has no equivalent for, since there is no deployment yet to object to.

None of this requires conceding that third-party audits are illegitimate as an accountability tool. Audits are a reasonable component of AI governance for large developers of general-purpose systems, where no single "deployment" defines the relevant risk surface. The objection here is specifically to *imposing* that mechanism on a blanket, undifferentiated, ex-ante basis — before any state-supervised deployment data exists to calibrate what the audit should even be checking for. An audit mandate layered onto sandbox-generated risk findings would be a very different, and more defensible, instrument than one legislated sight-unseen.

## Federalism is doing its job here

Utah is not alone, and that matters. Texas's Responsible AI Governance Act, effective January 1, 2026, includes its own [regulatory sandbox administered by the Department of Information Resources](https://www.americanbar.org/groups/business_law/resources/business-law-today/2025-july/texas-enters-ai-sandbox-with-traiga-implications-business-trials/), giving approved participants up to 36 months of protection from state enforcement actions related to the AI systems under test. Wyoming's legislature has not yet enacted a sandbox but its interim Blockchain, Financial Technology and Digital Innovation committee has [formally studied Utah's Learning Laboratory as a model](https://wyoleg.gov/InterimCommittee/2025/S19-20250514OtherStatesArtificialIntelligenceGovernanceLegislation-FINAL.pdf) alongside California's and Colorado's more prescriptive approaches, ahead of its own legislative decision. This is not a novel instrument invented for AI — Arizona ran the same playbook for fintech starting in 2018, becoming [the first state to let companies test new financial products under supervised, time-limited relief from licensing law](https://www.forbes.com/sites/astanley/2018/03/23/arizona-becomes-first-u-s-state-to-launch-regulatory-sandbox-for-fintech/) before writing permanent rules. AI sandboxes are that same, previously validated model applied to a new technology, not an improvisation.

That several states are now running variants of the same design — differing in duration (Utah's 12+12 months versus Texas's 36), scope, and administering agency — is a feature of competitive federalism, not a coordination failure. Multiple states experimenting with structurally similar-but-distinct sandbox parameters will, over the next two to three years, generate comparative evidence about which design choices matter, evidence no single state's audit-only approach could produce on its own.

## Recommendations

**For states currently drafting AI legislation** — and dozens are mid-cycle in 2026 — the sequencing matters more than the ultimate policy content. Build a sandbox mechanism with real teeth (mandatory incident reporting, revocability, narrowly scoped waivers, retained enforcement over everything not explicitly waived) before locking in a permanent, blanket compliance regime. A state can always legislate stricter rules once it has evidence; it cannot easily unwind compliance costs already built into every regulated company's operations, and vendors will simply route sensitive pilots to whichever state offers a workable path to real-world testing.

**For NIST, OMB, and congressional AI policy staff**, the near-term opportunity is not to mandate that states adopt sandboxes — that would be its own form of top-down, ex-ante prescription, and would contradict the evidence-first logic this brief is defending. It is to treat the accumulating multi-state sandbox record — Utah's four-and-counting supervised deployments, Texas's TRAIGA cohort, whatever Wyoming eventually enacts — as an evidentiary baseline the way [NIST's AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) already treats risk as tiered and context-dependent rather than uniform. A federal framework built on what sandboxes actually observed in health-tech, financial-services, and other regulated-sector deployments will be better calibrated than one built, as Illinois' was, on a compute threshold and a revenue line.

**For investors and counsel weighing where to pilot AI products in regulated industries**, the practical lesson is straightforward: states with functioning mitigation-agreement pathways are where you can generate the real-world performance data that will eventually matter to every other jurisdiction's rulemaking — including, eventually, Illinois'. The state that tests first writes the evidence base everyone else regulates from.
