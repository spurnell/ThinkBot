On April 27, China's National Development and Reform Commission ordered Meta to unwind its [$2 billion acquisition of Manus](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/27/meta-manus-china-blocks-acquisition-ai-startup.html), the Singapore-based agentic AI startup with Chinese founders that took the technology world by storm at its March 2025 launch. The NDRC's directive — framed in the neutral language of competition law — marks the first time Beijing has formally deployed its Anti-Monopoly Law to prevent a U.S. company from acquiring a Chinese-founded AI asset. Resist the antitrust framing. This is not a competition enforcement action — it is a geopolitical move wrapped in a legal suit, and Washington's response will define American AI investment policy for a decade.

## The AML as Strategic Theater

Beijing's legal justification, [reportedly grounded in the Anti-Monopoly Law](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-27/china-blocks-meta-s-2-billion-acquisition-of-ai-startup-manus), is a fig leaf. Chinese officials reportedly described the acquisition internally as a "conspiratorial" attempt to hollow out the country's technology base — language that belongs to industrial strategy, not merger review. The NDRC also reportedly barred Manus' two co-founders from leaving China during the investigation, a coercive step that competition authorities in liberal democracies simply do not take.

This is not a novel playbook. [China has weaponized its Anti-Monopoly Law as a strategic instrument](https://jipel.law.nyu.edu/navigating-the-storm-chinese-antitrust-actions-against-us-tech-titans/) for well over a decade, deploying it when and where it serves industrial policy goals. The Qualcomm saga — where Beijing's prolonged, inconclusive review effectively killed Qualcomm's proposed $44 billion acquisition of NXP Semiconductors in 2018 — established the template. Intel's terminated Tower Semiconductor deal in 2023 confirmed it. Now Manus completes the trilogy, with a twist: for the first time, the target is an AI asset, and the blocked acquirer is a U.S. platform giant rather than a chipmaker. [The episode signals](https://fortune.com/2026/04/28/china-blocks-meta-manus-deal-ai/) how far Washington and Beijing have drifted apart on AI specifically — a domain both governments now treat as existential.

U.S. policymakers should refuse the antitrust framing outright. Calling Beijing's action "merger review" launders an industrial policy move in neutral legal language — and concedes rhetorical ground that cannot be easily recovered.

## The Gap Washington Can't Ignore

The acute embarrassment here is not that China acted. It is that the United States has no coherent framework to respond — or, more precisely, to have anticipated this problem and addressed it prospectively.

CFIUS, the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States, is structurally limited to screening *inbound* foreign investment. The Meta-Manus deal was an *outbound* transaction: an American company trying to acquire a foreign target. CFIUS has no authority there. Its mandate simply does not reach the problem this incident dramatizes.

President Biden's [Executive Order 14105](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/jy2690) (August 2023) took the first meaningful steps toward filling this gap, establishing notification and prohibition requirements for U.S. investments in Chinese AI, semiconductor, and quantum companies. Final Treasury regulations went into effect in January 2025. The FY2026 NDAA, signed by President Trump in December 2025, then included the [Comprehensive Outbound Investment National Security Act](https://www.pillsburylaw.com/en/news-and-insights/fy2026-ndaa-statutory-framework-outbound-investment-restrictions.html), codifying and expanding the program — adding hypersonic systems and high-performance computing to the covered technology list and extending geographic scope to Russia, Iran, North Korea, and others.

Progress, certainly. But the architecture remains immature. The COINS Act gives Treasury 450 days to issue implementing regulations, meaning final rules aren't due until March 2027. The existing program relies on a blanket prohibition-and-notification structure rather than case-by-case review with individualized analysis — a blunt instrument aimed at an increasingly granular problem. The Meta-Manus episode reveals that gap with uncomfortable clarity: a high-profile AI acquisition, consummated in December 2025, fell outside the notification perimeter — only to be unwound by Beijing before Washington had a framework to evaluate it on U.S. terms.

## The Wrong Lesson: Blanket Restrictions

The tempting congressional response — restrict U.S. acquisitions of any Chinese-founded AI company, full stop — would be a self-inflicted wound dressed up as toughness.

America's AI leadership rests in significant part on the country's historic capacity to attract and integrate the world's best researchers, engineers, and founders — many of whom are from China, are Chinese-American, or built early careers in Chinese institutions. Manus itself exemplifies this dynamic: co-founded by Ji Yichao, who was raised in both Colorado and Beijing, [it was incorporated in Singapore](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/28/china-meta-manus-ai-deal.html) and developed by a globally distributed team that saw the U.S. technology ecosystem as the natural home for world-class work.

Treating national origin as a disqualifying attribute for AI M&A — rather than the specific character of the asset and the adversary-nation control question — would impose an innovation tax on U.S. acquirers, chill the talent pipelines that make the American AI ecosystem competitive, and hand Beijing a secondary victory it didn't even need to fight for. It would also be diplomatically corrosive and constitutionally fraught, effectively conditioning deal approvals on the ethnicity of founders rather than any articulable national security rationale.

## The Right Response: Targeted, Risk-Tiered Outbound Review

The policy answer is not to copy China's statist model. It is to build a principled framework the United States can defend on the merits.

A coherent outbound AI M&A review mechanism would have three features. First, *narrowness*: jurisdiction should be limited to acquisitions that would transfer strategic dual-use AI assets — foundation model weights, critical training infrastructure, sensitive datasets with military or surveillance applications — to entities with meaningful ties to adversary nations. National-origin screening is overbroad; adversary-nation asset-transfer analysis is the right lens. The distinction matters enormously for rule-of-law reasons and practical ones: a principled framework is one that survives court challenge and attracts allied cooperation.

Second, *procedural clarity*: unlike the current notification regime, a genuine review mechanism would involve individualized case-by-case analysis, defined criteria, and timely decisions — not open-ended probes that generate uncertainty without producing outcomes. CFIUS has spent four decades refining exactly this kind of institutional competence. The right institutional home for outbound AI review is either an expanded CFIUS mandate or a new body with equivalent procedural rigor, housed within the existing national security architecture rather than grafted onto the trade apparatus.

Third, *transparency*: rules should be published, criteria should be legible to practitioners, and outcomes should be explained. The legitimacy of a rules-based framework depends on its predictability. A purely discretionary veto authority — where deals live or die based on political salience — is closer to the EU's model. It is not ours, and adopting it would sacrifice one of the United States' core competitive advantages: the reliability of its regulatory environment for investment.

## The Strategic Stakes

[As Foreign Policy observed](https://foreignpolicy.com/2026/04/28/china-blocks-ai-meta-manus-deal-national-security/), this episode is a symptom of accelerating AI decoupling between the world's two leading technology powers. Beijing has demonstrated that it will use every legal instrument at hand — including ostensibly neutral competition law — to control the flow of AI assets it considers strategically significant. The rule of law, in Beijing's hands, is a tool of statecraft.

The answer to lawfare is not American lawfare. It is American clarity. A principled, transparent, risk-tiered outbound review framework — narrow in scope, robust in procedure, administratively housed in institutions with genuine national security expertise — is the proportionate and strategically sound response. Building it before the next Manus-sized incident forces the issue is not a defensive reflex. It is competitive strategy.

Congress has the COINS Act foundation. Treasury has the regulatory mandate. Meta-Manus has supplied the urgency. What remains is the institutional will to move before the architecture the United States needs is needed again — and again found missing.
