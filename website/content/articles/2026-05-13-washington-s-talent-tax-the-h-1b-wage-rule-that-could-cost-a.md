On March 26, 2026, the Department of Labor published a proposed rule that could reshape America's ability to recruit and retain the world's most consequential workforce. The rule, officially titled [*Improving Wage Protections for the Temporary and Permanent Employment of Certain Foreign Nationals*](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/03/27/2026-06017/improving-wage-protections-for-the-temporary-and-permanent-employment-of-certain-foreign-nationals), would overhaul the prevailing wage methodology underpinning the H-1B program — elevating minimum wage floors to their highest levels in more than twenty years. The public comment period closes on **May 26, 2026**. The window for policymakers and affected stakeholders to shape the regulatory record is closing fast.

This rule does not exist in isolation. It arrives alongside Rep. Eli Crane's [End H-1B Visa Abuse Act](https://crane.house.gov/2026/04/22/rep-crane-introduces-legislation-to-pause-and-reform-the-broken-h-1b-visa-process/) (introduced April 22, 2026), which would impose a three-year freeze on new H-1B petitions and permanently cut the annual cap from 65,000 to 25,000. It arrives as Sen. Jim Banks' [American Tech Workforce Act of 2025](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2821/text) — which would terminate the Optional Practical Training (OPT) program entirely — advances in the Senate Judiciary Committee. And it arrives as [USCIS data shows a 26.9% decline in eligible FY2026 H-1B registrations](https://www.gibney.com/alerts/immigration-by-the-numbers-key-stats-on-fy2026-h-1b-cap-lottery-and-next-steps/) — a pipeline already in contraction before any of these restrictions were contemplated.

Washington is constructing a multi-front policy barrier at the precise moment the United States is locked in a global race for AI talent supremacy. The real-world effect will not be an American workforce restored; it will be a Canadian workforce enriched, a British one expanded, and a German one replenished — at America's direct expense.

## The Wage Floor Math Is Punishing

The DOL rule's mechanics deserve scrutiny that most coverage has not provided. Under current methodology, Level I H-1B wages are calibrated to the 17th percentile of the occupational wage distribution. The proposed rule [anchors Level I wages at the 34th percentile](https://www.dol.gov/newsroom/releases/eta/eta20260326-0), with Level IV — covering independent, primary contributors — jumping to [the 88th percentile](https://www.duanemorris.com/alerts/dol_proposes_significant_increases_prevailing_wage_levels_h1b_perm_programs_0326.html). The DOL's own analysis estimates the rule would [raise the average certified wage by approximately $14,000 per year per worker](https://www.duanemorris.com/alerts/dol_proposes_significant_increases_prevailing_wage_levels_h1b_perm_programs_0326.html).

That $14,000 figure is an average that obscures the severity of the top-end impact. Software developers in high-cost metro areas — the exact labor market where AI labs compete for talent — face wage obligation increases well above the mean. [Analysis from Manifest Law](https://manifestlaw.com/blog/dol-proposed-prevailing-wage-increase-employer-impact/) finds that 88% of companies currently filing H-1B Labor Condition Applications for software developers at Level II are paying below the proposed new floor. The compliance gap is not marginal.

This is more than a payroll adjustment. For startups and early-stage AI labs — companies that cannot yet pay the 88th percentile for a principal researcher — the rule functions as a structural barrier to entry into the high-skilled immigration pipeline. For established labs, the aggregate effect across dozens of H-1B hires is substantial: not just in direct wage costs, but in the organizational complexity of restructuring compensation bands to accommodate a regulatory floor that bears no relationship to actual market conditions for direct-hire AI researchers.

The rule also covers H-1B1 and E-3 visa holders (nationals of Chile, Singapore, and Australia) and applies to PERM labor certification applications — meaning its wage inflation effect spreads across the entire employer-sponsored immigration system, not merely the H-1B cap.

## A Pipeline Already in Contraction

Before analyzing the rule's prospective damage, it is worth examining the baseline into which it would land.

USCIS data shows that [eligible FY2026 H-1B registrations fell to 343,981 — down 26.9% from 470,342 in FY2025](https://www.envoyglobal.com/news-alert/uscis-releases-data-on-fy-2026-h-1b-cap-selection-rates/). The agency attributes part of this decline to the beneficiary-centric selection process it implemented to combat registration fraud by outsourcing firms — a reform that is genuinely welcome. But the enrollment contraction also reflects the chilling effect of escalating administrative complexity, processing delays, and the broader hostile immigration signal emanating from federal policy.

The DOL rule, layered atop this contracting baseline, would compound the constraint precisely when AI talent demand is accelerating. The [Economic Innovation Group's analysis](https://eig.org/h-1b-workers-are-critical-for-ai-dominance/) documents that H-1B workers constitute 4.3% of the total US AI workforce — a share that vastly exceeds their 1.1% share of all H-1B approvals. Seventy percent of full-time graduate students in AI-related fields at American universities come from abroad. Sixty-five percent of the top AI companies in the United States have at least one immigrant founder. The talent pipeline that built American AI supremacy runs through the H-1B system in ways that aggregate statistics routinely understate.

## The Legislative Pile-On

If the DOL rule is the blunt instrument of the executive branch, Congress is preparing several of its own.

Rep. Crane's End H-1B Visa Abuse Act would not merely reform the program — it would functionally suspend it. The three-year freeze on new petitions, combined with a permanent post-freeze cap reduction from 65,000 to 25,000, would eliminate roughly 38% of annual H-1B issuances in perpetuity. The bill also proposes a $200,000 minimum salary floor — a figure calibrated to exclude most entry and mid-level roles at AI research institutions, universities, and nonprofit labs that are not yet generating commercial revenue. [Immigration law practitioners have characterized the bill as structurally unworkable](https://www.rnlawgroup.com/the-end-h-1b-visa-abuse-act-a-political-attack-disguised-as-reform/) for any employer outside Big Tech's top compensation tier.

The sleeper issue — underreported but potentially the most consequential — is the American Tech Workforce Act's proposed elimination of the Optional Practical Training program. OPT allows F-1 students to work in the US for up to 12 months after graduation, with a STEM extension of up to 36 months. For the majority of international STEM graduates at American universities, OPT is the on-ramp that keeps them in the US workforce long enough to qualify for and receive H-1B selection. It is, functionally, the bridge between American graduate education and American AI labs.

[Sen. Banks' bill](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2821/text) would terminate OPT entirely — not reform it, not audit it, not restrict it to direct employers. Terminate it. The effect would be to transform American research universities into world-class talent academies whose graduates are legally required to take their skills elsewhere the moment they receive their diplomas. Eliminating OPT is, in effect, a multibillion-dollar subsidy to Chinese and European universities — the very institutions competing to absorb the researchers America trained.

## The Competition Is Winning

American policymakers constructing these barriers appear to be operating in a closed-system mental model — one in which restricting access to the US labor market disciplines immigration flows rather than simply redirecting them. The global talent market for AI researchers does not operate that way.

Canada moved first and has moved aggressively. The [Global Talent Stream](https://www.canada.ca/en/employment-social-development/services/foreign-workers/global-talent/requirements.html) offers two-week processing for highly skilled technology workers. In late 2025, Ottawa announced a [CAD 1.7 billion Canada Global Impact+ Research Talent Initiative](https://www.cicnews.com/2025/12/canada-launches-new-streams-to-attract-and-retain-top-research-talent-from-abroad-1263246.html) — described as one of the largest targeted research talent recruitment programs globally — specifically targeting AI, quantum computing, and cybersecurity researchers. The program includes $40,000/year doctoral fellowships and $70,000/year postdoctoral awards for international researchers. Canada is not waiting to see what Washington decides.

The United Kingdom has restructured its [Global Talent Visa](https://www.gov.uk/global-talent) specifically to accelerate AI recruiting. Processing for AI and cybersecurity applicants [has been cut to three weeks](https://www.davidsonmorris.com/global-talent-visa/) — down from eight — with simplified application requirements. The program requires no job offer in advance, giving AI researchers the ability to relocate before securing employment, with full work authorization upon arrival.

Germany's [Fachkräfteeinwanderungsgesetz](https://www.make-it-in-germany.com/en/visa-residence/skilled-immigration-act) — the Skilled Immigration Act — took full effect in June 2024 and explicitly classifies AI engineers, machine learning specialists, and data scientists as shortage occupations, [offering reduced salary thresholds and an EU Blue Card accessible to IT specialists with three years of experience and no university degree required](https://jacksonandfrank.com/blog/germany-skilled-immigration-act-2024). Germany is projecting 200,000 professional visa issuances annually and has a stated policy goal of attracting 400,000 skilled immigrants per year.

These are not passive immigration regimes. They are active, well-resourced, and purposefully designed to recruit the exact researchers that Washington is making it harder and more expensive to retain. Every H-1B denial, every OPT termination, every prevailing wage floor that prices an AI researcher out of a direct-hire application is a candidate referral to Toronto, London, or Berlin.

[ITIF has documented this dynamic clearly](https://itif.org/publications/2025/09/22/trumps-de-facto-h-1b-ban/): restrictions on high-skilled immigration do not restore American jobs; they accelerate the offshoring of the work those workers would have performed. When AI research talent cannot enter or remain in US facilities, it does not cease to exist — it concentrates elsewhere.

## The Real Problem Has a Targeted Fix

None of this means the H-1B program is beyond criticism. It is not. Wage suppression by outsourcing-oriented staffing firms is a genuine, well-documented abuse — documented not by immigration restrictionists, but by the GAO, by the Economic Policy Institute, and by the program's own enforcement record.

[GAO-11-26](https://www.gao.gov/assets/a314508.html) found that abuses in the H-1B program are concentrated in staffing companies and smaller firms, with employers bringing in workers at below-market wages in ways that suppress compensation for the surrounding workforce. The outsourcing model — in which a staffing firm sponsors an H-1B worker and places them at a third-party client site — was responsible for a disproportionate share of this abuse. Thirteen of the top 30 H-1B employers have historically been outsourcing firms, accounting for roughly one-fifth of total annual H-1B cap issuances.

These abuses are addressable through mechanisms that do not require punishing the broader high-skilled immigration pipeline:

**Cap allocation reform**: Prioritize direct employer petitions over staffing-firm placements. The DOL's own wage-ranking proposal — however flawed in its wage-floor calibration — correctly identifies that selecting H-1B beneficiaries by wage level would reorient the program toward high-skill, high-compensation roles and away from low-cost outsourcing placements.

**Tiered enforcement**: Apply heightened compliance auditing to third-party placement arrangements rather than imposing system-wide wage mandates that hit direct-hire AI researchers who have nothing to do with outsourcing-firm wage suppression.

**Staffing-firm-specific wage floors**: If the DOL's concern is the outsourcing model, it can regulate the outsourcing model directly — imposing stricter wage obligations on staffing firms while maintaining current percentile anchors for direct employer petitions.

The DOL is not pursuing these targeted reforms. It is pursuing a blunt wage floor that affects every H-1B employer equally — from Infosys's IT staffing operations to a ten-person AI startup in Pittsburgh hiring its first machine learning engineer. That is regulatory design that ignores the actual structure of the problem it claims to solve.

## The Innovation Cost the DOL Forgot to Calculate

Every cost-benefit analysis in federal rulemaking is required under Executive Order 12866 to account for both costs and benefits. The DOL's analysis of the prevailing wage rule accounts for wage increases to covered workers and the transfer payments involved. What it does not credibly account for is the innovation cost — what the United States loses when AI researchers who trained at American universities take their skills to competitor nations because the regulatory cost of retaining them became prohibitive.

[NBER research by Kerr and Lincoln](https://www.nber.org/papers/w15768) established that H-1B visa reforms that expand admission increase immigrant science and engineering employment and patenting without displacing native workers — finding, if anything, small crowding-in effects for domestic researchers. High-skilled immigrants account for [nearly 50% of US workers with science and engineering doctorates](https://www.nber.org/reporter/2013number4/high-skilled-immigration-domestic-innovation-and-global-exchanges) and have accounted for the majority of growth in the US scientific workforce since the 1990s. The innovation externalities of constraining this pipeline are not zero — and the DOL's regulatory impact analysis should be required to model them.

[ITIF has called for the United States to retain international graduates through expedited green card pathways](https://itif.org/publications/2025/12/23/the-united-states-should-retain-international-graduates-to-meet-demand-for-stem-talent/) — specifically in computer science and engineering — as a structural response to the talent gap. That recommendation moves in the opposite direction from both the DOL rule and the Crane legislation.

## The Comment Period Closes May 26

The DOL's 60-day comment window closes on **May 26, 2026**. Policymakers, employers, university research offices, and innovation advocates have a narrow window to enter the regulatory record before it closes. Comments should press the DOL on at least three fronts: the absence of a credible innovation cost analysis; the failure to distinguish between direct-hire AI employers and outsourcing-firm placements in applying the new wage floors; and the availability of more targeted enforcement mechanisms that address documented abuse without imposing system-wide constraints on the high-skill pipeline.

On the legislative track, Congressional staff on the Judiciary and HELP committees should understand that the Crane bill's three-year freeze and permanent cap reduction — combined with OPT elimination — would create an AI competitiveness gap that no amount of domestic STEM investment can quickly close. The pipeline from international student to US-based AI researcher takes years to build; once disrupted, it does not rebuild on a political timetable.

The United States built the world's most productive innovation economy in no small part because it attracted the world's best talent and gave that talent reason to stay. Wage floors, visa caps, and OPT termination are not a defense of the American workforce. They are an abdication of the policy strategy that built American technological leadership — and a structural gift to every competitor nation that is, right now, actively engineering the alternatives.
