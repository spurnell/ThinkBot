The Senate Banking Committee [voted 15-9 on May 14](https://www.banking.senate.gov/newsroom/majority/chairman-scott-senate-banking-committee-advance-clarity-act-in-historic-bipartisan-vote) to advance the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act — the first crypto market structure legislation to clear a Senate committee in U.S. history. Two Democrats, Senators Ruben Gallego of Arizona and Angela Alsobrooks of Maryland, joined every Republican on the panel. The White House has [publicly targeted July 4](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/05/06/white-house-targets-july-4-for-clarity-act-passage-says-crypto-adviser-patrick-witt) for a signing. The House passed its version [294-134](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/3633/text) back in July 2025.

By most measures, this should be a bill with enough momentum to cross the finish line.

Instead, the period between committee clearance and Senate floor consideration has become a lobbying sprint by organized interests that have calculated — correctly, as a tactical matter — that the 60-vote cloture threshold gives them leverage to kill the bill without ever winning the policy argument. The American Bankers Association, Bank Policy Institute, and allied trade groups [released a joint statement](https://bpi.com/banking-trades-statement-on-senate-banking-committee-vote-to-advance-clarity-act/) the afternoon of the vote calling for "strengthening" the bill — industry shorthand for tightening provisions in ways that protect incumbent bank deposit franchises from stablecoin competition. Law enforcement groups have argued the bill would hamper crypto crime investigations. Labor organizations have raised concerns about consumer exposure.

Each of these objections deserves a direct answer. None of them survives scrutiny. And none of them offers an alternative to the problem the CLARITY Act is actually solving — which is not crypto permissiveness, but a decade-long regulatory vacuum that has simultaneously failed consumers and ceded American leadership in digital financial infrastructure.

## The Jurisdictional Vacuum Was the Consumer Risk

The standard critique of the CLARITY Act frames it as a deregulatory measure that relaxes oversight of a volatile and fraud-prone asset class. That framing inverts the actual history.

For the better part of a decade, the U.S. digital asset market operated in a space where neither the SEC nor the CFTC had unambiguous statutory authority over spot market transactions in the most widely held cryptocurrencies. The SEC asserted jurisdiction using the [Howey test](https://jolt.law.harvard.edu/digest/digital-commodities-consumer-protection-act-of-2022-a-controversial-effort-caught-in-the-crossfire-of-the-ftx-collapse-1) for investment contracts; the CFTC asserted commodity jurisdiction; neither agency had a clean statutory hook for centralized exchange oversight. The Financial Stability Oversight Council's [October 2022 report on digital asset financial stability risks](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/261/FSOC-Digital-Assets-Report-2022.pdf) identified limited direct federal oversight of the spot market for crypto-assets that are not securities as a critical systemic gap — and it did so one month before FTX collapsed.

FTX is the case study the opposition should be required to answer. The exchange commingled customer funds, used client assets to extend credit to an affiliated trading firm, and operated across multiple jurisdictions with no single regulator possessing comprehensive authority over its full structure. Its customers lost billions. As [a former CFTC enforcement director observed](https://cablj.org/the-fall-of-crypto-exchange-ftx-under-the-noses-of-the-sec-and-cftc/) in the aftermath: "It's a patchwork of global regulators — and even domestically there are huge gaps. That's the fault of a regulatory system that has taken too long to adjust to the advent of crypto."

The CLARITY Act's response to that failure is not deregulation — it is jurisdictional architecture. The bill assigns CFTC oversight to digital commodity spot markets and preserves SEC authority over digital assets that remain investment contracts. It establishes a statutory framework for customer fund protections, disclosure requirements, and custodial standards. It does what FSOC said in 2022 needed to be done: fills the gap. Critics who opposed the bill while offering no alternative to the jurisdictional vacuum are not protecting consumers from crypto risk; they are protecting the regulatory ambiguity that allowed FTX to slip through it.

## The Banking Lobby's Objection Is a Structural Moat, Not a Safety Argument

The ABA and its allied groups have been the most active and sophisticated opponents of the CLARITY Act. Their post-committee statement [acknowledges](https://bpi.com/banking-trades-statement-on-senate-banking-committee-vote-to-advance-clarity-act/) that the bill "marks an important step in establishing a regulatory framework" — and then calls for changes that, taken together, would substantially restrict stablecoin issuance by nonbank entities.

The specific target is stablecoin yield. The banking trades want to tighten [the Tillis-Alsobrooks compromise](https://www.coindesk.com/policy/2026/05/01/clarity-act-text-lets-crypto-firms-offer-stablecoin-rewards-while-shielding-bank-yield), which bans passive interest-equivalent returns on stablecoins but permits activity-based rewards tied to transactions or on-platform engagement. Bank groups have called this a ["loophole"](https://decrypt.co/367351/banking-industry-clarity-act-stablecoin-evasion) and want even activity-based rewards prohibited. Their argument is that any form of stablecoin reward will draw deposits away from banks and threaten local lending.

This argument is worth taking seriously — for about 30 seconds. Banks' concern is not that stablecoin rewards are unsafe; it is that stablecoin rewards are competitive. A payment stablecoin that offers activity-based incentives for using it as a medium of exchange is a consumer-facing product that competes with checking accounts. That is not a consumer protection problem. That is competition. The Tillis-Alsobrooks compromise already draws an appropriate line: no passive interest or deposit-equivalent yield, which prevents stablecoins from functioning as shadow bank deposits outside the prudential regulatory perimeter. Activity-based rewards tied to actual transactions are structurally different — they are loyalty mechanisms, not deposit products.

Congress negotiated this compromise carefully, with White House facilitation, over months. [Senators Tillis and Alsobrooks have signaled the compromise is final](https://punchbowl.news/article/finance/economy/tillis-alsobrooks-stablecoins/). The Senate should hold it. A campaign to relitigate the yield question in floor amendments is an attempt to use the legislative process to achieve a competitive moat that banking groups could not obtain in the market. The appropriate response is: no.

This observation is not anti-bank. It is an application of the same consumer welfare logic that should govern any competition policy argument. When incumbent institutions invoke consumer protection to restrict market entry by competitors, the burden of proof is on the incumbents to show actual consumer harm — not competitive pressure. The banking trades have not made that showing. They have made a deposit-substitution argument, which is a market competition argument dressed up in safety language.

## US Competitiveness Is the Affirmative Case

Every month the CLARITY Act spends in Senate floor limbo is a month in which the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation [deepens its claim as the global standard](https://www.esma.europa.eu/esmas-activities/digital-finance-and-innovation/markets-crypto-assets-regulation-mica). MiCA became fully applicable on December 30, 2024. Its transitional grace period [expires July 1, 2026](https://sumsub.com/blog/crypto-regulations-in-the-european-union-markets-in-crypto-assets-mica/). Crypto-asset service providers operating in the EU without a MiCA license after that date are in violation — not in a gray area. The EU has done what the U.S. has spent a decade failing to do: established a legal operating framework for the industry.

The UK has [passed comprehensive crypto-asset legislation](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/2025-crypto-regulatory-round-up/) aligned broadly with the U.S. approach. Singapore has issued more than 30 [Major Payment Institution licences](https://www.trmlabs.com/reports-and-whitepapers/global-crypto-policy-review-outlook-2024-25-report) under its digital asset framework. The UAE has built an active licensing ecosystem in the Abu Dhabi Global Market. Each of these jurisdictions made a policy choice to provide legal certainty; each has attracted institutional capital and development talent as a result.

The argument is not that the EU or Singapore has better digital asset regulation than the United States would have under the CLARITY Act. The argument is that they have *any* regulation at all in the form of predictable rules — and the United States currently does not. Digital asset firms that have spent 2024 and 2025 building for MiCA compliance do not need a U.S. framework in order to operate globally; they need U.S. legal certainty not to become a liability. Without the CLARITY Act, that certainty does not exist, and the marginal incentive is to route new business and new investment through jurisdictions that have resolved the question.

The July 4 deadline the White House has set is aggressive. It is also strategically correct. A bill that clears the Senate in July before the August recess has a plausible path to House reconciliation and enactment before MiCA's transitional period hardens global market practices around EU standards. A bill that slips to September starts competing with the pre-election calendar. A bill that slips to 2027 is a bill that will likely be written to accommodate MiCA's architecture as a baseline, rather than asserting American leadership in shaping global standards. Congress has a narrow window to lead. It should use it.

## The Illicit Finance Objection Doesn't Survive Scrutiny

Law enforcement organizations have argued that the CLARITY Act would hamper cryptocurrency crime investigations by establishing clearer regulatory boundaries that could complicate surveillance of illicit activity. This argument deserves the same direct engagement as the banking lobby's position — and fails for the same reason: it conflates the absence of law with the ability to investigate lawbreakers.

Start with the baseline. According to Chainalysis's [2026 Crypto Crime Report](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/2026-crypto-crime-report-introduction/), illicit activity as a share of total cryptocurrency transaction volume remains below 1 percent — despite the nominal dollar figure reaching $154 billion in 2025, a figure dominated by sanctions evasion by state actors rather than retail-level fraud. Compare that with cash. The United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime [estimates that 2-5 percent of global GDP](https://www.unodc.org/unodc/en/money-laundering/overview.html) is laundered annually through financial systems — with cash being the preferred instrument precisely because it leaves no permanent record. Blockchain, by contrast, creates a permanent public ledger: every transaction, including illicit ones, is recorded and traceable by default — a property the [Bank for International Settlements has identified as a structural advantage for anti-money laundering enforcement](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull111.pdf) relative to cash.

The Financial Crimes Enforcement Network already has [substantial Bank Secrecy Act authority](https://www.fincen.gov/resources/statutes-regulations/guidance/application-fincens-regulations-certain-business-models) over digital asset service providers, requiring anti-money laundering programs, customer identification, and suspicious activity reporting. FinCEN's existing authority covers custodial exchanges, brokers, and payment processors — the entities through which most illicit crypto activity is converted into or out of fiat currency. The CLARITY Act does not disturb this authority. It builds on it, extending clear regulatory requirements to a broader class of market participants who currently operate in gray zones precisely because jurisdictional ambiguity about their status has deterred enforcement rather than enabling it.

Law enforcement investigators do not benefit from regulatory ambiguity. They benefit from clear rules that create clean violations, defined reporting requirements that generate useful data, and jurisdictional structures that tell them which agency has primacy. The argument that clearer digital asset rules would hamper investigators would never survive application to any other financial instrument. No one contends that the Bank Secrecy Act hampers cash crime investigations by providing clear reporting thresholds. The same logic applies here. Regulatory clarity enables enforcement; regulatory limbo invites evasion.

## Pass the Bill — And Hold the Compromise

None of the opposition arguments against the CLARITY Act makes the case that the status quo — a market without coherent jurisdictional rules, operating through enforcement-by-litigation, and increasingly divergent from global frameworks — serves American consumers or investors better than a statutory framework would. The bill is not perfect. [As this publication detailed when the committee vote occurred](https://thinkbot.ai/articles/2026-05-21-clarity-act-senate-committee-reconciliation), the reconciliation fight with the Agriculture Committee on DeFi developer liability and the 60-vote floor math remain genuine challenges. Those are mechanics to be worked through, not arguments against the bill's core purpose.

The Senate should pass the CLARITY Act. It should hold the Tillis-Alsobrooks stablecoin yield compromise — a carefully negotiated middle ground that prevents deposit substitution without eliminating competitive pressure on incumbent banks, and which was finalized after extensive White House facilitation. It should reject floor amendments that tighten stablecoin restrictions beyond what the compromise established; those amendments are incumbency protection, not consumer protection. And it should do all of this before August, when pre-election calendar compression turns a manageable 60-vote count into a scheduling problem.

The case for the CLARITY Act is not a case for crypto permissiveness. It is a case for the most basic principle of the rule of law: market participants should be able to read the statute and know their obligations before they act. A decade of enforcement-by-litigation has produced neither clarity for legitimate builders nor effective deterrence of bad actors. FTX happened in that environment. The CLARITY Act is the answer to FTX — not the cause of the next one.

Regulatory limbo is not a consumer protection strategy. It is a choice to keep the game rigged against competitors who haven't yet figured out which regulator to lobby. Congress should make a different choice.
