# The Opt-Out Wave: How Copyright's $150,000-Per-Work Hammer Could Reshape AI Training Economics

The $1.5 billion settlement in *Bartz v. Anthropic* was supposed to be the moment the AI industry's original-sin copyright problem got priced, paid, and closed. Roughly 500,000 authors and rightsholders [claimed their share](https://authorsguild.org/news/anthropic-settlement-update-91-percent-of-books-claimed/) of a fund that works out to about $3,000 per book, and by the time [the fairness hearing was held on May 14](https://publishingperspectives.com/2026/05/anthropic-settlement-appears-to-cruise-through-its-final-fairness-hearing/), the claims rate had climbed to 92.77 percent — a level of participation lawyers on both sides read as evidence the deal was fair, reasonable, and adequate.

A small number of authors read the same number differently. On December 23, two-time Pulitzer winner John Carreyrou and five co-plaintiffs [opted out and filed suit](https://techcrunch.com/2025/12/23/john-carreyrou-and-other-authors-bring-new-lawsuit-against-six-major-ai-companies/) — not against Anthropic alone, but simultaneously against Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Meta, xAI, and Perplexity. The complaint alleges all six companies [downloaded pirated copies](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/ip-law/openai-anthropic-xai-hit-with-copyright-lawsuit-from-writers) of the plaintiffs' books from the same "shadow libraries" — LibGen, Z-Library, OceanofPDF — that anchored the *Bartz* piracy claims, and it asks for the Copyright Act's maximum: $150,000 per work, per defendant, for willful infringement. [As the filing puts it](https://www.publishersweekly.com/pw/by-topic/industry-news/publisher-news/article/99347-authors-file-new-lawsuit-against-ai-companies-seeking-more-money.html), the settlement's per-work rate is "a tiny fraction (just 2%) of the Copyright Act's statutory ceiling." Run the same works against six co-defendants and the arithmetic gets dramatic fast: up to $900,000 per book, 300 times the negotiated settlement figure, for works most of whose claimants took the $3,000.

This is not a fringe legal theory. It is a rational response to a genuine gap the settlement left open, and it is likely the first of many such suits. Understanding why requires separating two distinct questions: whether AI training on lawfully acquired data is fair use (it is, and courts are getting that right), and what happens when the same infringement claim can be brought against an entire industry at once for training on the same pirated corpus. The opt-out wave is a problem in the second category, and it is arriving at the exact moment a federal appeals court is reconsidering the first.

## The Mechanics: Why Six Lawsuits Beat One Settlement

Class settlements bind claimants to a negotiated, aggregate resolution in exchange for certainty and speed. Opting out preserves an individual's right to sue for actual or statutory damages instead — a real option under Rule 23, and one [a recruitment firm](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/ip-law/judge-torches-firm-luring-authors-out-of-1-5-billion-ai-pact) tried to exploit so aggressively that Judge William Alsup called its tactics "a fraud of immense proportions" and hauled its CEO in for an evidentiary hearing. Most authors who opted out did so for legitimate reasons: they judged $3,000 a low price for a willful infringement claim carrying a statutory ceiling fifty times higher.

What makes the Carreyrou suit structurally different is not the opt-out — it is the decision to sue six companies at once for training on the same works. Copyright's statutory damages provision, [17 U.S.C. § 504(c)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/504), authorizes up to $150,000 per work for willful infringement, and that ceiling applies per infringer, not per work in the abstract. If six companies each independently downloaded a copy of the same pirated book, each has committed its own infringing act, and each is separately exposed — that is doctrinally coherent, not double-counting. But it means the total exposure attached to any given book is now a function of how many defendants a plaintiff chooses to name, not of how badly any single company behaved. A work that would have settled for $3,000 under the *Bartz* formula can now anchor a $900,000 demand simply because six frontier labs happen to have scraped the same shadow-library dataset that flooded the open web between 2018 and 2023 — LibGen, Books3, Z-Library, and Bibliotik show up, in some combination, in nearly every major foundation model's disclosed or alleged training history. The industry's shared reliance on a small number of overlapping pirate corpora, once a footnote in individual cases, is now the mechanism by which a single set of works can generate coordinated eight-figure-and-up exposure across the sector simultaneously.

## A Consensus Not Yet Secure

None of this stacking risk would matter much if the underlying liability were confined, as it should be, to the piracy claims that both *Bartz* and *Kadrey v. Meta* treated as the operative dividing line. [Judge Vince Chhabria's ruling in *Kadrey*](https://www.loeb.com/en/insights/publications/2025/07/kadrey-v-meta-platforms-inc) found Meta's training highly transformative while explicitly declining to bless how the underlying books were acquired. Judge Alsup's ruling in *Bartz* went further, drawing [the line separating lawful training from piracy](/articles/2026-05-16-the-piracy-line-why-courts-are-getting-ai-copyright-right-an): training on lawfully acquired books is "quintessentially transformative" fair use; downloading pirated copies is not, and the $1.5 billion figure reflects Anthropic's exposure on the latter, not the former. That is a workable, narrow rule — it protects the legitimate business of building models from purchased, licensed, or public-domain text while preserving real consequences for companies that took a shortcut through shadow libraries.

That consensus is not yet the law of any circuit. The first federal appellate test, [*Thomson Reuters v. Ross Intelligence*](https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/70622297/thomson-reuters-enterprise-centre-gmbh-v-ross-intelligence-inc/), was argued before the Third Circuit on June 11, and it does not involve a generative chatbot at all — it involves Ross's use of Westlaw's copyrighted headnotes to train a legal search tool that competed directly with Westlaw. At the district level, [Judge Stephanos Bibas ruled against Ross](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2025/02/alerts-practices-ip-lit-court-rejects-fair-use-defense-in-ai-copyright-case), rejecting transformative-use protection where the output substitutes for the market the copyrighted material originally served. Briefing before the Third Circuit has centered on exactly that fourth fair-use factor — [market impact](https://law.fsu.edu/sites/default/files/2026-06/First%20AI%20Copyright%20Appeal%E2%80%99s%20Reach%20Hinges%20on%20Market%20Impact%20Issue.pdf) — and a ruling that reads market substitution broadly could unsettle the *Bartz*/*Kadrey* framework for any AI system whose outputs plausibly compete with the works it trained on, not just non-generative search tools. A decision is not expected until late 2026 at the earliest.

The timing could hardly be worse for coherence. Just as plaintiffs are learning to multiply piracy claims across every company that touched a common corpus, the doctrinal floor under the *other* half of the liability question — whether transformative training on lawfully acquired data stays protected — is still wet concrete. A circuit split, or a Third Circuit ruling that narrows fair use for any AI system with commercially adjacent outputs, would not just create uncertainty for future training runs. It would reopen the question of how much of *already-trained* corpora — the lawfully acquired majority that *Bartz* and *Kadrey* cleared — remains protected, expanding the stacking problem well beyond the piracy-tainted slice of the corpus where it currently lives.

## Whose Liability, Multiplied How Many Times

None of this is an argument that the piracy claims underlying the Carreyrou suit are illegitimate. Companies that downloaded pirated copies from LibGen or Z-Library made a choice, and copyright law's ordinary remedies — including enhanced damages for willfulness — exist precisely to make that choice costly. The line between lawful acquisition and piracy is the correct one, and strict accountability should attach to the latter regardless of how sophisticated the downstream use.

The policy problem is proportionality, not culpability. A liability regime in which the total exposure attached to a given set of works scales with the number of companies a plaintiff's counsel decides to name — rather than with any assessment of relative fault, actual market harm, or the settlement value the market itself has already established — stops functioning as a deterrent calibrated to wrongdoing and starts functioning as a lottery. Lotteries are not evenly distributed risks. A company with Alphabet's or Meta's balance sheet can absorb a jury verdict that runs into the hundreds of millions of dollars as a cost of doing business, litigate the marginal case to judgment, and adjust go-forward data practices with in-house counsel that already handles a dozen similar suits. A well-funded but not yet profitable frontier lab, or an open-model developer distributing weights that anyone can fine-tune on that same disputed corpus, cannot self-insure against $150,000-per-work exposure multiplied across every co-defendant a plaintiff chooses to sue. Insurance markets price catastrophic, low-frequency, high-severity tail risk poorly even when the exposure is bounded; they price it barely at all when the ceiling is a function of how many companies happen to get named in a complaint. The practical effect is to raise the cost of capital for exactly the firms — smaller labs, open-source developers, new entrants — whose survival keeps the American AI ecosystem competitive rather than consolidated among three or four companies with litigation budgets to match Alphabet's. That is an entrenchment risk dressed up as a creator-compensation dispute.

## A Calibrated Framework, Not a Bailout

Congress does not need to choose between protecting authors and protecting AI investment. It needs to separate two questions the opt-out wave has collapsed into one, and address each on its own terms.

First, for training on lawfully acquired works — purchased books, licensed datasets, public-domain material — Congress should codify the *Bartz*/*Kadrey* transformative-use standard into statute before the Third Circuit, or a future circuit split, has the chance to unsettle it. This is not a safe harbor for piracy; it is a floor under a rule courts have already reached independently, twice, on the merits. Locking it in removes the single largest source of prospective uncertainty for developers who did the sourcing right, and it does so without touching the piracy question at all.

Second, and more directly responsive to the Carreyrou suit's fact pattern, Congress should address the stacking problem on its own terms rather than pretending it doesn't exist. The Bartz settlement's roughly $3,000-per-work figure was a market-tested number, arrived at through adversarial negotiation between sophisticated counsel on both sides, informed by the same $150,000 statutory ceiling that now anchors the opt-out demands. That figure is a legitimate reference point for what "made whole, with a willfulness premium" looks like for infringement of a single work by a single company. What it was never designed to do is multiply cleanly by the number of companies that happen to share a training-data ancestor. A calibrated apportionment rule — capping the *aggregate* statutory damages recoverable across multiple independent infringers of the same predicate work at a reasonable multiple of the per-work benchmark the market itself has already set, while leaving each defendant's individual liability determination, and full liability for particularly egregious or knowing conduct, otherwise intact — would preserve deterrence without converting shared historical data practices into a sector-wide solvency event triggered by how many logos appear in a caption. This is a narrower, more surgical fix than a blanket damages cap, and it targets the actual mechanism — stacking across co-defendants for a common predicate act — rather than diluting accountability for the underlying piracy.

## A Related but Separate Front: Transparency Mandates

None of this should be confused with the disclosure debate playing out in parallel. The bipartisan [CLEAR Act](https://www.schiff.senate.gov/news/press-releases/news-sens-schiff-curtis-introduce-bipartisan-bill-to-protect-creators-work-implement-transparency-safeguards-in-ai-model-development/), introduced by Senators Adam Schiff and John Curtis, would require AI developers to file a notice with the Copyright Office identifying copyrighted works used in training before releasing a new model, applied retroactively to systems already on the market. That is a transparency mandate, not a liability standard — it does not resolve whether a given use is infringing, and it raises its own compliance-cost and retroactivity questions that deserve separate scrutiny. It is worth watching alongside the litigation wave because better disclosure could, in principle, make future stacking claims easier to substantiate — or easier to settle before they escalate. It is not, by itself, an answer to the damages-multiplication problem the opt-out suits have surfaced.

## The Stakes of Getting the Sequencing Right

The AI copyright docket has produced, on the whole, a coherent body of law: transformative training on lawfully acquired works is protected; training on stolen copies is not. That is the right outcome, reached the right way, through ordinary application of the four-factor fair use test. The opt-out wave does not challenge that framework so much as expose what happens when a sound liability rule meets an unbounded multiplication mechanism and an unresolved appellate question at the same time. Six lawsuits over the same books is not evidence the underlying claims are weak — several may well have merit. It is evidence that Congress, having watched courts do the hard work of drawing the fair-use line correctly, still has an unfinished job: making sure the penalty for crossing that line is proportionate, predictable, and payable by more than the five or six companies large enough to survive finding out the hard way.
