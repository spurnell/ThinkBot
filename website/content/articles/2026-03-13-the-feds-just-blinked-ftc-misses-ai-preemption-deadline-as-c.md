On March 11, 2026, a pair of deadlines embedded in President Trump's December 2025 AI Executive Order came and went. One was met. One wasn't. The gap between them tells you everything you need to know about Washington's ability to actually solve the state AI patchwork problem — and why Congress can no longer afford to sit this out.

## What Just Happened

The Trump AI Executive Order set March 11 as a dual deadline: the Commerce Department was required to publish an assessment of state AI laws deemed "onerous" to national AI competitiveness, and the Federal Trade Commission was required to release a policy statement asserting that certain state bias-mitigation mandates constitute deceptive conduct under the FTC Act — a legal predicate that would give the DOJ's AI Litigation Task Force grounds to challenge those laws in court.

Commerce delivered. Its [assessment](https://regulations.ai/regulations/RAI-US-NA-COMMERC-2026) named names: Colorado's AI Act, New York's RAISE Act, and multiple California statutes made the list. The report activates the Executive Order's sequenced enforcement process and puts those states on formal notice that the federal government views their laws as incompatible with a unified national AI framework.

The FTC said nothing.

That silence is not a minor procedural slip. The Administration's entire legal theory for preempting state AI law runs through the FTC. Without the policy statement, the DOJ AI Litigation Task Force lacks its key legal predicate. The chain is broken at the second link, and the states targeted by Commerce now have reason to believe the enforcement threat is considerably less credible than advertised.

## The Right Goal, the Wrong Instruments

Federal preemption of conflicting state AI laws is the correct policy outcome. A patchwork of 50 divergent state regimes is not a "laboratory of democracy" — it is a de facto compliance tax that disadvantages American AI developers against competitors operating under China's unified AI industrial policy. Companies building foundation models cannot architect compliance programs around Colorado's risk-tier definitions, New York's bias audit requirements, California's training data disclosure mandates, and whatever Texas and Illinois decide to do next — all simultaneously, all potentially conflicting.

The problem is that the Administration chose legal instruments that were never going to do the job.

Executive orders cannot preempt state law the way federal statutes can. The Supremacy Clause operates through Congress, not the White House. Even if the FTC had published its policy statement on schedule, courts were unlikely to find that a nonbinding document carries preemptive force over validly enacted state legislation. [As legal scholars and practitioners have noted](https://www.techpolicy.press/the-ftcs-ai-preemption-authority-is-limited/), the FTC's deception authority under Section 5 of the FTC Act has never been applied to characterize state regulatory mandates as "deceptive" — this is novel legal theory, and federal courts will say so.

[Ropes & Gray's analysis](https://www.ropesgray.com/en/insights/alerts/2026/03/examining-the-landscape-and-limitations-of-the-federal-push-to-override-state-ai-regulation) put it plainly: real preemption requires notice-and-comment rulemaking with clear statutory authority, a process that takes years even when an agency is fully committed. The FTC, which didn't meet a deadline to publish a policy statement, is not a credible vehicle for that kind of sustained regulatory campaign.

## The Funding Lever Is Real but Narrow

The Administration has one other card to play: conditioning states' access to BEAD broadband funds — a $42 billion program — on compliance with federal AI policy priorities. This is a creative use of spending-clause leverage, and it's not without precedent. But as [compliance analysts have noted](https://www.mondaq.com/unitedstates/new-technology/1755166/march-2026-federal-deadlines-that-will-reshape-the-ai-regulatory-landscape), the overlap between state AI compliance programs and broadband grant recipients is narrow. Colorado's AI Act governs high-risk automated decision systems used by employers and insurers — entities not primarily funded by BEAD grants. The leverage is asymmetric and will face immediate legal challenge under the Supreme Court's unconstitutional conditions doctrine.

Funding pressure may produce targeted negotiations with state legislators who want to protect their broadband programs. It will not produce coherent national AI governance.

## Companies Are in Compliance Limbo

While Washington sorts out its legal strategy, AI developers and deployers face [genuine compliance uncertainty](https://www.spglobal.com/market-intelligence/en/news-insights/articles/2026/3/companies-face-compliance-limbo-as-trump-administration-targets-state-ai-laws-99326115). Colorado's AI Act and several California statutes took effect January 1, 2026. New York's RAISE Act is advancing. Companies that deployed automated decision systems in good-faith compliance with those laws now face uncertainty about whether federal enforcement activity will shield them from state liability — or simply add a new layer of federal exposure.

The answer right now is: no one knows. And "no one knows" is not a compliance posture risk-averse legal teams can accept. The longer this uncertainty persists, the more companies will either over-comply with the most restrictive state requirements (the de facto California standard) or under-invest in AI deployment domestically. Neither outcome serves American AI leadership.

**ThinkBot's near-term guidance**: continue complying with state laws currently in effect. The Administration's enforcement threats are legally fragile and not yet backed by judicial action. Companies that stop complying with Colorado or California law because the Commerce Department published a report do so at their own litigation risk.

## Congress Must Act

The federal government's bid to establish unified national AI governance is worth fighting for — but it requires the right instrument. Executive orders are not that instrument. [The IAPP's analysis](https://iapp.org/news/a/a-view-from-dc-can-the-ftc-preempt-state-ai-laws-) is correct: the FTC's preemption authority is constitutionally and structurally limited. Courts will say so, probably within 18 months of the first filed lawsuit.

The only instrument that can actually preempt state AI law is a federal statute with an explicit preemption clause. Congress has the authority. What it lacks is urgency.

That urgency should now be clear. [As of January 1, 2026](https://www.kslaw.com/news-and-insights/new-state-ai-laws-are-effective-on-january-1-2026-but-a-new-executive-order-signals-disruption), multiple state AI laws are live and binding. The longer Congress waits, the more deeply the patchwork becomes entrenched — in corporate compliance architectures, in state enforcement budgets, in case law interpreting state-specific standards. Reversing entrenchment is exponentially harder than preventing it.

A federal AI framework need not be a regulatory-free zone. It can include baseline requirements for high-risk AI systems, transparency obligations, and safety standards — modeled on the [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf), with congressional teeth. What it must do is occupy the field on the core governance questions that states are now answering in incompatible ways.

The Administration's March 11 stumble is not a reason to abandon the goal of unified national AI governance. It is a reason to pursue it through the only channel that can actually deliver it.

The FTC blinked. Now it's Congress's turn.
