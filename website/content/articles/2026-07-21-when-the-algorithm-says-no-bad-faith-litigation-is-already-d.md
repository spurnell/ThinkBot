A federal judge in Minnesota has ordered UnitedHealth Group to turn over seven years of internal records on an algorithm called nH Predict — the AI system a certified class of Medicare Advantage beneficiaries alleges the company used to cut off payment for post-acute care while overriding treating physicians' own recommendations. A separate class action in California accuses Cigna of building a "PxDx" review process in which physicians spent an average of 1.2 seconds per claim, batch-denying more than 300,000 requests for payment over two months. Neither case has been decided on the merits. Both are still in discovery. And both are proceeding under law that has nothing to do with AI at all: breach of contract, and the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing that every insurance contract carries under state common law.

This is worth distinguishing sharply from the debate ThinkBot has tracked over the past several months around state signature-mandate bills — [Arizona's physician-sign-off requirement](https://www.azleg.gov/legtext/57leg/1R/bills/HB2175H.pdf), [Indiana's House Bill 1271](https://iga.in.gov/legislative/2026/bills/house/1271), [Louisiana's Senate Bill 246](https://www.sheppard.com/insights/blogs/state-legislatures-consider-oversight-of-artificial-intelligence-in-health-insurance-decisions) — which govern *how* a human must be inserted into the denial workflow before it happens. That debate is about front-end process design. This one is about back-end accountability: what happens, under law that predates any AI-specific statute, when a denial turns out to have been wrong. It is a different point in the insurance lifecycle, a different body of doctrine, and — this brief argues — a body of doctrine that is proving to work.

## Three cases, one structural pattern

The nH Predict litigation, formally *Estate of Gene B. Lokken v. UnitedHealth Group*, is the furthest along. In February 2025, Judge John Tunheim dismissed five of the plaintiffs' seven claims on Medicare Act preemption grounds — but let the breach-of-contract and good-faith-and-fair-dealing claims survive, reasoning that resolving them requires only "basic contract principles" rather than second-guessing a coverage determination Medicare law reserves to the agency ([Courthouse News](https://www.courthousenews.com/federal-judge-dismisses-several-claims-in-ai-denial-lawsuit-against-uhg-but-case-will-proceed/)). That ruling matters beyond this one case: it identifies a doctrinal lane — contract and bad faith — that survives preemption defenses insurers have relied on to keep AI-denial suits out of court entirely.

Then, in March 2026, the same court ordered UnitedHealth to produce internal policies on post-acute care claims dating to 2017, all documents analyzing nH Predict's performance, and materials on government investigations into the company's AI use — largely granting plaintiffs' motion to compel across six of seven document categories ([Becker's Payer Issues](https://www.beckerspayer.com/legal/judge-orders-unitedhealth-to-hand-over-broad-discovery-in-ai-coverage-denial-case/); [ArentFox Schiff](https://www.afslaw.com/perspectives/alerts/federal-court-orders-broad-discovery-against-uhc-ai-coverage-denial-lawsuit)). That order is the mechanism doing the actual work in this story. Plaintiffs allege nH Predict carried roughly a 90 percent reversal rate on the small share of denials that were formally appealed — and that UnitedHealth kept using the tool because only about 0.2 percent of policyholders ever appealed at all ([Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/sites/daraabasiita/2026/06/09/the-algorithm-that-counted-on-no-one-appealing/)). If that allegation holds up, it is not a claim that AI made a few bad calls. It is a claim that the company built a denial pipeline around the foreseeable fact that almost nobody would push back — and discovery is now the tool forcing the company to produce the internal record that would prove or disprove it.

The Cigna PxDx litigation follows the same structural logic through a different fact pattern. A 2023 ProPublica investigation reported that Cigna's medical directors spent an average of 1.2 seconds reviewing each of more than 300,000 claims denied over a two-month period, batch-rejecting requests without opening patient files ([ProPublica](https://www.propublica.org/article/cigna-pxdx-medical-health-insurance-rejection-claims)). The resulting class action, filed in California, alleges Cigna's delegation of medical-necessity review to that automated process breached the terms of the health plans themselves ([Healthcare Dive](https://www.healthcaredive.com/news/cigna-lawsuit-algorithm-claims-denials-california/688857/)) — and a federal court has since allowed those claims to proceed, again on contract grounds rather than any novel AI theory ([Courthouse News](https://www.courthousenews.com/judge-advances-class-claims-over-cigna-use-of-automated-algorithm-to-deny-benefits/)). Cigna disputes that PxDx involves AI or machine learning at all and says the denials represent under 1 percent of its total claim volume ([Cigna](https://newsroom.thecignagroup.com/pxdx)) — a defense that, notably, argues about the system's classification and scale, not about whether 1.2-second review satisfies a contractual promise of individualized medical review.

Line up the two cases and the pattern is structural, not incidental: high alleged error or reversal rates, functionally negligible per-claim human review time, and low patient appeal rates that plaintiffs argue were not just a byproduct of the system but a foreseeable — and exploited — feature of it. None of the three courts involved has needed to define "artificial intelligence" to let these claims proceed. They needed only to ask whether the insurer's process was consistent with the good-faith performance every contract implies.

## Why discovery beats an audit mandate

The alternative instrument on offer — a new AI-specific claims-denial statute requiring licensing, certification, or standardized audits of any algorithmic system touching a denial — would trade a mechanism that is currently producing the exact evidence at issue for one that produces compliance paperwork disconnected from any particular harm. Consider what the nH Predict discovery order actually compels: seven years of internal performance analysis, acquisition-related cost-savings documentation, and records of prior government scrutiny — material that goes directly to whether the company knew its tool was unreliable and used it anyway. A before-the-fact audit regime, by contrast, would require every insurer using any automated claims tool — reliable or not — to produce a generic compliance filing on a fixed schedule, whether or not that insurer's system was ever implicated in a pattern of wrongful denial. One instrument is calibrated to actual conduct; the other is calibrated to the mere fact of automation.

This is not an argument that litigation is fast. It plainly is not — the nH Predict case has been in federal court since November 2023 and still has not reached a merits ruling. But the comparison that matters is not litigation against an idealized instant remedy; it is litigation against the realistic alternative, which is a multi-year rulemaking and legislative process that would need to first define which systems count as "AI" for regulatory purposes — a definitional problem regulators have struggled with across every AI-adjacent statute attempted so far — before any compliance obligation could attach. A discovery order compelling disclosure of a specific algorithm's reversal rate is available now, under law already on the books, in a case already filed. State insurance departments do not need to wait on either court to rule on the merits, or on their own legislatures to act, to make use of what discovery in these cases is already surfacing.

## The harm is real; the question is remedy design

None of this is an argument that mass-scale denial with negligible per-claim review is a minor problem, or that litigation alone is an adequate systemic response. A process that resolves a claim in 1.2 seconds is not, on its face, a process capable of the individualized medical-necessity review that health plans promise their members — and a denial pipeline that becomes more profitable the less often patients understand they have a right to appeal is a harm regulators should take seriously regardless of how any single lawsuit resolves.

The claim here is narrower and more specific: that the correct locus of liability is the conduct and the outcome — inadequate review, foreseeable exploitation of low appeal rates, denial rates inconsistent with contractual promises — not the presence of AI in the workflow. A rules-based denial engine with the same 1.2-second review time and the same reversal rate would raise the identical bad-faith exposure under identical doctrine. The wrong is process failure. AI is the instrument, not the offense, and a remedy built around the instrument will both over-reach (chilling automation that correctly and quickly approves the overwhelming majority of valid claims) and under-reach (missing functionally identical harms produced by non-AI mass-denial systems that happen to fall outside whatever definition a new statute settles on).

## What state regulators should actually do

State insurance departments have the authority to act on this now, without new statutory grants. Every state already enforces unfair-claims-practices and bad-faith standards through its insurance code and the [NAIC's Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act](https://content.naic.org/sites/default/files/model-law-900.pdf), which underwrites the periodic market-conduct examinations that departments already run. What is missing is not authority — it is a clear evidentiary signal to examiners, courts, and insurers about how AI-era denial patterns map onto standards that predate AI.

Insurance commissioners should issue interpretive guidance — not new rules requiring rulemaking dockets and legislative sponsors — stating explicitly that minimal-review denial patterns (near-zero per-claim review time, high rates of reversal on the rare appeals that occur, and correspondingly low overall appeal rates) constitute relevant evidence of bad faith under existing unfair-claims-practices law. That guidance would not create a new compliance regime. It would tell insurers, in advance, which operational metrics regulators and courts already consider probative — giving carriers a reason to monitor and correct exactly those metrics before litigation forces the disclosure, rather than after.

That is the version of accountability worth building on: liability that tracks demonstrated harm through a process already capable of extracting the evidence that matters, rather than a categorical new layer of AI-specific compliance infrastructure sitting on top of doctrine that is already adapting to the technology in front of it.
