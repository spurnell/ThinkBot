On April 6, OpenAI published a 13-page policy blueprint — [*Industrial Policy for the Intelligence Age*](https://openai.com/index/industrial-policy-for-the-intelligence-age/) — proposing a tax on automated labor, a federally managed public wealth fund seeded by AI companies, and government-incentivized four-day workweeks. Sam Altman framed the document as a ["Superintelligence New Deal."](https://www.axios.com/2026/04/06/behind-the-curtain-sams-superintelligence-new-deal)

The instinct behind it is understandable. AI-driven labor displacement is a real concern, and anyone building frontier AI systems is right to take it seriously. But the blueprint's proposed remedies — a productivity penalty, government-managed capital allocation, and federally engineered workweek compression — are not solutions to disruption. They are policies that would slow the engine of growth, reduce the size of the pie, and hand Congress a set of dials it has a poor track record of operating well.

The answer to AI-driven disruption is not to tax the technology producing it. It is to build the reallocation infrastructure that lets markets do what they have always done with transformative technologies: create more than they displace.

## The Productivity Penalty

A [tax on automated labor](https://www.newsweek.com/sam-altman-proposes-robot-tax-as-american-economy-transforms-11788200) is, by definition, a tax on capital investment at its most productive. Every credible analysis of capital taxation — from the standard neoclassical growth model to more recent empirical work on investment responses — reaches the same conclusion: taxing the returns to capital reduces long-run output. Workers don't inherit a bigger share of the economy. They inherit a smaller economy.

This is not a theoretical edge case. The European Parliament [considered and rejected robot tax proposals in February 2017](https://newatlas.com/eu-robot-law-proposal-passes-parliament/47971/) precisely because the economic analysis was unfavorable. A tax that discourages automation discourages the productivity gains that — over time — raise real wages, lower prices, and fund the public services that support displaced workers. You cannot penalize the engine and expect a larger dividend.

The framing of a "robot tax" as worker-protective is appealing rhetoric. The mechanics run in the opposite direction.

## The Sovereign Wealth Fund Illusion

The public wealth fund proposal deserves equally hard scrutiny. The blueprint envisions a fund seeded by AI company contributions, managed at the federal level, and used to distribute the gains of the AI economy broadly. The Norway comparison will inevitably surface, so let's take it seriously: [Norway's Government Pension Fund Global](https://www.nbim.no/en/about-us/about-the-fund/) works because it is funded by finite oil extraction revenue, managed by Norges Bank Investment Management under strict political independence, and insulated from annual appropriations battles. Remove any one of those conditions and the model degrades rapidly.

None of those conditions hold in the proposed American context. A fund "seeded by AI companies" and governed through Congress will not be managed by an independent central bank. It will be managed by whatever political coalition controls the relevant committees. It will optimize for lobbying returns, not investment returns. It will pick winners — or rather, it will protect incumbents, because incumbents are the ones with the government relations infrastructure to shape how the fund operates. We have seen this pattern in industrial policy: the distortions are predictable, the corrections are slow, and the bureaucratic momentum is nearly impossible to reverse once installed.

Government-managed capital allocation is not a neutral default. It is an active choice to substitute political signals for price signals in one of the most dynamic sectors of the global economy, at precisely the moment when getting that allocation right matters most for American competitiveness.

## The Self-Interest Question

Policy analysis should focus on proposals' merits, not the motives behind them. But the timing and structure of this blueprint invite scrutiny that goes beyond the economic mechanics.

OpenAI is approaching a major IPO, facing regulatory scrutiny on multiple fronts, and operating in an environment where its market dominance invites antitrust attention. A high-profile proposal for broad wealth redistribution funded by AI companies — framed in progressive language about worker protection — accomplishes something useful for a dominant incumbent: it redefines the policy conversation around redistribution rather than competition, raises the compliance cost floor in ways that disadvantage smaller entrants, and allows the proposer to appear as a responsible actor while the specifics remain conveniently vague.

None of this proves bad faith. But policy observers should notice when proposals that sound worker-protective happen to align neatly with the regulatory interests of the organization advancing them.

## What Actually Works

The honest answer to AI-driven displacement is unglamorous and supply-side. It involves three things policymakers consistently underinvest in because they don't generate good press releases.

First, **occupational licensing reform**. The United States has approximately [1,100 licensed occupations](https://ij.org/report/license-to-work-3/) — most added in the last 50 years, many with no meaningful health or safety rationale. Licensing barriers are among the most significant obstacles preventing displaced workers from moving into adjacent trades where demand exists. Reform here directly helps workers without touching the investment side of the ledger.

Second, **skills infrastructure tied to labor market signals**. Community college and workforce training programs that respond to actual employer demand — not legacy curriculum — are the retraining mechanisms that work. Federal funding tied to employment outcomes in emerging occupational categories is more effective than broad redistribution precisely because it connects investment to the specific transitions the economy actually needs.

Third, **portable benefits**. Tying health insurance, retirement, and other benefits to employment relationships rather than individual employers leaves workers exposed during transitions. Benefits that travel with workers rather than jobs would reduce the friction cost of moving between roles and sectors — which is exactly what labor market dynamism requires.

None of these require taxing automation. None require Congress to manage a capital fund. All of them have stronger empirical support than the mechanisms OpenAI proposes.

## The Historical Record

Every major technological transition of the last 150 years — electrification, mechanized agriculture, the computer, the internet — produced net job creation. That outcome was not guaranteed, and it was not painless. Displacement was real. But the net positive result was driven, in each case, by the same mechanism: markets reallocating labor toward new tasks that new productivity enabled, while policy focused on the reallocation infrastructure rather than the disruption itself.

There is no credible example of a government-managed technology transition that outperformed market reallocation. There are many examples of managed transitions that slowed the adjustment, protected incumbents, and left workers worse off in the medium run than a faster, more dynamic process would have.

The "Superintelligence New Deal" framing is evocative. But the original New Deal's lasting policy successes — deposit insurance, labor standards, social insurance — were institutional, not redistributive machinery aimed at specific technologies. The proposals worth drawing from that era are the ones that reduced friction and lowered floor risk. The ones that tried to manage industrial output from Washington are the ones history quietly retired.

The AI productivity dividend is real. The question is whether policymakers let it flow through wages, prices, and employment — or try to capture it in Washington and redistribute it through mechanisms that will be captured long before they reach the workers they're meant to serve. History gives us a strong prior on how that choice resolves.
