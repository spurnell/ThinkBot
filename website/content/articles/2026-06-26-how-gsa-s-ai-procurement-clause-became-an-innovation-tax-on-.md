On June 17, 2026, the General Services Administration published a [Federal Register notice](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/06/17/2026-12205/general-services-acquisition-regulation-acquisition-of-information-and-communication-technology) reopening public listening sessions on GSAR 552.239-7001 — the agency's draft "Basic Safeguarding of Artificial Intelligence Systems" clause. A public session is scheduled for July 14 at George Washington Law School. Written comments are due August 3.

That timeline matters. GSA pulled the AI clause from [Schedule Refresh 32](https://gormgroup.com/2026/06/08/gsa-schedule-refresh-32-released-june-2026/) — released earlier this month without it — after industry pressure forced a revision. The listening sessions represent a genuine second-drafting moment, not a rubber stamp. GSA appears to be responding to criticism, which is precisely why critics should show up.

The problem is not that the government wants to secure its AI systems. It does, and it should. The problem is that GSAR 552.239-7001, even in its revised June 2026 form, pursues that legitimate goal through instruments that are architecturally incompatible with how commercial AI actually works. The result — if the clause is finalized without structural correction and becomes the template for a broader FAR AI clause — will not be a more secure federal AI enterprise. It will be a more stagnant one.

## The Clause's Journey: From Aggressive to Merely Problematic

GSA's original draft, published through [MAS Refresh 31 in March 2026](https://www.jenner.com/en/news-insights/client-alerts/ai-for-gsa-contractors-advanced-notice-of-mas-refresh-31-contains-significant-draft-changes-deadline-of-march-20-for-comments), was extraordinary in scope. The government would receive an "irrevocable, royalty-free, non-exclusive license" to use any AI system for "any lawful Government purpose." All data inputs, outputs, and "Custom Developments" — defined broadly to include modifications, customizations, configurations, enhancements, and the results of model training or fine-tuning — would automatically transfer to government ownership upon creation. Contractors were prohibited from using "foreign AI systems," defined as anything "manufactured, developed, or controlled by non-U.S. entities." And in the clause's most audacious provision, AI systems were required to "not refuse to produce data outputs or conduct analyses based on the Contractor's or Service Provider's discretionary policies" — effectively mandating that vendors disable their own safety guardrails for federal customers.

The reaction was swift. The [U.S. Chamber of Commerce](https://www.uschamber.com/technology/industry-response-to-proposed-gsar-clause-552-239-7001-basic-safeguarding-of-artificial-intelligence-systems) warned that the expansive definitions of "AI System" and "Custom Development" could inadvertently capture back-end infrastructure, embedded machine learning tools, and standard commercial software. The Business Software Alliance warned that the draft would "[decrease competition and increase the cost to the US government to procure AI products](https://www.nextgov.com/acquisition/2026/04/trade-and-industry-groups-warn-risks-gsas-draft-ai-procurement-guidance/412614/)" while materially compromising the government's ability to adopt frontier AI. [SIIA raised similar concerns](https://www.siia.net/siia-comments-on-gsa-proposed-ai-clause-gsar-552-239-7001/) about the clause's compliance burden and its likely effect on the vendor pool.

There were procedural objections too. The original clause emerged with a 14-day comment window — a fraction of the 60 days required under [41 U.S.C. § 1707](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title41-section1707&num=0&edition=prelim) for policies with "significant effect beyond internal operating procedures." For a clause explicitly designed to reshape the federal AI marketplace, that procedural posture was indefensible, and it may yet face legal challenge under the major questions doctrine established in [*West Virginia v. EPA*](https://www.scotusblog.com/cases/case-files/west-virginia-v-environmental-protection-agency/) and extended in [*Biden v. Nebraska*](https://www.scotusblog.com/cases/case-files/biden-v-nebraska/).

The June 2026 revision addressed some of this. As [Crowell & Moring has analyzed](https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/gsa-issues-proposed-ai-contract-clause-seeks-feedback), the revised clause narrows applicability to contracts where government data is actively processed by a large language model — exempting LLMs embedded in common commercial products and those where AI functionality is "incidental to the primary purpose." Contractors now retain ownership of underlying LLM models and preexisting proprietary content. Trade secrets, source code, and model weights are expressly protected from disclosure. Domestic sourcing softens from an outright ban to a requirement that contractors "maximize" use of U.S.-incorporated entities, with incidental foreign components permissible if they "pose no security risks." And the revised clause introduces contractor protections absent from the original: written notice before termination, cure periods, liability caps, and express trade secret protection.

These are genuine improvements. GSA deserves credit for listening. But the clause still has structural problems that the July 14 listening session should surface clearly.

## Problem One: The IP Provisions Remain Architecturally Incompatible With Commercial AI

The revised clause continues to assert government ownership over all "Custom Developments" — defined as modifications, enhancements, or configurations made specifically for the government, including model adjustments resulting from training or fine-tuning. It also grants the government an irrevocable license to use the underlying AI system for the specific purposes and scope of the contract.

The problem is definitional elasticity. In practice, nearly any interaction between a commercial AI system and a federal customer involves some form of system prompt configuration, retrieval-augmented generation setup, or fine-tuning on agency-specific data. Under the clause's logic, those configurations — developed at the contractor's or vendor's expense — automatically become government property. That outcome is categorically different from what [FAR 52.227-14](https://www.acquisition.gov/far/52.227-14) — the existing Rights in Data-General clause — contemplates. FAR 52.227-14 ties government rights to the funding source: the government gets unlimited rights in what the government paid to produce. GSAR 552.239-7001 departs from that principle by claiming rights in improvements made from the contractor's own resources, using the contractor's own technology, simply because the work touched a federal contract.

This matters most for the SaaS and API-first vendors that represent the actual frontier of AI capability. A company cannot grant the government irrevocable rights over configurations of its commercial models without those grants conflicting with terms extended to every other customer. As [Baker Botts has noted](https://ourtake.bakerbotts.com/post/102mnj1/gsas-new-ai-clause-major-changes-for-ai-procurement), the anti-refusal mandate requires AI systems to "not refuse to produce data outputs or conduct analyses based on the Contractor's or Service Provider's discretionary policies" — language that survived into the revised clause's "Unbiased AI Principles" requirement and effectively targets the operational safety layers — system prompts, content filters, use-case restrictions — that commercial vendors rely on for both safety and commercial differentiation.

The [Chamber's comment](https://www.uschamber.com/technology/industry-response-to-proposed-gsar-clause-552-239-7001-basic-safeguarding-of-artificial-intelligence-systems) is precise: portability requirements demanding proprietary components in "open and standard" formats "would effectively operate as a de facto ban on modern SaaS and COTS solutions" and "disqualify most commercially available enterprise solutions." The revised version softens but does not eliminate this tension. Any clause that compels commercial AI vendors to subordinate their standard licensing terms to government requirements will ultimately compel those vendors to exit the federal market rather than compromise their commercial stack. The government will be left buying from vendors whose primary customer is the government — which is precisely the legacy integrator capture the federal enterprise is trying to escape.

## Problem Two: "American AI" Is Definitionally Incoherent

The June revision softened the domestic sourcing mandate from a prohibition to a "maximize" directive, with carve-outs for incidental foreign components that pose no security risk. This is progress. But it does not solve the underlying problem: the concept of an "American AI system" does not map onto how modern AI systems are actually built.

Consider the supply chain of a typical frontier model deployed in a federal agency. The underlying model was pre-trained in the United States — but on data scraped from servers worldwide, by a research team that includes nationals of dozens of countries, using training infrastructure that incorporates open-source components from international contributors, running on chips fabricated in Taiwan, in data centers in Virginia. Is this an "American AI system"? The clause doesn't say. What it says is that contractors must "maximize" use of AI systems "developed, managed, and operated" by U.S.-incorporated entities — but the [Gibson Dunn analysis](https://www.gibsondunn.com/gsa-ai-procurement-rules-would-introduce-new-disclosure-and-use-rights-requirements-for-federal-contractors/) notes that the provision lacks workable definition for globally-built systems, leaving any AI company with non-U.S. cloud infrastructure or R&D labs exposed.

The national security concern motivating this provision is real. China's AI development is state-directed and subject to laws that require companies to cooperate with PRC intelligence services. The government has a genuine interest in ensuring that models processing sensitive federal data are not compromised at the infrastructure level. But that interest is best served by precision — targeted supply-chain security requirements that trace specific risks to specific components — not a definitional standard so broad it produces unresolvable classification disputes about internationally co-developed open-weight models. [CSET's research on AI supply chain security](https://cset.georgetown.edu) has long recommended exactly this kind of risk-tiered approach. GSA's clause, even as revised, doesn't achieve it.

A precedent exists for how to get this right: the [Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification (CMMC)](https://www.acq.osd.mil/cmmc/) framework, which applies tiered security requirements based on the sensitivity of the information handled, not blanket prohibitions on any component with international lineage. CMMC has its own problems — attestation compliance failures, rollout delays — but its basic architecture of risk-tiered, outcome-verified requirements is the right model. A domestic sourcing provision that requires attestation of specific security properties (no unauthorized data exfiltration, no undisclosed model provenance, incident reporting within 72 hours) would achieve genuine security goals. The current "maximize American AI" framing achieves vendor confusion.

## Problem Three: The Compliance Concentration Paradox

GSAR 552.239-7001 contains a structural irony worth stating directly: a clause designed to make the federal government more capable in AI will, as written, make it less capable.

The compliance architecture the clause creates — 30-day AI system disclosure requirements, mandatory flow-down to all service providers and subcontractors, 72-hour incident reporting to federal cybersecurity agencies, prime contractor liability for all upstream vendor compliance, and potential False Claims Act exposure for every non-compliant invoice — is not trivially absorbable overhead. It requires dedicated legal teams, contract management infrastructure, and engineering resources to audit AI supply chains that most commercial AI vendors have never contemplated auditing.

Only large defense contractors have this infrastructure at scale. Booz Allen Hamilton, Leidos, SAIC, General Dynamics — the legacy prime contractor ecosystem — can deploy compliance teams and absorb this overhead as a fixed cost spread across large contract portfolios. [Baker Botts notes](https://ourtake.bakerbotts.com/post/102mnj1/gsas-new-ai-clause-major-changes-for-ai-procurement) that vendors may face False Claims Act liability for upstream providers through "non-negotiable API agreements offering no visibility into compliance" — a risk profile that a $50 billion defense contractor can manage and that an AI startup with $500 million in annual revenue cannot.

The practical result: AI startups and commercial model providers — the companies developing and deploying frontier AI capabilities — exit the federal channel. Large defense integrators, who resell or integrate third-party AI tools behind their compliance umbrella, capture the contracts. The government ends up buying AI that is compliant but not capable, sourced from resellers rather than developers, at a markup that compensates for the compliance infrastructure the prime contractor is providing. [The Nextgov analysis](https://www.nextgov.com/acquisition/2026/05/gsa-preparing-ai-specific-acquisition-reform-rule/413855/) is right that GSA wants to be a "more predictable business partner" to original equipment manufacturers and AI developers — but GSAR 552.239-7001 structures the market so that OEMs and developers find federal contracts non-economic.

## The Right Architecture: Outcome-Based Standards, Not IP Seizure

The government has exactly two legitimate interests in the AI systems it procures: security and operational continuity. It wants to ensure that AI systems processing sensitive government data are not compromised. And it wants to ensure that if a vendor relationship ends, the government can continue to access the AI capabilities it has built its operations around.

Both interests can be served without IP seizure.

On security: GSA should require that AI systems deployed on federal contracts meet specific, verifiable security standards — audit trails, access controls, incident reporting, model provenance disclosure, prohibition on unauthorized data use for model training — aligned with the [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework). The NIST AI RMF's Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage functions provide a mature vocabulary for exactly these requirements. This is outcome-based security: the vendor must demonstrate that its AI system meets these properties, not transfer ownership of it to the government.

The revised clause's four-tier supply chain structure — LLM Developer, LLM System Operator, LLM System Integrator, LLM Service Provider — already shows GSA thinking in NIST AI RMF-adjacent terms about role-based risk allocation. That instinct is correct. The error is coupling it to IP assignment rather than to security outcome attestation. Separate the two: keep the role-based risk structure, replace the ownership claims with outcome-based attestation requirements.

On continuity: [FAR 52.227-14](https://www.acquisition.gov/far/52.227-14)'s existing framework for government rights in data, narrowly scoped to what is genuinely necessary for continued operations, is sufficient. The government should receive rights to the specific configurations and outputs it funded — not irrevocable licenses over underlying commercial models, not ownership of every fine-tuning run that touched government data. As [Holland & Knight's deep dive on the original clause](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/03/gsas-proposed-ai-clause-a-deep-dive) observed, the clause "departs significantly from standard FAR/DFARS frameworks by claiming contractor-acquired IP regardless of funding source" — an overreach the government rights framework has never before asserted in commercial software procurement. The revised version narrows this somewhat; the July 14 session should push to narrow it further, to FAR 52.227-14 parity.

[The Defense Innovation Unit](https://www.diu.mil) has spent years trying to connect the Defense Department with the commercial technology ecosystem precisely because the legacy procurement system freezes the government at yesterday's capability tier. [CISA's work on commercial AI adoption](https://www.cisa.gov/ai) reflects the same recognition. GSAR 552.239-7001, as currently architected, runs directly counter to both efforts.

## The FAR Propagation Risk

The stakes of getting this wrong extend beyond GSA. GSAR clauses apply to GSA-administered contracts — but a finalized, litigated, and politically legitimated GSAR AI clause is the obvious template for a FAR AI clause that applies across all federal civilian agencies. GSA has explicitly [framed GSAR 552.239-7001](https://www.crowell.com/en/insights/client-alerts/gsa-issues-proposed-ai-contract-clause-seeks-feedback) as applicable to "Government-wide contracts (e.g., Federal Supply Schedule, GWACs, and OASIS+)" — the contracting vehicles that govern the vast majority of federal AI procurement. If this clause survives in its current form and propagates into the FAR, every federal agency becomes subject to an IP seizure and domestic sourcing regime that has already driven commercial AI vendors to reconsider federal market participation.

The procedural window is narrow. Written comments are due August 3. The July 14 listening session is the moment for specific, technical objections — not general complaints about government overreach, but precise identification of definitional problems, compliance architecture failures, and specific alternative frameworks (NIST AI RMF, CMMC-style attestation, FAR 52.227-14-scoped continuity rights) that achieve the government's legitimate security goals without the collateral damage.

GSA has shown it will listen. The revised clause is meaningfully narrower than the March 2026 original. That responsiveness is admirable and should be rewarded with substantive engagement, not the cynical assumption that the outcome is predetermined. But the remaining structural problems are not minor drafting issues. They are architectural flaws that will determine whether the federal government can actually access the AI capabilities it needs over the next decade.

The choice is between an AI procurement regime built around outcome-based security standards that the commercial AI industry can comply with — and one built around IP seizure and definitional incoherence that narrows the federal supplier base to those with the compliance infrastructure to absorb it. The listening sessions are where that choice gets made.
