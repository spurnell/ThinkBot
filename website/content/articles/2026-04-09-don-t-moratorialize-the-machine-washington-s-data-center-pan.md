# Don't Moratorialize the Machine: Washington's Data Center Panic Is Strategic Self-Sabotage

Here is a useful heuristic for evaluating technology policy: if the effect of your bill is to slow American infrastructure investment while Chinese infrastructure investment continues unabated, you have not written a safeguard. You have written a concession.

Apply that test to the [AI Data Center Moratorium Act](https://www.sanders.senate.gov/press-releases/news-sanders-ocasio-cortez-announce-ai-data-center-moratorium-act/), introduced March 25 by Senator Sanders and Representative Ocasio-Cortez. The bill would freeze new AI data center construction nationwide until Congress enacts comprehensive AI safeguards — a condition that could take years to satisfy, or never be satisfied at all. Meanwhile, China is building AI compute capacity at a pace that carries no legislative preconditions.

This is not a safeguard. It is a surrender flag.

## The Geopolitical Stakes Washington Is Ignoring

AI compute is the strategic resource of the 21st century in the way that steel, petroleum, and semiconductors defined prior eras. The country that builds the most capable AI infrastructure at scale will train the most capable models, deploy the most productive applications, and accumulate the greatest economic and military advantages from the technology — an assumption embedded in every serious national security assessment coming out of Washington.

A US moratorium does not reduce global AI compute capacity. It redistributes that capacity to jurisdictions with less environmental oversight, less labor protection, and zero democratic accountability. [The Data Innovation Project's analysis](https://datainnovation.org/2026/03/the-sanders-aoc-data-center-moratorium-doesnt-add-up/) of the Sanders-AOC bill makes the arithmetic plain: the moratorium's stated energy and emissions rationale doesn't survive scrutiny, because US restraint doesn't slow global AI development — it just changes who leads it.

If that tradeoff were stated plainly on the Senate floor — "this bill pauses American AI infrastructure and accelerates Chinese AI infrastructure" — it would not pass.

## The Reporting-to-Rationing Pipeline

The moratorium is the most aggressive intervention on the table, but it is not the only one. Senators Warren and Hawley have [pushed the Energy Information Administration](https://www.warren.senate.gov/newsroom/press-releases/warren-hawley-lead-bipartisan-push-for-mandatory-energy-use-reporting-requirements-for-data-centers) to mandate annual energy-use reporting requirements on data centers — framed, naturally, as a transparency and consumer protection measure.

It sounds reasonable. It usually does at this stage.

Reporting mandates are the scaffolding for future regulation, not a substitute for it. The institutional logic is well established: Congress directs disclosure, agencies use the data to identify "problematic" concentrations of use, and the next legislative cycle produces caps, quotas, or efficiency mandates. The framing is transparency; the destination is compute rationing.

Mandatory EIA reporting of data center energy use is not a neutral measure. It is the first step in a policy sequence whose endpoint is visible from here. The bipartisan character of Warren-Hawley — two senators who agree on almost nothing else — does not validate the logic. It just makes the regulatory escalation risk harder to see.

## The Real Problem — And the Real Fix

None of this means the underlying energy concerns are manufactured. They aren't. AI data centers are large, power-hungry industrial loads, and where they interconnect to distribution grids without paying full cost-of-service rates, retail ratepayers can end up cross-subsidizing their electricity costs. That is a legitimate policy problem.

But it has a legitimate solution that isn't a construction freeze. [ITIF's analysis of the five key concerns about AI data centers](https://itif.org/publications/2026/04/06/five-concerns-about-ai-data-centers-and-what-to-do-about-them/) identifies the correct interventions: large industrial loads should pay direct interconnection costs, full cost-of-service rates, and bear the transmission upgrade costs their demand profile creates. Price signals — not prohibition — are how markets correct for externalities.

[FERC's April 30 deadline](https://www.gibsondunn.com/secretary-of-energy-directs-ferc-to-initiate-rulemaking-to-expedite-data-center-and-large-load-interconnection/) to finalize rules governing how large loads (20+ MW) interconnect to the transmission grid is precisely this kind of market-pricing mechanism — and the most consequential AI infrastructure policy action currently in motion. Make data centers pay their true infrastructure costs, let market signals determine where and how fast they build, and ratepayers are protected without handing Beijing a structural advantage.

## Fix the Bottleneck, Not the Demand

There is a further irony in the moratorium proposal. The reason AI data centers are straining portions of the grid isn't that we're building too many of them too fast. It's that we can't build the transmission infrastructure to serve them fast enough.

[ITIF's companion analysis](https://itif.org/publications/2026/04/07/four-reasons-new-ai-data-centers-wont-overwhelm-the-electricity-grid/) documents the core problem: FERC interconnection queue backlogs and NEPA review timelines mean transmission permitting routinely takes five to ten years. The grid can't keep up with load growth — from data centers, electric vehicles, manufacturing reshoring — because we have built a permitting system that treats new transmission lines as more environmentally hazardous than the fossil generation they would replace.

The Sanders-AOC instinct is to throttle demand because the supply side is broken. The correct instinct is to fix the supply side. Permitting reform and transmission queue rationalization expand grid capacity to serve the compute buildout — and every other electrification priority — rather than forcing a choice between them.

## What Pro-Infrastructure Policy Actually Looks Like

Washington is converging on a multi-front effort to slow, report, and restrict AI data center construction. The correct convergence is the opposite: accelerate permitting, clear transmission queues, and enforce market-based cost allocation so ratepayers aren't subsidizing industrial load growth.

Concretely: pass transmission permitting reform that streamlines NEPA review for grid infrastructure. Support FERC's large-load interconnection rulemaking as a legitimate cost-allocation mechanism — and hold the line against efforts to weaken or delay it. Reject the moratorium and the reporting mandate as instruments that treat American compute infrastructure as a liability rather than a strategic asset.

FERC's April 30 deadline is not a bureaucratic milestone — it is the first concrete test of whether Washington can govern AI infrastructure with precision rather than panic. Get that right, and build on it.
