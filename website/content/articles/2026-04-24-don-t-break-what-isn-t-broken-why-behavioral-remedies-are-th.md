Judge Leonie Brinkema's remedies ruling in [*United States v. Google LLC*](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/virginia/vaedce/1:2023cv00108/533508/1410/) sits in her chambers at the Eastern District of Virginia, weeks past the March 31 deadline she set for herself. When it lands — legal analysts expect it imminently — it will be one of the most consequential antitrust decisions in a generation. The Department of Justice is pressing for structural separation: full divestiture of AdX, Google's dominant open-web ad exchange, in what would be the most significant forced breakup of an American company since the Bell System in 1984. Google counters with behavioral guardrails — real-time bidding parity, publisher data portability, elimination of first-look and last-look pricing advantages.

The stakes warrant precision. The DOJ's divestiture demand dramatically overshoots the liability that was actually proven at trial, and [Judge Brinkema's own courtroom skepticism](https://www.adexchanger.com/online-advertising/doj-v-google-judge-brinkema-calls-for-less-ad-tech-window-dressing-in-the-adx-and-dfp-divestiture-debate/) — about phantom buyers, implementation timelines, and second-order antitrust complications — signals she understands this. A carefully designed behavioral remedy can restore competitive conditions in the publisher ad server and ad exchange markets. A forced breakup cannot, and should not, attempt to do more.

## The Liability Was Narrow. The Remedy Must Match.

In her [April 17, 2025 opinion](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/district-courts/virginia/vaedce/1:2023cv00108/533508/1410/), Judge Brinkema found Google liable for illegal monopolization in two specific markets: publisher ad servers (DoubleClick for Publishers, or DFP) and the open-web display ad exchange (AdX). She also found that Google had [illegally tied the two products together](https://digiday.com/marketing/googles-ad-tech-antitrust-remedy-phase-explained-doj-wants-googles-offerings-and-where-the-judge-might-land/), leveraging DFP's dominance among premium publishers to entrench AdX's position in the exchange market. It is a meaningful finding. It is also a bounded one.

Brinkema did not find that Google's entire advertising business is illegally structured. She did not find that Google Ads, its search advertising franchise, or its broader display network constitute antitrust violations. The liability is cabined to specific tying conduct between DFP and AdX in the open-web display market. Remedies law — running back through [*United States v. Microsoft*, 253 F.3d 34 (D.C. Cir. 2001)](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/F3/253/34/576095/), and cited by [both parties in closing arguments](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/cb2c3ded/what-you-need-to-know-from-closing-arguments-in-us-v-google) — requires proportionality. A remedy must unfetter the market from the proven anticompetitive conduct; it must not restructure an entire industry according to a regulator's aspirations about what that industry should look like.

The DOJ's divestiture demand fails this standard. Ordering Google to sell AdX is a structural response to a conduct violation — tying, self-preferencing, informational manipulation of publisher auctions. It punishes organizational scale rather than the specific behavior Brinkema found unlawful. Harm to competitors is not harm to competition, and a divestiture triggered by the former while the law only supports remedying the latter is legally vulnerable on appeal from the moment it is issued.

## No Credible Buyer Exists — and Brinkema Knows It

Even setting aside proportionality, the DOJ has no convincing answer to the buyer problem. [During the remedies phase](https://www.adexchanger.com/antitrust/closing-arguments-are-done-in-the-us-v-google-ad-tech-case/), Judge Brinkema interrupted DOJ counsel directly: "You haven't identified a potential buyer," she said, characterizing the divestiture proposal as "fairly abstract" and telling the government the court needed to be "far more down to earth and concrete."

She was right to push. Index Exchange CEO Andrew Casale revealed [discussions with the DOJ about potentially bidding for AdX](https://digiday.com/marketing/googles-ad-tech-antitrust-remedy-phase-explained-doj-wants-googles-offerings-and-where-the-judge-might-land/), suggesting at least one rival exchange has expressed interest. But a viable buyer for the world's largest open-web ad exchange faces a near-impossible antitrust gauntlet of its own. Any entity large enough to actually operate AdX at scale — to maintain the publisher relationships, the demand-side integrations, and the data infrastructure necessary to keep it functional — would almost certainly trigger Hart-Scott-Rodino review, and likely a full merger investigation. A firm like Microsoft, which operates Xandr (formerly AppNexus), would face separate competition scrutiny before closing. Smaller exchanges lack the capitalization and technical capacity to absorb AdX at its current volume.

The result is an acquisition paradox: the DOJ seeks divestiture to promote competition in the ad exchange market, but any entity capable of running AdX competitively attracts the antitrust scrutiny that would prevent the acquisition from closing. A spun-off AdX without a capable, cleared owner is not a competitive asset. It is an orphaned entity, likely to deteriorate, to consolidate with a distressed buyer at below-market value, or ultimately to be reacquired — vindicating none of the structural ambitions behind the remedy.

## The AT&T Analogy Is Seductive. It Is Also Wrong.

Breakup proponents invoke the 1984 Bell System divestiture as proof that structural antitrust remedies can work. The historical precedent is real — [*United States v. AT&T*](https://www.fjc.gov/history/spotlight-judicial-history/breakup-ma-bell) is the canonical American example of a forced breakup, and the long-distance market did eventually become more competitive in its wake. But the structural analogy between AT&T and Google's ad stack does not survive contact with the facts.

The Bell System operated [twenty-two local telephone networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breakup_of_the_Bell_System) — geographically distinct, physically separable infrastructure franchised by state governments as regulated natural monopolies. Each Baby Bell was a complete, self-sustaining business: its own customers, physical plant, employees, and regional territory. Divestiture was architecturally clean because the assets were discrete objects in physical space, bound to ZIP codes, not to software logic.

Google's ad stack is integrated code executing billions of real-time auctions daily. AdX's functionality depends on continuous data signals flowing between DFP (which sits on publisher pages), Google Ads (which represents advertiser demand), and AdX (which matches the two in milliseconds). Carving out AdX while leaving DFP, Google Ads, and the broader data infrastructure intact does not create a functioning competitive exchange — it creates an orphan deprived of the signal inputs it needs to operate at parity. The integration advantages that would be severed are not all the product of anticompetitive conduct; some reflect genuine network economies that are lawful and efficiency-generating. Divestiture cannot distinguish between the two.

Structural remedies are also not self-reinforcing. [AT&T reconsolidated over the following decades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_v._AT&T), absorbing SBC, BellSouth, and others to reassemble much of the original system. In a faster-moving software market, reconsolidation would likely occur faster. Judge Brinkema noted this dynamic herself: [the ad tech landscape could "change vastly in the coming years"](https://www.adexchanger.com/antitrust/closing-arguments-are-done-in-the-us-v-google-ad-tech-case/) as AI transforms digital advertising. A divestiture transaction requiring years of implementation would produce a remedy calibrated to the market as it existed in 2024, not the market that will exist when any deal closes.

## What a Real Behavioral Remedy Looks Like

Behavioral remedies have a deserved reputation for weakness — not because the concept is flawed, but because poorly drafted, inadequately monitored consent decrees deserve the criticism they receive. The DOJ [warned in closing arguments](https://www.adexchanger.com/antitrust/closing-arguments-are-done-in-the-us-v-google-ad-tech-case/) that Google would "test every word, every punctuation" of any behavioral ruling. That is a reason to draft behavioral remedies with precision and enforce them with real institutional teeth — not a reason to abandon them. The answer to weak consent decrees is stronger consent decrees, backed by explicit structural contingency if compliance fails.

As [ITIF's analysis of the DOJ's remedies proposal](https://itif.org/publications/2025/05/12/the-dojs-problematic-remedies-proposal-google-ad-tech-case/) concluded, a targeted behavioral approach is both legally proportionate and practically executable. A well-constructed remedy package for the AdX case would include:

**Real-time bidding parity.** Google should be required to make AdX's real-time bidding functionality available to all publisher ad servers — not just DFP — on nondiscriminatory terms. This directly addresses the tying conduct Brinkema found unlawful. [Google has proposed a version of this](https://blog.google/company-news/outreach-and-initiatives/public-policy/our-remedies-proposal-in-the-doj-ad-tech-case/); the court should impose it with enforceable benchmarks rather than accept voluntary compliance.

**First-look and last-look prohibition.** The specific informational advantages that allowed AdX to observe competing bids before finalizing its own must be categorically enjoined, with auditable compliance mechanisms and technical monitoring. Google's own proposal includes this; the question is enforcement rigor.

**Unified Pricing Rules reform.** Google's Unified Pricing Rules [effectively prevented publishers from setting differential price floors](https://digiday.com/marketing/googles-ad-tech-antitrust-remedy-phase-explained-doj-wants-googles-offerings-and-where-the-judge-might-land/) across competing exchanges, foreclosing a key tool publishers could use to favor AdX rivals. These must be enjoined in their current form.

**Publisher data portability.** Publishers should have the right to receive campaign performance data in interoperable, documented formats, enabling meaningful switching to competing ad servers without a data cliff. Google proposes "reasonable support" for data migration — the court should define "reasonable" with specificity.

**Independent technical monitor.** A court-appointed technical monitor with full audit rights over AdX's auction mechanics — not Google's self-certification — should verify ongoing compliance and report non-compliance directly to the court, with contempt exposure and the explicit possibility of escalation to structural separation if behavioral compliance is willfully evaded.

This is the approach that [Judge Brinkema's own questioning pointed toward](https://www.adexchanger.com/online-advertising/doj-v-google-judge-brinkema-calls-for-less-ad-tech-window-dressing-in-the-adx-and-dfp-divestiture-debate/): enforceable reform that restores competition without destabilizing the open web's advertising infrastructure. The court has contempt power. A failed divestiture producing an orphaned entity and years of transactional freeze is a far worse outcome — with far less enforcement leverage — than a monitored behavioral decree with real escalation risk.

## The Consumer Welfare Standard Cuts Against Breakup

The consumer welfare standard asks a specific question: does the challenged conduct harm consumers — publishers and advertisers in this case — through higher prices, foreclosed choice, or degraded quality? The answer is yes, in specific ways, for specific conduct: the tying, the first-look advantage, the pricing floor manipulation. Behavioral remedies target those specific harms and can be calibrated to eliminate them.

Divestiture asks a different question: is Google too large? That is not a cognizable antitrust question under U.S. law. It is structural preference dressed up in legal language. Advertisers and publishers who use Google's integrated stack benefit from real efficiencies — fill rates, match quality, latency reduction — that would be disrupted, not preserved, by forced structural separation. The fact that some of those efficiencies were built on a foundation that included anticompetitive conduct does not mean they evaporate when the conduct is enjoined. A behavioral remedy can strip the unlawful advantages while leaving the legitimate efficiencies intact. Divestiture simply cuts.

The remedial standard running through *Microsoft* requires that a remedy "unfetter a market from anticompetitive conduct" — not that it punish a firm for its size. Judge Brinkema found anticompetitive *conduct*. The remedy should match what she found: precise, enforceable, monitored, and proportionate. On those criteria, behavioral remedies win. On those criteria, AdX divestiture fails.

---

*This analysis was completed before Judge Brinkema issued her remedies ruling. The framework above provides the standard against which that decision should be evaluated whenever it arrives. A behavioral remedy — however imperfectly drafted — is legally proportionate and practically executable. A divestiture order, absent an identified buyer and a credible transaction structure, arrives before the Fourth Circuit carrying the very objections Judge Brinkema herself raised from the bench.*
