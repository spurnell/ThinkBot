On September 2, 2025, Judge Amit Mehta issued his remedies order in *United States v. Google* — the most consequential antitrust ruling against an American technology company in twenty-five years. The order's core logic is sound: prohibit the exclusive default distribution contracts that Judge Mehta found unlawful, mandate limited search data-sharing with qualified rivals, and establish a technical oversight committee to enforce compliance. What the order does not do — and was right not to do — is require Google to divest Chrome or Android.

The Department of Justice disagrees. In its [cross-appeal before the D.C. Circuit](https://searchengineland.com/doj-states-appeal-google-search-antitrust-remedies-ruling-468230), filed in February 2026 and joined by thirty-five state attorneys general, the DOJ presses for structural relief that Judge Mehta carefully considered and explicitly rejected. Google has filed its own appeal challenging the data-sharing requirements and the scope of the technical committee. Both parties ask the circuit court to second-guess the district court's remedies judgment.

The D.C. Circuit should decline both invitations. Behavioral remedies — prohibitions on exclusionary distribution agreements plus targeted data-access mandates — address the specific foreclosure mechanism the court found unlawful. The DOJ's demand for Chrome and Android divestiture is structurally untethered from the liability finding, would impose severe consumer welfare losses in exchange for speculative competitive benefits, and asks a post-*Loper Bright* circuit court to override a well-reasoned district court remedies record on nothing more than the agency's own structural preference. The doctrine should win this appeal — not either party.

## I. What the Liability Finding Actually Held

Before evaluating any remedy, precision about the wrong is essential. Judge Mehta's August 2024 liability opinion found that Google unlawfully maintained its monopoly in general search and search text advertising through exclusive default distribution contracts — agreements with Apple, Samsung, wireless carriers, and browser developers that guaranteed Google's placement as the pre-installed default search engine on virtually every consumer device in the United States. The mechanism of harm was foreclosure of the default access channel. Google's rivals — DuckDuckGo, Microsoft Bing, and potential future entrants — were structurally denied the default position that accounts for the overwhelming majority of search queries initiated on any given device.

The liability finding was not that Google acquired too many companies. It was not that vertical integration of a browser is anticompetitive per se. It was not that being large and successful in search is unlawful. The court found a specific exclusionary practice — pay-to-foreclose distribution agreements — and the remedy must address that practice. This is not a technicality. It is the load-bearing premise of any proportionate antitrust remedy.

## II. Remedies Doctrine: The Proportionality Requirement

The Supreme Court has been consistent on the relationship between violation and remedy. In [*Ford Motor Co. v. United States*](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/405/562/), 405 U.S. 562 (1972), the Court held that antitrust relief must be designed "to fit the violation" — structured to "unfetter a market from anticompetitive conduct" and restore competition to the state disturbed by the illegal conduct, not to engineer whatever market structure an agency prefers at a given moment. Antitrust remedies are not a license for courts to redesign industries; they are a tool for correcting specifically identified competitive distortions.

The D.C. Circuit applied this principle with particular force in [*United States v. Microsoft Corp.*](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/F3/253/34/489770/), 253 F.3d 34 (D.C. Cir. 2001) — binding precedent directly on point here. When Judge Jackson ordered Microsoft split into a Windows company and an applications company, the D.C. Circuit unanimously vacated the structural breakup. The court found the remedy insufficiently tethered to the specific anticompetitive conduct, the remedies proceedings inadequate, and the district court's record insufficient to support a structural separation of that magnitude. The case was remanded to Judge Colleen Kollar-Kotelly, who entered behavioral consent decrees. Microsoft's monopoly in PC operating systems did not outlast the decade that followed — not because of the consent decrees, but because technological change opened the field to competitors the company had worked to foreclose.

*Microsoft* is not merely illustrative. It is the circuit's controlling statement on the outer boundaries of antitrust remedies in platform monopoly cases, and it applies squarely to the cross-appeal here. The liability in the search case is about exclusive distribution contracts — structurally analogous to Microsoft's browser-tying conduct. The proposed structural remedy (Chrome divestiture) bears the same relationship to the violation that the Microsoft breakup bore to the IE bundling liability: it restructures the company far beyond what addressing the specific wrong requires. The D.C. Circuit got this right in 2001. It should get it right again.

## III. Chrome Divestiture: Structurally Untethered and Consumer-Harmful

The DOJ's cross-appeal rests on the theory that Chrome is a distribution bottleneck — that Google's ownership of the dominant browser is part of the mechanism through which it locked up default search placement. This is a creative framing, but it does not survive the liability record or consumer welfare analysis.

Chrome is not the liability. The liability is the exclusive contracts Google used to ensure that Chrome — and every other browser, device, and carrier relationship — carried Google as default. The remedy for exclusive contracts is prohibiting exclusive contracts, which Judge Mehta's order does. Requiring divestiture of Chrome would punish Google for owning a browser that was used as a vehicle for unlawful distribution agreements — not for the unlawful agreements themselves. That is remedial overreach by any standard, and it is the kind of aspirational industrial policy dressed up as antitrust enforcement that courts applying *Ford Motor* and *Microsoft* have consistently rejected.

The consumer welfare costs of Chrome divestiture are not speculative. [Chrome holds approximately 65% of global browser market share](https://gs.statcounter.com/browser-market-share), a position that reflects genuine product quality. The Chromium open-source project and the V8 JavaScript engine — both originating from Google's investment — underpin not only Chrome but Microsoft Edge, Brave, Opera, and the Node.js runtime environment that powers a substantial fraction of modern web infrastructure. Chrome's active participation in W3C and WHATWG web standards processes has materially shaped WebAssembly, Progressive Web App specifications, and browser security architecture. These are not incidental byproducts of an anticompetitive product; they are public goods that require sustained, revenue-dependent R&D investment.

That revenue dependence is the DOJ's critical unresolved problem. As the [American Action Forum](https://www.americanactionforum.org/insight/the-impact-of-googles-antitrust-remedies-on-the-future-of-monopolies/) has noted, the structural cross-appeal does not identify a viable monetization model for a standalone Chrome. The most analytically coherent outcomes of divestiture — acquisition by a firm with competing search or advertising interests, private equity ownership with attendant underinvestment in browser security and standards, or gradual quality decline — none of these outcomes improves competition in the general search market that the court found monopolized. What they do is fragment the open-web standards environment, degrade a browser that hundreds of millions of users depend upon, and generate permanent economic costs in exchange for a theoretical competitive benefit in an adjacent market.

When antitrust remedies produce costs in consumer welfare without proportionate gains in competitive welfare, the error cost asymmetry runs decisively against intervention. As [Judge Frank Easterbrook's foundational framework](https://laweconcenter.org/resources/the-limits-of-antitrust-by-frank-easterbrook/) established, false positives in antitrust enforcement — remedies that impose costs exceeding their competitive benefits — are systematically harder to undo than false negatives, because markets can erode monopoly through entry and rivalry while judicial errors persist indefinitely. A Chrome divestiture, once accomplished, cannot be reversed. The D.C. Circuit should demand that the DOJ explain the monetization model and the welfare calculus before endorsing any structural theory the district court already rejected on the record.

## IV. The Data-Sharing Remedy: Right in Design, Requires Rigorous Implementation

Google's appeal challenges the data-sharing requirements — specifically, the obligation to share user-interaction data (queries, clicks, and dwell times) with qualified search competitors. Google argues the mandate is technically overbroad and creates privacy risks. On the first point, Google is wrong. On the second, it raises a legitimate implementation concern that the D.C. Circuit can address without reversing the remedy's foundational logic.

Search quality is largely a function of the feedback loop between user behavior and algorithmic ranking. A search engine needs to observe what users click, how long they dwell, and when they immediately return to the results page — signals that distinguish relevant from irrelevant results. DuckDuckGo, Microsoft Bing, and potential future entrants cannot replicate that feedback loop without scale, and scale requires users, and users go where quality is already highest. This is the canonical data-network-effects barrier to entry: the incumbent's advantage compounds over time precisely because its quality lead grows as its user base grows.

Exclusive default prohibitions break the distribution barrier. Data-sharing addresses the data-scale barrier. Together, as the [Congressional Research Service's analysis of the remedies order](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11362) documents, these two interventions attack both prongs of the foreclosure mechanism in a targeted, reversible way that structural divestiture cannot match. The case for this approach has attracted scholarly support across the ideological spectrum: [The Regulatory Review's analysis of the remedies framework](https://www.theregreview.org/2025/11/16/spotlight-antitrust-remedies-after-google/), drawing on Professor Herbert Hovenkamp's assessment, finds that sharing user-interaction data enables genuine competition on quality — precisely what behavioral remedies are designed to achieve. The D.C. Circuit should affirm the data-sharing framework.

It should also, however, require that the technical committee's implementation address three identifiable risks that Google's appeal legitimately raises:

**Privacy externalities**: Query data is sensitive personal information. Any disclosure regime must include aggregation thresholds, de-identification requirements, and access controls that protect user privacy while serving legitimate competitive purposes. A data-sharing mandate that creates privacy externalities for users is not a public benefit even if it is a competitive one. The committee's technical specifications should be subject to review by independent privacy experts before going into effect.

**Competitive gaming of the disclosure regime**: A broadly drawn data-sharing requirement could incentivize rivals to reverse-engineer Google's ranking algorithms rather than invest in developing superior methodologies of their own. The competitive benefit of mandatory sharing accrues when rivals use the data to improve their products — not when they use it as a free-rider subsidy for R&D they would otherwise have to fund. The committee should monitor usage patterns and build in compliance metrics tied to genuine quality improvement, not mere data consumption.

**Duration and sunset provisions**: The data-sharing mandate should be calibrated to the time horizon needed for rivals to develop competitive scale — not treated as a permanent subsidy to incumbents who would have no reason to build data scale independently if the mandate removes the incentive. The D.C. Circuit should affirm the remedy with an instruction that the district court build sunset and review triggers tied to measurable entry metrics: default market share of rivals, query volume diversification, user choice rates following distribution prohibition enforcement.

These are implementation instructions, not grounds for reversal. The D.C. Circuit should affirm and remand with guidance, not vacate a remedy whose foundational design is proportionate and well-supported in the record.

## V. Post-*Loper Bright* and the Limits of Agency Remedial Theory

One doctrinal development since the liability finding deserves explicit attention: [*Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*](https://www.scotusblog.com/cases/case-files/loper-bright-enterprises-v-raimondo/), 144 S. Ct. 2244 (2024), which overruled *Chevron* deference and directed federal courts to independently determine what statutes require rather than defer to agency interpretations of ambiguous legal questions. *Loper Bright* is not an antitrust case. But its animating principle — that courts, not agencies, resolve questions of law — applies with force in the remedies context, where the DOJ asks a circuit court to override a detailed district court remedies record in favor of the agency's preferred structural outcome.

The standard of appellate review for antitrust remedies is abuse of discretion. Judge Mehta developed an extensive evidentiary record on the Chrome and Android divestiture questions — including expert testimony, industry analysis, and competitive impact assessments — before concluding that behavioral remedies were adequate and proportionate to the violation. For the D.C. Circuit to reverse that judgment, it must find not merely that the DOJ's structural theory is plausible, but that the district court's rejection of it was an abuse of discretion. Given the doctrinal support for behavioral remedies in *Microsoft* and *Ford Motor*, and the thoroughness of the remedies proceedings below, that standard is very difficult to satisfy.

The DOJ's cross-appeal is, in structural terms, an invitation for the circuit court to function as a deference mechanism for agency remedial preferences — elevating what the DOJ wanted over what an independent district court found appropriate after examining the full record. This is exactly the appellate posture that *Loper Bright* renders untenable. The D.C. Circuit that refused to defer to agency statutory interpretations should be no more inclined to defer to DOJ remedial theories that district courts have already considered and rejected on the merits. The DOJ is asking courts to expand the ceiling on antitrust remedies at precisely the moment when courts have reasserted their independence from agency interpretive authority — a posture the circuit should recognize and reject.

## VI. The AdX Stakes: Why This Case Frames the Next One

*United States v. Google* (search) does not exist in isolation. In April 2025, [Judge Leonie Brinkema found Google liable](https://www.stblaw.com/about-us/publications/view/2025/04/25/district-court-rules-google-is-a-monopolist-in-ad-tech) for monopolizing the publisher ad server and open-web ad exchange markets in *United States v. Google* (ad tech). The remedies phase in that case is pending; the DOJ seeks structural divestiture of Google's ad server (DFP) and its ad exchange (AdX) as independent entities — relief that would, if ordered, represent the largest corporate structural breakup since the AT&T divestiture in 1984.

The D.C. Circuit's framework-setting role in the search remedies appeal will directly shape the ad tech remedies debate. If the circuit affirms behavioral remedies in the search case — establishing that proportionality between remedy and violation is a mandatory doctrinal requirement, and that structural relief must be specifically justified by the liability record rather than chosen for its industrial policy appeal — it creates significant doctrinal headwind against the DOJ's structural demands in the ad tech remedies phase.

This sequencing matters because the ad tech divestiture question is, if anything, more technically fraught than the Chrome question. AdX is a software-based real-time auction exchange deeply integrated with DFP's supply-side infrastructure and Google Ads' demand-side bidding. As Judge Brinkema herself signaled during the remedies trial, identifying a viable acquirer for a divested AdX — an entity with the technical capacity and financial resources to operate a real-time exchange at scale, absent Google's search and advertising revenue base — is not a problem with an obvious solution. The structural remedy's empirical vulnerability in the ad tech case is precisely the viable-buyer problem that the search case helps establish as a doctrinal requirement.

Conversely, a D.C. Circuit ruling that granted the DOJ's structural cross-appeal and ordered Chrome divestiture would send a signal that courts are willing to impose structural remedies untethered from specific liability findings whenever the market at stake is sufficiently large. That signal would substantially expand the DOJ's remedial ambitions in the pending ad tech phase — where the case for behavioral alternatives, grounded in auction-design prohibitions, interoperability mandates, and data-access requirements, is at least as strong as in the search case. Affirming the behavioral framework here protects against remedial overreach there.

## Conclusion: The Doctrine Should Win

The strongest version of this argument is not that Google prevails in the D.C. Circuit. It is that the doctrine prevails. Remedies proportionality, consumer welfare analysis, and post-*Loper Bright* judicial independence from expansive agency remedial theories are the operative principles here, and they all point in the same direction.

Judge Mehta found Google liable for exclusive default distribution contracts. The remedy for exclusive default distribution contracts is to prohibit exclusive default distribution contracts — which the remedies order does — and to address the data-scale barrier those contracts created in favor of the incumbent — which the data-sharing mandate does. These are targeted, forward-looking, reversible interventions that restore access to the competitive arena the illegal conduct foreclosed. They are the antitrust equivalent of removing a thumb from the scale, not redesigning the scale.

Requiring Chrome divestiture would be something categorically different: a structural reorganization of a company's product portfolio imposed for conduct that the behavioral remedies already address, at enormous consumer cost, in pursuit of a market structure the DOJ prefers but the liability record does not require. That is industrial policy wearing antitrust's clothes. The Supreme Court identified this error in *Ford Motor*. The D.C. Circuit identified it in *Microsoft*. It should identify it again here.

The D.C. Circuit should affirm the behavioral framework, remand the data-sharing implementation with instructions on privacy safeguards and sunset provisions, and reject the DOJ's structural cross-appeal. The remedy should match the wrong. The doctrine requires nothing more — and should accept nothing less.
