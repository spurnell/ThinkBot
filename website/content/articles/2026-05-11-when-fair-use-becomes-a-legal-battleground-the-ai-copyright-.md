On May 5, five major publishers — Hachette, Macmillan, McGraw Hill, Elsevier, and Cengage — joined bestselling author Scott Turow in [filing a class-action lawsuit](https://www.npr.org/2026/05/05/nx-s1-5812623/scott-turow-meta-lawsuit) against Meta and its CEO, Mark Zuckerberg. The complaint alleges that Zuckerberg ["personally authorized and actively encouraged"](https://variety.com/2026/digital/news/meta-ai-mark-zuckerberg-copyright-infringement-lawsuit-publishers-scott-turow-1236738383/) Meta's use of millions of pirated books and journal articles — sourced from torrent repositories like LibGen and Anna's Archive — to train its Llama family of language models.

It is a striking allegation — and if the factual record bears it out, Meta should lose. Existing copyright law, applied by existing courts, is sufficient to make that happen.

But here is the problem: Congress is treating this lawsuit as a warrant for sweeping legislation that goes far beyond the piracy question at its center. Senator Marsha Blackburn's [TRUMP AMERICA AI Act](https://www.blackburn.senate.gov/2026/3/technology/blackburn-releases-discussion-draft-of-national-policy-framework-for-artificial-intelligence/3b3b6458-b6c7-478b-9859-374949586765) would amend Section 107 of the Copyright Act to declare that AI training on copyrighted works is categorically *not* fair use — a blanket prohibition written as if no distinction exists between a company that torrents pirated books and a researcher who trains on lawfully accessed public data. Meanwhile, the bipartisan [TRAIN Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2455/text) — a far narrower transparency mechanism — risks being overshadowed by Blackburn's more aggressive framework.

Courts, not Congress, are resolving this correctly. Federal judges are quietly developing a doctrinal distinction that maps onto the actual ethical fault lines: lawfully acquired data used for transformative purposes qualifies as fair use; pirated or unlicensed data does not. That is the right framework. Congress is about to legislate it out of existence.

## The Meta Lawsuit Is a Piracy Case, Not a Fair Use Case

It is worth being precise about what the publisher lawsuit actually alleges, because the distinction matters enormously for the legislative response.

The plaintiffs do not merely argue that Meta used their books to train Llama. They allege that Meta deliberately sourced training data from known pirate repositories — "notorious shadow libraries" built on theft — and that this decision was made at the highest levels of the company. [According to the complaint](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/articles/publishers-and-authors-file-class-action-lawsuit-against-meta-and-zuckerberg-for-willful-copyright-infringement-to-develop-llama-ai-models/), this was not inadvertent indexing of material that happened to be freely available online. It was, the plaintiffs contend, deliberate appropriation of pirated content with full knowledge of its origins.

If those allegations hold up under discovery, the fair use question is not a close call. The [U.S. Copyright Office's Part 3 report on generative AI training](https://www.copyright.gov/ai/Copyright-and-Artificial-Intelligence-Part-3-Generative-AI-Training-Report-Pre-Publication-Version.pdf), released in May 2025, was explicit on this point: "making commercial use of vast troves of copyrighted works to produce expressive content that competes with them in existing markets, especially where this is accomplished through illegal access, goes beyond established fair use boundaries." The existing copyright statute, applied by existing courts, handles this case. No new legislation is required.

What Congress is doing instead is using the most extreme, most sympathetic-to-plaintiffs version of the AI training dispute — a case involving alleged piracy, alleged personal executive direction, and alleged bad-faith conduct — to justify a statutory rule that governs every AI training scenario, including ones that look nothing like Meta's alleged conduct.

That is not targeted lawmaking. It is opportunism.

## Courts Are Building the Right Doctrine — Congress Should Let It Mature

Before Congress acts, its members would benefit from reading what federal judges have actually been doing on this question.

In June 2025, Judge Vince Chhabria of the Northern District of California [ruled in *Kadrey v. Meta*](https://www.goodwinlaw.com/en/insights/publications/2025/06/alerts-practices-aiml-northern-district-of-california-judge-rules) that Meta's use of a dataset of nearly 200,000 books to train its Llama model constituted transformative fair use. The ruling turned on a straightforward application of the four-factor fair use analysis: the training process ingested books to produce statistical representations of language — it did not reproduce the works themselves. As [Morrison Foerster observed in its 2026 AI copyright litigation analysis](https://www.mofo.com/resources/insights/260210-ai-trends-for-2026-copyright-litigation), courts are developing a "judicial consensus that training a general-purpose AI model is highly transformative," a factor that weighs heavily in favor of fair use.

Judge Chhabria was careful about what his ruling did and did not mean. The decision, he wrote, "stands only for the proposition that these plaintiffs made the wrong arguments and failed to develop a record in support of the right one." He left open the market-dilution theory — the argument that AI outputs compete directly with the works that trained them — as an avenue through which plaintiffs might yet prevail. That is sensible judicial caution: the court recognized a genuine fair use case while preserving doctrinal space for edge cases involving real market harm.

A second California judge [reached the same conclusion on nearly identical grounds](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/two-california-district-judges-rule-using-books-train-ai-fair-use), finding AI training on books to be fair use. Decisions involving OpenAI and Google are expected in 2026, which should further clarify the doctrine. As [Bochner PLLC's April 2026 analysis](https://www.bochner.law/news/publications/2026-04-10-beyond-the-training-data-the-shifting-battleground-in-ai-copyright-law) notes, the litigation battleground is already shifting from training-data acquisition — where a workable fair use framework is emerging — toward AI outputs, where the harder market-harm questions will be decided.

This is exactly how common law is supposed to work. Courts are fact-sensitive institutions capable of distinguishing between a company that torrents pirated libraries and a developer who fine-tunes a model on lawfully accessed public-domain texts. Congress, by contrast, writes categorical rules that apply equally to both. The [Computer & Communications Industry Association recognized this](https://ccianet.org/news/2026/04/ccia-urges-court-to-affirm-fair-use-protections-for-generative-ai-training/) when it filed an amicus brief earlier this year urging a federal court to affirm fair use protections for AI training — noting that [*Google LLC v. Oracle America, Inc.*](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/20pdf/18-956_d18f.pdf) already supports a transformative-use framework for software and data processing.

Congress should not legislate over a judicial doctrine that is maturing in the right direction.

## Blackburn's Categorical Prohibition Would Shatter the AI Ecosystem

Senator Blackburn's TRUMP AMERICA AI Act, introduced as a discussion draft in March 2026, contains a provision that would rewrite Section 107 of the Copyright Act to categorically exclude AI training from fair use. Section 1501 of the draft declares that unauthorized use of copyrighted works for "training, fine-tuning, developing, or creating AI" shall not constitute fair use.

The [Foundation for American Innovation](https://www.thefai.org/posts/the-trump-america-ai-act-is-a-disaster) was blunt in its assessment: "This provision is flatly incompatible with both the administration's stated position and a thriving American AI industry." That is an understatement. The White House itself, in its [National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence](https://www.whitehouse.gov/releases/2026/03/president-donald-j-trump-unveils-national-ai-legislative-framework/) released the same month, explicitly recommended that Congress avoid taking "any actions that would impact the judiciary's resolution of whether training on copyrighted material constitutes fair use" — a direct conflict with the Blackburn bill that the administration has yet to reconcile.

The practical consequences of a categorical prohibition deserve examination. Under the Blackburn framework, any AI developer who trains on data containing copyrighted works without an explicit license would be in statutory violation — regardless of how the data was obtained, regardless of whether the use is transformative, and regardless of whether any actual market harm occurred. This is not a calibrated legal rule. It is a content gatekeeper's veto.

The developers best positioned to comply are the ones who can afford to negotiate blanket licensing deals with major publishers, music labels, and news organizations. OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, and a handful of other frontier labs have the legal departments, the balance sheets, and the leverage to reach such agreements — or to structure their data pipelines around licensed corpora. Smaller independent research labs, academic institutions, and open-source AI projects do not. They depend on the fair use doctrine to train on public internet data — the same data that has driven most of the open-source AI progress of the past five years.

The effect of a categorical prohibition is straightforward: it cartelizes AI development. The three or four labs large enough to pay the content gatekeepers' innovation tax survive. Everyone else exits. The [open-source AI ecosystem](https://techcrunch.com/2025/10/06/meta-llama-everything-you-need-to-know-about-the-open-generative-ai-model/) — the Llama derivatives, the Mistral fine-tunes, the academic research models, the specialized domain models built by startups — collapses under compliance costs it cannot absorb.

If US-China AI competition is the frame through which Congress is viewing this legislation, it should recognize that China's AI developers would face none of these constraints. Restricting fair use for AI training is not a safety measure. It is a unilateral disarmament of the American open-source AI community.

## The TRAIN Act Is the Proportionate Intervention

Not all AI copyright legislation deserves the same critique. The TRAIN Act — introduced as [S.2455](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2455/text) in the Senate and [H.R. 7209](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/7209/text) in the House on a bipartisan basis — takes a meaningfully different approach.

Rather than adjudicating the fair use question through statute, the TRAIN Act creates an administrative subpoena mechanism: a copyright holder who has a good-faith belief that their work was used in AI training can petition a federal district court clerk to issue a subpoena compelling disclosure of the relevant training data records. The mechanism is discovery-oriented, not prohibition-oriented. It gives rights holders information — which is what they actually need to bring viable litigation — without presuming that the underlying use was unlawful.

This is the right model. It is targeted, proportionate, and consistent with the principle that courts — not Congress — should decide the merits of specific fair use claims. A rights holder who discovers through the TRAIN Act process that their work was used in training can then decide whether to bring suit, negotiate a licensing arrangement, or take no action. The legislation itself does not predetermine the outcome.

The TRAIN Act's transparency mechanism represents the appropriate legislative intervention in this space. It preserves judicial doctrine maturation while giving creators meaningful information rights — without collapsing the crucial distinction between lawfully obtained transformative training and deliberate piracy into a single categorical prohibition.

## Compulsory Licensing Would Backfire — History Shows Why

When categorical prohibition is rejected, its defenders often propose a fallback: compulsory licensing. Under this model, AI developers would be legally required to pay into a statutory licensing pool — administered by the Copyright Office or a collective rights organization — in exchange for the right to train on copyrighted works. The surface appeal is obvious: creators get compensated, developers get legal certainty.

The music streaming industry demonstrates why this does not work as advertised.

The compulsory mechanical license established by Congress has been the subject of chronic litigation, administrative proceedings, and legislative patches for over a century. Even after the [Music Modernization Act](https://www.copyright.gov/music-modernization/) of 2018 attempted to rationalize the system for the streaming era, disputes over statutory rates continue to drag through the Copyright Royalty Board, with platforms and rights holders locked in adversarial pricing cycles that serve neither party particularly well. A [Stanford Law Review analysis](https://review.law.stanford.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/3/2020/04/Victor-72-Stan.-L.-Rev.-915.pdf) found that compulsory licensing regimes systematically suppress market-clearing negotiations and create chronic underinvestment in both licensing infrastructure and new platform development.

Transpose this dynamic to AI training data. A statutory licensing pool would require determining the value of billions of documents across thousands of content categories, setting rates that apply across radically different model architectures and training objectives, and administering payments to hundreds of thousands of rights holders. The Copyright Royalty Board, which struggles to handle music streaming rates after years of institutional experience, is not equipped for this task. The result would be years of rate proceedings, followed by litigation over whether the rates are adequate, followed by legislative patches to fix the dysfunction — all while the licensing uncertainty those patches were supposed to resolve continues to chill AI development.

Rights holders have legitimate interests in fair compensation for genuinely infringing uses. The mechanism for vindicating those interests is copyright litigation under existing law, enhanced by the discovery tools the TRAIN Act would provide — not a compulsory licensing regime that history suggests will fail both creators and developers.

## What Congress Should Actually Do

The legislative agenda on AI copyright should be short and targeted:

**Pass the TRAIN Act.** The administrative subpoena mechanism is workable, proportionate, and consistent with the judicial fair use framework that is developing correctly. Rights holders who believe their works were used in training without lawful basis will have the discovery tools to find out. Courts will adjudicate the merits.

**Reject the Blackburn categorical prohibition.** Section 1501 of the TRUMP AMERICA AI Act should be stripped from any legislation that advances. It is in direct tension with the Trump administration's own stated position, inconsistent with federal courts' emerging fair use analysis, and would achieve incumbent entrenchment rather than creator protection. The White House has said as much.

**Let courts develop the doctrine.** Decisions in the OpenAI and Google training-data cases are expected this year. The outputs-focused litigation — including [*Disney and Universal v. Midjourney*](https://www.law.georgetown.edu/tech-institute/research-insights/insights/disney-nbc-universal-and-dreamworks-file-major-ip-lawsuit-against-ai-image-generator-midjourney/), which probes whether AI-generated content copies expressive elements of specific works — will address the harder market-harm questions. Congress should not foreclose doctrinal options that courts are actively refining.

**Enforce existing law against actual piracy.** If the Meta lawsuit's allegations of deliberate torrent-sourced training data are accurate, they describe conduct that violates copyright law as currently written. No new statute is needed to reach bad-actor piracy. Courts are capable of distinguishing willful infringement from lawful transformative use — it is precisely the distinction that Judge Chhabria drew. The lesson of *Kadrey v. Meta* is not that AI training always evades fair use liability; it is that the specific plaintiffs in that case did not develop the factual record needed to overcome a strong transformative use argument. Subsequent cases, including the new publisher suit, may develop a different record.

---

The AI copyright dispute is real, and the interests of creators who depend on copyright protection for their livelihoods deserve serious engagement. But serious engagement means distinguishing piracy from transformative use, courts from Congress, and targeted transparency tools from content gatekeepers' vetoes over foundational AI research. Right now, the legislative response is doing none of these things. Congress should take a step back before it flattens a developing judicial doctrine into a blunt statutory rule that entrenches incumbents, eliminates open-source development, and achieves none of its stated goals.
