Congress cannot hold two positions simultaneously. It cannot declare China the existential AI threat and defund the university labs that train American AI researchers. It cannot proclaim American technological preeminence a national security imperative and then systematically dismantle the foundational research infrastructure that built that preeminence. Yet that is precisely the contradiction embedded in the current federal approach to science funding — and if the House Appropriations Committee gets its way in the FY2027 budget process, it will become the law of the land.

The damage is not projected. It is happening now.

## The Collapse Is Real, Not Theoretical

The clearest window into the current crisis comes from [*Granted AI*'s analysis of federal research funding](https://grantedai.com/blog/federal-research-funding-bottleneck-nih-nsf-billions-unspent-agencies-2026), which confirms that the National Science Foundation has awarded just 613 grants in FY2026 to date — roughly one-fifth of the 3,000-plus awards the agency would typically have made by this point in the fiscal year. The dollar value of those awards is running at approximately one-third of historical averages. The [American Physical Society's April 2026 assessment](https://www.aps.org/apsnews/2026/04/nsf-lags-trump-proposes-cuts) corroborates these numbers and documents the administration's simultaneous push for another round of deep cuts in the coming fiscal year.

The mechanics behind this collapse are not mysterious. Under the DOGE reorganization, NSF absorbed [1,752 grant terminations representing $1.4 billion in committed research](https://cossa.org/nsf-releases-list-of-terminated-grants/). The STEM Education directorate alone lost 839 active grants worth $888 million. The Social, Behavioral, and Economic Sciences directorate lost 320 more. These were not future appropriations — they were active awards to working researchers, with graduate students, lab infrastructure, and multi-year experimental designs built around them. When a grant is terminated mid-stream, the downstream losses — doctoral timelines disrupted, research partnerships dissolved, preliminary findings that will never be published — compound far beyond the nominal dollar figure.

Operational capacity has been gutted alongside the grant portfolio. NSF's staff has been reduced by approximately 35 percent. Peer-review standards have been quietly degraded: [as *Science* reported](https://www.science.org/content/article/nsf-pares-down-grant-review-process-reducing-influence-outside-scientists), the minimum number of external reviewers per proposal fell from three to two, with one slot opened to NSF staff rather than independent outside experts. Panel discussions were made optional, and written summaries compressed to three-to-five sentences. The result, as [*Nature* has documented](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-01287-0), is a system running at one-fifth its normal grant-award cadence — not because the research has been deemed unworthy, but because the institution has been restructured into functional paralysis.

Congress did push back in the FY2026 regular appropriations cycle. The final [Commerce, Justice, Science appropriations package signed into law in January 2026](https://aas.org/posts/news/2026/01/congress-passes-fiscal-year-2026-spending-bills-nsf-nasa-and-doe) provided NSF with $8.75 billion — a Senate-driven outcome that rejected the House's $7 billion proposal and the administration's 55 percent cut request. That bipartisan floor matters. But it has not arrested the operational collapse, because a topline appropriation does not restore terminated grants, rehire departed staff, or undo institutional damage to peer-review infrastructure. A statutory funding level and actual research output are two different things.

Now the administration has proposed steep cuts again in its FY2027 budget request, and the House is expected to endorse significant reductions — another round of attrition layered on top of a system already running at emergency capacity.

## The Pipeline That Built American Tech Leadership

The case for protecting this infrastructure is not sentimental. It is historical and causal.

The internet began as [ARPANET](https://www.darpa.mil/news/features/arpanet), a 1969 DARPA experiment in resilient communications between research facilities. GPS began as a Department of Defense navigation system in the 1970s and 1980s. Capacitive touchscreen technology traces to federally supported university research. The mRNA vaccine platform that compressed COVID-19 vaccine development from years to months was built on decades of NIH-funded basic research into ribonucleic acid mechanisms — research that no pharmaceutical company would have funded speculatively with a 25-year horizon and no guaranteed commercial application.

Most relevant to the current policy conversation: the neural network architectures underlying every major AI system in commercial deployment today emerged from decades of academic research with no immediate commercial application. Geoffrey Hinton, Yoshua Bengio, and Yann LeCun — the [2018 Turing Award laureates](https://awards.acm.org/about/2018-turing) often called the "godfathers of deep learning" — spent years building theoretical foundations in academic research environments at a time when the commercial case for neural networks was entirely non-obvious. DARPA commissioned a [Neural Network Study in 1987](https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/citations/ADA207580) when that case remained unclear. The [Networking and Information Technology R&D Program](https://www.nitrd.gov/pubs/FY2024-NITRD-NAIIO-Supplement.pdf) — a federal interagency initiative — sustained AI and computing research through decades of academic investment that preceded commercial viability by a generation.

This is not coincidence. It is the standard model of American technological leadership: the federal government funds the curiosity-driven, long-horizon, pre-competitive research that markets will not touch; private companies build products on the resulting intellectual infrastructure; the economy captures the returns. Applied R&D and basic science are not substitutes in this model — they are sequential stages of a pipeline. Industry harvests what basic research seeds. Eliminating the seed corn does not simply slow innovation; it breaks the pipeline upstream in ways that will not become visible in competitive terms for a decade, and will not be reversible for two.

As [*Science*/AAAS has documented](https://www.science.org/content/article/darpa-and-decline-us-computer-science-research), DARPA's withdrawal from open, university-based computer science research in prior decades produced measurable downstream effects on American research capacity. The current moment is a more severe version of the same dynamic — affecting not just DARPA's portfolio but the broader NSF research ecosystem from which the next generation of AI, quantum, and biotech researchers is supposed to emerge.

## The Market Failure Congress Cannot Outsource

The objection will be made — it is already being made — that the private sector will fill the gap. It will not.

This is not an ideological claim. It is a structural one. Industry R&D is extraordinarily productive at the applied end of the innovation spectrum: taking known principles and transforming them into engineered products, optimizing existing systems, and generating the near-term proprietary returns that justify shareholder investment. American companies spent approximately $540 billion on R&D in 2023, and that investment produces enormous economic value. But it is structurally incapable of substituting for pre-competitive basic science, for three reasons.

First, the time horizon. No publicly traded company will fund a research program with a 15-to-20-year expected payoff. Quarterly earnings pressure, capital allocation discipline, and the absence of patent protection for fundamental discoveries all preclude it. The venture capital model, similarly, requires an 8-to-10-year fund cycle with identifiable exits — not decade-spanning bets on speculative scientific questions.

Second, the positive externality problem. Basic research findings cannot be fully appropriated by the funder. When NSF-funded researchers publish results in open-access journals, the spillover benefits accrue to the entire economy — competitors, adjacent industries, foreign firms, and future generations of researchers. This is precisely why basic research generates [higher social returns than private returns](https://itif.org/publications/2021/01/25/five-free-market-myths-about-increasing-federal-research-funding/), and why private investment will be systematically below the socially optimal level. This is a textbook market failure requiring a public-sector response.

Third, the organizational model. The research programs that produced the internet, GPS, and the foundational neural network architectures did not emerge from product roadmaps — they emerged from researchers pursuing interesting scientific questions with patient, non-directive funding. That organizational model exists in universities and is supported by federal grants. It does not exist in corporate R&D departments, and it cannot be replicated there.

Acknowledging the market failure for basic research does not undermine a pro-market worldview. It defines the precise scope of government's legitimate function: correcting for market failures in the allocation of long-horizon, non-appropriable, spillover-generating scientific investigation. The argument for NSF is not a general defense of government spending. It is a narrow, well-grounded case for the one category of innovation investment that markets structurally cannot provide.

## China Is Running the Opposite Playbook

While Congress debates further reductions to America's innovation infrastructure, China is executing an explicit strategy of accelerating investment in the same upstream research the United States is dismantling.

According to [China's National Bureau of Statistics](https://www.stats.gov.cn/english/PressRelease/202510/t20251010_1961462.html), China's total R&D investment exceeded 3.92 trillion yuan (approximately $569 billion) in 2024, representing 2.8 percent of GDP. Basic research funding specifically reached nearly 280 billion yuan — exceeding 7 percent of total R&D for the first time in China's history, a level the Ministry of Science and Technology explicitly celebrated as a milestone. China has [outlined a 2026–2030 innovation strategy](https://english.www.gov.cn/archive/statistics/202603/05/content_WS69a96a44c6d00ca5f9a09933.html) targeting an annual average increase of at least 7 percent in nationwide R&D spending, with prioritized investment in AI, advanced semiconductors, and — critically — basic research.

This is a deliberate, strategic choice by a government that has read the same history of American innovation that Congress appears to be ignoring: the countries that invest in foundational research today will dominate the technology landscape in 20 years. China's leadership has understood the lesson. American policymakers are currently unlearning it.

The [CHIPS and Science Act of 2022](https://www.nsf.gov/chips), which directed more than $200 billion toward scientific research, STEM education, and new technology development to counter exactly this threat, now risks becoming an aspirational document. Its authorization levels are meaningless if annual appropriations undermine the operational capacity of the agencies meant to execute them, and if DOGE-driven restructuring destroys the institutional infrastructure through which those investments would flow.

## The Legislative Moment: What Congress Must Do

The [AIP FYI science policy update for May 4, 2026](https://www.aip.org/fyi/the-week-of-may-4-2026) confirms that the appropriations and budget process for the coming fiscal year is active. The Senate's consistent posture — maintaining NSF's topline at or near current levels against administration pressure for dramatic reductions — is the right floor. But it is only a floor, and floors are not the same as solutions.

**1. Restore the terminated grant portfolio.** The 1,752 DOGE-terminated grants represent $1.4 billion in committed, peer-reviewed research that was canceled after award. Congress should appropriate the funds necessary to reinstate these awards and direct NSF to reconnect with affected research teams. The institutional damage compounds daily as researchers accept alternative employment or abandon research careers.

**2. Reverse the peer-review degradation.** The reduction in external reviewers and the gutting of panel deliberations should be reversed by congressional direction. Peer review is the quality filter that distinguishes legitimate grant-selection reform from capability destruction. If Congress has concerns about NSF's portfolio priorities, the remedy is better peer review — not degraded peer review.

**3. Stabilize NSF staffing.** A 35 percent staff reduction in an agency managing a $9 billion research portfolio is not efficiency — it is operational incapacity. The bottleneck that has reduced grant awards to 20 percent of historical cadence is as much a staffing and administrative failure as a funding one.

**4. Index NSF's appropriation to GDP.** The annual budget brinksmanship that puts foundational research on the chopping block every appropriations cycle is itself a form of capability destruction. Authorizing committees should explore a GDP-indexed floor for NSF and core federal research agencies that insulates pre-competitive basic science from the political variables of any given fiscal year.

**5. Protect the CHIPS Act science authorizations.** The CHIPS and Science Act's investment in NSF, NIST, and DOE science offices was an explicit strategic decision to out-invest China in the innovation inputs that determine long-run technological leadership. As the [Congressional Research Service's analysis of federal R&D funding](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48694) makes clear, these authorization levels reflect a coherent theory of competitive advantage — one that appropriators should treat as a binding commitment, not a suggestion.

## The Strategic Incoherence Congress Must Confront

No one in Congress believes the United States should lose the AI race to China. No serious policymaker argues that semiconductor leadership, quantum computing advantage, and biotechnology supremacy are anything other than national priorities. These positions command broad bipartisan consensus and drive legislation from the CHIPS Act to export controls to the AI executive order.

But declared priorities must be matched with institutional investments. The university labs where the next generation of AI researchers will be trained run on NSF grants. The graduate students who will design tomorrow's quantum algorithms are being defunded in real time. The foundational research programs that produced every technology America currently leads — from the internet to GPS to the neural networks underlying frontier AI — emerged from exactly the kind of patient, curiosity-driven, federally supported science that is now being systematically dismantled.

NSF's entire discretionary budget — approximately $8.75 billion — is less than two-tenths of one percent of federal spending. The fiscal argument for further reduction is arithmetic noise against the defense budget and mandatory outlays. The strategic cost of continued research attrition, measured in competitive position against a China that is accelerating its foundational research investment, is orders of magnitude larger than any near-term savings.

Congress must choose. It cannot credibly hold both positions.
