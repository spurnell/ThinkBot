This November, the National Association of Insurance Commissioners will vote at its fall national meeting on whether to convert a 12-state pilot into the nationwide standard for examining insurer AI systems. The vote is on course to proceed with limited industry pushback, no formal innovation-impact assessment, and no demonstrated gap in existing regulatory authority. If it succeeds, every insurer doing business in the United States will face a new layer of AI-specific examination procedures layered on top of the market conduct and financial examination authority that state commissioners already possess.

That machinery is the [NAIC AI Systems Evaluation Tool](https://www.fenwick.com/insights/publications/naic-expands-ai-systems-evaluation-tool-pilot-program-to-12-states-key-updates-for-insurers-and-ai-vendors-supporting-insurers), currently being piloted across California, Colorado, Connecticut, Florida, Iowa, Louisiana, Maryland, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Vermont, Virginia, and Wisconsin. The pilot runs from March through September 2026. Results feed into an October refinement period, and the fall meeting adoption decision follows. In parallel, the NAIC's Third-Party Data and Models Working Group is developing a vendor registry framework that would impose a second compliance layer on AI model providers — without, critically, transferring any insurer accountability to those registered vendors.

Both frameworks warrant close examination before November's adoption vote.

---

## What the Tool Does — and What It Presupposes

According to [the pilot's official project summary](https://content.naic.org/sites/default/files/call_materials/Pilot%20Project%20Summary.pdf), the AI Systems Evaluation Tool organizes its examination inquiry across four exhibits. Exhibit A inventories an insurer's AI deployments, including vendor-sourced models and ML features embedded in larger platforms. Exhibit B assesses governance structures: oversight policies, accountability chains, documentation practices. Exhibit C focuses on high-risk AI systems with direct policyholder impact — underwriting, pricing, claims, fraud detection — requiring documentation on model design, training data, validation, and bias testing. Exhibit D evaluates data sources, quality controls, and potential proxy discrimination in rate-setting data.

The architecture is coherent. The problem is not what the tool examines — it is what the tool presupposes.

The tool presupposes that existing state insurance examination authority is insufficient to catch AI-driven harms. It does not argue this. It does not document a case where an AI underwriting model produced discriminatory outcomes that existing market conduct examination authority failed to detect and remedy. It simply assumes the gap exists and builds machinery to fill it.

Regulators bear the burden of proving that gap before creating new procedural frameworks. On the current record, that burden has not been met.

---

## Existing Examination Authority Is Adequate — Demonstrate the Gap First

State insurance commissioners already have sweeping market conduct examination authority. They can compel document production, examine underwriting files, review claims handling consistency, audit pricing models, and take enforcement action under unfair trade practice statutes that have been on the books for decades. States like Colorado, California, New York, and Illinois have gone further, enacting [specific algorithmic underwriting restrictions](https://doi.colorado.gov/for-consumers/sb21-169-protecting-consumers-from-unfair-discrimination-in-insurance-practices) backed by their own examination authority.

The NAIC's own [Model Bulletin on the Use of Artificial Intelligence](https://content.naic.org/insurance-topics/artificial-intelligence), adopted at the end of 2023 and now in force across 24 states, is explicitly principle-based — it requires governance, documentation, and audit procedures enforced through existing unfair trade practice statutes. The bulletin did not require new examination machinery because the drafters understood that existing authority was adequate to enforce the principles it articulates.

The AI Systems Evaluation Tool represents a different institutional logic: rather than using existing authority to examine AI outcomes, it creates a separate procedural audit of AI governance documentation. The examiner's question shifts from "did this AI system produce discriminatory pricing outcomes?" to "does this insurer's AI governance documentation satisfy our process checklist?" These are not the same question, and the second is a substantially weaker accountability mechanism than the first.

If state commissioners have identified specific instances where AI-driven underwriting harms escaped detection under existing exam authority, the record of those failures should be public. Before November's adoption vote, NAIC delegates should demand it.

## A Pilot in 12 States Is a Patchwork in Waiting

The Model Bulletin adoption history is the most instructive precedent available, and it is not reassuring. Since the NAIC published the model bulletin in late 2023, [24 states have adopted it](https://www.quarles.com/newsroom/publications/nearly-half-of-states-have-now-adopted-naic-model-bulletin-on-insurers-use-of-ai) — while Colorado, California, New York, and Texas each developed separate AI guidance frameworks. Insurers operating nationally are already navigating compliance stacks that layer model bulletin requirements atop incompatible state-specific variations. A 2025 Norton Rose Fulbright survey found that "[insurers operating in multiple states should be prepared for varying requirements](https://www.nortonrosefulbright.com/en/knowledge/publications/5accc826/ai-and-the-insurance-sector-balancing-benefits-with-regulatory-complexity)" — a mild formulation for what is, in practice, a bespoke compliance program for each major state market.

The AI Systems Evaluation Tool is structurally positioned to replicate and amplify this dynamic. The [Sidley Data Matters summary](https://datamatters.sidley.com/2026/04/14/regulatory-update-national-association-of-insurance-commissioners-spring-2026-national-meeting/) of the NAIC Spring 2026 meeting notes that pilot states are each independently selecting which carriers to examine and integrating the tool into their existing workflows — some embedding it in market conduct reviews, others using it during financial examinations. This is not harmonization; it is 12 parallel implementations of the same four-exhibit framework, each developing its own institutional understanding of what satisfactory responses to Exhibits B and C look like.

When November's adoption vote converts the pilot into the national standard, the result will not be 50 states applying a uniform examination tool. It will be 50 states applying their own variant of the tool — a national framework in name, a patchwork in operation. This is exactly how the Model Bulletin produced coordinated fragmentation: apparent uniformity at the framework level, genuine divergence at the level of examination practice. Smaller insurers and insurtech entrants — who cannot maintain compliance teams calibrated to 50 state-specific examination expectations — absorb this cost most heavily. Incumbent carriers with established compliance infrastructure absorb it least.

The NAIC is aware of this dynamic; it has lived with the Model Bulletin version of it for two years. The question is whether it builds the same structural flaw into the evaluation tool before anyone formally measures the cost.

## The Vendor Registry: Doubled Compliance Burden, Diffuse Accountability

The [Third-Party Data and Models Working Group's](https://content.naic.org/committees/h/third-party-data-models-wg) companion registry framework compounds the problem. Under the proposed framework, AI model vendors whose products affect pricing, underwriting, claims, utilization review, marketing, or fraud detection would be required to register with state regulators before their models can be used in consumer-facing insurance functions. Registration requires filing model descriptions, training data sources and date ranges, documented testing methodology including bias testing, known limitations, and change-management practices.

The framework is explicitly not licensure, and it does not transfer accountability. As the [Alston & Bird Spring 2026 meeting summary](https://www.alstonprivacy.com/key-ai-cybersecurity-and-privacy-takeaways-from-the-naic-2026-spring-meeting/) records, the registry "is not intended to relieve insurers of their existing vendor diligence and management obligations." Carriers cannot treat vendor registration as a compliance safe harbor. An insurer using a registered model remains fully accountable for that model's behavior.

This means both sides of the vendor relationship now face independent compliance obligations, with no corresponding clarity on responsibility when a registered model produces a disputed outcome. The vendor has filed its paperwork; the insurer has conducted its own independent evaluation and monitoring; neither can point to the other's compliance record as evidence of adequate oversight. The accountability structure is additive, not substitutive.

The [Mondaq Working Group analysis](https://www.mondaq.com/unitedstates/insurance-laws-and-products/1765580/naic-spring-2026-meeting-third-party-data-and-models-h-working-group-march-23-2026) notes that the framework targets "transparency between regulators and third-party providers" rather than full licensure — a reasonable goal. But transparency gained through a registration filing is not the same as accountability enforced through examination authority. Regulators will have a registry of vendors; they will not have resolved who is responsible when a registered vendor's registered model misprices risk for a protected class. For smaller AI vendors weighing whether the insurance sector is worth the compliance overhead, this ambiguity will register as a reason to exit.

## AI Underwriting Expands Access — Restrictions Contract It

The strongest argument for the NAIC's examination machinery is the discrimination risk: algorithmic underwriting can encode historical inequities into pricing models, producing disparate-impact outcomes that are legally prohibited even when insurers cannot articulate discriminatory intent. This is a real risk, and it demands a real regulatory response.

But the policy question is not whether AI underwriting should be accountable — it is whether the AI Systems Evaluation Tool is the right accountability mechanism. The evidence on the access side of the ledger is important context for answering that question.

Machine learning-driven risk modeling is expanding coverage access in markets that have historically been underserved by actuarially crude pricing. [LIMRA's 2024 survey of final expense products](https://www.limra.com/en/newsroom/news-releases/2025/life-insurers-council-final-expense-insurance-new-annualized-premium-increased-16-in-2024/) — a segment disproportionately serving middle- and lower-income consumers — shows 16 percent premium growth, driven partly by simplified-issue underwriting enabled by algorithmic risk assessment. The [OECD has documented](https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2023/12/leveraging-technology-in-insurance-to-enhance-risk-assessment-and-policyholder-risk-reduction_4844de05/2f5c18ac-en.pdf) that technology-enhanced risk assessment in insurance can improve coverage availability by enabling more accurate individual risk pricing. More accurate pricing means fewer cross-subsidies — which means fewer low-risk consumers in underserved segments priced out of markets by conservative actuarial assumptions designed for less-granular risk pools.

This dynamic is not a justification for tolerating discriminatory AI underwriting. It is a reason to ensure that the regulatory response to discrimination risk is calibrated against the access costs of over-restriction. Colorado's [SB 21-169](https://doi.colorado.gov/for-consumers/sb21-169-protecting-consumers-from-unfair-discrimination-in-insurance-practices) and analogous state restrictions imposed compliance architectures on algorithmic underwriting before actuarial researchers had fully characterized the access effects of those constraints. The NAIC's evaluation tool risks repeating that sequencing error at a national scale — imposing examination machinery designed for the harm case without first measuring what the innovation case is worth to the consumers it enables.

## The Right Model: Outcome Accountability Through Existing Channels

None of this argues against accountability for AI underwriting. It argues against the wrong kind of accountability.

The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework) offers a useful alternative architecture. NIST's core functions — Govern, Map, Measure, Manage — are outcome-oriented. The "Measure" function focuses on monitoring actual performance, trustworthiness, and risk outcomes, not governance documentation. Applied to insurance examination, this means examiners should be asking: What are this insurer's disparate-impact ratios by protected class across underwriting decisions? Is pricing accuracy consistent with sound actuarial principles, or are there anomalies suggesting proxy discrimination? Is claims handling consistent across demographic cohorts? These questions can be answered through existing market conduct examination authority, with standardized outcome metrics developed by NAIC's actuarial and consumer protection working groups.

This is what outcome accountability looks like. It does not require a new four-exhibit framework interrogating AI governance documentation. It requires examiners with the technical capacity to analyze underwriting data and standardized outcome metrics that make disparate-impact measurement consistent across states.

NAIC's existing Center for Insurance Policy and Research has the actuarial expertise to develop those metrics. State commissioners have the examination authority to enforce them. The infrastructure exists; what is missing is the will to use it rather than build something new.

The NAIC should also formally assess the innovation impact of its examination tool before November. The global insurtech market reached approximately [$20 billion in 2025](https://natlawreview.com/article/impact-ai-regulations-insurtech), with P&C insurtech funding surging 90 percent in Q1 2025 alone. This is a sector in the middle of a consequential innovation cycle. Examination machinery calibrated for the compliance capacity of large incumbent carriers — and indifferent to the overhead it imposes on smaller market entrants — does not merely constrain incumbents. It shapes who can afford to be in the market at all.

---

## Before November

The NAIC's AI Systems Evaluation Tool pilot is well-intentioned. State regulators genuinely want to understand how AI is changing the risk of discriminatory underwriting, and building a structured examination tool is a reasonable response to a perceived governance gap. The problem is structural, not motivational: a tool piloted across 12 states in 12 different implementation contexts, feeding a national adoption vote six months later, without a formal gap analysis of existing authority or an innovation-impact assessment, is a patchwork in waiting.

NAIC delegates who vote in November will be making a decision that shapes insurance AI governance for the next decade. Before that vote, three things should happen. First, the Big Data and AI Working Group should document specific instances where existing market conduct examination authority failed to detect AI-driven underwriting harms — or acknowledge, on the record, that no such documentation exists. Second, the AI Systems Evaluation Tool should be revised to center outcome metrics — disparate-impact ratios, pricing accuracy, claims consistency — rather than process audits of governance documentation. Third, the Third-Party Data and Models Working Group should resolve the accountability ambiguity in the vendor registry before it chills entry into the insurance AI vendor market.

Done right, the NAIC can produce a national framework that catches actual discriminatory harm, preserves the access benefits of accurate risk modeling, and imposes compliance costs proportionate to demonstrated regulatory need. Done wrong — adopted in November as currently designed, with state-specific variants proliferating into a 50-state patchwork — the evaluation tool will become an innovation tax on the very insurtech sector most likely to expand coverage access for underserved consumers.

The examination machinery should serve the accountability goal. Right now, there is a real risk the relationship runs the other way.
