Congress does not need a hypothetical to understand why prescriptive, one-size-fits-all AI mandates are hard to execute. It has a live one, running inside its own executive branch, on a public clock.

On April 13, 2026, the Government Accountability Office published [*Artificial Intelligence Acquisitions: Agencies Should Collect and Apply Lessons Learned to Improve Future Procurements*](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107859) (GAO-26-107859), a review of AI procurement practices at the Department of Defense, the Department of Homeland Security, the General Services Administration, and the Department of Veterans Affairs. The finding, reported in plain language: none of the four agencies has a systematic process for capturing and sharing what it learns from buying AI. Officials told GAO they struggled to find technical experts to evaluate contractor proposals, couldn't reliably price AI-related costs, ran into intellectual-property and data-rights complications, and had no consistent way to define testing requirements or timelines — and then, having lived through all of that, had no policy requiring them to write any of it down for the next contracting officer or the next agency to use ([Nextgov/FCW](https://www.nextgov.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/04/agencies-are-missing-step-share-information-better/412801/)). GAO recommended that all four agencies update their policies to require systematic lessons-learned collection, feeding into a government-wide repository. All four agencies concurred.

That repository requirement is not new. [OMB M-25-22](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-22-Driving-Efficient-Acquisition-of-Artificial-Intelligence-in-Government.pdf), issued in April 2025, already directed GSA to stand up exactly this kind of shared best-practices infrastructure and told agencies to update their own acquisition policies to feed it. A year later, GAO found the sampled agencies still weren't prepared to meet that obligation. This is the crux of the story: the government does not lack a rulebook for governing its own AI acquisition. It lacks the operational muscle to execute the rulebook it already wrote for itself — a distinction that should matter enormously to anyone drafting the next rulebook for private industry.

## Why now: a compliance deadline with teeth

This is not an abstract governance critique; it's timed to a real deadline. [OMB M-25-21](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-21-Accelerating-Federal-Use-of-AI-through-Innovation-Governance-and-Public-Trust.pdf), the companion memo to M-25-22, requires agencies to apply minimum risk-management practices — pre-deployment testing, impact assessments, adverse-impact monitoring, human oversight, appeal channels, user feedback mechanisms — to every AI use case they classify as "high-impact," meaning its output serves as a principal basis for decisions with legal, material, or safety consequences for the public. Agencies had 365 days from the memo's issuance to comply or discontinue the use case. That first deadline, April 3, 2026, already came and gone this spring. The results were mixed: the Departments of Labor and Veterans Affairs, NASA, and GSA posted updated inventories confirming full compliance; State and EPA also posted updated inventories, though FedScoop found State's disclosures missing appeal-process information and EPA's compliance status still being finalized; the Departments of Homeland Security, Justice, Commerce, Transportation, and Health and Human Services had not posted updated inventories or responded to inquiries by the time reporters checked in ([FedScoop](https://fedscoop.com/federal-agencies-ai-inventory-risk-management-deadline/)). Brookings' Darrell West was blunt about agencies that missed a deadline they'd had a year's notice to prepare for: "I don't think the shutdown is an excuse for not meeting legal obligations," he told the outlet — a pointed rebuttal to agencies citing the fall 2025 government shutdown as an excuse.

The accountability doesn't end there. M-25-21 requires agencies to be prepared to report their high-impact AI risk-management practices to OMB on an ongoing basis — through periodic accountability reviews, the annual use-case inventory, or upon request ([Wiley](https://www.wiley.law/alert-Trump-Administration-Revamps-Guidance-on-Federal-Use-and-Procurement-of-AI)). That reporting obligation lands against a backdrop that has grown substantially since the last full accounting: the government's April 2026 use-case inventory recorded 3,611 AI use cases across 56 submitting agencies, of which 445 — over 12 percent — are classified high-impact ([Nextgov/FCW](https://www.nextgov.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/04/agencies-report-over-3000-ai-use-cases-2025/412898/)). That is up from the roughly 1,110 total use cases GAO found across just 11 sampled agencies in 2024, itself nearly double 2023's count of 571, with generative AI use cases alone growing ninefold, from 32 to 282 ([GAO-25-107653](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-25-107653)). Agencies are being asked to demonstrate rigorous risk management for a portfolio that has roughly tripled in scope in under three years, using an institutional apparatus that — per GAO-26-107859 — still can't reliably tell one contracting officer what the last one learned.

## The natural experiment Congress should actually study

There is a reflexive conclusion available here, and it's the wrong one: that visible struggle inside the executive branch is evidence the government needs a tougher, more codified statutory AI compliance regime. That gets the diagnosis backward. M-25-21 and M-25-22 are not weak rules. They already impose use-case inventories, high-impact risk-tiering, mandatory testing and appeal processes, competitive-marketplace requirements, and a shared-knowledge repository mandate — a fairly comprehensive governance architecture, built and imposed by the same executive branch that is now missing its own deadlines. The problem GAO documented is not an absence of standards; it's an absence of execution capacity to meet standards that already exist. Bolting a new statute on top of an execution gap doesn't close the gap — it just adds another compliance obligation to the pile that agencies are already failing to keep current.

That is precisely the lesson horizontal AI legislation for the private sector should absorb. State-level algorithmic-accountability statutes and the various omnibus bills introduced in recent Congresses share a structural assumption: that a detailed, prescriptive, ex-ante compliance checklist — impact assessments, documentation requirements, audit trails, specific testing protocols — will produce disciplined AI risk management if only it's mandatory enough. The federal government's own experience under M-25-21/M-25-22 is a controlled test of that assumption, running under close to ideal conditions: full legal authority, budget appropriations, a captive workforce, and no adversarial relationship between rule-writer and rule-follower. Even there, DHS, Justice, Commerce, Transportation, and HHS missed a deadline they'd known about for a year. If a unified executive branch, governing itself, struggles to operationalize rigorous AI risk management at the pace its own memo demands, Congress should be skeptical that a statutory mandate — layered onto thousands of independent private companies with far less institutional capacity to absorb compliance overhead, no common CAIO structure, and adversarial incentives to litigate ambiguity — will perform better. The honest reading of GAO-26-107859 is not "government needs stricter rules." It's "rules alone, absent execution infrastructure, don't produce the outcome regulators want" — a caution that applies with even more force to a private sector Congress cannot staff, fund, or directly manage.

## What the fix actually looks like — and it isn't a new statute

GAO's own remedy is instructive because it is not regulatory in the traditional sense. The recommendation was that DOD, DHS, GSA, and VA update internal policy to require systematic lessons-learned collection feeding a shared, GSA-managed repository — exactly the kind of decentralized knowledge infrastructure that should substitute for rigid rulemaking in fast-moving technology domains. Lessons-learned repositories work because they compound: agency A's discovery that a vendor's pricing model obscures compute costs becomes agency B's negotiating leverage next quarter, without anyone having to litigate a new rule to make that transfer happen. That's iterative governance, not compliance theater, and it is precisely the model M-25-22 already gestures toward when it directs agencies toward a competitive AI marketplace that avoids vendor lock-in and rewards interoperability. That market-based mechanism deserves more attention than it gets: it is a pro-market way of disciplining AI vendor behavior — through switching costs and interoperability standards — that doesn't require a single new prescriptive testing mandate. It should be read as validation of an approach that trusts markets over checklists, not as evidence the current framework has failed.

None of this is an argument for weakening M-25-21's or M-25-22's substantive risk-tiering. High-impact use cases should face more scrutiny than routine back-office automation — that differentiated, risk-proportionate structure is the right design, and OMB deserves credit for building it rather than a uniform checklist that would treat a chatbot drafting internal memos the same as an algorithm making benefits-eligibility determinations. The critique here is narrower and more useful: the execution layer beneath that risk-tiered design is thin, and Congress's job right now is to strengthen that layer, not to write a parallel statutory regime — for government or for industry — on the assumption that more binding language solves an implementation problem.

## Concrete, non-legislative recommendations

Three fixes would matter more than a new bill, and none requires one:

**Stand up the cross-agency AI lessons-learned clearinghouse GAO recommended, with teeth.** GSA should be resourced and directed — through existing OMB authority, not new legislation — to make the M-25-22 repository a living, searchable, actively populated resource rather than a compliance checkbox. Congressional appropriators overseeing GSA's technology-modernization budget are better positioned to drive this than authorizers writing new statutory text.

**Build sunset and refresh cycles into high-impact use-case determinations.** A use case classified high-impact in 2025 based on its deployment context may look different eighteen months later as the underlying model, vendor, or application changes. Static classifications invite exactly the kind of "reclassify and move on" behavior some agencies engaged in while scrambling to meet the April 2026 deadline. A mandatory reassessment cadence — not a new compliance mandate, but a procedural discipline OMB can impose through guidance — keeps risk-tiering honest without adding statutory rigidity.

**Preserve OMB's risk-tiered, agency-flexible structure against pressure to codify it into a uniform statute.** Every high-profile compliance stumble — and DHS, Justice, Commerce, Transportation, and HHS missing the April 2026 deadline is exactly the kind of headline that invites a legislative response — generates a temptation to convert flexible guidance into rigid statutory command. That temptation should be resisted for the same reason horizontal AI statutes for the private sector are the wrong tool: rigidity forecloses the ability to adjust as agencies (or companies) build capacity, as models change, and as the failure modes GAO is documenting today get fixed through iteration rather than litigation.

## The bottom line for oversight staff

Nearly 3,611 AI use cases and growing, 445 of them high-impact, and no working system to capture what four major agencies have learned buying the technology that runs them — nearly three years after Congress and OMB began building an AI governance framework for the executive branch itself. That is not evidence for more prescriptive statutory command. It is a demonstration, in real time, of the gap between writing a good rule and operationalizing one — a gap regulators should confront honestly in their own house before they legislate it into everyone else's.
