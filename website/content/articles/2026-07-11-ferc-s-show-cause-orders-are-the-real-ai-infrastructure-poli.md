Washington's AI infrastructure debate keeps looking for the fight in the wrong place. Export controls, model safety frameworks, and compute clusters get the headlines. The proceeding that will actually determine whether gigawatt-scale AI data centers get built in the United States on a competitive timeline is a set of show-cause orders that the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission issued on June 18, 2026, to six regional grid operators — and the response deadline is August 17.

On that date, [PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, ISO-NE, and NYISO](https://www.mcguirewoods.com/client-resources/alerts/2026/6/ferc-issues-section-206-show-cause-orders-directing-all-six-rtos-isos-to-justify-or-reform-large-load-integration-rules/) must show the Commission why their interconnection tariffs — the rulebooks that determine how a new electricity user gets connected to the grid — should not be reformed to handle 500-megawatt-to-gigawatt AI data center requests. (ERCOT, the Texas grid operator that hosts a large share of announced AI data center capacity, sits outside FERC's jurisdiction because it doesn't engage in interstate transmission — a jurisdictional gap worth remembering every time Texas is invoked as the fix for slow-moving RTOs elsewhere.) This is a live [Federal Power Act Section 206 proceeding](https://www.whitecase.com/insight-alert/ferc-orders-grid-operators-promptly-revise-or-justify-interconnection-rules-data) with a real deadline, not a discussion draft. It deserves more attention than it is getting, because it is the mechanism most likely to actually unstick the AI buildout — if Congress doesn't undercut it first.

## The queue was never built for this

Interconnection queues exist to answer one question: can the grid safely absorb a new source of supply or demand without destabilizing everyone already on the system? For fifty years, the queue's design assumption was that new entrants were power plants — intermittent, incremental, arriving one at a time, often abandoned before completion. First-come-first-served processing and cluster studies made sense when the marginal request was a 100-150 megawatt solar farm.

That assumption has collapsed. [Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory's 2025 interconnection queue survey](https://emp.lbl.gov/publications/queued-2025-edition-characteristics) found roughly 10,300 active projects representing some 2,290 gigawatts of generation and storage capacity sitting in queues nationwide, with the typical project reaching commercial operation in 2024 spending 55 months — nearly five years — from interconnection request to power flowing. Only 13 percent of everything that entered a queue between 2000 and 2020 has reached commercial operation; three-quarters was withdrawn. That backlog was already a problem for clean-energy deployment. It becomes a categorically different problem when the new entrant isn't a 150-megawatt solar farm that can wait out a multi-year study cycle, but a hyperscale data center campus requesting a gigawatt of firm, always-on load with a construction and equipment-procurement clock that hyperscalers are running in months, not years. PJM alone [projects roughly 30 gigawatts of new large-load demand by 2030](https://www.ferc.gov/news-events/news/ferc-launches-aggressive-targeted-action-speed-large-load-integration) and expects to host the majority of new US data center capacity. A queue process built to sort speculative generation projects by application date has no good answer for a load request that is both enormous and commercially real.

FERC's own show-cause orders name the specific failure points: study processes never designed for large-load interconnection studies, cost allocation rules that let a handful of gigawatt customers dump network upgrade costs onto the broader ratepayer base (or, just as often, get stuck behind speculative projects that never materialize), and no functioning framework for co-location — data centers built adjacent to existing generation, which is one of the fastest ways to add gigawatt-scale load without waiting for new transmission. These are not edge cases. They are the reason developers describe five-to-seven-year interconnection timelines as the single biggest obstacle to bringing new AI compute online, ahead of chip availability or capital.

## Why the show-cause approach is the right instrument

FERC had other options. It could have issued a rulemaking dictating a uniform national large-load interconnection standard, overriding each RTO's own tariff. It didn't. Instead, it used Section 206 to force each of the six grid operators to either justify their existing rules as reasonable or propose their own reforms across five defined areas — application and study processes, cost transparency and cost-shifting protection, co-location rates, service arrangements for flexible and behind-the-meter load, and interconnection terms for large loads sited near existing infrastructure — on a real clock: intervention by July 9, informational resource-adequacy reports by July 20, full responses by August 17, stakeholder comment by September 16.

That structure is worth defending on the merits, not just as a matter of process. It responds to a demonstrated dysfunction in rules the grid operators themselves administer, rather than imposing a one-size-fits-all federal template on regions with genuinely different generation mixes, load growth trajectories, and stakeholder governance processes. PJM's capacity crunch is not MISO's, and CAISO's co-location math involving existing renewables is not ISO-NE's. A prescriptive mandate from Washington risks solving the problem RTOs had in 2024 while missing the one they'll have in 2027. A show-cause order under a hard deadline forces the operators who actually run these markets to fix their own rules — and gives FERC the leverage to reject an inadequate answer — without Congress or the Commission substituting its judgment for regional expertise. That is the correct division of labor: Washington sets the requirement that queues be reformed and the standard they must meet; the RTOs, who have the operational knowledge, design the mechanism.

## The off-grid bypass is a symptom, not a cure

The dysfunction FERC is targeting has already produced a legislative response that gets the diagnosis right and the treatment wrong. Senator Tom Cotton's [DATA Act of 2026](https://www.cotton.senate.gov/news/press-releases/cotton-introduces-bill-to-lower-energy-costs-for-arkansans/) would create a new "consumer-regulated electric utility" category exempting AI data center operators from federal interconnection, rate, and reliability rules entirely — provided the facility builds its own generation and stays physically disconnected from the interstate grid. The bill's premise is correct: if the queue takes five years and off-grid generation takes eighteen months, capital will go around the bottleneck rather than through it. But subsidizing the exit ramp does nothing to fix the on-ramp everyone else still has to use, and it invites a real cost: a data center that builds isolated gas or nuclear generation to dodge the queue still relies on the interconnected grid for backup power, black-start capability, and reliability services during outages, without contributing to the network upgrades and capacity payments that keep those services available. That is a stranded-cost problem for the ratepayers who stay on the grid, not a solution to it. Nothing here counsels banning off-grid power — permissionless self-generation is a legitimate hedge against genuine queue dysfunction, and firms should be free to build it. But treating a bypass bill as the AI infrastructure fix, rather than a symptom of an unreformed queue, would let Congress declare victory while leaving the underlying allocation problem — and the cost-shifting risk — untouched.

The competing [GRID Act from Senators Hawley and Blumenthal](https://www.hawley.senate.gov/hawley-blumenthal-introduce-bill-to-prevent-data-centers-from-increasing-electricity-costs-for-americans/) is not the corrective it might appear to be. It does not fix cost allocation on the shared grid at all — it mandates the same exit the DATA Act merely subsidizes. New data centers above 20 megawatts would have to build independent power or pay escalating "rate effect credits," with existing facilities facing a ten-year compliance deadline and civil penalties of up to $1 million a day. As ThinkBot has argued elsewhere, that is a more coercive version of the DATA Act's flaw, not a fix for it: it still leaves the interconnection queue, and the cost-allocation rules governing everyone who stays connected, exactly as broken as before.

## States are filling the vacuum, badly

Where federal queue reform stalls, state legislatures are reaching for blunter instruments, and the trend line since June is not encouraging. Virginia's new budget imposes a [$0.011-per-kilowatt-hour data center electricity consumption tax](https://www.williamsmullen.com/insights/news/legal-news/virginia-budget-creates-new-electricity-consumption-tax-data-centers), effective July 1 and capped at $600 million a year in revenue — the first tax of its kind in the country, landing in the state that hosts the largest concentration of data center capacity in the world. Arizona has enacted a three-year pause on its data center sales tax exemption running through mid-2029; Illinois has directed its Department of Commerce and Economic Opportunity to [pause new agreements](https://crokefairchild.com/2026/06/governor-pritzker-directs-dceo-to-pause-data-center-investment-program-agreements-effective-july-1-2026/) under its Data Center Investment Program as of July 1 while honoring existing agreements; Ohio has paused its own incentive program. None of these moves reform an interconnection queue or add a megawatt of capacity. They are retail-electricity-bill politics — a response to voters who increasingly blame data center load growth for rising rates — dressed up as industrial policy.

That is the predictable consequence of federal drift, not an independent state policy failure. When the entity that actually controls the bottleneck — the grid operator managing the queue — faces no binding deadline to fix cost allocation, state legislatures are left to improvise with the only lever they control: taxes and incentive eligibility. The result is a patchwork where the same gigawatt data center proposal faces a fundamentally different regulatory and fiscal environment depending on which side of a state line it sits, with new taxes and moratoria arriving on a rolling, unpredictable basis. That unpredictability is itself a competitiveness cost. It is precisely the dynamic the CHIPS and Science Act's implementation should have taught Washington to avoid.

## The CHIPS Act lesson, applied to electrons instead of silicon

The defining finding from three years of CHIPS Act implementation is that subsidy size was never the binding constraint on where fabs actually broke ground — permitting speed was. [CSET's "No Permits, No Fabs" analysis](https://cset.georgetown.edu/publication/no-permits-no-fabs/) documented that environmental impact statements averaged 4.5 years to complete between 2013 and 2018, and reporting on CHIPS-funded projects found [Micron's New York fab, Amkor's Arizona packaging facility, and SK hynix's Indiana HBM plant](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/semiconductors/chips-act-beneficiares-mired-in-nimby-fights-and-two-year-permits-delays-to-micron-amkor-and-sk-hynix-ny-fabs-costing-usd5m-per-day) all mired in local permitting fights and multi-year review timelines that cost an estimated $5 million a day in delay, regardless of how generous the federal subsidy check was. Money could not buy a faster permit.

Grid interconnection is the same mechanism wearing a different label. No amount of federal AI compute investment, export-control carve-out, or state incentive package matters if the gigawatt of power a data center needs sits in a five-year queue behind a resource-adequacy study designed for a different era of grid planning. The lesson from CHIPS is not that industrial policy failed — it's that the binding constraint on any capital-intensive buildout is the permitting and interconnection stack, and that constraint has to be fixed directly. Subsidies and tax exemptions, whether offered by Congress or withdrawn by state legislatures, were never going to substitute for that fix. FERC's show-cause orders are the first serious federal attempt to fix it directly for AI load, which is exactly why they matter more than the next round of chip policy headlines.

## What Congress should actually do

Reports that a broader energy permitting reform deal is close in the Senate create a real opportunity, and Congress should use it correctly. The right move is not to preempt FERC's proceeding or dictate specific interconnection rules to individual RTOs — that would repeat the one-size-fits-all mistake FERC itself avoided by using a show-cause order instead of a rulemaking. The right move is for Congress to codify, through permitting reform legislation, the baseline standards that should govern every region's response: large loads must be studied on realistic timelines, network upgrade costs must be allocated to the load that causes them rather than socialized onto existing ratepayers, and co-location arrangements need a durable statutory footing so they aren't relitigated tariff by tariff. That legislative baseline would let RTOs retain implementation discretion — exactly the flexibility FERC's approach already preserves — while ending the fifty-state scramble that Virginia, Arizona, Illinois, and Ohio are currently running through, each with a different fiscal answer to the same underlying interconnection failure.

If Congress instead spends its energy legislating an off-grid carve-out or letting the state tax patchwork keep expanding, it will have chosen the second-best fix — subsidizing exit and fragmenting the map — over the first-best one already in motion at FERC. August 17 is the date to watch. It will tell Congress, and everyone trying to site the next gigawatt of AI compute, whether American grid operators can fix the rule that matters most before Washington and the states make it worse.
