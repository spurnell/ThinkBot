## Abstract

*United States v. Google* (Ad Tech) presents the most consequential antitrust remedies question of 2026: whether structural divestiture of Google's AdX exchange and DFP publisher ad server can restore competition in programmatic advertising. This paper applies three analytical frameworks to that choice. First, Rochet and Tirole's two-sided market theory establishes that the proven harms — First Look, Last Look, Unified Pricing Rules, and Project Poirot — are auction-design manipulations that broke AdX's neutral-intermediary condition, and that this condition can be restored by targeted behavioral prohibitions without mandatory ownership restructuring. Second, structural remedy economics, anchored in the 1984 AT&T divestiture precedent, identifies three conditions for structural remedy success — technical separability, viable independent operation, and enforceable access obligations — of which at most one is clearly present for a divested AdX. Third, a welfare comparison across scope-targeting, proportionality, and error-cost asymmetry establishes that a targeted behavioral package — prohibiting the four proven auction manipulations, mandating Prebid interoperability, and requiring real-time bid-data transparency — is the consumer welfare-maximizing remedy response to the specific harms adjudicated. The paper concludes that error-cost asymmetry between reversible behavioral remedies and irreversible structural separation should be the organizing principle when courts confront technically complex platform markets in which competitive entry is already occurring.

## Introduction

The liability question in *United States v. Google* (Ad Tech) is settled. On April 17, 2025, Judge Leonie Brinkema of the Eastern District of Virginia [issued a ruling](https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/66753787/united-states-v-google-llc/) finding Google liable for monopolizing the open-web display publisher ad server market and the open-web display ad exchange market, and for unlawfully tying its publisher ad server (DoubleClick for Publishers/DFP) to its ad exchange (AdX). The liability finding is the starting point of this paper, not its subject. What remains analytically unresolved — and consequential far beyond the parties — is the remedies question: having found that Google engaged in systematic auction manipulation across the programmatic advertising stack, what remedy design best serves consumer and publisher welfare?

This is not an academic abstraction. [Closing arguments in the remedies phase concluded on November 21, 2025](https://www.adweek.com/media/doj-google-adtech-monopoly-remedies-trial-breakup/), and a remedies opinion is now expected in the coming months. The Department of Justice is seeking a structural remedy — forced divestiture of AdX and disclosure of DFP's auction code — that would constitute the largest corporate breakup mandated by an American court since the 1984 AT&T divestiture. Google has proposed a targeted behavioral package: prohibiting the specific auction manipulations the court found anticompetitive, requiring server-to-server interoperability with Prebid and third-party ad servers, and mandating real-time bid-data sharing with publishers. [Judge Brinkema has expressed concern](https://natlawreview.com/article/judge-leans-away-breakup-during-closing-arguments-google-ad-tech-case/) about the commercial feasibility of the DOJ's divestiture proposal, and specifically about whether a viable independent buyer for AdX exists.

The purpose of this paper is to bring the economic literature on structural remedy design to bear on that choice. Three analytical tracks drive the analysis. First, the programmatic advertising stack is a two-sided market — AdX simultaneously serves publishers (sellers of ad impressions) and advertisers (buyers) — and two-sided market theory, beginning with Rochet and Tirole's foundational 2003 framework, generates specific predictions about what happens to platform competition when the exchange operator is simultaneously a participant on one side of its own auction. Second, the AT&T structural remedy precedent is the most directly relevant historical analog, but it is instructive precisely because of the conditions it required that are partially absent here: physical separability of the bottleneck facility, structural self-sufficiency of the divested entities, and behavioral access obligations that prevented discrimination post-separation. Third, a welfare comparison between structural divestiture and calibrated behavioral remedies — evaluated on scope-targeting, proportionality, and error-cost asymmetry — suggests a clear hierarchy when remedy design is disciplined by the specific harm the court found proven rather than by Google's general market power.

The paper's analytical commitment is worth stating plainly at the outset. The question is not whether harm occurred — Judge Brinkema found that it did, through specific auction-design manipulations that extracted value from publishers and suppressed competition at the exchange layer. The question is whether the proposed structural remedy addresses that harm with precision, or whether it severs genuinely pro-competitive integration efficiencies that the manipulation does not implicate. The distinction matters enormously: antitrust remedies are not punishments; they are calibrated instruments for restoring competitive conditions. As the Supreme Court held in *United States v. Grinnell Corp.*, the remedy "must be related to the violations proven." Applied here, that principle suggests a remedy targeted at auction-design prohibitions, interoperability mandates, and data-access requirements — not a restructuring of corporate ownership that goes beyond the harms the court adjudicated.

One further framing note: the paper treats Judge Brinkema's decision as establishing the liability foundation and does not revisit it. Readers looking for a challenge to the court's theory of harm will not find one here. What they will find is a rigorous application of economic tools to the remedies question that the court must still answer.

## 1. Google's Vertical Position in the Programmatic Advertising Stack

To assess the remedies question rigorously, one must first understand the technical supply chain at issue. The programmatic advertising ecosystem that the DOJ challenged — and that Judge Brinkema found partly monopolized — consists of three functionally distinct layers that together execute billions of real-time auctions daily, matching digital advertising demand with publisher inventory in the time it takes a webpage to load.

**The Three-Layer Stack**

At the bottom of the stack is the *publisher ad server* — in Google's case, DoubleClick for Publishers (DFP), now branded Google Ad Manager. The publisher ad server is the inventory management system that publishers use to organize, price, and allocate their ad impressions. When a user loads a page on a news website or blog, the publisher's ad server determines which impressions are available, applies floor prices set by the publisher, and reaches out to ad exchanges to solicit bids. DFP controls approximately [90 percent of the publisher ad server market](https://digiday.com/marketing/googles-ad-tech-antitrust-remedy-phase-explained-doj-wants-googles-offerings-and-where-the-judge-might-land/) — a concentration that is effectively unchallengeable in the near term because switching publisher ad servers is a costly, months-long integration project requiring code changes across every publisher property. That switching cost is not itself a violation; it is the commercial reality that gives the publisher ad server its bottleneck character.

At the center of the stack is the *ad exchange* — in Google's case, AdX. The exchange is the trading venue: it receives impression opportunities from publisher ad servers, runs a real-time bidding (RTB) auction among competing demand sources, and returns a winning bid and creative within roughly 100 milliseconds. In a fully competitive exchange, the operator functions as a neutral intermediary whose revenue — a take rate typically in the range of 15 to 20 percent of the winning bid — is earned by efficiently matching supply and demand. AdX held a dominant position in the open-web display exchange market during the period at issue. [The DOJ's complaint alleged AdX handled more than 50 percent of open-web display exchange transactions by volume](https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/66753787/united-states-v-google-llc/), while Google contested both the market definition and the [appropriate baseline metric](https://www.adexchanger.com/antitrust/the-doj-and-google-sharpen-their-remedy-proposals-as-the-two-sides-prepare-for-closing-arguments/). Judge Brinkema's liability opinion accepted the DOJ's narrower market definition for the exchange tier.

At the top of the stack is the *demand-side platform* (DSP) — in Google's case, DV360 (formerly DoubleClick Bid Manager). The DSP bids on impressions on behalf of advertisers, evaluating each auction opportunity against the advertiser's targeting parameters, budget constraints, and willingness to pay. Multiple DSPs compete to buy impressions through the same exchanges — The Trade Desk, Amazon's DSP, and DV360 are among the most significant — and in a healthy market, this buy-side competition drives efficient price discovery. [A standard taxonomy](https://adtech.eu/dsp-vs-ssp-vs-ad-exchange-understanding-the-programmatic-advertising-ecosystem/) of the DSP/SSP/exchange ecosystem clarifies these functional roles: DSPs aggregate demand, supply-side platforms (SSPs) aggregate supply, and exchanges are the clearing venues that sit between them. AdX operates both as an exchange *and*, for publishers using DFP, as an SSP, collapsing two roles into one — a technical integration at the center of the DOJ's structural complaint.

**The Structural Problem: Operating Both Sides of the Auction**

The critical feature of Google's position in this stack is not market share at any single layer; it is simultaneous participation across every layer. Google's dual role — as the operator of AdX (the trading venue) and DV360 (a significant buyer *on* that venue) — structurally destroys the neutral-intermediary condition that makes an exchange function as a genuine price-discovery mechanism. An exchange operator that simultaneously bids on its own auctions has both the opportunity and the incentive to design auction rules that advantage its buy-side tool at the expense of competing buyers, and to design publisher ad server integrations that route premium inventory to its own exchange before competing exchanges can access it. The vertical position does not make anticompetitive conduct inevitable — but it creates a structural architecture in which manipulation is easily accomplished, difficult to detect, and highly profitable. That architecture is what the DOJ set out to prove, and what Judge Brinkema found that it had.

**What the Liability Finding Did Not Reach**

One boundary of the liability record is critical for remedy design. Judge Brinkema [declined to find Google liable](https://www.courthousenews.com/judge-hands-google-partial-defeat-in-ad-tech-monopoly-case/) for monopolization of the advertiser DSP market. DV360 operates in a competitive environment — The Trade Desk, Amazon, and others are viable alternatives — and the court did not find that Google had leveraged vertical integration to monopolize the buy side. This matters enormously for the scope of any remedy. The adjudicated harm is localized to the exchange layer and the publisher ad server's relationship with it. A remedy that extends to DV360's conduct — or that severs Google from advertiser-side tools entirely — would exceed the liability finding and impose costs untethered to proven violations. This constraint structures the welfare comparison that Section 8 develops: the question is not how far back Google's vertical integration should be unwound, but whether the specific exchange-layer and publisher ad server harms the court found can be cured precisely, without collateral disruption to the efficiency gains embedded in the stack.

## 2. The Liability Record: What Judge Brinkema Found — and Didn't

The remedies question in *United States v. Google* (Ad Tech) cannot be answered without a precise account of the liability record — not as a general finding that Google monopolized something, but as a specific catalogue of the mechanisms through which that monopolization operated. [Judge Brinkema's April 2025 opinion](https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/66753787/united-states-v-google-llc/) identified four discrete anticompetitive practices, each a distinct manipulation of auction design rather than a consequence of Google's vertical integration per se. Understanding each mechanism — and their collective character — is essential because remedy design follows liability: the question the court must answer is not whether Google is large, but whether the instruments it deployed to entrench its position can be specifically neutralized.

**First Look: Inventory Access Before the Auction Opens**

The first mechanism is what the record calls "First Look." Under this arrangement, AdX received preferential access to DFP publisher inventory before competing exchanges could submit bids. When a publisher's page loaded and a DFP-managed impression became available, AdX was given the first opportunity to fill the slot at or above the publisher's floor price — before any header-bidding wrapper or competing exchange could participate in a genuine open auction. The practical effect was to allow AdX to cherry-pick high-value impressions at floor price without competing for them. For publishers relying on DFP, the arrangement was invisible: their ad server was structured to route premium inventory to the integrated exchange before activating open-market competition, suppressing effective yield.

**Last Look: The Unfair Matching Right**

The second mechanism — "Last Look" — is perhaps the most analytically striking because it inverted the basic rules of competitive bidding. After competing exchanges submitted their bids through header-bidding wrappers, AdX was given the opportunity to observe those bids and match the highest competing offer. This is not auction competition; it is the elimination of auction risk. A bidder who can see all competing offers before deciding whether to win faces no price discovery problem — it simply matches any price that would otherwise clear to a competitor. The economic consequence is that competing exchanges' investments in demand aggregation were captured by AdX without the competitive discipline that motivates such investment. [As AdExchanger's coverage of the liability opinion noted](https://www.adexchanger.com/online-advertising/doj-v-google-judge-brinkema-calls-for-less-ad-tech-window-dressing-in-the-adx-and-dfp-divestiture-debate/), Brinkema was particularly pointed in rejecting Google's framing of Last Look as a routine auction feature.

**Unified Pricing Rules: Eliminating Publisher Floor Price Discrimination**

The third mechanism is [Unified Pricing Rules (UPR), which Google imposed on publishers using DFP beginning in May 2019](https://www.tercept.com/2019/07/02/google-ad-manager-unified-pricing-rules-google-ad-exchange-first-price-auctions-demystified/). Prior to UPR, publishers could set different floor prices for different exchanges — a rational pricing strategy that allowed them to extract higher revenue from exchanges with premium demand. UPR eliminated this flexibility: publishers were required to apply the same floor price to all exchanges, including AdX. The anticompetitive effect operated asymmetrically. Because AdX enjoyed structural advantages through First Look and Last Look, competing exchanges needed to offer higher bids to win impressions. UPR prevented publishers from calibrating floors to reflect this reality, effectively protecting AdX's pricing position. The mechanism did not benefit publishers — it constrained their ability to maximize yield — and it served no efficiency purpose except to suppress competitive pressure on AdX's take rate.

**Project Poirot: Algorithmic Bid Suppression on the Buy Side**

The fourth mechanism was internal to Google's buy-side operations. Project Poirot was an algorithmic program within DV360 designed to detect when competing ad exchanges were winning impressions at prices lower than AdX would have charged and to suppress DV360's bids in those contexts. The effect was to reduce competitive demand flowing through non-Google exchanges, weakening their ability to attract publishers and build scale. Where First Look and Last Look operated on the sell side, Poirot operated on the buy side — completing a manipulation strategy that touched every point in the auction stack.

**What the Record Establishes — and Does Not**

These four mechanisms share a defining characteristic that is critical for remedy design: they are *auction-design* harms. Each one manipulates the rules of the bidding process — who sees what, when, at what price, with what information — rather than reflecting an inherent disadvantage of vertical integration. That distinction matters because auction-design harms are, in principle, specifically addressable: prohibit First Look, prohibit Last Look, restore publisher floor-price flexibility, prohibit buy-side bid suppression targeting competitor exchanges. The violation catalogue maps directly onto a targeted behavioral remedy.

Equally important is what [Judge Brinkema did not find](https://www.techpolicy.press/doj-vs-google-back-to-court-for-remedies-to-break-digital-ads-monopoly/). The court declined to hold that Google monopolized the advertiser DSP market. DV360 competes against The Trade Desk, Amazon, and others in a market the court found structurally competitive. That non-finding is a limiting principle of the first order: any remedy that imposes obligations on DV360's conduct, or that severs Google from the advertiser-side stack entirely, exceeds what the liability record supports. The proven harm is exchange-layer and publisher ad server harm. The remedy should match that scope precisely — a principle the welfare comparison in Section 8 develops in full.

## 3. Two-Sided Market Theory and the Programmatic Exchange Layer

The programmatic advertising exchange is not a conventional product market. It is a *platform market* — a two-sided market in which the exchange simultaneously serves two distinct customer groups whose value is interdependent. Understanding this structure is not merely a theoretical formality; it directly determines what remedy can restore competitive conditions without destroying the welfare gains embedded in the platform's architecture.

**The Rochet-Tirole Framework**

Jean-Charles Rochet and Jean Tirole's foundational [2003 paper in the *Journal of the European Economic Association*](https://academic.oup.com/jeea/article-abstract/1/4/990/2280902) established the analytical baseline for two-sided platform economics. Their core insight is that two-sided platforms exhibit *cross-side network externalities*: participants on one side of the platform derive value from the number and quality of participants on the other side. AdX is a textbook instance. Publishers (sellers of ad impressions) value AdX more as more advertisers bid through it — more competition on the buy side drives higher clearing prices for publisher inventory. Advertisers value AdX more as more publishers route premium inventory through it — broader supply improves targeting reach and reduces per-impression costs through competitive clearing. Neither side benefits from the exchange in isolation; the platform's value is generated by the conjunction.

This two-sidedness creates a well-known pricing challenge that [Rochet and Tirole formalized](https://www.tse-fr.eu/sites/default/files/medias/doc/by/rochet/rochet_tirole.pdf): the efficient exchange operator must set prices on each side such that the total surplus generated by the platform is maximized, which generally means subsidizing the more price-sensitive side and charging the less price-sensitive side. In the advertising context, this has historically meant that publisher-side pricing (the SSP take rate) and advertiser-side pricing (the DSP commission) must be calibrated jointly — a task that an integrated AdX/DFP operator could, in principle, perform efficiently if it were a genuinely neutral intermediary.

**The Broken Neutral-Intermediary Condition**

The critical implication for *United States v. Google* is what happens when the exchange operator ceases to be a neutral intermediary. A neutral AdX, operating without a proprietary buy-side stake, has every incentive to design auction rules that maximize total platform surplus: publishers bring more inventory when auctions are competitive; advertisers pay more when inventory is abundant and well-targeted. The efficient auction design maximizes the joint surplus of both sides.

When Google operates DV360 — a major buy-side participant — simultaneously with AdX, this incentive structure is distorted. A firm with a proprietary DSP on the buy side can design auction rules that transfer value from the publisher side (by suppressing effective competition for impressions) and from competing DSPs (by systematically disadvantaging their bids) to the integrated operator's own exchange take rate and DSP margins. This is precisely the harm the liability record documents: First Look, Last Look, Unified Pricing Rules, and Project Poirot are each, in the two-sided market framework, mechanisms for extracting platform surplus from publishers and competing exchange participants rather than maximizing joint welfare.

Importantly, the two-sided market framework clarifies that this harm was not an inevitable consequence of vertical integration — it was a consequence of the specific auction-design choices Google made while vertically integrated. Vertical integration across buy and sell sides creates the *opportunity* for manipulation, but a neutral vertically integrated operator could, in principle, preserve competitive auction design. The liability record shows that Google did not.

***Ohio v. American Express* and the Welfare Analysis Complication**

The Supreme Court's 2018 decision in [*Ohio v. American Express Co.*](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/17pdf/16-1454_5h26.pdf) introduced a significant complication into two-sided platform antitrust analysis that the parties in *United States v. Google* vigorously contested. The Court, in a 5-4 decision, held that in two-sided transaction platform markets, competitive harm cannot be assessed by looking at one side of the platform in isolation — both sides must be analyzed together to determine net welfare effects. American Express had imposed anti-steering provisions preventing merchants from directing customers to competing payment networks; the Court held that analysis confined to merchant-side harm was insufficient without accounting for cardholder benefits.

Google invoked *Amex* throughout the remedies phase to argue that any buyer-side advantages to DV360 must be weighed against publisher-side efficiencies generated by the integrated stack. The DOJ's counter — and Judge Brinkema's implicit agreement — is that *Amex*'s holistic-welfare requirement does not immunize manipulation of auction rules that the court found anticompetitive. The distinction tracks a line that [the DOJ's own framework on two-sided market analysis](https://www.justice.gov/sites/default/files/atr/legacy/2011/05/12/270430.pdf) drew: *Amex* requires accounting for both sides' welfare in market definition and harm assessment, but it does not require treating the gains from anticompetitive conduct as an efficiency offset. The proven First Look and Last Look advantages were not publisher-welfare-enhancing; they were publisher-welfare-reducing. *Amex* therefore does not protect them.

**Implications for Remedy Design**

The two-sided market framework generates a specific prediction for remedy design: the remedy that restores competition most efficiently is the one that reinstates the neutral-intermediary condition at the exchange layer. There are two candidate mechanisms. Structural divestiture severs the buy-side stake (DV360) from the exchange (AdX), reconstituting AdX as an operator without a proprietary bidder on its platform. Behavioral remedies prohibit the specific auction-design rules that broke the neutral-intermediary condition — First Look, Last Look, UPR — and mandate transparency and interoperability rules that allow competing exchanges to compete for the same inventory on equal terms.

The two-sided market framework does not dictate which mechanism is superior — it identifies the *condition to be restored*. That question requires the structural remedy economics and welfare comparison analysis that Sections 5 and 8 develop. What the Rochet-Tirole framework establishes is that the harm is not structural in origin — it is auction-design in origin — and therefore that the harm could, in principle, be addressed by precisely targeted auction-design prohibitions without mandatory separation of complementary assets.

## 4. The Efficiency Justification: Integration Gains vs. Anticompetitive Self-Dealing

No serious economic analysis of the remedies question can proceed without engaging Google's efficiency defense on its merits. The efficiency argument is not merely litigation strategy — it reflects a genuine technical reality that a well-designed remedy must confront. The question is not whether Google's vertical integration across DFP and AdX generates any real efficiencies, but whether those efficiencies justify the specific auction-design conduct the court found anticompetitive, and whether structural separation would destroy them or whether they are replicable through targeted behavioral obligations.

**Google's Efficiency Defense**

[Google's September 2025 behavioral remedy counter-proposal](https://blog.google/company-news/outreach-and-initiatives/public-policy/doj-ad-tech-case-sept-2025/) advanced three categories of integration efficiency. First, *latency optimization*: real-time bidding auctions operate under severe time constraints — an impression opportunity must be cleared and a winning creative returned within approximately 100 milliseconds of the page-load event. When an ad server and exchange share a unified technical infrastructure, auction calls, bid responses, and creative delivery traverse a single internal pipeline rather than crossing external API boundaries. Google's technical team argued that this integration reduces per-impression latency by a meaningful margin, and that latency improvements directly increase publisher yield by reducing auction timeouts. Second, *signal integration*: publisher ad servers accumulate rich user-level context — audience segments, prior engagement history, publisher-defined content categories — that meaningfully improves advertiser bid precision. Google argues that AdX's access to DFP's publisher-side signals through integrated infrastructure, rather than through an API handshake, improves match quality and increases the value of impressions for both publishers and advertisers. Third, *yield management*: when the publisher ad server and exchange share auction data without API overhead, publishers can implement sophisticated floor-price optimization, frequency capping, and audience packaging that enhances their competitive position.

These are not frivolous claims. The programmatic advertising industry has documented the latency sensitivity of RTB environments extensively, and [the convergence of SSP and DSP capabilities](https://www.exchangewire.com/blog/2025/04/29/the-evolution-of-ssps-dsps-are-we-headed-for-a-converged-future/) reflects a broader industry recognition that integration across stack layers can produce real technical gains.

**The Whinston Framework**

Michael Whinston's canonical [1990 analysis of tying](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.80.4.837) in the *American Economic Review* provides the relevant framework for evaluating these efficiency claims. Whinston established that tying — conditioning the sale of one product on the purchase of another — is anticompetitive when it forecloses rivals in the tied product market, but can be pro-competitive when integration generates genuine efficiency gains that cannot be contractually replicated between separate firms. The critical question is replicability: if the efficiency can be achieved through an API contract or interoperability standard without vertical integration, the efficiency argument does not justify integration that forecloses competition; if the efficiency requires deep technical coupling that no arm's-length agreement can replicate, the calculus shifts. Joseph Farrell and Carl Shapiro's [cognizability framework for merger efficiencies](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=502846) — developed in *Scale Economies and Synergies in Horizontal Merger Analysis*, 68 Antitrust L.J. 685 (2001) — similarly requires that claimed efficiencies be integration-specific, achievable through combination but not through less restrictive alternatives, before they can be credited in a structural analysis.

**Testing the Efficiency Claims Against the Liability Record**

Applied to the Google ad tech context, the Whinston-Farrell-Shapiro framework distinguishes two categories of claimed efficiency. The latency and signal-integration gains are, at minimum, partially real — the question is whether they are *integration-specific* or *manipulation-specific*. The efficiency of a shared internal pipeline between DFP and AdX is a genuine architectural advantage. But it does not require First Look, Last Look, or Unified Pricing Rules. Those mechanisms did not reduce latency or improve match quality; they manipulated who won auctions and at what price. Judge Brinkema's opinion found them anticompetitive precisely because they extracted surplus from publishers rather than enlarging total platform welfare — the opposite of what a genuine efficiency generates. The efficiency defense, properly understood, applies to the integration architecture, not to the auction-manipulation mechanisms that happened to be implemented on top of that architecture.

Critically, the server-to-server integration protocols that underpin programmatic advertising have become increasingly standardized. Prebid's open-source header bidding wrapper, OpenRTB protocol standards, and emerging seller-defined audiences frameworks have already demonstrated that meaningful interoperability between competing ad servers and exchanges is achievable without vertical integration. [Prebid's growth](https://prebid.org/overview/what-is-prebid-org/) — now implemented by a majority of large publishers — is direct market evidence that the efficiency gains Google claims for DFP-AdX integration are at least partially replicable through behavioral access requirements rather than exclusive integration.

**The Partial Validity Conclusion**

The efficiency defense survives scrutiny in one limited respect: the genuine interoperability gains from a unified technical pipeline — latency reduction and signal integration — are real and should not be casually disregarded in remedy design. Structural divestiture that requires DFP and AdX to operate entirely independently, without access to shared audience and auction data, would impose a transition cost on publishers that goes beyond curing the proven harm. But this conclusion is a reason to design behavioral remedies carefully — not a reason to leave the proven auction manipulations unremediated. First Look, Last Look, Unified Pricing Rules, and Project Poirot generated no publisher welfare gains; they were pure extraction mechanisms. Their prohibition imposes no efficiency cost. The genuine efficiency gains are preserved by requiring API-based interoperability standards that allow any exchange to access publisher signals on non-discriminatory terms — precisely the remedy architecture that server-to-server integration protocols make technically feasible.

## 5. Structural Remedy Economics: The AT&T Precedent and Technically Interdependent Markets

Structural divestiture is the most powerful instrument in the antitrust remedies arsenal — and the most dangerous to misapply. The economic literature on structural remedies identifies three conditions that, when present jointly, predict that mandatory separation will produce durable competition rather than market fragmentation, regulatory burden, or eventual reconsolidation. The 1984 AT&T divestiture is the canonical case from which those conditions are derived. Applying them to a divested AdX reveals a structural mismatch that the DOJ's proposal must answer.

**Three Conditions for Structural Remedy Success**

The first condition is *technical separability of the bottleneck facility*. A structural remedy works when the monopolized bottleneck can be physically or technically disaggregated from complementary services without destroying the interoperability value that both sides of the market depend on. If separation requires recreating through costly API integration what integration once delivered natively — at meaningful performance cost to end users — the structural remedy imposes transition costs that a behavioral remedy could avoid.

The second condition is *viable independent operation of the divested entity*. Structural divestiture creates a new firm. That firm must be able to attract customers, maintain infrastructure, fund operations, and invest in innovation as a standalone enterprise. The economic literature on merger retrospectives — and the FTC's post-*Whole Foods* experience with required divestitures in grocery — documents the pattern of failed divestitures: where the divested entity lacks the scale economies, supply relationships, or brand equity to compete independently, the divestiture produces a failing firm rather than a competitive one. A viable acquirer who will run the divested entity sustainably is not a formality; it is a structural prerequisite.

The third condition is *enforceable access obligations on retained assets*. Even a technically separable, independently viable divested entity will fail if the incumbent retains assets that can be used to discriminate against it. Structural separation must therefore be accompanied by behavioral conditions — equal-access mandates, non-discrimination requirements, audit mechanisms — that prevent the incumbent from leveraging retained market power to disadvantage the new competitor.

**AT&T 1984: Conditions Met**

[*United States v. AT&T*](https://www.fjc.gov/history/spotlight-judicial-history/breakup-ma-bell) offers the most instructive application of these three conditions. Judge Harold Greene's [1982 Modification of Final Judgment](https://scholar.google.com/scholar_case?case=7419767286213982960) separated AT&T's local exchange operations — the "local loops" that connected homes and businesses to the telephone network — from its long-distance switching and Bell Labs research operations. The local loop was the bottleneck: no alternative local connection to the national telephone network existed for the vast majority of American households, and AT&T's control of local access enabled it to discriminate against competing long-distance carriers like MCI and Sprint. All three structural remedy conditions were satisfied. Physical separability was not in question: local copper plant, central office switches, and regional operations were geographically discrete assets that could be owned and operated by independent regional entities without any interoperability degradation. The seven resulting Baby Bells were constituted from existing regional infrastructure — AT&T's own operating companies had functioned as semi-autonomous divisions — and each held a regional monopoly franchise sufficient to generate the revenue needed to sustain independent operation from day one. And Greene's equal-access conditions required the Baby Bells to provide MCI and Sprint with interconnection on terms identical to what AT&T's own long-distance operation received, directly addressing the discriminatory conduct the case had proven.

The performance record supports the conditions analysis. Long-distance competition was restored rapidly — AT&T's share of the long-distance market fell from near-total dominance to below 50 percent within a decade as MCI and Sprint expanded. [Watzinger and Schnitzer's empirical study of the 1984 AT&T divestiture](https://www.monika-schnitzer.com/uploads/4/9/4/1/49415675/watzinger_schnitzer_breakup_of_bell.pdf) found that structural separation generated measurable innovation spillovers among downstream telecommunications firms, with an estimated 19 percent increase in total patenting by non-Bell firms in the post-divestiture period, consistent with the hypothesis that monopoly control had been suppressing downstream innovation. The long-run narrative is more complicated: reconsolidation through SBC/AT&T (2005) and Verizon/MCI (2005) suggests that the structural separation produced sustainable competition during a technological transition period but did not permanently foreclose market consolidation pressures. Still, the immediate competitive restoration the divestiture achieved validates the conditions-based framework: where the three conditions were met, the structural remedy worked.

**AdX: Conditions Partially Absent**

Applying the same three conditions to the DOJ's proposed AdX divestiture reveals a more complicated picture — one that is instructive not because the AT&T analogy fails entirely, but because it reveals precisely where the structural remedy case is weakest.

On *technical separability*, the comparison is mixed. AdX is not physically discrete from DFP in the way that AT&T's copper local loops were geographically distinct from its long-distance switching fabric. The auction logic that runs an impression opportunity through AdX is deeply integrated with DFP's ad server call — the same code stack handles impression valuation, floor price enforcement, auction mechanics, and creative delivery in a unified millisecond sequence. Separation requires either replicating this shared pipeline through an external API — an architecture that, as Section 4 established, imposes latency and signal-integration costs — or building new server-to-server protocols between an independent AdX and a Google-retained DFP that approximate what integration currently delivers natively. The OpenRTB and Prebid standards demonstrate that such protocols are technically achievable; the question is whether they replicate the performance characteristics of native integration closely enough that publishers do not experience meaningful yield degradation during transition. This is a genuine uncertainty, not a knock-down argument against divestiture — but it is a condition AT&T did not face.

On *viable independent operation*, the structural case is weakest. [Judge Brinkema herself expressed significant skepticism](https://www.adexchanger.com/antitrust/closing-arguments-are-done-in-the-us-v-google-ad-tech-case/) during the November 2025 closing arguments about whether any credible buyer for AdX exists. The viable acquirer problem is not merely logistical. AdX's value as an exchange depends on the cross-side network effects established in Section 3: publisher inventory and advertiser demand are symbiotic, and both are currently anchored partly by AdX's deep integration with DFP. A divested AdX operating without that integration anchor would need to rebuild those network effects from a structurally weaker position, in direct competition with The Trade Desk, Amazon Publisher Services, and Index Exchange — all of which have been growing their publisher relationships precisely because header bidding has already reduced AdX's First Look advantage. Private equity ownership — the acquirer category most likely to emerge in the absence of a strategic buyer — introduces additional concerns about investment horizon and whether a financially engineered acquirer would sustain the infrastructure investment necessary to compete as a genuine neutral exchange.

On *enforceable access obligations*, the picture is more favorable to the structural remedy case. If AdX is divested while DFP remains with Google, the central behavioral risk is that Google designs DFP's auction calls to discriminate against an independent AdX — routing premium inventory to Google-retained exchange assets, recreating preferential access through technical means rather than prohibited auction rules. MacAvoy and Robinson's [analysis of the AT&T settlement](https://openyls.law.yale.edu/handle/20.500.13051/7868) emphasized that equal-access obligations were operationally specific and technically auditable in the local-loop context: interconnection standards were well-defined, and violations were detectable through network engineering review. [Reports on Judge Brinkema's structural remedy skepticism](https://www.thecurrent.com/data-privacy-judge-structural-remedies-google-ad-tech-monopoly-case) reflect a parallel concern in the AdX context: DFP's auction code is proprietary, its routing logic is opaque, and monitoring compliance with non-discrimination obligations would require sustained technical oversight that no existing consent decree apparatus has demonstrated at this level of sophistication.

The AT&T analogy, properly read, supports caution rather than confidence in the structural remedy. Where the 1984 divestiture succeeded, it did so because the three conditions were met jointly and robustly. The AdX context satisfies at most one of them with confidence, partially satisfies a second, and leaves the third — viable independent operation — as an open empirical question that Judge Brinkema herself has flagged as a serious concern. As Paul W. MacAvoy and Kenneth Robinson demonstrated in their foundational analysis of the AT&T settlement, [*Winning by Losing: The AT&T Settlement and Its Impact on Telecommunications*](https://openyls.law.yale.edu/handle/20.500.13051/7868), 1 Yale J. on Reg. 1 (1983), structural remedies succeed when the divested entity inherits the market structure it needs to compete — not when it is asked to build competitive scale from a weakened position against established incumbents. That asymmetry is the core finding of this section: the structural precedent that the DOJ invokes most heavily is, on close inspection, the strongest argument for behavioral alternatives.

## 6. EU Behavioral Remedy Precedents and Their Limits

The economic case for behavioral remedies in digital platform antitrust does not rest on theory alone. The European Union has administered behavioral remedies in major technology antitrust cases for over two decades, producing a track record that illuminates both what calibrated intervention can achieve and the institutional conditions it requires. Three episodes — the 2004 Microsoft case, the 2017 Google Shopping decision, and the ongoing Digital Markets Act enforcement regime — constitute the most directly relevant comparative evidence. The pattern they establish is consistent: behavioral remedies can restore meaningful competitive access in technically complex markets, but only when accompanied by operationally specific obligations, credible monitoring infrastructure, and enforcement willingness to escalate.

**The 2004 Microsoft Decision: Interoperability Over Structural Separation**

The Commission's March 2004 decision against Microsoft is the EU's most instructive behavioral remedy precedent for the AdX context. Faced with Microsoft's dominant position in PC operating systems and its leveraging of that dominance into work-group server markets, the Commission chose interoperability disclosure over structural separation. Rather than ordering Microsoft to divest Windows Server or its networking stack, the Commission required Microsoft to disclose the communication protocols that Windows client computers used to interact with Windows workgroup server software — enabling rival server operating systems to interoperate with existing Windows client deployments on equal terms.

The remedy was architecturally significant: the Commission identified the bottleneck as *protocol information*, not as ownership structure. The [Protocol Freedom Information Foundation's agreement with Microsoft](https://www.samba.org/samba/PFIF/), reached in December 2007 after the EU General Court [upheld the Commission's decision](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:62004TJ0201) in September 2007, gave the Samba development team access to the server protocol documentation Microsoft had withheld for years. The effect was material: Samba 4 developed into a [genuine Active Directory-compatible domain controller](https://www.infoworld.com/article/2616395/samba-4-threatens-microsoft-s-enterprise-lock-in.html) that enterprises could deploy as a Windows server alternative — a competitive outcome that structural separation almost certainly would not have produced more efficiently.

The Microsoft remedy's limits are equally instructive. [Economides and Lianos's critical appraisal](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/laws/sites/laws/files/economides_lianos_appraisal_remedies_antitrust_microsoft_cases.pdf) documented that server market shares shifted slowly despite interoperability access, reflecting network-effect lock-in that protocol disclosure alone could not overcome. The Commission's monitoring trustee mechanism — the body responsible for verifying Microsoft's compliance — was the single institutional positive that commentators consistently identified: it provided an ongoing technical audit function that generic consent decree monitoring cannot replicate. The lesson is not that the remedy failed; Linux-based servers now power a substantial portion of enterprise infrastructure. The lesson is that effectiveness took years, required a dedicated monitoring institution, and depended on a downstream open-source project with the technical capacity to use the disclosed protocols productively.

**Google Shopping (2017): The Behavioral Remedy's Compliance Problem**

The Commission's 2017 Google Shopping decision — a €2.42 billion fine and a behavioral remedy requiring equal treatment of rival comparison shopping services in Google's search results — illustrates the compliance risk that behavioral remedies face when obligations are underspecified or monitoring is insufficiently technical. Google satisfied the formal requirement by [establishing its shopping unit as a nominally separate entity](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=1f90adb7-0526-4f1c-bfa9-ddde8e735201) that bid for placement in its own auction — a structural formality that left Google's shopping service with advantages no competitor could replicate. Empirical [assessments of the remedy's market impact](https://www.hausfeld.com/what-we-think/competition-bulletin/antitrust-remedies-in-digital-markets-lessons-for-enforcement-authorities-from-non-compliance-with-eu-google-decisions) consistently found that competing comparison shopping services did not materially improve their position in Google search results after implementation.

The Google Shopping failure carries a specific lesson for the AdX context: behavioral remedies fail not because behavioral intervention is inherently inadequate but because vague compliance standards invite evasion. An auction-design behavioral remedy that prohibits "self-preferencing" in abstract terms is gameable; one that mandates open-source publication of auction allocation logic, real-time bid-data sharing on non-discriminatory terms, and independent technical monitoring of routing rules is substantially harder to game without detectable violation.

**The DMA as Ex-Ante Behavioral Remedy Infrastructure**

The EU's [Digital Markets Act (Regulation 2022/1925)](https://digital-markets-act.ec.europa.eu/latest-news_en) represents the institutionalization of behavioral remedies as a prospective regulatory architecture rather than a litigation-triggered response. By designating large platforms as "gatekeepers" and imposing interoperability, data portability, and non-discrimination obligations ex ante, the DMA eliminates the compliance lag inherent in case-specific consent decrees. The Commission's 2025 [preliminary non-compliance findings against Alphabet](https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/detail/en/ip_25_811) — covering Google Search's self-preferencing of own services and Google Play's restrictions on developer steering — demonstrate both the DMA's enforcement ambition and its ongoing implementation challenges. Compliance is contested, gaming is occurring, and the Commission's enforcement apparatus is being stress-tested in real time.

The DMA precedent is not directly applicable to *United States v. Google* (Ad Tech) — there is no US equivalent of ex-ante gatekeeper regulation, and Judge Brinkema is constrained to remedy the specific violations proven. But the DMA's design philosophy is instructive: behavioral obligations at sufficient specificity, backed by mandatory technical documentation, real-time audit rights, and credible escalation penalties, can maintain competitive conditions in platform markets without requiring ownership restructuring. The reversibility that DMA-style behavioral intervention offers — the ability to tighten, adjust, or expand obligations as market conditions evolve — is itself a structural advantage over the irreversible disruption of corporate separation in technical markets that, as Section 7 establishes, are already correcting through competitive entry.

## 7. The Counterfactual: Can a Stand-Alone AdX Sustain Competition?

The remedies question in any antitrust case is ultimately a counterfactual question. The court must compare two projected market states: the market under the proposed remedy versus the market under a less interventionist alternative. For structural divestiture of AdX, the relevant counterfactual is not a perfectly competitive exchange market arising the moment Google relinquishes ownership; it is a specific market configuration — a stand-alone AdX, separated from DFP, operating under some ownership structure yet to be determined, competing against established independent exchanges while managing the transition costs of disaggregation. Three structural vulnerabilities in that counterfactual deserve close examination.

**The Viable Acquirer Problem**

The first vulnerability is the most immediate. Structural divestiture requires not merely a legal separation of AdX from Google's corporate structure, but a transfer of operating control to an entity capable of running AdX as a competitive exchange. [Judge Brinkema expressed pointed skepticism about this question](https://www.adexchanger.com/antitrust/closing-arguments-are-done-in-the-us-v-google-ad-tech-case/) during the November 2025 closing arguments — a skepticism that is analytically grounded, not merely procedural caution.

No strategic buyer with the scale to sustain AdX's publisher and advertiser network effects has publicly expressed interest or been identified in the remedies record. The candidates available — private equity sponsors, smaller ad tech companies, sovereign wealth funds — each present distinct problems. Private equity ownership introduces an investment-horizon mismatch: running a technically intensive, millisecond-latency exchange infrastructure that competes against deeply capitalized platform operators requires sustained capital expenditure and engineering talent that a leveraged buyout structure does not naturally support. A smaller ad tech company lacks the balance sheet to maintain the exchange at scale and the auction volume necessary to preserve the cross-side network effects that make AdX valuable to publishers and advertisers alike. [ITIF's May 2025 analysis](https://itif.org/publications/2025/05/12/the-dojs-problematic-remedies-proposal-google-ad-tech-case/) catalogued multiple weaknesses in the DOJ's structural proposal, identifying the absence of any credible acquirer as among the most critical practical obstacles.

This is not a theoretical objection. The FTC's long experience with merger remedy divestitures has produced a documented pattern: where divestitures are completed to buyers who lack the operational infrastructure, brand relationships, or scale economies to compete effectively, the divested entity underperforms and often fails. A failing AdX, unable to sustain publisher inventory and advertiser demand simultaneously, would not produce the competitive exchange market the DOJ seeks; it would produce a consolidation event that concentrates the programmatic advertising stack among the surviving large players.

**The Orphaned Exchange Problem**

The second vulnerability concerns the relationship between AdX and DFP after structural separation — a relationship the DOJ's own conditional DFP divestiture provisions acknowledge is structurally problematic. If DFP is retained by Google (or divested on a different timeline from AdX), publishers operating on DFP have a path-of-least-resistance incentive to route premium inventory through whatever exchange DFP most efficiently integrates with after the divestiture. That exchange, in the immediate post-separation period, is likely to be a Google-retained ad exchange asset or a successor product that inherits DFP's API integration priority.

This is not speculation about Google's future bad faith; it is a prediction grounded in market structure. [As Digiday's remedies coverage documented](https://digiday.com/marketing/googles-ad-tech-antitrust-remedy-phase-explained-doj-wants-googles-offerings-and-where-the-judge-might-land/), publishers using DFP have extremely high switching costs — years of historical data, audience configurations, and revenue optimization are embedded in DFP's infrastructure. If the auction routing architecture of a post-divestiture DFP gives a non-AdX exchange even a marginal technical advantage in integration quality, publisher inventory will flow there through market incentives rather than through prohibited rule manipulation. The orphaned exchange problem is thus a risk that structural divestiture could recreate the preferential access pattern through market structure — without any prohibited auction rules for the court to adjudicate — rather than eliminating it.

**The Market Self-Correction Problem**

The third vulnerability may be the most consequential for the long-run welfare analysis: the programmatic advertising market has been correcting through competitive entry at a pace that complicates the structural remedy case. [The National Law Review's coverage of the closing arguments](https://natlawreview.com/article/judge-leans-away-breakup-during-closing-arguments-google-ad-tech-case) noted that Judge Brinkema pressed DOJ counsel on this issue directly — whether the harm the court found was still being actively reproduced or whether market forces had already substantially reduced it.

The evidentiary record supports the question — but the market self-correction argument requires mechanism-level precision. [Header bidding](https://digiday.com/media/wtf-header-bidding/) — the publisher-side technology that routes impression opportunities to multiple exchanges simultaneously before selecting a winner — has been [widely adopted by major publishers since its emergence in 2015-2016](https://prebid.org/overview/what-is-prebid-org/), and has materially reduced AdX's First Look advantage for publishers who deploy it: when publishers use header bidding wrappers, all exchanges receive the impression opportunity before any single exchange clears the auction, effectively eliminating the sequential-access structure on which First Look depended. However, header bidding did not address the other three proven violations. Last Look's matching right operated at the post-auction reconciliation layer, upstream of header bidding wrapper logic; Unified Pricing Rules constrained publisher floor-price flexibility regardless of whether publishers had deployed header bidding wrappers; and Project Poirot's buy-side bid suppression operated entirely within DV360's internal algorithms, invisible to header bidding architecture. Market forces have partially neutralized one of the four proven mechanisms. The behavioral remedy package must address all four.

Beyond the header bidding correction, [The Trade Desk delivered $2.44 billion in revenue in 2024, representing 26 percent year-over-year growth](https://investors.thetradedesk.com/news-and-events/news/news-details/2025/The-Trade-Desk-Reports-Fourth-Quarter-and-Fiscal-Year-2024-Financial-Results/default.aspx), reflecting substantial buy-side expansion by the most significant non-Google DSP. Amazon Publisher Services has extended its exchange presence, and Index Exchange has grown its publisher relationships. Meanwhile, [Google Network revenues — the segment that includes AdX's publisher monetization business — declined 4 percent in Q1 2026](https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0001652044/000165204426000043/googexhibit991q12026.htm), continuing a downward trend as advertising spend migrates toward walled gardens, retail media environments, and connected TV that AdX does not serve. The market Google was accused of monopolizing in 2023, when the original complaint was litigated, looks meaningfully different in 2026.

None of this renders the proven violations irrelevant — the liability record documents real harm that operated for years. But it does raise the welfare question that structural remedy analysis must confront: if behavioral auction-design prohibitions and interoperability mandates are imposed on a market already partially self-correcting through competitive entry, and if those behavioral prohibitions address all four proven mechanisms — including the three that market forces have not yet addressed — the behavioral remedy may be sufficient to prevent recurrence without the disruption of a structural separation imposed on a market mid-correction.

Together, these three vulnerabilities — the viable acquirer problem, the orphaned exchange problem, and the market self-correction problem — constitute the counterfactual case against structural divestiture as the primary remedy instrument. They do not establish that behavioral remedies alone are sufficient; that is the question Section 8 addresses directly. What they establish is that the DOJ's structural proposal rests on a counterfactual in which a stand-alone AdX competes more effectively than the structural analysis predicts, and that each of the three assumptions that counterfactual requires is empirically contested.

## 8. Behavioral vs. Structural Alternatives: A Welfare Comparison

The preceding sections have established four factual predicates that now must be synthesized into a policy conclusion. First, the proven harms in *United States v. Google* (Ad Tech) are four specific auction-design manipulations — not harms inherent to vertical integration across the stack. Second, AdX is a two-sided platform whose efficiency depends on the neutral-intermediary condition; that condition can be restored either by severing Google's buy-side stake or by prohibiting the specific auction rules that broke it. Third, the AT&T structural remedy precedent is instructive but inapposite: two of its three enabling conditions are at best partially present for a divested AdX. Fourth, the programmatic advertising market is already correcting through competitive entry, header bidding adoption, and a viable-acquirer vacuum that the DOJ's divestiture proposal has not resolved. Against these predicates, three criteria determine which remedy design is welfare-superior.

**Criterion One: Scope-Targeting**

Antitrust doctrine requires that remedy scope match the violation scope. The D.C. Circuit stated the principle clearly in [*United States v. Microsoft Corp.*](https://caselaw.findlaw.com/court/us-court-of-appeals-district-of-columbia-circuit/1277360.html): a remedy "must be 'tailored to fit the wrong creating the occasion for the remedy.'" [*Ford Motor Co. v. United States*](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/405/562/) similarly held that structural remedies must bear a rational relationship to the specific anticompetitive effects proven, not to the defendant's size or general market presence. Judge Brinkema's liability finding identified four auction-design mechanisms as the operative violations. The welfare question that scope-targeting doctrine poses is whether structural divestiture of AdX and DFP is rationally related to those four mechanisms — or whether it exceeds them by addressing Google's general vertical position, which the court assessed and partially declined to condemn.

A targeted behavioral remedy package maps directly to the violation catalogue. Prohibition of First Look terminates AdX's preferential access to DFP inventory before other exchanges can bid. Prohibition of Last Look eliminates the matching right that allowed AdX to observe competing bids before committing. Restoration of publisher floor-price flexibility reverses Unified Pricing Rules' suppression of inter-exchange price discrimination. Buy-side non-discrimination requirements address Project Poirot's algorithmic bid suppression targeting competing exchanges. Each violation has a behavioral analog. Structural divestiture of AdX and DFP, by contrast, also severs the genuine integration efficiencies Section 4 identified — latency optimization, signal integration, yield management — that the liability record did not find anticompetitive. On scope-targeting grounds, the behavioral remedy package is better calibrated. As Herbert Hovenkamp frames the standard in *Federal Antitrust Policy* § 15.3 (6th ed. 2020): divestiture "should be employed only when a behavioral remedy cannot adequately restore competition, not as a first resort for large defendants."

Beyond the core prohibitions, a complete behavioral package would add interoperability mandates — requiring DFP to offer server-to-server integration with Prebid and competing exchanges on non-discriminatory terms and at parity latency — and real-time bid-data transparency obligations requiring Google to disclose auction allocation logic to an independent technical monitor. [Google's own September 2025 behavioral counter-proposal](https://blog.google/company-news/outreach-and-initiatives/public-policy/doj-ad-tech-case-sept-2025/) accepted the core prohibition and interoperability framework while contesting the DOJ's demand for auction code divestiture; the DOJ's principal objection was that behavioral obligations are unenforceable without a structural backstop. The enforceability objection is real — Section 6's EU precedent review identified monitoring specificity as the key variable distinguishing effective behavioral remedies from gameable ones — but it argues for rigorous monitoring infrastructure rather than structural separation as a default.

**The DV360 Incentive-Architecture Counterargument**

The DOJ's strongest objection to the behavioral remedy framework is not that the four specific auction-design prohibitions are technically unenforceable — it is that the underlying incentive architecture survives behavioral remedies intact. As long as Google operates DV360 alongside AdX, Google retains a financial stake in routing buy-side spend to exchanges where DV360 performs well, creating an ongoing competitive incentive to develop new technical mechanisms that advantage AdX without triggering the explicit prohibitions. On this account, structural divestiture eliminates the incentive problem at its source, not merely its current manifestations.

The counterargument has real force, but three considerations limit its weight. First, the behavioral remedy package extends beyond the four proven mechanisms to include prospective buy-side non-discrimination requirements covering any algorithmic routing or bidding strategy that systematically disadvantages competing exchanges — addressing the incentive-architecture concern through obligation rather than ownership restructuring. Second, real-time bid-data transparency requirements make future self-preferencing patterns detectable: if DV360 systematically wins at prices below what independent DSPs bid on comparable inventory routed through AdX, the pattern becomes visible to an independent technical monitor and auditable against the non-discrimination obligation. DV360's incentive to self-preference survives the behavioral remedy; its ability to do so invisibly does not. Third, the DOJ's incentive-architecture argument, extended to its logical limit, would require dissolving all vertical integration in two-sided platform markets where a firm participates on both sides of its own exchange — a standard far broader than the consumer welfare framework endorses and one that the liability record itself does not support: Judge Brinkema declined to find that DV360's participation alongside AdX was independently anticompetitive.

**Criterion Two: Proportionality**

The DOJ's structural remedy would constitute the largest mandatory corporate breakup an American court has ordered since the 1984 AT&T divestiture — applied to a software-defined exchange operating at millisecond latency in a market that did not exist in its current form fifteen years ago. Proportionality analysis asks whether the remedy's magnitude is commensurate with the harm proven. The proven harm is auction-design manipulation across a specific layer of the programmatic advertising stack, over a bounded period. [ITIF's May 2025 analysis](https://itif.org/publications/2025/05/12/the-dojs-problematic-remedies-proposal-google-ad-tech-case/) identified the mismatch between the scope of the violations and the DOJ's proposed remedy as a fundamental structural weakness in the government's case. AT&T's divestiture was predicated on half a century of regulated local loop monopoly across all American telephony. Structural separation of AdX responds to four auction-design practices over roughly a decade in one segment of digital advertising. The disproportionality has real welfare consequences: divestiture imposes organizational transition costs, infrastructure separation costs, and network-effect disruption costs that are borne by the publishers and advertisers the remedy is meant to benefit.

**Criterion Three: Error-Cost Asymmetry**

The most important criterion for remedy design under genuine uncertainty about market response is asymmetry of error costs. The antitrust error-cost literature — developed most rigorously in [Frank Easterbrook's foundational 1984 framework](https://chicagounbound.uchicago.edu/journal_articles/1153/), *"The Limits of Antitrust,"* 63 Tex. L. Rev. 1 (1984) — identifies two types of remedy errors: under-remedying (behavioral constraints prove too weak to prevent recurrence of the violation), and over-remedying (structural separation produces unnecessary disruption to efficient integration). These two error types are not symmetric in cost or reversibility.

Behavioral remedies are correctable. If First Look and Last Look prohibitions prove insufficient because Google finds new technical mechanisms to preference AdX inventory, the court retains jurisdiction under a consent decree to tighten obligations, impose transparency requirements, or escalate to structural conditions as a backstop. The error is recoverable because the remedy is adjustable. Structural divestiture is not correctable in the same way: once AdX is separated from DFP under a judicial order and transferred to a new owner, the organizational disruption, network-effect losses, and publisher transition costs cannot be judicially reversed if the divested exchange proves non-viable or the market does not respond as the DOJ's model predicts. The structural remedy, if wrong, produces irreversible harm; the behavioral remedy, if wrong, produces correctable underenforcement.

**The Monitoring Specificity Imperative**

Acknowledging that behavioral remedies are correctable does not resolve the DOJ's enforceability objection unless the monitoring architecture is sufficiently specified to detect and deter evasion. The Microsoft monitoring trustee precedent established the operative model: a technically qualified independent monitor with ongoing access to the defendant's system code, auction routing logs, and bid-allocation records, empowered to flag compliance concerns for court escalation and to request technical documentation on defined notice. Applied to the AdX context, the monitoring framework would require, at minimum: (1) code-escrow or open-source disclosure of AdX's auction allocation logic to the independent monitor, enabling direct verification that the specific rules prohibited by the consent decree have been removed and not replaced by functionally equivalent substitutes; (2) real-time bid-data sharing with publishers covering all competing exchange bids and AdX's own clearing prices in each auction, allowing publishers to independently verify that their inventory is being routed on non-discriminatory terms; and (3) quarterly technical audit reports filed with the court documenting compliance with the four auction-design prohibitions, with a standing DOJ right to petition for enhanced monitoring conditions upon demonstrating a pattern of potential evasion. The Google Shopping failure, as Section 6 established, resulted from compliance standards too underspecified to prevent algorithmic evasion — not from behavioral intervention's inherent inadequacy. Compliance standards at this level of technical specificity are substantially harder to game without producing detectable violations.

In markets as technically complex and rapidly evolving as programmatic advertising — where the specific competitive dynamics the liability record addressed have already partially shifted through header bidding adoption and new exchange entry — the error-cost asymmetry strongly favors the reversible instrument. This asymmetry should be the organizing principle when courts face the choice between calibrated behavioral remedies and structural separation in platform markets where the underlying technology is not static.

The welfare comparison yields a clear hierarchy: on all three criteria — scope-targeting, proportionality, and error-cost asymmetry — the targeted behavioral remedy package is welfare-superior to structural divestiture as a primary remedy instrument. This does not foreclose structural conditions as a compliance backstop if behavioral remedies are demonstrably evaded; it establishes that the burden of proof for proceeding directly to structural separation has not been met by the liability record as adjudicated.

## 9. Conclusion

The remedies question in *United States v. Google* (Ad Tech) presents a specific choice between two instruments — structural divestiture and calibrated behavioral remedies — and the economic literature is not neutral between them in this application. Three independent analytical tracks converge on a single policy conclusion.

The two-sided market framework established in Section 3 identifies the harm as a broken neutral-intermediary condition: Google's simultaneous operation of an ad exchange (AdX) and a buy-side bidder (DV360) created the incentive architecture for the auction-design manipulations the court found proven. That framing is critical because it specifies what a remedy must accomplish — restore the conditions under which AdX functions as a genuine price-discovery mechanism — without dictating the ownership structure through which that condition is restored. Two candidate mechanisms satisfy the Rochet-Tirole criterion: severing Google's buy-side stake through divestiture, or prohibiting the specific auction rules that broke the neutral-intermediary condition. The two-sided market framework alone cannot choose between them; it establishes the target, not the instrument.

The structural remedy analysis in Section 5 applies the three conditions that predicted AT&T's 1984 success to the AdX divestiture scenario and finds two of the three conditions absent or seriously contested. AT&T's local loop was physically separable from its long-distance operations without performance cost; AdX's auction logic is software-defined and entangled with DFP's ad server pipeline in ways that impose measurable latency cost on API-mediated separation. The Baby Bells were constituted from existing regional operating companies with guaranteed franchise revenues from day one; a divested AdX faces a viable-acquirer problem that Judge Brinkema flagged herself and that the DOJ's closing arguments did not resolve. As MacAvoy and Robinson demonstrated in [*Winning by Losing*](https://openyls.law.yale.edu/handle/20.500.13051/7868), structural remedies succeed when the divested entity inherits the market structure it needs to compete — not when it is asked to build competitive scale from a weakened position against established incumbents in a market already undergoing competitive correction.

The welfare comparison in Section 8 synthesizes these findings against three doctrinal criteria. On scope-targeting: the proven violations are four auction-design mechanisms, each with a direct behavioral analog; structural divestiture also severs integration efficiencies the liability record did not find anticompetitive, running afoul of [*United States v. Grinnell Corp.*](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/384/563/)'s requirement that the remedy be "related to the violations proven." On proportionality: the DOJ's structural proposal is the largest mandatory corporate separation since 1984, applied to four auction-design practices in one layer of digital advertising — a disproportion that imposes transition costs on the publishers the remedy is designed to benefit. On error-cost asymmetry: behavioral remedies are tightenable and reversible; structural divestiture is neither. In a market in which competitive entry is already occurring and the specific manipulations proven can each be specifically prohibited, the asymmetry between recoverable underenforcement and irreversible disruption is decisive — provided monitoring architecture is specified with the technical precision that the Microsoft monitoring trustee model, and the negative example of Google Shopping's vague compliance standards, together prescribe.

The broader implication extends beyond the parties. *United States v. Google* (Ad Tech) will be the most significant federal remedies decision in tech antitrust since the D.C. Circuit's *Microsoft* remand in 2001. The template Judge Brinkema establishes will govern how future courts approach behavioral violations in technically interdependent platform markets. The organizing principle this paper proposes — that error-cost asymmetry between reversible behavioral intervention and irreversible structural separation should govern instrument choice when harm is specifically addressable and competitive entry is already underway — is not a principle derived from sympathy for large defendants. It is derived from the consumer welfare standard's core demand: that antitrust remedies maximize competitive output, not redistribute corporate ownership.

This paper has not challenged whether Google's conduct warranted the liability finding — Judge Brinkema answered that question, and this analysis takes the finding as established. What it has argued is that a remedy designed to cure the specific disease the court diagnosed — four auction-design manipulations at the exchange layer — is both doctrinally required and economically superior to a remedy designed to punish the patient for its scale.
