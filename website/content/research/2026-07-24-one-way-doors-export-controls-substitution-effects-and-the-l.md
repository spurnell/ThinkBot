## Abstract

On January 15, 2026, the Bureau of Industry and Security replaced a presumption of denial with case-by-case review for advanced AI chip exports to China, reopening a market NVIDIA had once controlled almost completely. Six months later, NVIDIA's China market share has not recovered in step with the policy that reopened it: trackers disagree sharply on magnitude — from roughly 55 percent of broad AI-accelerator shipments to single digits of licensed direct sales — but agree on direction, with Huawei and Cambricon converting three years of restricted access into durable domestic share. This paper treats that gap as a test of an assumption embedded in "surgical" export-control design: that tightening and loosening a control are symmetric, reversible operations, the way raising and lowering a tariff are. Testing three candidate explanations — Chinese industrial-policy mandates, CUDA-ecosystem switching costs, and buyer caution born of policy volatility — against the market data, the paper develops a distinction between price-mediated instruments (tariffs), whose effects reverse when the instrument is removed, and adoption-mediated instruments (sustained export controls), which move buyers across discrete, costly-to-reverse thresholds — supplier, software stack, qualified-vendor list — that persist after the restriction lifts. A natural-experiment comparison of the 2018 ZTE denial order (thirteen weeks, full reversal) against the 2019 Huawei Entity List action (seven-plus years, no reversal) shows this durability threshold predicts, rather than merely accommodates, which past controls proved reversible. The policy implication is deliberately narrow: irreversibility is a cost that belongs in the calculation made before a control is imposed, not a lesson read backward from a failed attempt to loosen one.

## 1. Introduction: A Control Loosened, a Market Not Regained

On January 15, 2026, the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) did something export-control hawks had spent three years arguing against: it made China easier to sell chips to. The [revised license review policy](https://www.bis.gov/press-release/department-commerce-revises-license-review-policy-semiconductors-exported-china) replaced a presumption of denial for advanced-computing exports to China and Macau with case-by-case review, covering NVIDIA's H200 and equivalents, so long as exporters could certify sufficient US supply, no diversion of domestic foundry capacity, adequate end-user security, and independent third-party performance testing (see the [Federal Register notice](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2026/01/15/2026-00789/revision-to-license-review-policy-for-advanced-computing-commodities) for the full certification regime). By the administration's own logic, this was a calibration: the 2022–2025 control regime had done its job of slowing China's frontier-AI buildout, and a more permissive licensing posture could now let US firms compete for commercial revenue without compromising the national-security rationale for the original restrictions.

Six months later, the market has not responded the way that logic predicted. NVIDIA's own executives have described the company's China AI-accelerator share as having collapsed from a position of near-total dominance — on the order of 90 percent as recently as 2022–2023 — to roughly half that level or lower by mid-2026, even after the licensing door reopened. Third-party trackers disagree on the precise magnitude (estimates for NVIDIA's 2026 China share range from the high single digits to the mid-50s depending on methodology and product scope — a discrepancy this paper treats as data, not noise, in Section 3), but every tracker agrees on direction: Huawei's Ascend line and Cambricon have converted the license-denial period into durable domestic share, and the January loosening has not reversed that. [Reporting on Huawei and Cambricon's continued gains through mid-2026](https://www.scmp.com/tech/article/3358322/huawei-and-cambricon-lead-chinas-ai-server-chip-surge-piling-pressure-nvidia) describes a Chinese AI-chip market that has, in important respects, already moved on.

This is the puzzle this paper takes up. If export controls were a simple valve — tighten to restrict, loosen to restore — NVIDIA's China share should have started recovering within a quarter or two of the January policy shift, the kind of snap-back one would expect from a straightforward price-mediated instrument (Section 5 develops this comparison formally). It has not, or at best has recovered only partially and slowly. That gap between what the "surgical control" model predicts and what the data show is not just an NVIDIA earnings-call footnote. It is a test of an assumption embedded in a policy design philosophy this publication has itself advocated: that export controls, applied narrowly, can be tuned up and down as circumstances warrant, with tightening and loosening as symmetric, reversible operations.

The parallel [Section 232 tariff on H200 and MI325X chips](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/01/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-takes-action-on-certain-advanced-computing-chips-to-protect-americas-economic-and-national-security/) is a useful foil precisely because it behaves as a canonical reversible instrument — a price wedge that disappears, along with most of its market-share effects, when the tariff is lifted, exactly as ITIF's own modeling of the tariff assumes (developed fully in Section 5). This paper asks whether export controls belong in the same category, or whether they operate through a different mechanism — one that reshapes buyer behavior, supplier ecosystems, and technical dependencies in ways that persist after the formal restriction ends. We call this the distinction between **price-mediated** and **adoption-mediated** policy instruments.

The stakes are not academic. BIS and its counterparts are actively drafting the next generation of controls — on advanced packaging, electronic design automation (EDA) tools, and next-generation accelerators — using much the same "narrow, calibrated, reversible" design assumptions that shaped the 2022–2026 chip-control regime. If those assumptions are wrong — if adoption-mediated controls create switching costs and domestic-supplier lock-in that outlast the control itself — then the appropriate response is not to abandon surgical design in favor of open-ended, permanent restriction. It is to raise the ex-ante bar: to treat irreversibility as a cost that must be weighed before a control is imposed, not discovered after an attempt to loosen it fails. Section 7 develops that implication in full; the sections between here and there build the empirical and theoretical case for it.

## 2. Background: The 2022–2026 Control-and-Loosen Cycle

The January 2026 licensing shift was not a single, isolated decision. It was one of nine documented policy actions in a four-year sequence that has tightened and loosened US chip-export policy toward China at a pace close to two actions a year — a cadence closer to a policy oscillation than to the "one control, calibrated once, maintained steadily" model that the surgical-controls framework implicitly assumes. Understanding why the reversals kept happening, and why each loosening arrived only after the prior tightening had already reshaped the market, is necessary before Section 3 can interpret the share data as anything more than noise.

Six tightening actions and three loosening actions in under four years is the empirical backdrop against which NVIDIA's China market share must be read. A single, cleanly reversible policy experiment this is not — and that complicates any simple before/after comparison of the kind Section 3 now undertakes.

NVIDIA's response to the October 2022 threshold was immediate and instructive for everything that follows: it engineered the A800 and H800, chips deliberately calibrated to fall just under the control's performance line while preserving most of the commercial value of the A100 and H100 for Chinese customers. BIS closed that loophole in the October 2023 rules above, which tightened the technical parameters and extended the license requirement to additional third countries to prevent circumvention. Each subsequent round followed the same pattern the October 2022 rule established: NVIDIA (and to a lesser extent AMD) engineered a compliant successor product; BIS closed the gap; NVIDIA engineered the next one. This is the "whack-a-mole" dynamic Section 4 revisits when weighing the CUDA-lock-in explanation for why the January 2026 loosening has not restored NVIDIA's position — the compliance chips China's buyers grew accustomed to purchasing were never full-performance parts, and the market's adaptation to that constrained product tier is itself part of the story.

The Biden administration's final act, the January 15, 2025 AI Diffusion Rule, attempted to convert this bilateral, chip-by-chip approach into a global, tiered licensing framework covering dozens of countries by risk category. It never took effect: the incoming Trump administration rescinded it on May 13, 2025, arguing — in terms this publication largely endorsed at the time — that treating NATO allies as second-tier risks alongside adversary states was both diplomatically counterproductive and unnecessarily bureaucratic. What replaced it, however, was not a return to a stable baseline but a second oscillation specific to China: the April 2025 decision to bar even the H20, a chip NVIDIA had designed specifically to satisfy the October 2023 thresholds, followed within three months by a July 15, 2025 reversal restoring H20 license approvals after direct lobbying by NVIDIA's CEO. Higher-end chips — the H200 and AMD's MI325X — remained under a presumption-of-denial standard through the rest of 2025 before President Trump's December 8, 2025 announcement and the January 15, 2026 rule that opened this paper moved them to case-by-case review, while next-generation Blackwell-class parts remained restricted.

## 3. The Data: NVIDIA's China Market Share Trajectory

Start with the baseline everyone agrees on. Before the October 2022 rule, NVIDIA supplied somewhere in the neighborhood of 90–95 percent of the AI accelerators sold into China — a figure CEO Jensen Huang himself has repeated in earnings-adjacent commentary, including a much-quoted remark at Citadel Securities' Future of Global Markets forum on October 6, 2025 that NVIDIA's China share had gone "from 95 percent market share to 0 percent" ([Fortune's report on the remark](https://fortune.com/2025/10/19/jensen-huang-nvidia-china-market-share-ai-chips-trump-trade-war/)). That endpoint — zero — was not a metaphor so much as a description of a specific event: on April 9, 2025, BIS informed NVIDIA that the H20, the compliance-tier chip NVIDIA had engineered to satisfy the October 2023 thresholds, now itself required a license, and the company subsequently confirmed there were no H20 sales to China-based customers at all in its fiscal Q2 2026 (the quarter ended July 27, 2025), after taking a $4.5 billion charge tied to excess H20 inventory in the prior quarter (per [NVIDIA's Q2 FY26 results release](https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/nvidia-announces-financial-results-for-second-quarter-fiscal-2026)). NVIDIA's own quarterly disclosures bracket the scale of the trough without requiring a disputed third-party revenue estimate: the company recognized zero China data-center compute revenue in that quarter, having built China into a multi-billion-dollar quarterly business as recently as fiscal 2025 — a signal from the firm most exposed to the actual numbers that the "denied to under review" period did lasting commercial damage, whatever the precise year-over-year percentage attaches to it.

That is the trough the January 15, 2026 loosening was meant to pull the market out of. Six-plus months on, the recovery has been muted at best, and the trackers disagree sharply enough about magnitude that the disagreement itself is informative. IDC data reviewed by Reuters puts NVIDIA in the lead for full-year 2025 shipments — about 2.2 million AI-accelerator cards, or roughly 55 percent of a China AI-accelerator market IDC estimated at roughly 4 million total units, with Chinese vendors collectively shipping 1.65 million cards (41 percent) and Huawei alone accounting for about 812,000 of those ([Reuters' IDC-sourced reporting, via Yahoo Finance](https://finance.yahoo.com/sectors/technology/articles/chinese-chipmakers-claim-nearly-half-091441757.html)). That figure matters for what it is not: it describes calendar-year 2025 shipments, reported in April 2026, not a post-loosening rebound. It is a pre-January-2026 baseline, and reading it as evidence the January rule change "worked" would misdate the data by several months. Tom's Hardware, citing separate industry figures for the same broad shipment category, reports a consistent picture — NVIDIA's China share "falls to less than 60 percent" as domestic suppliers collectively shipped 1.65 million AI GPUs ([Tom's Hardware coverage](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/nvidia-market-share-in-china-falls-to-less-than-60-percent-chinese-chip-makers-deliver-1-65-million-ai-gpus-as-the-government-pushes-data-centers-to-use-domestic-chips)). But other trackers describe a far bleaker position for NVIDIA: FourWeekMBA's analysis puts Huawei at roughly 50 percent of the China AI-chip market against an NVIDIA share down to single digits ([FourWeekMBA](https://fourweekmba.com/ai-huawei-nvidia-china-ai-chip-market-share-export-controls/)), and TrendForce's own forward-looking estimate — cited in the same reporting on Huawei and Cambricon's gains — has combined NVIDIA-plus-AMD-plus-other-foreign-supplier share falling to just 21 percent of the China AI-chip market in 2026, down from 34 percent in 2025, with domestic Chinese suppliers rising to 56 percent and internet-company ASICs accounting for the remaining 23 percent ([SCMP's report on the TrendForce forecast](https://www.scmp.com/tech/article/3358322/huawei-and-cambricon-lead-chinas-ai-server-chip-surge-piling-pressure-nvidia)).

### Data and Methods: Why the Trackers Disagree

This is not a case of trackers simply disagreeing due to sloppy methodology; the spread tracks real differences in scope. The higher NVIDIA figures (55–60 percent) appear to measure shipments of GPUs and AI accelerators broadly, a category where NVIDIA's gray-market and Southeast-Asia-routed volumes still register, and where the 2025 baseline discussed above still weighs heavily. The lower figures (single digits to 21 percent) more often measure the narrower category of *licensed, direct sales into China's data-center market* — the category the January 2026 rule was actually meant to unlock — and it is in that narrower category where the loosening has visibly failed to move the needle. As of NVIDIA's own guidance issued in May 2026 for its fiscal Q2 2027, the company stated it was "not assuming any data center compute revenue from China" at all, even though BIS had by then cleared H200 sales to more than ten Chinese companies ([reporting on the guidance](https://www.mlex.com/mlex/articles/2480408/nvidia-excludes-china-data-center-revenue-from-outlook-amid-h200-delay)). The bottleneck, in other words, had shifted: Washington reopened the door, but Beijing had not yet approved H200 imports on its own side, an asymmetry Section 4 treats as evidence for the Chinese-industrial-policy explanation of the stalled recovery. Readers should treat "NVIDIA's China share" as shorthand for at least two distinct, only loosely correlated metrics — total shipment volume and licensed direct sales — rather than a single number; this paper's argument rests on the latter, narrower metric, which every tracker agrees has not recovered.

The companion series — what Huawei and Cambricon did with the room the 2022–2025 restrictions gave them — explains why "reopen the door" was never going to be sufficient on its own. Huawei shipped roughly 812,000 Ascend-series units in 2025, about 20 percent of the total China AI-chip market and enough to rank second only to NVIDIA among all suppliers; Cambricon shipped roughly 116,000 units, about 2.9 percent, tied for third ([Silicon UK's summary of the shipment data](https://www.silicon.co.uk/ai-2/huawei-cambricon-ai-630499/amp)). Huawei has stated it plans to roughly double 910C production to about 600,000 units in 2026 and expects AI-chip revenue to grow at least 60 percent, to approximately $12 billion, up from $7.5 billion in 2025 ([Huawei Central's coverage of the production plan](https://www.huaweicentral.com/huawei-set-to-dominate-chinas-ai-server-market-phasing-out-nvidia/)). None of that capacity evaporates because BIS changed a certification requirement in Washington. Whatever the precise NVIDIA share number one credits — 55 percent, 21 percent, or something in between — every series here points the same direction: a market that spent three years building durable, state-subsidized domestic supply does not snap back to a foreign supplier's prior dominance within two fiscal quarters of that supplier regaining a license. Section 4 now turns to why.

## 4. Testing the Explanations

Three candidate explanations compete for why NVIDIA's China share has stayed depressed after Washington reopened licensing. They are not mutually exclusive, and distinguishing which does the most work matters for Section 5's framework: a stalled recovery driven mainly by a legal instrument Beijing could rescind tomorrow is a different policy problem than one driven by switching costs no license can undo.

**Explanation 1: Chinese industrial policy has closed the door from its own side.** This is the most direct and best-evidenced explanation, and it is the one Section 3 already gestured at with NVIDIA's own May 2026 guidance. Beijing did not merely decline to rush back to NVIDIA; it built a legal barrier of its own. Reporting in November 2025 described state-funded data-center projects being ordered to strip out already-installed NVIDIA, AMD, and Intel hardware if construction was less than 30 percent complete, following an August 2025 rule requiring at least 50 percent domestic-chip sourcing in new builds ([Tom's Hardware's coverage of the domestic-sourcing mandate](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/semiconductors/china-bans-foreign-ai-chips-from-state-funded-data-centers)). By June 2026 that policy had hardened into a reported $295 billion national AI-computing-grid plan built around roughly 80 percent domestic-chip content, explicitly structured to exclude NVIDIA and AMD from state-backed infrastructure ([Tech Times' reporting on the domestic-chip mandate](https://www.techtimes.com/articles/318868/20260622/china-ai-data-center-grid-locks-out-nvidia-295-billion-domestic-chip-mandate.htm)). This explanation predicts exactly the asymmetry Section 3 documented: Washington can approve an export license, but if the buyer is legally barred from using the product, the license clears nothing. It is also, notably, the explanation most consistent with the "reversible instrument" model this paper is testing against — a directive is a policy choice Beijing could unwind as unilaterally as it imposed it, which is precisely why it cannot, on its own, explain why analysts expect the domestic share gains to persist even if the mandate eventually loosens.

**Explanation 2: CUDA-ecosystem switching costs make the shift durable independent of any mandate.** NVIDIA's moat has never been silicon alone; it is the sixteen years of accumulated software — CUDA, cuDNN, NCCL, and the libraries built on top of them — that make NVIDIA hardware the default target for machine-learning code. Migrating a codebase optimized for CUDA to Huawei's CANN toolchain or Cambricon's equivalent is not a driver update; industry estimates put the engineering cost at weeks to months for simple inference workloads but six to twelve months of dedicated effort for distributed training pipelines with custom CUDA kernels ([SoftwareSeni's breakdown of CUDA switching costs](https://www.softwareseni.com/cuda-lock-in-unpacked-the-software-moat-the-real-switching-costs-and-how-they-are-changing/)), a picture corroborated by more institutional analysis: a public survey of how leading Chinese AI labs have actually responded to hardware-centric export controls found that most continued to rely heavily on NVIDIA's software stack specifically because full localization's tooling costs remained prohibitive relative to workarounds that preserved CUDA compatibility ([arXiv's "Whack-a-Chip" survey of export-control evasion and adaptation](https://arxiv.org/html/2411.14425v1)). Once a Chinese AI lab has paid that cost — and the 2022–2025 control period forced many to pay it whether they wanted to or not — reopening the NVIDIA spigot does not refund the sunk investment or erase the institutional knowledge now built around Ascend or Cambricon toolchains. Bruegel's analysis of the US-China AI rivalry describes this as the contest moving "beyond chips alone" to a battle over which full software-and-hardware stack captures developer mindshare, noting that China's push toward PyTorch-compatible backends like `torch_npu` is specifically designed to lower the switching cost for the next cohort of developers even as it locks in the current one ([Bruegel's "Stack Battles" analysis](https://www.bruegel.org/analysis/stack-battles-us-china-artificial-intelligence-rivalry-moving-beyond-chips-alone)). This is a genuinely adoption-mediated mechanism: it does not require any legal instrument to persist, only inertia.

**Explanation 3: Rational buyer caution about supply reliability, independent of both mandate and switching cost.** A subtler possibility is that Chinese buyers are not primarily responding to a domestic-sourcing mandate or to sunk software costs, but to the four-tightening/three-loosening cadence documented in Section 2 itself. A purchasing manager who watched the H20 go from compliant, to banned, to un-banned, to superseded by a new certification regime within eighteen months has been given a strong rational reason to treat NVIDIA as an unreliable long-run supplier regardless of this month's license status — a dynamic consistent with what PIIE's research on the broader control regime describes as Chinese firms' "design-out" and "design-around" responses, undertaken not necessarily because a domestic part is superior but because it removes exposure to a policy variable outside any Chinese firm's control ([PIIE working paper on the semiconductor trade war](https://www.piie.com/publications/working-papers/how-united-states-marched-semiconductor-industry-its-trade-war-china)). A companion empirical study of Chinese firms' R&D responses to the controls found the restrictions measurably shifted research investment toward basic, foundational work — the kind that pays off regardless of which foreign supplier is licensed in a given quarter — a pattern more consistent with firms hedging against policy volatility than with firms making a one-time technical switch ([the ScienceDirect study on export restrictions and Chinese firms' research orientation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/org/science/article/abs/pii/S0144357725000520)).

Weighed against the data, these explanations are not competitors so much as a sequence. The industrial-policy mandate is the proximate, best-documented cause of the near-term stall — it is the only one of the three that maps directly onto NVIDIA's own admission that it is modeling zero China data-center revenue despite cleared licenses. But CSIS's Entity List efficacy work cautions against reading the mandate as the whole story: enforcement and market-structure effects of export-control-era policy consistently outlast the specific legal instrument that triggered them, because "the compliance ecosystem, once built, does not require the original rule to remain the state of the art" ([CSIS's analysis of the limits of chip export controls](https://www.csis.org/analysis/limits-chip-export-controls-meeting-china-challenge)). That is the CUDA-switching-cost and buyer-caution explanations doing their work underneath the mandate: even in a counterfactual where Beijing lifted its domestic-sourcing rule tomorrow, a meaningful share of NVIDIA's lost China business would not return immediately, because the software has been rewritten, the procurement contracts re-signed, and the risk calculus permanently updated. Section 5 formalizes why that distinction — durable adoption effects versus a reversible legal directive — is the central theoretical fault line between export controls and instruments like tariffs.

## 5. A Framework: Price-Mediated vs. Adoption-Mediated Instruments

The Section 232 tariff on H200 and MI325X units, discussed in Section 1, is best understood as a price wedge: it raises the cost of a transaction that both parties otherwise still want to make, and the buyer responds by purchasing a bit less at the margin. That is exactly the mechanism ITIF's modeling assumed when it projected a 0.18-percentage-point GDP-growth drag in year one, widening to 0.76 points by year ten, as the tax works its way through investment decisions ([ITIF's *Short-Circuited* report](https://itif.org/publications/2025/05/21/short-circuited-how-semiconductor-tariffs-would-harm-the-us-economy/)). Nothing about that mechanism is discontinuous. Raise the tariff, quantity demanded falls smoothly along the existing demand curve; cut the tariff, quantity demanded rises back along the same curve. The instrument changes a price. It does not, on its own, change what the buyer is buying, who else the buyer has learned to buy it from, or what the buyer has invested in being able to use.

That is the sense in which a tariff is what this paper calls a **price-mediated instrument**: its effect runs entirely through the cost of a marginal transaction, and because nothing about the underlying product, the buyer's tooling, or the buyer's supplier relationships has changed, the effect reverses when the price wedge is removed — modulo ordinary frictions like restocking lags. This is also, not coincidentally, the assumption embedded in the standard trade-policy toolkit CBO and ITIF use to score tariff proposals: an elasticity applied to a price, symmetric in both directions.

Export controls of the kind BIS applied to China between 2022 and 2026 look superficially similar — a license requirement functions, for a firm that cannot obtain one, as a price of effectively infinite height, and case-by-case review functions as a lower, uncertain price. But Section 4 showed that the actual market response to the 2022–2025 restrictions was not a movement along NVIDIA's China demand curve. It was a series of Chinese buyers permanently relocating to a different curve entirely — a different chip architecture, a different software stack, a different set of qualified suppliers — and January 2026's loosening did not pull them back. The mechanism responsible is what this paper calls an **adoption-mediated instrument**: one whose binding constraint, once buyers respond to it, is no longer the policy itself but the fixed costs buyers paid to route around it.

The economics of why that response is durable rather than reversible is not novel to this paper — it is the economics of switching costs and network lock-in, applied to a new setting. Paul Klemperer's foundational work on markets with consumer switching costs shows that once customers have sunk the cost of adopting a supplier's product — in Klemperer's language, developed "brand loyalty" through transaction or learning costs rather than genuine preference — a firm's *current* customer base becomes a durable asset independent of ongoing price competition, because rivals cannot profitably bid those customers away without absorbing their switching costs too ([Klemperer's *Competition when Consumers have Switching Costs*](https://ora.ox.ac.uk/objects/uuid:fdf43d66-6b25-456e-a5d6-ca036b9c5165)). Applied here: every Chinese AI lab that spent 2023–2025 porting training pipelines to Huawei's CANN toolchain, qualifying Ascend-based servers, and building institutional expertise in a non-CUDA stack has, in Klemperer's terms, been "switched" — and reopening NVIDIA's licensing window in January 2026 is, from that lab's perspective, merely a rival firm cutting its price. It does not refund the switching cost already paid, and it competes against a domestic supplier who no longer needs to win on price alone, only to be good enough given the sunk investment already made in its ecosystem.

A second, complementary literature describes why the shock itself — not just the buyer's response — outlasts its own removal. Richard Baldwin's work on "hysteresis" in international trade found that temporary exchange-rate shocks large enough to justify entry into a new export market create effects that persist even after the exchange rate returns to its original level, because the sunk costs of establishing distribution, certification, and buyer relationships are not un-sunk by a reversal in the triggering price ([Baldwin's NBER working paper, "Hysteresis in Import Prices: The Beachhead Effect"](https://www.nber.org/papers/w2545)). Baldwin's "beachhead" — a foreign competitor who used a temporarily favorable exchange rate to establish a permanent foothold in a market it would never have entered under normal conditions — is a near-exact structural analogy for what the 2022–2025 export-control regime did for Huawei and Cambricon: a temporary policy shock (in this case, the *absence* of a competitor rather than a price advantage) large enough and durable enough to finance the fixed costs of establishing a beachhead that survives the shock's reversal.

The synthesis is a testable distinction, not just a metaphor. Price-mediated instruments move a continuous variable — quantity purchased at a given price — and are reversible because reversing the instrument restores the original price and, with it, the original quantity response. Adoption-mediated instruments move a discrete variable — which supplier, which technology stack, which qualified-vendor list a buyer has fixed costs sunk into — and are reversible only if the restriction is lifted before buyers cross the threshold at which paying that fixed cost becomes rational. That threshold is a function of the restriction's expected duration and severity, not its formal legal status: a two-month license delay rarely justifies a twelve-month CUDA migration, but a three-year presumption of denial, of the kind BIS actually imposed, comfortably does. Section 6 tests whether this threshold logic — not merely "export controls are generally sticky" — actually predicts which past control episodes proved reversible and which did not.

## 6. Historical Comparison: Does This Pattern Recur?

If the durability threshold from Section 5 is doing real explanatory work — rather than being fitted after the fact to the one case this paper opened with — it should predict, not just accommodate, the outcomes of earlier control episodes. Two cases from the same decade offer a near-controlled comparison, because they differ sharply on the one variable the framework says should matter (how long the restriction actually bound before it was lifted) while sharing almost everything else: both targeted major Chinese telecommunications-equipment makers, both were framed as national-security actions, and both provoked predictions at the time that the targeted firm would be crippled.

**ZTE, 2018: short duration, full reversal.** On April 15, 2018, BIS activated a denial order against ZTE after the company was found to have made false statements about disciplining employees involved in illegal shipments to Iran and North Korea — a lapse-of-a-prior-settlement action, not a fresh national-security designation, but one that produced an immediate and total effect: US firms, including Qualcomm, which supplied the applications processors in nearly all of ZTE's smartphones, were barred from selling ZTE anything at all ([Covington's summary of the April 2018 order](https://www.cov.com/en/news-and-insights/insights/2018/04/department-of-commerce-activates-denial-order-against-chinese-telecommunications-company-zte)). ZTE's major operations reportedly ceased within days. Then, after a May 2018 White House intervention and a June 8, 2018 superseding settlement in which ZTE paid a $1 billion fine, placed $400 million in escrow, replaced its board and senior leadership, and installed a US-selected compliance team, BIS terminated the denial order on July 13, 2018 — thirteen weeks after it took effect ([BIS's termination order](https://www.bis.doc.gov/index.php/2011-09-13-13-22-03/17-regulations/1446-bis-issues-an-order-terminating-the-denial-order-issued-on-april-15-2018-against-zte); [Federal Register notice](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2018/07/23/2018-15633/order-terminating-denial-order-issued-on-april-15-2018-against-zhongxing-telecommunications)). ZTE resumed sourcing Qualcomm chips and Android licenses essentially where it had left off. There is no meaningful evidence that ZTE's global handset or network-equipment customers used the thirteen-week freeze to re-architect their supply chains around a non-American alternative — thirteen weeks is not enough time to qualify a new chipset in a phone design, port firmware, or requalify a telecom-equipment production line, so on the framework's own terms no fixed cost was ever paid that a restored license would need to "unpay." The episode looks, in retrospect, almost exactly like a price-mediated shock: severe, briefly, but reversible because it ended before switching costs came due.

**Huawei, 2019: long duration, no reversal.** The Commerce Department added Huawei and 68 affiliates to the Entity List on May 16, 2019 ([Covington's contemporaneous summary of the action](https://www.cov.com/en/news-and-insights/insights/2019/05/commerce-department-adds-huawei-and-68-huawei-affiliates-to-entity-list)), following the Meng Wanzhou arrest and broader concerns about Huawei's role in Chinese state surveillance and 5G infrastructure. The immediate effect targeted the same chokepoint as the ZTE order — Google's suspension of Huawei's Android license, cutting off Google Mobile Services for future Huawei devices ([CNBC's contemporaneous report](https://www.cnbc.com/2019/05/20/google-stops-some-business-with-huawei-could-hit-its-global-smartphone-ambitions.html)) — but unlike ZTE's, this restriction was never terminated. It is, as of this writing in mid-2026, still in force, seven years on. The market response tracks the framework's prediction precisely. Huawei was on pace to become the world's largest smartphone maker in 2019, having briefly overtaken Samsung in Q2 with 56.1 million units shipped that quarter; by Q4 2020, shipments had fallen 41 percent year-over-year to 33 million units, and the company had dropped to sixth place globally ([CNBC's Q4 2020 shipment report](https://www.cnbc.com/2021/01/28/huawei-q4-smartphone-shipments-plunge-41percent-as-us-sanctions-bite.html)); TrendForce projected Huawei's global smartphone share would bottom out near 4 percent in 2021 ([Bloomberg's report on the TrendForce forecast](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2020-11-24/huawei-s-phone-market-share-to-slump-to-4-in-2021-trendforce)). Crucially, Huawei did not sit still during those seven years the way a firm waiting out a temporary shock would: it sold its Honor sub-brand for roughly $15 billion in 2020, deliberately structuring the sale to move a large share of its consumer-device business outside the reach of US restrictions, and redirected engineering resources into HarmonyOS as an Android substitute and into Kirin chip development with SMIC as a domestic foundry partner. None of that is reversible in the way ZTE's Qualcomm relationship was reversible — HarmonyOS's device ecosystem, Honor's independent ownership, and Huawei's now-substantial domestic chip-design relationship with SMIC are fixed costs that would not disappear even if BIS removed Huawei from the Entity List tomorrow. ITIF's October 2025 retrospective on the episode, tellingly titled "Backfire," quantifies the durability: US technology firms lost more than $33 billion in sales to Huawei alone between 2021 and 2024, while Chinese state support — including China Mobile's 2021 decision to award Huawei 60 percent of a 5G base-station contract specifically to backstop it against the controls — helped Huawei's domestic ICT market share climb to a near-tie with Xiaomi's by 2025 ([ITIF's "Backfire" report](https://itif.org/publications/2025/10/27/backfire-export-controls-helped-huawei-and-hurt-us-firms/)).

The comparison is close to a natural experiment on the framework's central variable. Both actions hit the same kind of target (a Chinese telecom-equipment maker), through the same kind of chokepoint (denial of a critical American input — chips or software licenses), under the same broad national-security rationale. What differed was duration: thirteen weeks for ZTE, seven-plus years and still running for Huawei. The threshold logic from Section 5 predicts exactly what happened: ZTE's freeze ended before any customer or supplier had reason to pay a switching cost, so the relationship snapped back; Huawei's freeze ran long enough, and looked durable enough at the time, that Huawei itself, its component suppliers, and its downstream customers all made irreversible investments in an alternative — and once those investments were made, the restriction's own removal stopped being the variable that mattered. This is the same mechanism Section 4 documented for NVIDIA's China accelerator share, playing out one product generation earlier, with Huawei now cast as the domestic beneficiary of switching costs rather than merely their victim. The recurrence across two independent episodes, five years apart, is the strongest evidence in this paper that irreversibility is a structural feature of long-duration export controls, not an artifact particular to the 2022–2026 AI-chip case. Section 7 asks what that recurrence should change about how the next generation of controls gets designed.

## 7. Policy Implications: Designing Controls Under Irreversibility

The evidence assembled in Sections 3 through 6 supports a specific, narrower claim than it might first appear to license, and the distinction matters enormously for what follows. The claim this paper defends is: **export controls of sufficient duration and severity behave as adoption-mediated instruments whose market effects persist after the formal restriction is lifted, in a way tariffs generally do not.** The claim this paper explicitly does not make, and warns against inferring, is: **because loosening the NVIDIA China controls failed to restore NVIDIA's position, the original tightening was therefore validated, and the lesson is to control more, not less.** That inference is a non sequitur dressed up as an empirical finding, and it is worth being explicit about why, because it is the most tempting — and most damaging — misreading of this paper's data.

Irreversibility is not evidence of efficacy. A control that permanently reshapes a market has demonstrated that it is *sticky*, not that it was *well-designed* or that its underlying threat model was *correct*. Indeed, the Huawei comparison in Section 6 cuts against the efficacy inference directly: ITIF's "Backfire" retrospective found that seven years of Entity List restriction cost US firms upward of $33 billion in sales to Huawei alone while helping — not hindering — Huawei's transition into a self-sufficient domestic supplier now competing with Xiaomi for leadership of the Chinese ICT market. If the goal of the 2019 action was to degrade Huawei's global position, durability alone does not tell you whether it succeeded; it tells you that whatever effect the control had, that effect will not un-happen quickly. Durability is a property of the instrument's mechanism, not a verdict on the policy's wisdom. Conflating the two — treating "the market didn't snap back" as proof that "the restriction was justified" — is precisely the reasoning error this paper's title, "one-way doors," is meant to guard against: a decision that cannot be cheaply reversed deserves *more* scrutiny before it is made, not retroactive vindication after the fact.

The correct policy inference runs the other direction. Because adoption-mediated instruments are difficult to walk back once buyers cross the switching-cost threshold identified in Section 5, the ex-ante bar for imposing them should be higher than the bar for a price-mediated instrument like a tariff — not lower. A tariff that turns out to be miscalibrated can be adjusted with next quarter's trade data; the cost of getting it wrong is measured in basis points of GDP growth, as ITIF's own modeling of the chip tariff showed. An export control that turns out to be broader or longer than the threat it addresses cannot be adjusted with next quarter's data, because by the time the data arrives showing the control is unnecessarily costly, the buyers it affected may already have paid the fixed cost of routing around it — and that cost, once paid, does not reverse when the control does. This is an argument for *narrower, more deliberately scoped* controls, calibrated with the durability threshold from Section 5 explicitly in view, not an argument for abandoning surgical design in favor of blunter, broader instruments on the theory that reversibility was never achievable anyway.

Concretely, that means three design changes for the next generation of controls now being drafted — on advanced packaging, EDA tools, and next-generation accelerators, per the dockets CRS Report R48642 tracks.

**First, build sunset review into the control's initial design, not as an afterthought.** The ZTE/Huawei comparison in Section 6 suggests a rough operational proxy for the durability threshold: if a restriction is expected to bind for a period long enough that a buyer's fastest plausible switching investment (CUDA migration estimates run six to twelve months for training workloads) would pay for itself, the control should carry an automatic, calendar-triggered review — not a review contingent on political will to revisit it, which the six-tightening/three-loosening cadence documented in Section 2 shows is unreliable in either direction.

**Second, weight target selection toward inputs with thin substitution markets, not toward inputs China has already demonstrated it can domestically source at scale within the control's likely duration.** The Huawei Ascend and Cambricon trajectories in Section 3 show that a three-year, high-severity restriction on GPUs — a product category China had a domestic industrial base motivated and able to backfill — bought a temporary security benefit at the cost of a permanent market one. A control on a genuine bottleneck input with no near-term domestic substitute (extreme ultraviolet lithography remains the standard example) does not face the same tradeoff, because the adoption-mediated cost only materializes if the target has somewhere else to go.

**Third, treat BIS's own January 2026 case-by-case certification framework — requiring proof of adequate US supply, no domestic diversion, and end-user verification — as a template for making the *initial* control conditional and narrow, rather than as a belated corrective bolted onto an already-broad restriction after three years of collateral market loss.** Case-by-case review calibrated at the point of imposition preserves optionality; case-by-case review calibrated only after a blanket restriction has already triggered a beachhead effect, as Section 5's Baldwin citation describes, arrives too late to recover what the restriction cost.

None of this is an argument that export controls are never warranted, or that this paper's own tightening-friendly conclusions elsewhere in ThinkBot's portfolio need revisiting wholesale. Some threats — a chip that measurably accelerates an adversary's weapons-relevant compute — justify accepting an irreversible cost. The argument is narrower and, this paper contends, more useful: that irreversibility is a cost, it should be priced into the control decision at the moment the control is imposed, and BIS's own four-year, nine-action search for the right calibration on China chip policy is best read not as evidence that the search failed, but as evidence that the search should have started from a design that accounted for irreversibility from the outset.

## 8. Conclusion

This paper set out from a single, concrete anomaly: BIS reopened China's licensing door for advanced AI chips on January 15, 2026, and NVIDIA's China market share did not walk back through it. Six-plus months on, the trackers disagree on magnitude — estimates range from the mid-50s to the single digits depending on whether one measures broad shipments or licensed direct sales — but they agree on the shape of the story, and that shape is the finding this paper defends: NVIDIA's collapse from a roughly 90–95 percent China share in 2022 to a fraction of that today has not reversed in step with the policy that triggered it.

Section 4 showed that no single mechanism fully accounts for the stall. Chinese industrial policy — the domestic-sourcing mandates and the reported $295 billion state computing-grid plan — is the best-documented proximate cause, consistent with NVIDIA's own admission that it is modeling zero China data-center revenue despite cleared licenses. But CUDA-ecosystem switching costs and buyer caution born of four years of policy whiplash are doing real work underneath that mandate, and would keep a meaningful share of the market away from NVIDIA even if Beijing's own restrictions lifted tomorrow. Section 5 formalized why: export controls of sufficient duration are adoption-mediated instruments, moving buyers across a discrete threshold — a different supplier, a different software stack — rather than along a continuous demand curve the way a tariff's price wedge does. Once a buyer has paid the fixed cost of crossing that threshold, removing the instrument does not refund the cost. Section 6's comparison of ZTE (2018, thirteen weeks, full snapback) against Huawei (2019, seven-plus years and counting, no snapback) showed that this durability threshold predicts, rather than merely accommodates, which past control episodes proved reversible.

The policy implication is deliberately narrower than "export controls don't work" or its mirror image, "the controls worked, so tighten further." Neither follows from this paper's evidence. What follows is that irreversibility is a cost that belongs in the calculation made *before* a control is imposed, not a lesson learned only after an attempt to loosen it fails. BIS's four-year, nine-action search for the right calibration on China chip policy is best read as a search that started from the wrong assumption — that tightening and loosening are symmetric, reversible operations — rather than as a search that ultimately vindicates tightening. The same assumption is now embedded in the next generation of controls under discussion, on advanced packaging, EDA tools, and next-generation accelerators. Section 7's three design changes — calendar-triggered sunset review keyed to realistic switching-cost timelines, target selection weighted toward genuine bottleneck inputs rather than substitutable ones, and case-by-case certification built in from the start rather than retrofitted after years of blanket restriction — are offered as a way to keep the instrument surgical in practice, not just in name, once irreversibility is priced in from the outset.

The broader lesson for ThinkBot's innovation-policy portfolio is one of instrument literacy: not every restriction that looks narrow behaves like a tariff, and treating a one-way door as if it swings both ways is the design error this paper's data were assembled to make visible.
