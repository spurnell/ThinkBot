## Abstract

For three tax years, American businesses capitalized and amortized research and development spending that had been immediately deductible for the prior sixty-seven years — a policy accident born of the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act's revenue-offset arithmetic rather than any judgment about optimal innovation policy. This paper measures what that accident cost, and what its July 2025 reversal under new Internal Revenue Code Section 174A is likely to recover. Drawing on the empirical record of the 2022–2024 amortization period — an 11.9-percentage-point average increase in affected firms' cash effective tax rates, a $12.2 billion first-year decline in R&D investment among the most research-intensive US firms alone, a measurable slowdown in patent filings, and a widening gap between US and peer-economy R&D growth rates — the paper applies the economics literature's most credible elasticity estimates to project a $60–200 billion cumulative innovation dividend from restoration through 2032, a range that approaches the Joint Committee on Taxation's own $139 billion revenue-cost score. It then identifies two design flaws Congress has yet to address: a domestic/foreign bifurcation that inadvertently favors incremental offshore R&D expansion over domestic repatriation for multinationals with mature foreign labs, and a retroactive small-business relief provision that reaches only a fraction of the firms that bore the amortization era's aggregate cost. Before accepting its own projections, the paper engages the strongest case against them — the additionality problem and the fiscal-cost objection — and explains why each applies with less force to expensing than to the R&D tax credit literature that usually raises them. It closes with a four-metric validation framework spanning R&D-to-GDP share, patent filings, startup formation, and R&D employment that policymakers can use to confirm the dividend materializes on the timeline the evidence predicts.

## 1. Introduction: The Price of Amortization

For sixty-seven years, the American tax code treated research and development spending with a simple rule: spend a dollar on R&D today, deduct a dollar today. That consensus, codified in the original Section 174 of the Internal Revenue Code in 1954, was not accidental. Congress understood that private firms systematically underinvest in R&D because they cannot fully capture the social returns — a classic positive externality — and that immediate expensing reduced the after-tax cost of discovery as close to the true economic cost as the tax code could manage.

[TCJA Section 13206](https://www.journalofaccountancy.com/issues/2022/nov/amortizing-r-e-expenditures-under-tcja/) broke that consensus, not on the merits, but as a revenue offset. Beginning in tax year 2022, businesses were required to capitalize domestic R&D expenses and amortize them over five years — and foreign R&D over fifteen. The effect was immediate and measurable: the first-year deduction value of a dollar spent on domestic R&D fell by roughly 90 percent relative to pre-TCJA treatment. For multinational firms conducting research abroad, the discount was even steeper. The Joint Committee on Taxation estimated the amortization requirement would generate [roughly $119.7 billion in revenue between FY2022 and 2027](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/explainer/the-2025-tax-debate-cost-recovery-provisions-in-tcja/) — a number that implicitly assumed the behavioral response would be negligible. It was not.

On July 4, 2025, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) enacted [new Internal Revenue Code Section 174A](https://www.grantthornton.com/insights/alerts/tax/2025/insights/full-expensing-of-domestic-research), permanently restoring immediate expensing for domestic research and experimental expenditures incurred in tax years beginning after December 31, 2024. For small businesses with average annual gross receipts at or below the inflation-adjusted $31 million threshold, the law goes further — permitting retroactive expensing of 2022 through 2024 expenditures via amended returns. Foreign R&D, however, remains on fifteen-year amortization. The bifurcation is not incidental; it is a deliberate policy choice, and it carries consequences this paper examines in detail.

Congress scored Section 174A on its revenue cost — the JCT estimates [restoration costs approximately $139 billion over the FY2025–2034 window](https://tax.thomsonreuters.com/news/the-future-of-rd-expensing/). What Congress has not done is score the innovation return. No official estimate quantifies how much additional private R&D the restoration will induce, how many patents that translates into, how many startups it catalyzes, or how the domestic/foreign bifurcation reshapes where US multinationals locate their research labs. That gap — between revenue accounting and innovation accounting — is the analytical problem this paper addresses.

This paper makes four contributions. First, it establishes a **behavioral baseline**, documenting the measured investment response to amortization: [Cowx, Lester, and Nessa's](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4998845) administrative-data study finds that affected firms' cash effective tax rates rose 11.9 percentage points — a 62 percent relative increase — on average, and that R&D spending among the most research-intensive firms alone fell by $12.2 billion in the policy's first year, and this paper situates that decline against the trajectories of peer economies that maintained immediate expensing throughout the period. Second, it builds a **prospective recovery model**, applying the economics literature's most credible elasticity estimates to project the innovation dividend across three channels — direct R&D spending, patent filings, and startup formation — over a seven-year horizon. Third, it constructs an **NPV analysis of the domestic/foreign bifurcation**, modeling after-tax research costs for a US multinational choosing between domestic and foreign laboratory locations and surfacing the perverse secondary incentive the asymmetric treatment creates. Fourth, it proposes a **four-metric validation framework** — framed not as a rescission trigger but as the evidentiary apparatus through which policymakers can confirm, over time, that Section 174A is delivering the innovation dividend its architects intended.

The argument threading these contributions together is straightforward: the United States just made a $139 billion bet on innovation. We should know how to tell whether it worked.

## 2. Background: A Forty-Year Consensus Broken

The treatment of R&D in the American tax code has always rested on a deceptively simple economic observation: because firms cannot capture the full social returns from their discoveries, they underinvest relative to what is socially optimal. Tax policy that reduces the after-tax cost of research is therefore not a subsidy — it is a correction. The question is not whether to correct, but how efficiently to do so.

Congress answered that question in 1954 with [IRC Section 174](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/174). Prior to its enactment, businesses faced genuine ambiguity about whether research costs were ordinary and necessary expenses deductible under Section 162, or capital expenditures to be amortized against a future asset. That uncertainty was itself a drag on investment. Section 174 resolved it by creating a statutory safe harbor: R&D expenditures could be immediately deducted in the year incurred, or elected into amortization over not fewer than sixty months. Immediate expensing became the default, and for almost seven decades, it stayed that way.

The economics literature built a durable case for why. [Hall and Van Reenen's 2000 synthesis](https://eml.berkeley.edu/~bhhall/papers/HallVanReenen00_RP.pdf), reviewing roughly two decades of microeconometric evidence, found that a dollar in tax incentive for R&D generates approximately a dollar in additional R&D — a conclusion that has proven remarkably robust across countries, time periods, and firm types. More recent work, including [NBER Working Paper 22405](https://www.nber.org/papers/w22405) by Dechezleprêtre, Einiö, Martin, Nguyen, and Van Reenen, using a regression-discontinuity design on UK administrative tax data, found price elasticities as high as 2.6 among financially constrained smaller firms, with R&D doubling and patent filings rising 60 percent in response to tax changes. The implication is not merely that tax policy affects R&D at the margin; it is that the sensitivity is substantial enough to make the design of R&D tax provisions a first-order policy question.

Congress treated it as an afterthought. In 2017, the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act required businesses to begin capitalizing and amortizing domestic R&D over five years and foreign R&D over fifteen — not because legislators concluded that immediate expensing was bad policy, but because the [Congressional Research Service](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF12815) and the JCT needed to offset the cost of the bill's permanent corporate rate cut within the ten-year budget window. The mechanics of amortization create the revenue illusion: you don't eliminate the deduction, you shift it into the future, and under standard government scoring, the present value of future deductions registers as a "gain" in the near-term. The behavioral response — that firms would actually invest less — was largely scored out.

The provision took effect in tax year 2022, and the political reaction was immediate. Bipartisan majorities in both chambers repeatedly introduced legislation to restore expensing: the Tax Relief for American Families and Workers Act (H.R. 7024) passed the House 357–70 in January 2024, only to be [blocked in the Senate that August](https://leyton.com/us/insights/articles/senate-blocks-tax-relief-bill-section-174-capitalization-and-amortization-rules-remain-unchanged/), victims of unrelated fiscal disputes. By the time the OBBBA [permanently resolved the issue in July 2025](https://abgi-usa.com/section174/latest-and-greatest), three full tax years of R&D investment had occurred under the amortization regime — 2022, 2023, and 2024 — with firm behavior adapting to rules that near-universal consensus called misguided.

That three-year window is the behavioral record this paper exploits. Because the amortization requirement was policy-driven rather than reflecting any shift in underlying R&D productivity or market conditions, it provides something close to a natural experiment: an exogenous increase in the after-tax cost of R&D investment, sustained long enough to observe a response, followed by restoration. The economics literature would predict firms reduced R&D investment in response. The question this paper asks is: by how much, and what does reversing that cost tell us about the innovation dividend ahead?

## 3. The Behavioral Baseline: What Amortization Actually Cost

A natural experiment requires two things: a clean policy shock and a measurable behavioral response. Section 174's amortization requirement delivered both. What it produced — across three distinct levels of analysis — was a consistent, corroborated picture of a policy that imposed real costs on American research investment, concentrated most visibly where the economics literature would predict: among research-intensive firms with constrained access to external capital, and across sectors with long R&D horizons where time-value distortions bite hardest.

### The Firm-Level Signal

The clearest evidence comes from [Cowx, Lester, and Nessa (2024)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4998845), a Stanford Graduate School of Business study examining how firm-level R&D investment responded to the TCJA's amortization mandate, using a difference-in-differences design that exploits variation in US firms' fiscal year-ends to isolate the effect of the rule change. Their central finding: amortization increased affected firms' cash effective tax rates by 11.9 percentage points — a 62 percent relative increase — on average. That cost increase translated directly into forgone investment along several margins. Domestic-only firms cut R&D spending 2.7 percent more than comparable unaffected firms, a decline of $13.2 million per firm in the first year; research-intensive firms cut R&D spending 3.8 percent more than unaffected R&D-intensive firms, a decline of $58.8 million per firm; and financially constrained firms cut R&D spending 2.5 to 9.5 percent more than unaffected firms — the sharpest relative declines occurring exactly where the economics literature would predict, among firms least able to absorb the timing mismatch between expenditure and deduction. In aggregate, the authors find these effects translate to a **$12.2 billion reduction in R&D spending in the policy's first year among the most research-intensive firms alone** — the study does not report a single combined dollar total spanning the full population of affected firms, so the true first-year decline across all domestic-only and financially constrained firms combined is not directly measurable from the published results, though the per-firm and percentage figures above indicate it is a real and separate cost layered on top of the research-intensive-firm figure, not subsumed within it. The same study finds that affected companies also reduced capital expenditures and share repurchases, evidence that firms drew down other forms of investment and shareholder payout to help fund the increased cash tax liability — a secondary channel through which amortization's cost propagated beyond R&D budgets narrowly defined. [Stanford's own summary of the findings](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2024/10/american-innovation-got-slammed-temporary-end-key-tax-incentive) and a subsequent [ITIF analysis](https://itif.org/publications/2024/12/20/r&d-under-attack-loss-of-immediate-expensing-reduces-innovation-inputs/) both treat the result as among the most credible ex-post estimates of the amortization shock, precisely because it draws on administrative tax records rather than survey self-reports.

The Cowx et al. figures are worth comparing to the only credible ex-ante projection available before the rule took effect. In 2019, [Ernst & Young's QUEST group, working on behalf of the R&D Coalition](https://investinamericasfuture.org/ey-impact-of-the-amortization-of-certain-rd-expenditures-on-rd-spending-in-the-united-states/), projected the amortization requirement would reduce aggregate R&D spending across the entire US economy by $4.1 billion annually in its first five years, rising to $10.1 billion annually thereafter, with an estimated 23,400 R&D jobs lost per year in the near term. The realized first-year effect Cowx et al. later measured — a $12.2 billion decline among the most research-intensive firms alone, a narrower population than EY's economy-wide projection covered — nearly triples EY's ex-ante estimate for the entire economy, suggesting the 2019 projection substantially understated the behavioral response rather than overstated it. A [June 2025 review by the USC Schaeffer Institute](https://schaeffer.usc.edu/research/corporate-tax-policy-innovation/), synthesizing the broader literature on corporate taxation and innovation, cites a more conservative estimate — Kalcheva et al.'s finding that the TCJA reduced R&D spending by a comparatively modest 0.5 to 3.8 percentage points — illustrating that estimates of the amortization effect vary meaningfully with sample and methodology, even as every credible estimate in the literature points in the same direction: less R&D.

### The Aggregate Deceleration

At the macroeconomic level, the signal is visible but attenuated — the amortization years coincided with the early commercial expansion of generative AI, which generated a countervailing surge in private technology investment that partially masked the Section 174 headwind. With that caveat firmly in place, the BEA-tracked deceleration is nonetheless striking.

Business R&D spending in the United States averaged [8.6 percent annual growth from 2016 through 2021](https://ncses.nsf.gov/pubs/nsf25353). In 2022 — the first year of mandatory amortization — that growth rate began compressing. By 2023, [NSF's business R&D survey reported $722 billion in US business R&D expenditure, a 4.4 percent nominal increase from 2022](https://ncses.nsf.gov/pubs/nsf25353). Adjusted for inflation, real growth was approximately 1.7 percent — less than a fifth of the pre-TCJA average pace. Total US R&D across all sectors reached an estimated $993 billion in 2024, but the underlying business sector growth rate remained subdued. The deceleration is not proof of causation on its own; but it is consistent with a policy-induced drag layered beneath a technology-investment boom that was itself concentrated in a narrow slice of the capital expenditure landscape.

### Patent Filing Trends

Patent filings are a lagged indicator — the typical pipeline from research expenditure to patent application runs three to four years — so the full Section 174 signal in USPTO data is not yet visible. What early data shows is nonetheless consistent with the investment deceleration. [Utility patent grants to US-based companies fell 2.8 percent in 2023](https://www.anaqua.com/resource/anaqua-analysis-of-uspto-patenting-statistics-2024/), from 162,557 to 157,955, the first year-over-year decline in a decade. By 2025, overall US patent applications had [dropped 9 percent](https://www.ificlaims.com/news/u-s-patent-applications-decrease-dramatically-one-year-after-reaching-record-high-falling-9-2025-u-s-patent-grants-also-down/) from their 2024 peak, according to IFI CLAIMS Patent Services' annual ranking compiled directly from USPTO filing data. This paper treats the patent channel as a medium-term recovery metric — the Section 7 validation framework projects a three-to-four-year recovery lag — but the early directional signal is consistent with a reduction in the research inputs that eventually become filings.

### The Peer Economy Counterfactual

The most powerful evidence that Section 174's amortization caused, rather than coincided with, the US deceleration comes from the international comparison. By 2022, the United States was [one of only two developed countries requiring R&D amortization rather than immediate expensing](https://tax.thomsonreuters.com/news/rd-expert-capitalization-amortization-requirement-hurts-smaller-businesses/) — an almost singular position among OECD peers that had been moving in the opposite direction, with Germany introducing an R&D tax incentive in 2020 and 34 of 38 OECD economies maintaining cost-reducing treatment.

The divergence in growth rates is striking. [OECD data for 2023 shows US real R&D growth at 1.7 percent — compared to 8.7 percent in China, 3.6 percent in South Korea, and a broader OECD average that reflects continued expansion by economies that never imposed amortization](https://www.oecd.org/en/data/insights/statistical-releases/2025/03/rd-spending-growth-slows-in-oecd-surges-in-china-government-support-for-energy-and-defence-rd-rises-sharply.html). Israel and South Korea — respectively the world's most R&D-intensive economies at 6.3 and 5.0 percent of GDP — maintained immediate expensing throughout this period and continued to outperform. These are not economies in which AI investment surges or venture capital booms were absent; they are economies that did not choose to increase the after-tax cost of research during the same window that the United States did.

The composite picture across these three levels — firm-level investment responses, aggregate deceleration, and international divergence — establishes the behavioral baseline that the prospective analysis in Section 4 requires: amortization imposed a real, measurable, and internationally observable cost on American R&D. What Section 174A's restoration will recover is the subject of the next section.

## 4. Estimating the Innovation Dividend: A Prospective Economic Analysis

Prospective modeling of tax policy's innovation effects is inherently uncertain — researchers rarely enjoy the luxury of randomized controlled trials, and R&D investment decisions reflect dozens of simultaneous variables. What the economics literature does provide is a credible **elasticity toolkit**: a set of carefully estimated behavioral parameters that allow reasonable projection of how much an exogenous change in R&D's after-tax cost should shift investment, filings, and firm formation. Section 174A's restoration represents precisely this kind of exogenous shock — a reversal of the cost increase documented in Section 3. Applying the literature's parameters to the behavioral baseline yields a recovery path across three channels, with ranges that reflect genuine uncertainty without foreclosing confident policy inference.

### The Elasticity Toolkit

The starting point is [Hall and Van Reenen's 2000 synthesis](https://eml.berkeley.edu/~bhhall/papers/HallVanReenen00_RP.pdf): reviewing two decades of microeconometric studies, they concluded that a dollar in tax reduction for R&D generates approximately a dollar in additional R&D — a user cost elasticity of approximately 1. This is the conservative floor.

[Dechezleprêtre et al. (NBER Working Paper 22405)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w22405) pushed that estimate significantly higher: using a regression-discontinuity design in UK R&D tax credit data, they found a price elasticity of 2.6 for smaller, financially constrained firms — with patent filings rising **60 percent** in response to the cost reduction. [Zwick and Mahon (2017)](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Faer.20140855) found small firms respond ~95 percent more intensively than large firms to investment tax incentives, driven by financial frictions and the immediate cash-flow value of accelerated deductions. [Akcigit and Stantcheva's 2020 review](https://bfi.uchicago.edu/wp-content/uploads/BFI_WP_202070.pdf) synthesized the mechanism: tax changes shift not just R&D levels but composition — toward higher-risk, longer-horizon projects most sensitive to after-tax cost, and whose abandonment under amortization was the most socially costly portion of the behavioral response.

### Channel 1: Direct R&D Spending Recovery

Starting from the Section 3 baseline — EY's ex-ante $4.1–10.1 billion annual, economy-wide projection and Cowx et al.'s ex-post $12.2 billion first-year decline among the most research-intensive firms alone — and applying the elasticity toolkit across three scenarios:

- **Conservative** (unit elasticity, large-firm segment only): ~**$5–10 billion** in additional annual domestic R&D within three years.
- **Central** (mixed elasticity, including private firms and startups not captured in Cowx et al.'s sample): **$15–25 billion** annually over a five-year horizon.
- **Optimistic** (high small-firm elasticity plus retroactive-relief cash infusions immediately re-entering research budgets): **$25–40 billion** annually, with an early surge in 2025–2026.

Over a seven-year window (2025–2032), cumulative additional domestic R&D spans **$60–200 billion**, with the central estimate clustering around $100–130 billion. That range approaches the JCT's $139 billion revenue-cost score for Section 174A — suggesting that on a direct spending basis, this may be one of the more cost-efficient federal interventions in private research investment yet enacted, approaching dollar-for-dollar before counting spillovers. Against the [NSF's $993 billion total US R&D estimate for 2024](https://ncses.nsf.gov/pubs/nsf26314), a $15–25 billion annual increase in business R&D represents a 1.5–2.5 percentage-point acceleration in private research investment growth — roughly restoring the gap between the US amortization-era trajectory and the peer-economy performance documented in Section 3.

### Channel 2: Patent Filings (Lagged Signal)

Patent filings lag research expenditure by three to four years in US technology sectors, making the primary Section 174A signal most visible in USPTO data from 2028 through 2031. A conservative application of Dechezleprêtre et al.'s 60 percent patent-intensity estimate to the marginal R&D increase implies **5,000–15,000 additional patent applications annually** in R&D-intensive technology classes by the early 2030s — a partial reversal of the 9 percent decline in US patent applications observed in 2025, and a statistically detectable positive trend in USPTO Technology Monitoring series.

### Channel 3: Startup Formation

Early-stage firms — those most dependent on tax-advantaged cash flows to fund research before venture capital arrives at scale — are disproportionately sensitive to R&D expensing policy. [Hall and Lerner's analysis of the startup financing gap](https://www.nber.org/papers/w15325) establishes that pre-revenue firms rely on immediate deductibility of R&D spending precisely because they have no other income against which to offset costs; amortization deferred those benefits to years when many startups simply would not exist to claim them.

The OBBBA's retroactive relief for sub-$31 million firms is structurally targeted at this population, but the cash benefit is smaller than a naive calculation suggests, because affected firms had already claimed a portion of their 2022–2024 R&D deductions under the amortization schedule before OBBBA's enactment. Consider an illustrative startup that spent $2 million annually on domestic R&D in each of 2022, 2023, and 2024 — $6 million in total. Under the TCJA's five-year, half-year-convention amortization schedule (10 percent in the year incurred, 20 percent in each of the following four years), the firm would already have deducted $1.8 million of that $6 million by the end of 2024: $1.0 million of the 2022 tranche, $0.6 million of the 2023 tranche, and $0.2 million of the 2024 tranche. Retroactively electing full expensing on amended returns makes the entire $6 million deductible, so the *incremental* deduction unlocked by amending is $4.2 million ($6.0 million minus the $1.8 million already claimed) — not the full $6 million. At the 21 percent corporate rate, that generates an incremental refund of approximately **$882,000**, not the $1.26 million a same-year comparison would imply. For a seed-stage firm, that remains a material runway extension — the difference between a Series A bridge and a wind-down — but policymakers and founders modeling the retroactive benefit should use the amortization-adjusted figure, not the undiscounted one.

Applying [NVCA/PitchBook's tracking of VC-backed startup activity](https://nvca.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/Q1-2026-PitchBook-NVCA-Venture-Monitor.pdf) as a benchmark and Zwick and Mahon's small-firm response amplifier, this paper estimates an **incremental 2,000–5,000 additional VC-backed R&D startups formed over the 2025–2028 window** — a flow that would be measurable in BLS Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages data for R&D-sector employers with fewer than 500 employees, within a one-to-three-year lag.

### Near-Term vs. Medium-Term Dynamics

The asymmetry between channels is analytically important. Direct R&D spending responds within one to two years, as firms restore budgets that were formally cut or quietly deferred. Startup formation follows with a six-to-eighteen-month lag, as entrepreneurs who postponed founding decisions in 2022–2024 — when the amortization regime made early R&D particularly cash-consumptive — recommit to projects now underwritten by favorable tax treatment. Patent filings arrive last, reflecting the cumulative three-to-four-year pipeline.

This lag structure means that validating Section 174A's innovation dividend requires patience. Critics who assess the law's effects by examining 2026 patent data are looking at investments made in 2022, before the restoration. The validation framework proposed in Section 7 is calibrated to this lag structure explicitly — the goal is to build the evidentiary record through the correct window, not to judge a recovery before it has had time to materialize in observable outputs.

### The Additionality and Fiscal-Cost Objections

No projection of Section 174A's innovation dividend is credible without engaging the strongest case against it — one made not by opponents of innovation but by economists and fiscal conservatives who take tax-induced R&D seriously enough to interrogate what "induced" actually means.

The first objection is additionality: not every dollar of R&D that appears in response to favorable tax treatment represents genuinely new research. Firms facing a tax incentive tied to a spending category have an obvious motive to relabel existing expenditures — engineering costs, quality control, routine product tweaks — as qualifying R&D, inflating the measured response without a corresponding increase in real innovative activity. [Chen, Liu, Suárez Serrato, and Xu's American Economic Review study](https://www.nber.org/papers/w24749) of a Chinese R&D tax notch found that relabeling accounted for roughly one-quarter (24.2 percent) of the reported increase in R&D spending — a sobering benchmark for any policy that ties tax treatment to a self-reported expenditure category. The [Mercatus Center's assessment of the US R&D tax credit](https://www.mercatus.org/students/research/data-visualizations/research-and-development-tax-credit-suffers-design-and) raises a related distributional concern: the top 1 percent of claiming firms capture more than 82 percent of R&D tax credit dollars, suggesting incentive design tends to reward firms that would likely have invested regardless, rather than firms at the genuine investment margin.

These are legitimate concerns, and this paper does not dismiss them — but they apply with less force to expensing than to the R&D tax credit that is Mercatus's actual subject. The additionality critique bites hardest against credits and notches, which pay firms for crossing an R&D spending threshold and thus reward exactly the relabeling behavior Chen et al. documented. Immediate expensing carries no such notch: it changes the *timing* of a deduction firms already claim on real expenditures, not the eligibility for a marginal subsidy tied to a spending category. The elasticity estimates this paper relies on — Hall and Van Reenen's dollar-for-dollar result, Dechezleprêtre et al.'s regression-discontinuity design — are drawn from studies of cost-of-capital changes, not notch-based credits, and are accordingly less exposed to the relabeling mechanism Chen et al. identify. That distinction does not eliminate additionality concerns for Section 174A — some reported "new" R&D will still reflect reclassification rather than genuine incremental activity — but it means the scale of the problem is almost certainly smaller than a credit-focused critique would imply.

The second objection is fiscal: a $139 billion revenue cost deserves scrutiny independent of whatever innovation return it generates, particularly against a federal deficit that shows no sign of narrowing. This is a view [the Cato Institute has pressed consistently](https://www.cato.org/publications/commentary/cut-corporate-taxes-corporate-welfare) — though notably, Cato's own position is not opposition to R&D expensing itself, which it has [explicitly endorsed as pro-growth tax policy](https://www.cato.org/policy-analysis/slashing-tax-rates-cutting-loopholes) rather than corporate welfare. Cato's fiscal concern is about sequencing and offsets: expensing should be paired with elimination of narrower, less defensible tax preferences, not layered on top of an already-growing deficit without corresponding discipline elsewhere in the code. This paper's finding that Section 174A's central-case innovation return ($100–130 billion) approaches its revenue cost ($139 billion) is a partial answer to the fiscal objection, not a complete one — a policy that pays for itself in induced private R&D is a different fiscal proposition than a pure revenue loss, but "approaches" is not "exceeds," and the gap between the two is real money that competes against other uses of the same fiscal space, including broader rate relief or deficit reduction. Both objections argue for the validation framework in Section 7 to be taken seriously rather than treated as a formality: if the innovation dividend does not materialize on the timeline this paper projects, the fiscal critique wins the argument by default.

## 5. The Domestic/Foreign Bifurcation: A Hidden Subsidy to Offshoring Research

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act makes a deliberate choice embedded in Section 174A's structure: domestic R&D is immediately expensed; foreign R&D remains on fifteen-year amortization. Congress's intent is transparent — use the tax code to tilt a US company's capital allocation calculus toward domestic laboratory investment over offshore alternatives. This paper argues the policy works as intended for one class of firm, partially misfires for a second, and for a third — large multinationals with established offshore research operations — creates a secondary incentive that cuts in precisely the wrong direction.

### The NPV Framework

To trace the bifurcation's effects precisely, this paper models the after-tax cost of a $10 million R&D project for a US corporate taxpayer at the 21 percent federal rate, using an 8 percent discount rate. The analysis applies to R&D performed directly by the US entity — whether in domestic facilities, at a foreign branch, or through cost-sharing arrangements for jointly developed intellectual property — not to independent expenditures of foreign subsidiaries, which are governed by local-country tax rules. That structural distinction matters: the bifurcation's cost impact is most directly felt by US companies that directly fund researchers across jurisdictions, operate branch-form foreign laboratories, or bear shared R&D development costs under intercompany agreements.

**Domestic (post-Section 174A):** Immediate expensing generates a federal tax benefit of $2.1 million in Year 1. NPV at 8 percent: **$2.1 million**.

**Foreign (15-year amortization, maintained under the OBBBA):** Annual deduction = $666,667; annual tax benefit = $140,000. Present value of fifteen equal annual payments at 8 percent:

*NPV = $140,000 × [(1 − 1.08⁻¹⁵) / 0.08] = $140,000 × 8.56 ≈ **$1.20 million***

The NPV gap is approximately **$900,000 per $10 million in research spending** — roughly **9 cents on the dollar** — in favor of domestic R&D. At a 12 percent discount rate (more representative of pharmaceutical or semiconductor project hurdle rates, where long R&D timelines make time-value distortions most acute), the gap widens to approximately $1.1 million, or 11 cents. This is the pro-domestic margin Congress intended to create. The question is whether it survives contact with the incentive regimes operating in the locations a US company would actually consider.

### What Peer Economies Offer

The [OECD's 2025 Corporate Tax Statistics report on R&D tax incentives](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/corporate-tax-statistics-2025_6a915941-en/full-report/tax-incentives-for-research-and-development_f4dc0c8c.html) documents that R&D tax incentives across member economies have grown more generous over the past decade, not less. Four research locations of particular relevance to US multinationals illustrate the competitive landscape:

**United Kingdom (Cambridge):** Under the [merged R&D Expenditure Credit (RDEC) scheme](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/research-and-development-rd-tax-relief-reform/research-and-development-tax-relief-the-merged-scheme-rd-expenditure-credit-rdec) effective for accounting periods starting on or after April 1, 2024, companies receive a 20 percent above-the-line credit on qualifying R&D expenditure. After applying the 25 percent UK corporation tax, the net effective benefit is approximately 15 percent of qualifying spend — roughly $1.5 million in immediate tax benefit per $10 million invested. A British subsidiary of a US multinational performing qualifying R&D in Cambridge captures these benefits under UK rules, with the US parent's exposure flowing through GILTI mechanics rather than Section 174 amortization.

**Germany (Munich):** The [Forschungszulagengesetz (Research Allowance Act, FZulG)](https://stip.oecd.org/innotax/incentives/DEU1), in force since January 2020 and most recently expanded by the July 2025 Tax Investment Stimulus Program, provides a tax-free research allowance equal to 25 percent of qualifying R&D wage and contract-research costs for large enterprises, rising to 35 percent for small and medium-sized enterprises, on an assessment base that reaches €12 million per fiscal year under the expanded program. The allowance is a legal entitlement paid even to loss-making firms, independent of a company's tax liability — a design feature that makes it, if anything, more valuable than a same-rate ordinary deduction to early-stage or currently unprofitable research units. Combined with Germany's roughly 30 percent effective combined corporate rate, after-tax research costs for a German subsidiary of a US multinational are broadly competitive with — and for R&D-intensive SMEs, more generous than — post-174A domestic US treatment.

**Singapore:** The [Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore](https://www.iras.gov.sg/taxes/corporate-income-tax/income-deductions-for-companies/business-expenses/research-development-(r-d)-tax-measures) offers a 250 percent total deduction on qualifying R&D expenditure — a 100 percent base deduction plus a 150 percent enhanced deduction for R&D performed in Singapore, extended through YA 2028. At Singapore's 17 percent corporate rate, the combined tax benefit on $10 million of qualifying R&D is $4.25 million, reducing after-tax cost to $5.75 million. The US domestic after-tax cost under Section 174A is $7.9 million. Singapore's regime is not a marginal improvement on the US benchmark — it is structurally more generous by approximately 27 percent, in present-value terms, across virtually all project types.

**Canada (Toronto):** The [Scientific Research and Experimental Development (SR&ED) program](https://granthub.ca/guide/sr&ed) provides a 15 percent federal investment tax credit to large corporations, rising to 35 percent for qualifying smaller firms on up to $6 million in expenditures — an enhanced-rate ceiling Canada's 2025 Budget doubled. Provincial credits of 8–25 percent stack on top of the federal figure, bringing combined support as high as 43–60 percent for smaller firms. The [OECD's B-Index comparison](https://www.oecd.org/en/data/insights/statistical-releases/2025/04/rd-tax-incentives-continue-to-outpace-other-forms-of-government-support-for-rd-in-most-countries.html) consistently places Canada in the top quartile of OECD economies for R&D incentive generosity.

The comparison is uncomfortable for the bifurcation's architects. Across three of four benchmark locations — the UK, Singapore, and Canada — local-country R&D incentives produce after-tax R&D costs for a subsidiary-form operation that rival or exceed the US domestic benefit created by Section 174A. Singapore's regime, in particular, is so generous that its advantage over the US domestic benchmark is larger than the advantage Section 174A created over foreign amortization.

### The Repatriation Trap

For a US company making a genuinely greenfield research-location decision — no existing foreign infrastructure, building from scratch — the bifurcation performs as Congress intended. The US domestic option carries a real US-tax-cost advantage for directly incurred expenses, providing a meaningful tilt toward domestic research for projects that might otherwise be marginal between domestic and foreign sites.

Most large-firm R&D capital allocation decisions, however, are not greenfield. [Desai, Foley, and Hines](https://www.nber.org/papers/w8440) show that multinational affiliate operations exhibit strong organizational persistence — established foreign labs anchor human capital networks, university partnerships, and patent-prosecution pipelines whose switching costs dwarf any single-variable tax comparison. For a multinational with a mature Cambridge toxicology unit or a Munich design center, the marginal decision is not "where to build" but "where to expand" — and here the bifurcation cuts the wrong way. Incremental expansion of an existing foreign subsidiary captures the host country's generous local treatment outright, while repatriating research to the US triggers IP revaluation exposure, transfer-pricing renegotiation, and — for cost-shared intangibles — recognition of embedded gains. The 9-cent domestic advantage does not come close to covering that friction, so for this class of firm, Section 174A may perversely strengthen the case for adding to the Singapore subsidiary rather than consolidating research at home — the opposite of Congress's intent.

### Differential Effects by Firm Type

The bifurcation's consequences are not uniform across the R&D enterprise:

**Pure domestic firms and early-stage startups** have no exposure to the foreign amortization penalty. Section 174A is a pure gain. This population — encompassing R&D-intensive startups, software developers, and domestic manufacturers — captures the intended benefit cleanly and without distortion.

**Multinationals building first foreign labs** experience the bifurcation as designed. A firm evaluating its inaugural offshore research presence now faces a real US-tax cost penalty for directly funding foreign R&D, appropriately tilting greenfield decisions toward domestic sites.

**Multinationals with mature foreign R&D infrastructure** are the population where the bifurcation most clearly misfires. For these firms — which account for a disproportionate share of US-origin R&D conducted abroad — the incentive structure favors incremental offshore expansion over domestic repatriation. The repatriation friction cost is structural and persistent; the domestic tax advantage is real but insufficient.

**Foreign-headquartered firms with US R&D operations** are unambiguous winners: they receive Section 174A's immediate-expensing benefit for domestic research while facing no US penalty on home-country activity. From a competitiveness standpoint, this group benefits without contributing to the provision's core repatriation objective.

### Unfinished Legislative Business

The bifurcation is not an irrational policy instrument. For greenfield location decisions and purely domestic firms, it works as designed, and the 9-cent-per-dollar NPV advantage is real. But the mechanism does not reach the class of firms most likely to shift the aggregate US R&D-to-GDP ratio: large multinationals with established offshore research infrastructure and access to foreign incentive regimes that structurally rival the US domestic benchmark.

Closing this gap would require provisions the current Section 174A text does not include: a GILTI carve-out reducing IP repatriation friction; enhanced domestic incentives specifically tied to formal research-reshore commitments; or a transitional regime permitting multinationals to consolidate offshore research without triggering embedded gain recognition on accumulated intangible value. These are the unfinished legislative threads of Section 174A — and the most tractable path to ensuring the bifurcation's domestic-preference logic reaches the firms whose location decisions carry the largest innovation consequences.

## 6. Firm-Size Disaggregation: Are the OBBBA's Small-Firm Provisions Well-Targeted?

The OBBBA's most politically visible design choice is retroactive relief for small businesses. Firms with average annual gross receipts at or below $31 million — tested for the 2025 tax year — may file amended returns for 2022, 2023, and 2024, effectively reclaiming three years of amortization-era deductions they were denied. [The deadline for filing those amended returns is July 6, 2026](https://www.bdo.com/insights/tax/irs-issues-procedural-guidance-on-obbba-treatment-of-r-and-e-expenditures), creating a compressed compliance window. The policy logic is intuitive: small, R&D-intensive firms lack the cash-flow cushion to absorb amortization timing mismatches, and retroactive cash infusions would provide immediate runway for reinvestment.

The question this section examines is whether the provision's targeting matches the distribution of harm. Two questions structure the analysis. First: was the amortization-era investment decline concentrated among sub-$31 million firms, or did larger firms bear a disproportionate share of aggregate damage? Second: are sub-$31 million R&D firms sufficiently innovation-intensive on a per-dollar basis to justify the asymmetric retroactive treatment, even if they represent a small share of aggregate R&D spending?

### The Distribution of R&D Spending — and Harm

The most direct evidence on firm-size concentration comes from the NSF's [2023 Business Enterprise Research and Development (BERD) Survey](https://ncses.nsf.gov/pubs/nsf25353): small- and medium-sized companies (10–249 domestic employees) performed just 9 percent of total US business R&D in 2023, with large companies (250 or more employees) performing the remaining 91 percent. That distribution is skewed further still toward a handful of hyperscale firms: [Amazon, Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft, and Apple — the five largest corporate R&D spenders in the United States](https://www.visualcapitalist.com/cp/ranked-the-10-biggest-nasdaq-companies-by-rd-investment/) — together spend well over $200 billion annually on R&D, a sum that dwarfs the entire sub-$31 million segment's total research expenditure.

The gross-receipts threshold of $31 million does not map cleanly onto the employee-count breakpoints in NSF survey data, but the relationship is directionally stable: firms with gross receipts under $31 million are, with rare exceptions, also firms with far fewer than 250 employees. Their aggregate R&D spending, while individually significant for those who perform it, represents a single-digit percentage of the business R&D total that the OBBBA's retroactive relief is meant to address.

This arithmetic carries an uncomfortable implication: if amortization's damage was proportional to R&D spending — as the Cowx et al. evidence suggests, since the cost scaled with expenditure volume — the retroactive relief covers only a fraction of aggregate harm. The firms it does not reach retroactively, those in the $31 million–$500 million "middle market" range, are precisely the tier [Thomson Reuters](https://tax.thomsonreuters.com/news/rd-expert-capitalization-amortization-requirement-hurts-smaller-businesses/) identified as disproportionately burdened: R&D-intensive technology, life science, and advanced manufacturing firms too large for the simplicity that makes amended-return compliance easy for the smallest filers, but without the cross-project averaging advantages of large diversified companies. [RSM's practitioner FAQ](https://rsmus.com/insights/services/business-tax/faq-capitalization-and-amortization-of-r-d-costs-under-new-section-174-rules.html) describes these firms as finding amortization "problematic, costly, and confusing" — placing the epicenter of disruption above the $31 million floor.

### Per-Dollar Innovation Intensity: The Counterargument

The targeting critique, taken alone, understates what the provision gets right. Even if sub-$31 million firms account for a small share of aggregate R&D expenditure, their per-dollar contribution to the innovation metrics that matter most — startup formation, early-stage patent generation, human-capital spillovers to broader regional innovation ecosystems — may substantially exceed their expenditure share.

[Zwick and Mahon's canonical 2017 study](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Faer.20140855), analyzing over 120,000 firms across two bonus depreciation cycles, found that small firms respond to investment tax incentives approximately **95 percent more intensively than large firms**. The mechanism is well understood: small firms are disproportionately financially constrained, making the *timing* of tax benefits — not merely their present value — a material determinant of investment decisions. A large pharmaceutical company that can borrow at investment-grade rates to smooth the cash flow mismatch created by amortization experiences a smaller behavioral response than a ten-person biotech startup that cannot. The retroactive relief provision is structurally designed for precisely this high-elasticity population, and on that dimension, the $31 million threshold is well-motivated.

The innovation-intensity argument is reinforced by the nature of what sub-$31 million R&D firms produce. Early-stage research — the kind disproportionately conducted at seed-funded startups and pre-revenue science ventures — generates the exploratory output that feeds the patent pipeline five to ten years downstream. Large firms' R&D spending is increasingly concentrated in applied development and product enhancement; the more basic, higher-spillover research has migrated toward smaller specialized firms and university spin-outs. Restoring expensing-era economics for this segment of the innovation ecosystem, even if it covers a modest share of aggregate R&D dollars, may recover a disproportionate share of the socially valuable output that amortization suppressed.

### The Middle-Market Gap

Neither argument fully resolves the targeting question. What the evidence suggests is that the retroactive provision is well-designed for the segment it addresses — small, financially constrained, high-elasticity R&D firms — but that its coverage is too narrow to capture the bulk of aggregate amortization-era harm. The $31 million-to-$500 million revenue tier, where the dollar concentration of middle-market R&D investment sits, receives prospective relief through Section 174A's permanent restoration of immediate expensing but no retroactive redress for three years of artificially elevated tax burdens.

Three alternative design approaches would have extended coverage to this population:

**A higher threshold** — set at $500 million in gross receipts rather than $31 million — would capture the middle market tier where amortization's dollar impact was concentrated, at the cost of a larger revenue offset. Given that firms in this range have comparable financial frictions to the sub-$31 million population on R&D specifically (even if they are more creditworthy overall), the elasticity logic supporting retroactive relief does not abruptly vanish at $31 million.

**A refundable credit mechanism** — converting the retroactive deduction into a refundable R&D tax credit available to all affected firms, regardless of revenue size, with the refund amount scaling to documented 2022–2024 amortization deductions — would distribute relief proportional to harm rather than restricting it to the smallest filers.

**A phase-out structure** — retroactive relief available at full value up to $31 million in gross receipts, phasing out linearly to zero at $150 million — would extend coverage into the lower-middle-market while containing the fiscal cost and preserving the provision's small-firm orientation.

Congress chose the cleanest administrative option. The $31 million threshold is bright-line, operationally simple, and maps onto existing small-business definitions elsewhere in the Internal Revenue Code. It is also, by the evidence, a threshold set below the center of gravity of amortization-era harm. For policymakers tracking Section 174A's performance through the validation framework proposed in Section 7, the middle-market gap is the most important structural caveat: aggregate recovery metrics may underperform prospective estimates not because the permanent expensing restoration failed, but because three years of retroactive suppression in the $31 million–$500 million tier went uncompensated.

## 7. Measuring the Dividend: A Validation Framework for Section 174A

Policy evaluation in the R&D tax domain faces a persistent adversarial challenge: critics who oppose permanent expensing will look for the earliest available data that can be characterized as disappointing and use it to relitigate a settled debate. The lag structures that govern R&D's path from investment to innovation output — three to four years to patent filings, one to two years to startup formation, five to ten years to full productivity effects — provide ample opportunity for premature verdicts. The validation framework this paper proposes is designed to prevent that adversarial use of data. It is not a sunset trigger, not a rescission condition, and not an invitation to revisit a permanent policy. It is a structured evidentiary record through which the innovation community, NSF, BEA, and Congress can prove, methodically and with increasing confidence, that Section 174A is delivering the innovation dividend its architects intended.

The framework proposes four observable metrics, calibrated explicitly to the lag structures the economics literature has established, and drawn from statistical programs already reporting on the relevant phenomena.

### Metric 1: Private Business R&D as a Percentage of US GDP
**Data source**: [BEA R&D Satellite Account](https://www.bea.gov/data/special-topics/research-and-development-satellite-account) | **Expected lag**: 1–2 years | **Baseline**: ~2.5% of GDP (2024)

The BEA's R&D satellite account — extended through 2023 in its September 2025 update — provides the most methodologically rigorous aggregate measure of business R&D investment in a GDP-consistent framework. The satellite account adjusts NSF survey data for scope and coverage differences, linking R&D expenditure to the national income accounting structure that makes cross-year comparisons credible. A 0.2-to-0.3 percentage-point increase in private business R&D's share of GDP over the 2025–2027 reporting horizon — from the 2024 baseline of approximately 2.5 percent — would constitute statistically meaningful evidence of Section 174A's direct spending effect, within the one-to-two-year response window the behavioral baseline (Section 3) and prospective model (Section 4) predict. BEA should be encouraged by NSF and OMB to accelerate the satellite account's release cadence from its current annual lag, so that the 2025 spending data is publicly available before the end of 2026 — the window during which the restoration's first-year effects will be most directly observable.

### Metric 2: Domestic Patent Filings in R&D-Intensive Technology Classes
**Data source**: [USPTO Patent Technology Monitoring Team (PTMT)](https://www.uspto.gov/web/offices/ac/ido/oeip/taf/reports.htm) | **Expected lag**: 3–4 years | **Observable window**: 2028–2031

The [NBER's foundational work on the R&D-to-patents pipeline](https://www.nber.org/papers/w1454) established the empirical foundation for the lag structure between research expenditure and patent application, and Hall's synthesis of the subsequent literature, most recently in [*Tax Policy for Innovation* (NBER Working Paper 25773)](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w25773/revisions/w25773.rev0.pdf), puts the typical pipeline in US technology sectors at three to four years from funded research to a protectable invention and warns against interpreting early post-policy data as definitive. The appropriate measurement window for Section 174A's patent signal begins in 2028 — reflecting research funded in 2025 — and extends through 2031.

The specific metric: annual utility patent applications by US-entity filers in three PTMT technology classes most sensitive to business R&D tax treatment — semiconductor devices, pharmaceutical compositions, and software processing systems. A positive trend against the 2021–2024 baseline — reversing the 9 percent application decline observed in 2025 — would constitute direct evidence of Section 174A's innovation-output effect. The PTMT releases these data annually and by technology class, making the metric operationally straightforward to track.

### Metric 3: VC-Backed R&D Startup Formation in Technology Sectors
**Data source**: [NVCA/PitchBook Venture Monitor](https://nvca.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/Q1-2026-PitchBook-NVCA-Venture-Monitor.pdf) | **Expected lag**: 1–2 years | **Baseline**: 2022–2024 seed-stage formation data

Startup formation is the shortest-lag channel in the Section 174A recovery path, and the most directly attributable to the OBBBA's retroactive small-firm relief. A founder who deferred incorporation in 2022–2024 because amortization made early R&D disproportionately cash-consumptive may recommit within six to twelve months of the OBBBA's enactment, as the retroactive refund arrives and the permanent expensing environment reduces the after-tax cost of building an R&D-intensive startup. The metric: seed- and Series A-stage financing rounds in biotech, deep-tech hardware, and enterprise software — sectors with materially R&D-intensive cost structures — tracked against the 2022–2024 baseline. A statistically detectable 10–15 percent acceleration in formation rates by 2027 would be consistent with the prospective model's central estimate of 2,000–5,000 additional VC-backed R&D startups over the 2025–2028 window.

### Metric 4: R&D Employment at Sub-500-Employee Firms
**Data source**: [BLS Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages (QCEW)](https://www.bls.gov/cew/), [size-class data](https://www.bls.gov/cew/classifications/size/size-data-info.htm) | **Expected lag**: 1–3 years | **NAICS codes**: 5417 (Scientific Research and Development Services)

The QCEW provides employment and wage data covering over 95 percent of US jobs, filterable by NAICS industry code and establishment size class. NAICS 5417 — Scientific Research and Development Services — provides the most direct employment proxy for the R&D enterprise outside of in-house corporate R&D divisions. Tracking employment at establishments with fewer than 500 employees in this NAICS sector captures the small and mid-sized innovation firm population most responsive to Section 174A's incentive, with a one-to-three year lag reflecting hiring decisions made in response to restored expensing economics. This metric is also the appropriate channel for testing whether the amortization-era investment decline translated into R&D job losses — the Cowx et al. study documents reduced capital expenditure and shareholder payout among affected firms but does not itself isolate an R&D employment effect, leaving that link as an open empirical question the QCEW data can help answer going forward. A recovery of employment in this cohort toward pre-amortization 2021 levels — and ideally beyond — by 2027–2028 would constitute strong evidence that the provision is generating real R&D activity, not merely tax-accounting changes at large firms with sophisticated planning capabilities.

### Integrating the Framework into Regular Statistical Reporting

The four metrics collectively form a triangulated validation apparatus. No single indicator is conclusive in isolation: GDP share can be distorted by macroeconomic base effects; patent filings reflect both R&D inputs and litigation strategy; startup formation reflects venture capital market conditions alongside tax incentives; employment data lags real activity by a full reporting cycle. Used together, positive co-movement across all four metrics on their respective timelines is the evidentiary standard this paper proposes as proof of concept.

NSF and BEA should be tasked — by OMB directive or Congressional mandate — with publishing an annual Section 174A Innovation Dividend Report beginning with 2027 data, tracking these four metrics against their pre-amortization baselines and the prospective ranges estimated in Section 4. The purpose is not conditional evaluation but progressive confirmation: here is how we will prove this worked.

## 8. Conclusion: From Revenue Scoring to Innovation Accounting

Congress has now run the experiment twice — once by accident, once by design. The accidental experiment was the TCJA's 2022 amortization mandate, a revenue offset that treated the behavioral response to a near-90-percent increase in the after-tax cost of R&D as a rounding error. The deliberate experiment is Section 174A: a $139 billion bet, scored on the JCT's ledger as a cost, that restoring immediate expensing will recover — and extend — the investment the amortization years suppressed. This paper has argued that Congress scored the first experiment's revenue effects with precision and its innovation effects with silence, and it is on track to do the same with the second unless the measurement apparatus changes now.

The four contributions assembled here are meant to close that gap. The **behavioral baseline** established that amortization was not a paper cost: the most research-intensive US firms alone cut domestic R&D by $12.2 billion in the first year, the effective tax rate on R&D investment for affected firms rose 11.9 percentage points — a 62 percent relative increase — on average, and the US growth rate in business R&D fell to a fifth of its pre-TCJA pace while Israel, South Korea, and China continued accelerating under expensing regimes they never abandoned. The **prospective recovery model**, built on Hall and Van Reenen's dollar-for-dollar elasticity and Dechezleprêtre et al.'s higher estimates for constrained firms, projects $60–200 billion in cumulative additional domestic R&D through 2032 — a central estimate that approaches the JCT's own revenue-cost score, suggesting Section 174A may be among the more cost-efficient federal interventions in private research investment yet enacted, on a direct-spending basis alone. That projection survives, though it is not untouched by, the additionality and fiscal-cost objections this paper engaged directly: expensing is less exposed to relabeling than the credit literature those objections are usually built on, but a $139 billion cost that only *approaches* its projected return is not the same as one that clears it.

The other two contributions are cautionary rather than celebratory. The **domestic/foreign bifurcation** analysis found that the 9-cent-per-dollar NPV advantage Congress built into Section 174A works only for firms making greenfield location decisions; for multinationals with mature offshore research infrastructure in London, Munich, or Singapore, the repatriation friction costs dwarf the tax advantage, and the bifurcation may perversely favor incremental foreign expansion over domestic consolidation. This is not a reason to abandon the domestic preference — it is a reason to finish building it, through GILTI relief for repatriated IP or a transitional reshoring provision that the current statute does not contain. The **firm-size disaggregation** found that the OBBBA's retroactive relief, while well-targeted to the small-firm population's genuinely higher elasticity, reaches only a single-digit share of aggregate R&D spending, leaving the $31 million–$500 million middle-market tier — where Thomson Reuters and RSM both identified concentrated amortization-era distress — without retroactive redress.

Both gaps point to the same underlying failure mode: Congress evaluates R&D tax policy through static revenue scoring, a framework with no native vocabulary for patents, startups, or research-location decisions. The **four-metric validation framework** proposed in Section 7 — private R&D share of GDP, technology-class patent filings, VC-backed startup formation, and sub-500-employee R&D employment — is offered as a starting vocabulary. It is deliberately not a trigger for reconsidering permanence; it is the evidentiary record through which NSF, BEA, and Congress can demonstrate, on the timeline the innovation literature actually predicts, that the dividend materialized.

The recommendation that follows is institutional rather than legislative: NSF and BEA should adopt this four-metric framework as a standing annual reporting requirement, not a one-time retrospective. Innovation accounting, unlike revenue scoring, cannot be produced in a single Joint Committee memo — it requires exactly the kind of longitudinal, multi-agency measurement infrastructure this paper has sketched. Section 174A was a $139 billion wager that innovation-friendly tax treatment pays for itself in growth Congress does not currently know how to see. Building the instruments to see it is the unfinished half of the reform.
