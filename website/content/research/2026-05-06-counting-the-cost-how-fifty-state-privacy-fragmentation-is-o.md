## Abstract

The United States entered 2026 with nineteen comprehensive state privacy laws on the books, eleven effective within the preceding twelve months, and no federal privacy standard. The primary empirical anchor in the preemption debate — the Information Technology and Innovation Foundation's 2022 projection that a full fifty-state patchwork would cost businesses $98 to $112 billion per year in out-of-state compliance costs — has not been systematically updated against what has actually happened: nineteen enacted laws (not fifty), enforcement significantly escalated in California, and two federal legislative vehicles failed. This paper provides that update, offering a six-dimension structural audit of all nineteen enacted state privacy laws against the ADPPA as a federal baseline; a disaggregated cost reality-check that separates the aggregate cost ceiling (which warrants downward revision) from per-firm burden (which does not, and may warrant upward revision); a political economy map of the three structural deadlocks that have blocked three consecutive Congresses; and an explicit feasibility ranking of three second-best alternatives.

The paper finds that fragmentation is structural, concentrated in specific fault lines, and directionally accelerating — and that the per-firm compliance burden for California-exposed businesses is at or above ITIF's projections, not below, principally because the California Privacy Protection Agency's emergence as a proactive enforcement institution was not incorporated in the 2022 model. The argument for federal preemption is stronger when grounded in measured divergence than when it rests on the projection of a legislative scenario that has not fully materialized.

## Executive Summary

This paper is a structured empirical examination of the state privacy patchwork as it exists in 2026 — not as it might exist if all fifty states enacted comprehensive legislation. It proceeds in four steps and produces four correspondingly specific findings.

**What the paper covers.** Section 2 conducts a six-dimension structural audit of all nineteen enacted state privacy laws, comparing each against the ADPPA as a federal baseline across scope thresholds, consumer rights portfolios, enforcement architectures, private rights of action, sensitive data categories, and exemption structures. The audit reveals fragmentation that is concentrated, structural, and directionally accelerating — states that enacted laws in 2024 and 2025 innovated jurisdictionally rather than harmonizing with existing frameworks. Section 3 updates the ITIF compliance cost projections against 2025–2026 evidence, separating two claims that the headline figure conflates: the aggregate cost ceiling (which warrants downward revision because fifty-state enactment has not materialized) and the per-firm burden (which does not, and may warrant upward revision given California's enforcement cost multiplier). Section 4 maps the political economy of three legislative Congresses, identifying the three structural deadlocks — private right of action scope, preemption ceiling, and multi-principal enforcement architecture — that have proven more durable than the bipartisan will to legislate. Section 5 assesses three second-best alternatives against both what each achieves and what each structurally cannot, ranking them explicitly on feasibility.

**What the paper finds.** The six-dimension audit confirms that divergence is real, concentrated in fault lines that resist market solutions, and widening rather than narrowing. The cost reality-check finds that ITIF's aggregate cost ceiling warrants downward revision — proponents of federal preemption should not cite the $1 trillion headline without that qualification — but that per-firm burden for businesses operating in the enacted states, particularly California, is at or above ITIF's per-firm projections. Privacy compliance infrastructure is semi-fixed overhead; per-firm costs do not attenuate proportionally as the number of enacted states falls below fifty. The political economy map confirms that three specific deadlocks have blocked three Congresses, and that the structural conditions have not materially shifted — with one important exception: the CPPA's enforcement footprint is expanding into sectors and companies headquartered in California, which is the variable most likely to change the political calculus inside the California delegation that has anchored the preemption deadlock.

**What the paper recommends.** Second-best alternatives cannot substitute for comprehensive federal legislation. State reciprocity compacts exclude California and Minnesota — the states that make fragmentation expensive. FTC rulemaking under Magnuson-Moss cannot preempt state law, faces a three-to-five year timeline, and produces a rule that layers on top of the nineteen-state patchwork rather than displacing it. The [SECURE Data Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/8413), the live 119th Congress vehicle, is architecturally close to the minimum viable deal structure: full preemption with concurrent state AG enforcement authority, FTC primary enforcement, standardized consumer rights, and no private right of action. Senate viability remains the binding constraint, and the California caucus calculation on preemption is the variable most likely to determine whether it is resolvable in this Congress or the next.

## 1. Introduction

The United States entered 2026 with nineteen comprehensive state privacy laws on the books, eleven of them effective within the preceding twelve months, and no federal privacy standard. This is not a hypothetical fragmentation scenario that might materialize if Congress fails to act. It is the operational reality that businesses, regulators, and consumers are navigating today.

The theoretical case for federal preemption is well established and need not be rehearsed at length here. What is less well established — and what this paper attempts to provide — is a structured empirical accounting of where the patchwork actually stands: how the laws structurally diverge, what they cost in practice, why legislation has failed, and what options remain. The argument for preemption is stronger when it is grounded in measurable divergence than when it rests on generic warnings about regulatory complexity. The argument against accepting inadequate substitutes is stronger when the limits of those substitutes are specified rather than asserted. Both tasks require going further than the existing literature has gone.

The primary empirical benchmark in the federal preemption debate has been the Information Technology and Innovation Foundation's January 2022 analysis, which projected that a full fifty-state patchwork would impose $98 to $112 billion per year in out-of-state compliance costs and cost businesses $1 trillion more over a decade than a single federal standard. That projection entered the policy debate as the dominant economic argument for preemption — and it has been cited, recited, and occasionally misread ever since without systematic update against what has actually happened. Four years later, nineteen states have enacted laws (not fifty), enforcement has escalated significantly in California, and two successive federal legislative vehicles have failed. This paper's first contribution is a structured reality-check against the ITIF model: what has tracked, what requires revision, and what the ITIF methodology could not have anticipated given the California Privacy Protection Agency's emergence as a proactive, well-resourced enforcement agency.

The federal legislative record over the same period is a puzzle that requires explanation beyond generic partisan dysfunction. The [American Data Privacy and Protection Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/117th-congress/house-bill/8152) (ADPPA), introduced in the 117th Congress by a bipartisan quartet spanning both chambers and both parties' senior Commerce Committee members, passed the House Energy and Commerce Committee by 53–2 in July 2022. The American Privacy Rights Act (APRA), its [118th Congress successor](https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/house-bill/8818), carried bipartisan co-sponsorship from the most senior members of the relevant House committee. Neither reached a floor vote. Explaining that outcome requires mapping not generic political gridlock but three specific structural deadlocks — over private rights of action, preemption scope, and multi-principal enforcement architecture — that have proven more durable than the bipartisan will to legislate. Understanding those deadlocks precisely is a prerequisite for assessing whether the conditions for resolution are changing.

The paper proceeds in four steps. Section 2 conducts a six-dimension structural audit of all nineteen enacted state privacy laws against the ADPPA as a federal baseline, producing a composite portrait of where divergence is concentrated and where it is directionally accelerating. Section 3 updates the ITIF cost projections against 2025–2026 evidence, separating the aggregate cost ceiling (which warrants downward revision given incomplete enactment) from the per-firm burden (which does not, and may warrant upward revision given California's enforcement cost multiplier). Section 4 maps the political economy of federal gridlock in the 117th through 119th Congresses, identifying what has and has not shifted between the ADPPA and the SECURE Data Act. Section 5 assesses three second-best alternatives — comprehensive framework revival, state reciprocity compacts, and FTC rulemaking under Magnuson-Moss — against both what each achieves and what each structurally cannot, ranking them explicitly on feasibility.

As the [IAPP's 2026 effective-date summary](https://iapp.org/news/a/new-year-new-rules-us-state-privacy-requirements-coming-online-as-2026-begins) documents, Delaware, Iowa, Nebraska, New Hampshire, New Jersey, Tennessee, and Minnesota all entered enforcement in 2025–2026. The patchwork is not still arriving. It has arrived. The question is what to do about it.

## 2. The Structural Audit: Six Dimensions of Divergence

### Methodology

This section compares all nineteen enacted comprehensive state privacy laws across six structural dimensions: scope thresholds, consumer rights portfolios, enforcement architectures, private rights of action, sensitive data categories, and exemption structures. The six dimensions were selected because they represent the fault lines where divergence is most consequential for compliance program design — areas where firms cannot satisfy multiple states with a single unified architecture and must instead build jurisdiction-specific modules. Dimensions where states have broadly converged (e.g., core data subject rights at the access-deletion-correction-portability level) are noted but not analyzed at length.

**Sources.** Each state law was characterized using three primary inputs: the statutory text itself; the [IAPP's U.S. state privacy laws tracker](https://iapp.org/resources/article/us-state-privacy-laws-overview), which provides continuously updated comparative analysis of enacted laws; and the Future of Privacy Forum's state law comparison charts, which map specific provisions across the enacted landscape. Where specific provisions are cited — bill numbers, section references — statutory text is the primary source.

**Analytical choices.** The ADPPA was chosen as the federal baseline because it represents the most fully negotiated comprehensive federal privacy framework to have cleared committee in this legislative era, passing the House Energy and Commerce Committee 53–2. Using ADPPA as a baseline does not imply that ADPPA's specific choices on any dimension are normatively correct; it provides a fixed reference point for measuring where state laws diverge from what a federal standard would have required. The six-dimension framework is analytical, not exhaustive — it is designed to identify where divergence concentrates rather than to catalogue every provision of every law.

**Limitations.** This analysis reflects statutory law as enacted through early 2026. Several states had pending regulatory proceedings — notably California's CPPA rulemaking on automated decision-making technology and cybersecurity audits — that would add further compliance obligations when final. State AG enforcement priorities, which can effectively alter the operative scope of statutory requirements, are noted where material but not systematically compared. Laws that have been enacted but not yet taken effect are included in the statutory comparison but flagged where their enforcement status affects the compliance picture.

### A. Scope Thresholds

The most fundamental divergence begins with who the law covers. States have adopted three distinct threshold architectures that collectively create significant compliance uncertainty for businesses operating nationally.

The first group — Nebraska and Texas — applies no consumer count threshold at all. Any business processing personal data of Nebraska or Texas residents that does not qualify for an exemption is covered, regardless of scale. This is the broadest possible scope, and it means that the consumer count floor used by every other state law is irrelevant for a business with meaningful presence in either state. The second group clusters at 100,000 consumers: California, Colorado, Indiana, Iowa, Kentucky, Minnesota, New Jersey, Oregon, Utah, and Virginia all use this trigger, though revenue-based secondary thresholds vary — Rhode Island uses 20 percent of gross annual revenue, while Indiana, Iowa, Kentucky, and Utah use 50 percent. A third group applies lower count thresholds — 35,000 consumers in Connecticut, Delaware, Maryland, New Hampshire, and Rhode Island — that bring more mid-market businesses into scope. Tennessee stands alone at 175,000 consumers, the narrowest consumer-count trigger of any enacted law.

Florida's Digital Bill of Rights is the structural outlier. Under [Fla. Stat. § 501.702](https://www.leg.state.fl.us/statutes/index.cfm?App_mode=Display_Statute&Search_String=&URL=0500-0599/0501/Sections/0501.702.html), it applies only to companies with more than $1 billion in annual global gross revenues whose primary service is an online platform or consumer smart speaker with a virtual assistant — effectively limiting scope to a handful of the largest technology companies. As the [IAPP's comprehensive state privacy laws overview](https://iapp.org/resources/article/us-state-privacy-laws-overview) notes, Florida's law functions less as a general consumer privacy regime than as a targeted platform regulation — a category of one among the nineteen.

The ADPPA, by contrast, would have covered any entity that collects or processes personal data, with a small business carve-out for firms with fewer than 200 employees, less than $35 million in annual revenue, and fewer than 200,000 consumers — a scope that would have captured a meaningfully larger share of the economy than the 100,000-consumer threshold used by most states while protecting small businesses from compliance overhead disproportionate to their data processing footprint.

---

### B. Consumer Rights Portfolios

All nineteen states provide a core rights portfolio: access, deletion, correction, and portability. These are the rights consumers can reliably assert regardless of which state law applies. The divergence begins immediately beyond that core. Opt-out rights — from targeted advertising, sale of personal data, and profiling — are universal in form but heterogeneous in definition. "Sale" means something different in California (broad transfer-for-value) than in Virginia (narrower, excluding first-party sharing), and compliance programs built to satisfy California's definition do not automatically satisfy Virginia's — or vice versa.

Secondary rights present in fewer than five states represent the highest-complexity tier. Minnesota's Consumer Data Privacy Act and Oregon's Consumer Privacy Act both include rights to opt out of profiling in employment decision-making contexts — a category no other enacted state law covers, requiring jurisdiction-specific program modules rather than a unified national compliance architecture. The [Minnesota Consumer Data Privacy Act](https://www.revisor.mn.gov/statutes/cite/325O) grants consumers the right to opt out of profiling used to make automated decisions with legal or similarly significant effects — including decisions affecting employment opportunities — and gives consumers the right to be informed of the reason a profiling decision produced a particular outcome and to request a reevaluation if inaccurate data was used. No other enacted state law reaches employment profiling with equivalent specificity.

California and Oregon additionally require businesses to provide consumers with a list of specific third parties to whom their data was disclosed — a data-mapping obligation that goes well beyond what ADPPA's notice requirements would have demanded. Rights present in only one or two states impose disproportionate compliance cost per affected consumer: firms must build and maintain entire program modules — technical implementations, consumer interfaces, internal review workflows — for rights they discharge a handful of times per year.

The pattern is consistent: as the geographic coverage of a rights category narrows to one or two states, the compliance cost per affected consumer rises sharply relative to the consumer protection benefit delivered.

---

### C. Enforcement Architectures

California's CPPA is a first-of-its-kind dedicated privacy regulator — independent of the Attorney General's office, with its own rulemaking authority, investigative staff, enforcement division, and, as of February 2026, a standalone Audits Division under inaugural Chief Privacy Auditor Sabrina Boyson Ross. By early 2026, the agency was running more than 100 active investigations simultaneously, autonomously scanning public-facing websites for Global Privacy Control non-compliance, broken opt-outs, and dark patterns.

Recent enforcement actions illustrate the difference in kind from AG-based enforcement. A [$1.35 million penalty against Tractor Supply Company](https://cppa.ca.gov/announcements/2025/20250930.html) for failures to honor opt-out requests and provide adequate privacy notices, and a [$375,703 settlement with Ford Motor Company](https://privacy.ca.gov/2026/03/ford-to-change-practices-pay-fine-for-adding-unnecessary-friction-to-opt-out-process/) for adding an impermissible email verification step that created unnecessary friction in the opt-out process, both reflect an enforcement posture qualitatively different from the reactive, complaint-driven model all other states rely on.

Every other enacted state privacy law channels enforcement through the state Attorney General, operating on a complaint-driven basis with no dedicated privacy investigative infrastructure. Cure periods — typically 30 to 60 days — provide a first-violation buffer that the CPPA has largely eliminated. This creates a two-tier enforcement reality: California-exposed firms face proactive regulatory scrutiny from a well-resourced agency actively expanding its audit capacity; firms exposed only to the other eighteen states face a materially lower enforcement probability. For compliance investment decisions, this enforcement asymmetry distorts resource allocation in ways that a single federal standard would eliminate.

---

### D. Private Rights of Action

The private right of action is where state legislation and federal negotiation have traveled in opposite directions — and where that divergence most directly exposes the dysfunction of the current moment.

Among the nineteen enacted state comprehensive privacy laws, only California provides any private right of action, and it is narrow: the CCPA's breach-only provision authorizes individual suits for violations of the statute's data security requirements where a breach results from a company's failure to implement reasonable security measures. No California law provision authorizes private suits for substantive privacy violations — failure to honor deletion requests, unlawful data sale, or unauthorized processing of sensitive data. The remaining eighteen states provide no private right of action whatsoever; enforcement is exclusively the province of the state attorney general. As [Byte Back Law's analysis of the Minnesota Consumer Data Privacy Act](https://www.bytebacklaw.com/2024/05/minnesota-legislature-passes-consumer-data-privacy-act/) documents, state legislatures have treated broad private rights of action as a systemic risk — a litigation industry driver that would generate class action exposure disconnected from actual consumer harm.

The ADPPA proposed something materially more expansive: a full federal private right of action allowing individuals to sue covered entities in federal court for damages, injunctions, and attorneys' fees, beginning two years after enactment. A small business exemption and a 45-day pre-suit cure window for injunctive relief were added to mitigate business opposition, but they were insufficient — the PRA was the primary reason House Majority Leader Pelosi declined to schedule a floor vote in 2022, and it remained the central negotiating obstacle in APRA discussions in 2024. The result is a structural paradox: state legislatures have voted with near unanimity against broad PRAs in practice, while consumer advocates and Democratic members have treated federal PRA scope as a non-negotiable red line in negotiations. The convergence at the state level has not translated into federal negotiating flexibility — an asymmetry Section 4 examines in detail.

---

### E. Sensitive Data Categories

Every enacted state law designates a category of sensitive personal data subject to heightened protection — typically opt-in consent rather than opt-out — encompassing at minimum biometric identifiers, health information, precise geolocation data, racial and ethnic origin, and data about children. At this level, convergence is genuine. The compliance problem lies in the definitions.

State legislative activity in 2024–2025 moved consistently toward expanding sensitive data categories and tightening data minimization requirements rather than harmonizing with existing state frameworks. [Colorado H.B. 24-1058](https://leg.colorado.gov/bills/hb24-1058), signed by the Governor on April 17, 2024 and effective August 7, 2024, added neural data — defined as information generated by the measurement of the activity of an individual's central or peripheral nervous systems — to Colorado's sensitive data definition, making Colorado the first state to enact this category. Oregon amended its Consumer Privacy Act with [H.B. 2008](https://olis.oregonlegislature.gov/liz/2025R1/Measures/Overview/HB2008), signed June 2025 and effective January 1, 2026, to prohibit the sale of precise geolocation data that can identify a consumer's location within a 1,750-foot radius — a more prescriptive and restrictive standard than the general "precise geolocation" coverage used by most other states, which focus on the sensitivity of the data category without specifying a geographic radius. California's CPRA sensitive data scope is the broadest on net, encompassing financial account credentials, precise geolocation, and the contents of communications — a definition substantially wider than Virginia's or Utah's.

The compliance consequence is the highest-common-denominator problem. Firms operating nationally cannot design a single sensitive data consent workflow calibrated to an average state standard; they must design to the most restrictive applicable definition in each category, then maintain that architecture across all states regardless of whether it is legally required. Every new state amendment that expands a sensitive category without harmonizing with existing definitions adds another tier to that floor.

---

### F. Exemption Structures

Exemption architecture is where compliance fragmentation becomes operationally intractable. Employee data and B2B data exemptions — present in eighteen of nineteen enacted state laws — illustrate the point precisely.

California eliminated its employee data and B2B data exemptions at the start of 2023, when the CPRA took full effect and the legislature declined to extend the temporary exemptions that had been in place since CCPA's original enactment. As [Morgan Lewis documents](https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2022/10/california-consumer-privacy-act-employee-and-b2b-exemptions-expire-january-1-2023), every other enacted state law maintains these exemptions. The practical consequence: a company with a national workforce processes employee personal data subject to California's CPRA — notice obligations, access rights, deletion rights, sensitive data restrictions for health or biometric data collected in the employment context — while the same data in an Indiana or Texas employee file is fully exempt from state privacy law. There is no way to run a unified employee data privacy program that satisfies California without over-applying it in every other state, a cost-inefficient outcome that falls hardest on mid-market employers without dedicated privacy counsel in each jurisdiction.

Nonprofit exemptions follow a similar fault line. Colorado, Connecticut, Virginia, and several others exempt nonprofits from comprehensive privacy law coverage; California's CPRA does not. Oregon exempts nonprofits from certain obligations but not others. For large nonprofits operating nationally — universities, hospital systems, advocacy organizations — the exemption patchwork means inconsistent compliance obligations for the same data processing activities depending solely on which state's residents are involved.

The six-dimension audit produces a consistent finding: divergence is not random noise across state laws. It is structural, concentrated in the same fault lines — scope, enforcement, PRAs, sensitive data, exemptions — that would require legislative resolution to address.

---

### Synthesis

Taken together, the six-dimension audit reveals a fragmentation landscape that is structural, directional, and accelerating. Across every dimension — from who is covered to who enforces and how aggressively — the trajectory from 2022 to 2026 has been toward greater divergence, not convergence. The states that passed new laws in 2024 and 2025 did not harmonize with existing frameworks; they innovated jurisdictionally, adding neural data categories, tightening geolocation sale prohibitions, and introducing employment profiling opt-outs that no other state had enacted.

The ADPPA's failure left the patchwork not as a static structure but as a dynamic one — expanding with each legislative session. A composite reading of the six dimensions confirms that the compliance engineering challenge facing a national business in 2026 is materially greater than it was in 2022, and greater still than ITIF's landmark cost projections, published that year, had anticipated. Section 3 takes up that cost question directly.

## 3. The Cost Reality-Check

In January 2022, the Information Technology and Innovation Foundation published the most comprehensive economic analysis of state privacy law compliance costs to that point, projecting that a full fifty-state patchwork would impose between $98 billion and $112 billion per year in out-of-state compliance costs on businesses and an additional $20 billion to $23 billion per year on small businesses alone. [The headline framing](https://itif.org/publications/2022/01/24/50-state-patchwork-privacy-laws-could-cost-1-trillion-more-single-federal/) — that a fifty-state patchwork would cost $1 trillion more over a decade than a single federal law — entered the policy debate as the primary empirical anchor for the federal preemption argument. With nineteen states enacted, enforcement accelerating, and the 119th Congress carrying its own privacy framework discussions, a structured reality-check against those projections is overdue.

---

### A. The ITIF Model Revisited

[The ITIF model](https://www2.itif.org/2022-state-privacy-laws.pdf) rested on three core methodological choices that require updating against 2025–2026 evidence.

First, the model used California as the economic anchor — estimated to impose approximately $78 billion per year on out-of-state businesses — then scaled across a hypothetical fifty-state landscape using a composite Privacy Restrictiveness Linkage index weighting data-intensive industries more heavily. Second, it assumed full fifty-state enactment as its baseline: a reasonable scenario in early 2022, but one that has not materialized with only nineteen states enacted and no new comprehensive laws in 2025. Aggregate absolute costs are therefore substantially lower than projected — though not necessarily per-firm costs. Third, it calibrated enforcement aggressiveness to the pre-CPPA, AG-only reactive model — an assumption that is no longer valid for the state anchoring the entire estimate.

Each of these choices was defensible in 2022. None reflects the 2026 enforcement environment.

---

### B. Where Projections Track

For larger enterprises, ITIF's cost projections have broadly tracked realized compliance experience. [IAPP's 2024 Privacy Governance Report](https://iapp.org/resources/article/privacy-governance-report) finds that budget adequacy is strongly correlated with actual privacy spend and that organizations with below-median budgets overwhelmingly report compliance confidence deficits — suggesting the cost pressure is real, not over-reported. [NBER Working Paper 32146](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32146), studying GDPR firm-level responses, found compliance costs ranging from $1.7 million for SMBs to $70 million for large firms, with smaller firms experiencing significantly higher per-dollar cost increases — a heterogeneity pattern structurally consistent with ITIF's small-business burden finding.

Where projections most demonstrably underestimated costs is California enforcement. The CPPA's [2025 Annual Report](https://cppa.ca.gov/pdf/2025_annual_report.pdf) documents 8,265 consumer complaints from July 2023 through September 2025 (approximately 150 per week), hundreds of ongoing investigations, and enforcement actions including the $1.35 million penalty against Tractor Supply Company and the $375,703 settlement with Ford Motor Company for "unnecessary friction" in opt-out request processing — a novel liability theory that AG-only regimes have not attempted. ITIF's 2022 model, calibrated to the pre-CPPA enforcement environment, could not have incorporated this escalating cost multiplier.

---

### C. Where Projections Diverge — and Why It Matters

The aggregate cost picture requires more careful disaggregation than ITIF's headline number invites. With nineteen states enacted instead of fifty, and no new comprehensive state privacy laws passed in 2025, total absolute compliance expenditure across the economy is substantially below ITIF's projected ceiling. That revision should be stated plainly: the $1 trillion over a decade figure assumes a fully saturated legislative landscape that has not arrived.

The correction in absolute terms does not reach the per-firm burden — and the distinction matters enormously for policy analysis. [NBER Working Paper 30705](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w30705/w30705.pdf) on the economics of digital privacy documents that privacy compliance costs do not scale proportionally with firm size or consumer volume. The majority of compliance infrastructure — data mapping, consent management platforms, privacy notice frameworks, vendor due diligence programs, employee training — is semi-fixed overhead. A business processing personal data across eleven states does not incur half the compliance cost of a business processing data across all nineteen enacted states; the overhead of maintaining eleven jurisdiction-specific programs is structurally close to the overhead of maintaining nineteen, because the marginal cost of adding each additional state layer is lower than the fixed cost of building the cross-jurisdictional compliance infrastructure in the first place.

This semi-fixed cost structure means that per-firm burden does not attenuate as the number of enacted states falls below fifty. A mid-market e-commerce business with customers in fifteen of the nineteen enacted states does not benefit proportionally from the absence of a sixteenth state law; it has already incurred the fixed cost of the compliance architecture. ITIF's absolute projections warrant downward revision for the incomplete legislative landscape. ITIF's per-firm burden findings for small and mid-market businesses do not. If anything, California's emergence as an active enforcement state that systematically mines for non-compliance concentrates enforcement risk on firms without the resources to maintain jurisdiction-specific programs — precisely the firms the downward absolute cost revision most plausibly understates.

---

### D. The Enforcement Cost Multiplier

The variable ITIF's 2022 model was structurally unable to incorporate is the CPPA's emergence as a proactive, well-resourced, dedicated enforcement agency. The agency's operational posture in 2025–2026 represents a qualitative departure from the complaint-driven enforcement model on which the model's California-anchor estimates were calibrated.

The enforcement cost multiplier operates through two channels. The first is direct: fines, settlements, and remediation costs. The [Tractor Supply enforcement action](https://cppa.ca.gov/announcements/2025/20250930.html) and the [Ford settlement](https://privacy.ca.gov/2026/03/ford-to-change-practices-pay-fine-for-adding-unnecessary-friction-to-opt-out-process/) both involved violations that would not have been surfaced by a reactive, complaint-driven AG investigation. The CPPA's automated website scanning for Global Privacy Control non-compliance effectively converts a passive compliance gap into an active enforcement finding, dramatically shortening the time between a technical violation and regulatory exposure.

The second channel is indirect: the compliance investment required to reduce enforcement risk to an acceptable level. As the CPPA's Audits Division moves toward systematic sector-specific audits — standing up in February 2026 with an initial focus on connected vehicle manufacturers and data broker registration compliance — businesses in targeted sectors face mandatory documentation production, audit response costs, and potential remediation orders independent of whether any enforcement action follows. The compliance cost of a CPPA audit, even a concluded one with no penalty, is material: external counsel coordination, employee time, documentation of programs and technical architectures, and remediation of any gap identified in the process.

Together, these two channels suggest the enforcement cost multiplier for California-exposed businesses is meaningfully higher in 2026 than ITIF's 2022 model assumed — and it is growing rather than stabilizing. The CPPA's budget has expanded with each legislative cycle; its investigative and audit capacity is not at a steady state. CPPA rulemaking activity in 2025 — covering automated decision-making technology, cybersecurity audits, and risk assessments — will add further compliance triggers when those rules take effect.

The Section 3 reality-check yields two distinct conclusions. First, the aggregate cost ceiling is below ITIF's $98–112 billion annual projection because fifty-state enactment has not materialized — a correction that proponents of state-level approaches should cite honestly, rather than allowing the headline number to stand unchallenged. Second, the per-firm burden for businesses operating in the enacted states — particularly California — is at or above ITIF's per-firm projections, with the enforcement cost multiplier providing upward pressure the 2022 model could not have anticipated. The policy implication is the same in either direction: the structurally relevant cost of fragmentation is per-firm burden, not aggregate expenditure — and per-firm burden is not declining.

## 4. The Political Economy of Federal Gridlock

The central puzzle of federal privacy legislation is not ideological. It is structural. The problem has been diagnosed, the legislative text has been drafted, and bipartisan majorities have expressed support — yet no bill has reached the floor of either chamber for a final vote. Understanding why requires mapping not generic partisan dysfunction but the specific, named disagreements that have blocked three consecutive Congresses from passing a law most of their members claim to want.

---

### A. The Bipartisan Paradox

The ADPPA, introduced in 2022 by a bipartisan quartet — Energy and Commerce ranking member Cathy McMorris Rodgers, Commerce Committee Chairman Frank Pallone, Senate Commerce Chairman Roger Wicker, and ranking member Maria Cantwell — passed the House Energy and Commerce Committee by a vote of 53–2 in July 2022. APRA similarly carried bipartisan sponsorship. Neither bill reached a floor vote. The ADPPA was blocked when House Majority Leader Nancy Pelosi declined to schedule floor consideration, citing the objections of the California Democratic caucus and the bill's preemption of California's stronger state law. APRA died in the 118th Congress without a Senate floor vote, stalled on the same fault lines.

As [IAPP's tracking of the politics of federal privacy legislation](https://iapp.org/resources/article/federal-privacy-legislation-tracker/) documents, the pattern across both legislative attempts was identical: early committee momentum, bipartisan co-sponsorship, substantive markup progress — followed by failure to advance. The source of that failure is not a mystery. It is three structural disagreements that have proven more durable than the bipartisan will to legislate.

---

### B. Three Structural Deadlocks

**Deadlock One: The Private Right of Action.** The PRA question is the most litigated negotiating disagreement in federal privacy history — and the most paradoxical. State legislatures have resolved it consistently in one direction while federal negotiations have remained gridlocked in the other. As Section 2D documented, only California provides any private right of action among the nineteen enacted state laws, and even California's PRA is breach-only — no state has authorized private suits for substantive privacy violations. Consumer advocates and Democratic members have nonetheless treated a broad federal PRA as a non-negotiable floor in federal negotiations.

The ADPPA proposed a full federal PRA with a two-year delayed effective date and a small business exemption; business groups and Republican members viewed any PRA as a litigation industry trigger. APRA attempted to thread this needle with a narrowed PRA — limited to certain categories of violations, with enhanced pre-suit notice requirements — but the compromise was insufficient to close the gap. The disconnect between state practice (effectively no PRA) and federal negotiating demands (meaningful PRA) remains one of the most structurally durable deadlocks in the debate.

**Deadlock Two: Preemption Scope.** The second deadlock is less about legal principle than about California. Full federal preemption — a ceiling on state law — has been the Republican and business community position since the ADPPA negotiations. The California Democratic caucus's position has been equally consistent: any federal law must function as a floor, not a ceiling, preserving California's ability to enact more protective measures. This is not an abstract argument about federalism; it is a concrete dispute about whether the CPRA and its successor regulations remain valid. California's consumer privacy infrastructure — the CPRA, the CPPA, the Audits Division, the rulemaking program — represents a multi-year institutional investment that the state's delegation has shown no willingness to subordinate to a federal standard, regardless of how protective that standard is.

As the [Congressional Research Service's analysis of preemption and privacy law](https://crsreports.congress.gov) documents, the full doctrinal landscape of what preemption would and would not reach is complex — but the political disagreement is not primarily doctrinal. It is a straightforward question of whether California accepts a federal ceiling, and the California caucus's answer has been consistently no.

**Deadlock Three: State AG Enforcement Authority.** The third structural deadlock receives less public attention but is equally blocking. States that have enacted privacy laws — and their attorneys general — have a strong institutional interest in concurrent enforcement authority even under a preemptive federal framework. The argument is not unreasonable: federal enforcement capacity is finite, states have existing consumer protection infrastructure, and a federal-only enforcement model creates a resource bottleneck. The FTC's counterargument — that multi-principal enforcement creates inconsistent interpretation, conflicting orders, and jurisdictional uncertainty for businesses attempting to comply — is also not unreasonable. Neither position has yielded.

The ADPPA proposed concurrent state AG authority for a defined subset of state-specific provisions; the FTC enforcement community resisted that structure. APRA incorporated a broader concurrent enforcement mechanism that satisfied neither state AGs (who wanted more) nor businesses (who wanted less). The three-way tension among federal enforcement primacy, state AG authority, and business demand for compliance clarity has not been resolved in any legislative text to date.

---

### C. What Has and Has Not Shifted

Mapping what has changed between the 117th and 119th Congresses matters for assessing whether federal legislation is genuinely closer or simply recurrently discussed.

What has shifted: APRA demonstrated that the PRA deadlock is negotiable in its scope if not in its existence. The APRA draft included a narrower, more targeted PRA than ADPPA — suggesting that business-side opposition to any PRA may be softening at the margins, even if a full walk-back remains untenable for consumer advocates. The 119th Congress House Privacy Working Group, organized in early 2025 under new House leadership, represents continued Republican institutional investment in the preemption-first model and signals that the majority has not abandoned the legislative effort. Republican control of both chambers in the 119th Congress arguably increases the likelihood that a preemption-heavy bill can pass the House — the question is whether it can attract the Senate Democratic support needed for cloture.

What has not shifted: the California caucus position on preemption has shown no meaningful movement between ADPPA and the current Congress. California's CPPA spent the intervening years building exactly the institutional infrastructure — enforcement capacity, audit functions, rulemaking authority — that a preemptive federal law would displace. The institutional stakes for the state have increased, not decreased, making concession on preemption scope costlier politically for California members with each passing session. As the [Brookings Institution's analysis of bridging the federal privacy legislative gaps](https://www.brookings.edu/research/bridging-the-gaps-in-federal-privacy-legislation/) notes, the preemption question requires California to accept a federal ceiling on future state action — and the CPPA's emergence makes that concession harder, not easier, to justify to a California constituency.

The most likely catalyst for recalculation is not a change in political composition but a change in the enforcement cost environment. If the CPPA's audit program and enforcement actions begin to impose compliance costs on California's own business community — the tech industry headquartered in the state that has historically been the loudest voice for CPRA preservation — the political calculus inside the California caucus may shift. The Ford enforcement action for opt-out friction, the Tractor Supply penalty, and the connected vehicle audit initiative all suggest the CPPA is expanding its enforcement footprint into sectors and practices that affect California-based companies as much as out-of-state ones. That is the signal most worth watching in the 119th Congress.

## 5. Second-Best Alternatives: A Feasibility Ranking

Section 4's analysis yielded a sobering but specific conclusion: the political obstacles to federal privacy legislation are structural, not generic. Three deadlocks — PRA scope, preemption ceiling, and multi-principal enforcement architecture — have blocked three consecutive Congresses. With the structural deadlocks intact and the 119th Congress operating under Republican unified government, the relevant question shifts from "what would the ideal federal law look like?" to "what can actually happen, and what does each option achieve?"

This section assesses three alternatives in order of their structural capacity to address the fragmentation problem documented in Sections 2 and 3. They are ranked explicitly on feasibility: highest-feasibility, most structurally sufficient options first; stopgaps last.

---

### A. Comprehensive Federal Framework Revival

**What it achieves.** A comprehensive federal privacy framework with preemptive effect is the only alternative that structurally resolves the fragmentation documented in Section 2. The [SECURE Data Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/8413) (H.R. 8413), introduced on April 22, 2026 by Rep. John Joyce (R-PA) as output of the House Privacy Working Group established by Energy and Commerce Chairman Brett Guthrie in February 2025, is the live 119th Congress vehicle. The bill's architecture maps closely onto the minimum viable deal structure this paper identifies: full preemption of state law, FTC primary enforcement with concurrent state AG authority, and no private right of action. Consumer rights are standardized at a national level; sensitive data categories are harmonized; the enforcement structure directly addresses Deadlock Three from Section 4 by preserving state AG roles without creating an unmanaged multi-principal enforcement environment.

**What it leaves unresolved.** The Senate viability question is the same one that killed ADPPA and APRA. Sixty votes are required to overcome a filibuster, which means Senate Democratic support — and a California Democratic caucus that has blocked two predecessor bills over preemption. The SECURE Data Act contains full preemption with no floor for state law; the California caucus has not indicated any shift. The PRA question may be less obstructive in the 119th Congress than historically: the SECURE Data Act contains no PRA — consistent with the state-level consensus documented in Section 2D — and Republican unified control reduces the leverage that consumer advocates previously had to demand PRA inclusion.

**Political feasibility.** Not achievable in the 119th Congress absent California caucus recalculation on preemption. Achievable in the 120th Congress if CPPA enforcement escalation into California-headquartered technology companies accelerates the domestic political cost calculation inside the state delegation. The CPPA's connected vehicle audit initiative and automated GPC scanning represent the most plausible near-term catalysts — the Ford enforcement action is a preview of what happens when California's own automotive and technology industries become enforcement targets at the same rate as out-of-state retailers.

---

### B. State Reciprocity Compacts

**What it achieves.** The Uniform Law Commission's [Uniform Personal Data Protection Act](https://www.uniformlaws.org/committees/community-home?CommunityKey=a1a9b9a9-2bbe-4591-9b7a-43be4aa1b8f8) (UPDPA), finalized in July 2021, provides a model legislative text that could theoretically serve as the basis for mutual-recognition compacts among structurally similar states. For businesses operating in the convergent cluster — Virginia, Indiana, Iowa, Kentucky, Tennessee, Utah — a reciprocity arrangement would reduce the need for jurisdiction-specific program variants and consolidate compliance documentation.

**What it leaves unresolved.** The structural limitation is categorical: it cannot include the states where the compliance burden is highest. California and Minnesota — the two states with the most divergent regulatory architectures, the most aggressive enforcement postures, and the broadest consumer rights portfolios — are also the states with the largest consumer populations and greatest commercial volume. Neither the CPRA nor Minnesota's Consumer Data Privacy Act is structurally compatible with a UPDPA-based compact. California alone anchors ITIF's $78 billion annual cost estimate; excluding California from any compact means excluding the state that makes fragmentation expensive.

**Political feasibility.** Moderate among structurally convergent smaller states — but operationally marginal as a solution to the fragmentation this paper diagnoses. The ULC's UPDPA has been available since 2021 and has produced no reciprocity compacts in five years. States have enacted privacy laws through their own legislative processes, not through model-act adoption — a pattern fully consistent with Section 4's finding that states have continued to innovate jurisdictionally rather than converge. A compact that excludes California is not a solution to the problem the nineteen-state audit documents.

---

### C. FTC Rulemaking Under Magnuson-Moss

**What it achieves.** The Federal Trade Commission has broad authority under Section 5 of the FTC Act to prohibit unfair or deceptive acts and practices, and supplementary rulemaking authority under the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act to promulgate substantive consumer protection rules with the force of law. In principle, the Commission could use these authorities to establish baseline data minimization requirements, mandate standardized privacy notices, prohibit certain categories of sensitive data processing without consent, and impose civil penalty authority on violators — without waiting for Congress.

A comprehensive FTC rulemaking under Magnuson-Moss would establish a national floor: businesses subject to FTC jurisdiction would face a consistent federal standard for certain baseline privacy practices. For businesses currently managing eleven effective state privacy law programs simultaneously, a federal rule that clarifies opt-out requirements, standardizes privacy notice obligations, and establishes a clear sensitive data consent framework would reduce the worst jurisdictional inconsistencies.

**What it leaves structurally unresolved.** The FTC's rulemaking authority under Magnuson-Moss is procedurally burdensome in ways that fundamentally limit its utility as a fragmentation fix. As the [American Action Forum's analysis of Magnuson-Moss rulemaking](https://www.americanactionforum.org/research/a-primer-on-magnuson-moss-rulemaking/) documents, the procedural architecture — notice of proposed rulemaking, advance notice, informal hearings, written submissions, oral presentations, cross-examination of witnesses, and a substantial evidence standard on judicial review — was designed in 1975 and courts have used it consistently to overturn FTC rules that fail to clear its demanding threshold. The timeline from initiation to final rule is typically three to five years; the FTC's commercial surveillance rulemaking, initiated in 2022, has not produced a final rule.

The deeper structural limitation is jurisdictional: FTC rulemaking cannot preempt state law. The FTC Act's savings clause and the general presumption against federal agency preemption of state consumer protection regimes mean that an FTC privacy rule would layer a federal standard on top of existing state laws rather than displacing them. The nineteen-state patchwork documented in Section 2 would remain entirely intact; businesses would face an additional federal compliance obligation without any reduction in their state-by-state program requirements. This is not a technicality. It is the central reason why FTC rulemaking cannot substitute for legislation: the fragmentation problem is a preemption problem, and the FTC lacks the authority to preempt. Congress would need to delegate that authority explicitly — at which point Congress has, in effect, legislated.

Congress also retains override authority over any FTC rule through appropriations riders, Congressional Review Act resolutions, or direct statutory override — a political vulnerability that makes any FTC privacy rule a target for reversal rather than a durable institutional settlement.

**Political feasibility.** High to initiate; structurally insufficient to resolve. A rulemaking that takes three to five years, cannot preempt state law, faces enhanced judicial review, and can be overridden by Congress at any time is not a solution to the fragmentation documented in Section 2. It is a stopgap — one that may reduce the most visible inconsistencies in privacy notice requirements and data sale opt-outs, but that leaves untouched the scope threshold divergence, the enforcement asymmetry between California and other states, the sensitive data definitional conflicts, and the exemption architecture gaps that constitute the structural core of the patchwork problem.

This option ranks third among the three assessed — not because the FTC lacks enforcement capacity, but because the structural problem this paper diagnoses is a preemption problem that the FTC cannot solve. Framing FTC rulemaking as a substitute for legislation mislocates the constraint. The constraint is Congress's failure to act, not the FTC's failure to fill the gap.

---

## 6. Conclusion

This paper set out to do something the preemption debate has needed for four years: ground the argument in measurement rather than projection, and be honest about what the measurement shows.

The six-dimension structural audit in Section 2 confirms that fragmentation is real and directionally accelerating. Nineteen enacted state laws diverge from a federal baseline — and from each other — in ways that are structurally concentrated in the fault lines that resist market solutions: scope thresholds, consumer rights portfolios, enforcement architectures, private rights of action, sensitive data definitions, and exemption structures. The trajectory is not toward convergence. States that enacted laws in 2024 and 2025 did not harmonize with existing frameworks; they innovated jurisdictionally, adding neural data categories, employment profiling opt-outs, and geolocation sale prohibitions that no other state had enacted. The patchwork is dynamic, not static, and it is widening.

The cost reality-check in Section 3 requires two distinct conclusions — and intellectual honesty about both. ITIF's aggregate cost ceiling warrants downward revision: fifty-state enactment has not materialized, and nineteen enacted laws impose substantially lower total absolute compliance expenditure than the $98–112 billion annual projection assumed. Proponents of federal preemption should not cite the headline number without this qualification. But the per-firm burden finding does not warrant revision downward — and may warrant upward revision. Privacy compliance infrastructure is semi-fixed overhead; per-firm costs do not attenuate proportionally as the number of enacted states falls below fifty. The enforcement cost multiplier that the CPPA's emergence has added to the California-anchor estimate — proactive scanning, novel liability theories, an active Audits Division with sector-specific focus — was not incorporated in ITIF's 2022 model. Per-firm burden for California-exposed businesses is at or above ITIF's projections, not below.

The political economy map in Section 4 is sobering but specific: three structural deadlocks — PRA scope, preemption ceiling, multi-principal enforcement — have blocked three consecutive Congresses, and the structural conditions have not materially shifted between the ADPPA and the SECURE Data Act. The California caucus has not moved on preemption. What has changed is the enforcement environment: the CPPA is now an active regulatory institution with a growing audit program, and its enforcement footprint is expanding into sectors and companies headquartered in California. That is the political signal most worth watching. If CPPA enforcement actions against California-based automotive and technology companies accelerate — as the connected vehicle audit initiative and the Ford opt-out friction settlement suggest they might — the domestic political cost of CPRA preservation inside the California delegation will increase in ways that congressional abstraction about federalism has not.

### Policy Recommendation

The 119th Congress House Privacy Working Group should organize its process around the minimum viable deal structure identified in Section 5A: full preemption with concurrent state AG enforcement authority, FTC primary enforcement, a consumer rights portfolio standardized at a national level, no private right of action, and a sensitive data framework that harmonizes the definitional conflicts the Section 2 audit documents. The SECURE Data Act's architecture is close to this structure. The Senate viability question — sixty votes for cloture — remains the binding constraint, and the California caucus calculation on preemption is the variable most likely to determine whether it is resolvable in this Congress or the next.

Second-best alternatives cannot substitute for that legislation. State reciprocity compacts exclude California and Minnesota — the states that make fragmentation expensive. FTC rulemaking under Magnuson-Moss cannot preempt state law, faces a three-to-five year timeline, and produces a rule that sits on top of the nineteen-state patchwork rather than displacing it. Both options reduce friction at the margins. Neither resolves the structural fragmentation that the Section 2 audit measures.

The empirical case for federal action is not weaker in 2026 than it was in 2022. It is more specific, more measurable, and — with the CPPA's enforcement escalation creating new political pressure inside California — more actionable. The patchwork has arrived. The cost is real. The deadlocks are named. The path is narrow but it exists.
