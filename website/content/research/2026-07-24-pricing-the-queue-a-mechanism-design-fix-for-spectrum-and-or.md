## Abstract

U.S. spectrum policy treats scarcity as a fixed physical constraint to be rationed through auctions and orbital-slot registration. This paper argues that framing is incomplete: a meaningful share of what looks like spectrum and orbital scarcity is actually a *coordination failure* — an unpriced congestion externality in orbital allocation, and a classic multilateral holdout problem in terrestrial incumbent relocation — that the current first-come-registration-plus-auction regime fails to solve efficiently. Drawing on recent orbital-economics literature (Doyle 2026; Rao, Burgess & Kaffine, *PNAS*), the empirical record of AWS-3 and C-band mid-band relocation, and the mechanism-design literature on dominant assurance contracts (DACs), the paper shows that incumbents can use registration priority and holdout leverage to raise coordination costs independent of physical scarcity, and that the FCC's own accelerated-relocation payments function as holdout premiums rather than efficient cost reimbursement. It proposes two mechanism-design fixes on different clocks: a DAC pilot for terrestrial relocation, actionable now inside the existing OBBBA spectrum pipeline, and congestion-based orbital pricing, which requires plurilateral coalition-building with the EU and Japan ahead of WRC-27 rather than unilateral U.S. action. A comparative survey of Ofcom's spectrum-trading regime, the EU's centralized Mobile Satellite Services proposal, and China's use-it-or-lose-it ITU filing strategy shows both that market-based reallocation has real institutional precedent and that the entrenchment dynamic this paper identifies is already being exploited at scale. The paper closes with four sequenced, legislatively scoped recommendations and an explicit accounting of what remains provisional.

## 1. Introduction: Two Scarcities, One Regime

Spectrum policy in the United States runs on a simple mental model: spectrum is finite, so government must decide who gets to use it, and the two tools for doing that are auctions (for exclusive-use terrestrial licenses) and orbital-slot registration (for satellite operators, coordinated internationally through the [International Telecommunication Union](https://www.itu.int/en/ITU-R/space/Pages/default.aspx)). Both tools treat scarcity as a fixed physical constraint — a stock of usable frequency-orbit combinations to be rationed. The policy debate that follows is almost entirely about rationing mechanics: reserve prices, set-asides, relocation payments, filing priority.

That model is incomplete, and the gap matters more now than it has in a decade. The current U.S. spectrum pipeline — the multi-year inventory and auction schedule mandated by the [One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/1/text) and now working through [NTIA's spectrum pipeline planning process](https://www.ntia.gov/issues/spectrum-management), alongside the FCC's [upper C-band auction proceeding](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DOC-422694A1.pdf) — is not slow because there is too little spectrum. It is slow because clearing incumbents, whether federal agencies vacating mid-band frequencies or satellite operators contesting orbital priority, is a coordination problem with no mechanism to solve it cheaply. ThinkBot has covered the surface symptoms of this in two prior pieces: our analysis of the [FCC's satellite spectrum modernization vote](https://thinkbot.ai/articles/2026-04-26-the-fcc-s-satellite-spectrum-vote-is-a-rare-win-washington-s) argued that replacing rigid equivalent power-flux-density limits with performance-based interference thresholds was a rare regulatory win, and our piece on [the AWS-3/upper C-band pipeline test](https://thinkbot.ai/articles/2026-05-03-the-spectrum-pipeline-test-can-the-fcc-hit-its-aws-3-deadlin) flagged that OBBBA's statutory deadlines were colliding with the practical reality of incumbent relocation timelines. Both pieces described the friction. Neither asked why the friction exists at the mechanism-design level. This paper does.

The central claim is this: a meaningful share of what looks like spectrum scarcity is actually a *coordination failure* — specifically, a combination of unpriced congestion externalities (in the orbital case) and classic holdout dynamics (in the terrestrial case), both of which the current first-come-registration-plus-auction regime fails to solve efficiently. Incumbents are not merely occupying scarce capacity; in the orbital context, they are able to use the registration-priority structure itself to raise the coordination cost borne by new entrants, entrenching position independent of the physical scarcity of orbital slots. This is not a story about market power or antitrust remedy — it is a pricing and mechanism-design failure, and the fix lies in redesigning the *allocation mechanism*, not in restructuring *who holds licenses*.

If that claim is right, the policy implication is sharper than "auction faster" or "clear more spectrum." It is that mechanism design — specifically, dominant assurance contracts (DACs) for terrestrial relocation and congestion-based pricing for orbital-slot allocation — can outperform the status quo on the metric that actually matters: time-to-clear and total coordination cost, for both incumbents and entrants.

The paper builds this argument across six analytical sections and a conclusion. Sections 2 through 7 do the analytical work in sequence — orbital congestion, terrestrial holdout, the DAC mechanism, orbital congestion pricing, comparative regimes, and a synthesized policy framework — and Section 8 concludes. Specifically: Section 2 documents the orbital congestion mechanism, drawing on recent empirical work showing collision-risk and coordination costs scale disproportionately against new entrants. Section 3 turns to the terrestrial analogue, using the AWS-3 and C-band relocation record as an empirical baseline for holdout cost. Section 4 introduces dominant assurance contracts as a mechanism-design fix for terrestrial holdout, including a direct treatment of their limitations. Section 5 extends congestion-pricing logic to orbital allocation and confronts the harder problem of multilateral ITU coordination. Section 6 surveys comparative regimes — the UK's Ofcom spectrum-trading model in particular — to show that market-based reallocation already has real-world precedent, even if no jurisdiction has yet implemented DAC-style conditional commitments specifically. Section 7 synthesizes these into a hybrid reallocation framework scoped to what is legislatively actionable within the existing OBBBA pipeline architecture, and Section 8 concludes.

## 2. How Orbital Congestion Functions as an Entrenchment Mechanism

The clearest empirical treatment of orbital congestion as an economic distortion — rather than a physical inevitability — comes from Chris Doyle's "[Orbital congestion and satellite broadband competition: Oligopoly, innovation, and second-best regulation](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0308596126000480)" (DOI: [10.1016/j.telpol.2026.103198](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.telpol.2026.103198)), published in *Telecommunications Policy* (Vol. 50, Issue 6, 2026). Doyle builds a two-stage model in which satellite operators first choose constellation size and R&D investment, then compete in quantities subject to binding capacity constraints imposed by collision risk. As characterized in the paper's own abstract, the analysis models LEO broadband as a capacity-constrained infrastructure market with an orbital congestion externality, introducing oligopolistic competition with endogenous innovation in contrast to prior orbital-debris models that assume purely competitive operators; the paper's central welfare result is that congestion-management instruments such as orbital-use fees can improve welfare even in concentrated markets but cannot eliminate the inefficiencies that arise specifically from market power interacting with the congestion externality. The mechanism-level implication this paper draws on — that operators are privately exposed to only a fraction of the congestion damage their deployment decisions impose on the system as a whole — follows from that capacity-constrained oligopoly framework: aggregate orbital congestion risk scales with total deployment across all operators, but each operator's cost function only prices the marginal collision risk it internalizes, not the risk it exports to everyone else sharing the same orbital shell. That is a textbook unpriced externality, structurally identical to a factory dumping effluent into a shared river: the deployment decision that is privately optimal for an incumbent constellation operator is not the deployment decision that minimizes total system-wide collision risk. (Readers seeking the full derivation should consult the paywalled article directly; the summary here is drawn from the publicly available abstract and Elsevier's indexed summary rather than a page-level citation into the full text.)

The distortion has a second dimension that matters more for market structure than for orbital safety per se. Doyle's framework shows regulatory interventions shifting innovation toward higher-throughput satellite architectures rather than larger constellations — implying that R&D investment interacts with congestion risk through its effect on throughput per satellite, creating a substitution margin between "more satellites" and "smarter satellites." An incumbent with an established constellation and sunk R&D can extract more capacity per unit of congestion risk than a new entrant building a network from scratch — meaning the entrant faces a strictly worse congestion-to-capacity tradeoff than the incumbent it is trying to compete against, independent of any deliberate strategic behavior by the incumbent. Congestion cost is not neutral across firm age on this reading; it is regressive against newcomers by construction.

A companion 2026 *Telecommunications Policy* paper, "[OrbitShare: The policy imperative for satellite internet mega-constellation sharing](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0308596126000479)," identifies the mechanism by which this asymmetry compounds through the ITU's coordination process specifically. Under the ITU's radio regulations, new satellite network filings are coordinated on a "[first-come, first-served](https://www.unoosa.org/documents/pdf/copuos/stsc/2022/40_ITU_-_First_Come_First_Served_Concept_to_Access_spectrum-orbit.pdf)" basis: a newcomer's filing does not itself confer priority, but the newcomer must seek interference-avoidance agreement from every earlier-filed network with which it might conflict. The number of counterparties an entrant must coordinate with rises directly with how densely an orbital shell is already occupied — meaning the coordination *cost* of entry increases with each additional incumbent filing, even before any single incumbent takes an adversarial position. OrbitShare's authors describe this as a "collaboration dilemma": full multilateral coordination is globally efficient, but for a dominant incumbent already holding priority filings, it is privately unattractive relative to continuing to file preemptively into the remaining shell capacity, and the transaction costs of coordinating "disproportionately burden smaller operators" who lack the legal and engineering staff to manage dozens of simultaneous bilateral negotiations. The [FCC's international satellite coordination process](https://www.fcc.gov/space/international-satellite-coordination) is the U.S. administration's downstream implementation of exactly this ITU framework, meaning the entrenchment dynamic is not a foreign artifact — it runs directly through the domestic licensing queue.

It is worth being precise about what this is and is not. It is not a story about incumbents exercising market power in the antitrust sense — no operator is excluding rivals through predatory pricing, exclusive dealing, or a merger that concentrates control. It is a pricing failure: the ITU/FCC filing-priority queue allocates a congestible, shared resource (collision-risk-adjusted orbital capacity) without pricing the congestion externality each additional filing imposes on the system, and the coordination-cost structure that results happens to fall more heavily on new entrants because they must coordinate with more incumbents than any single incumbent must coordinate with them. Doyle's proposed remedies — Pigouvian satellite charges or tradable conjunction-risk permits (tradable rights to a defined share of allowable collision-risk exposure within an orbital shell) — are pricing mechanisms, not structural ones; they leave existing operators' licenses and constellations untouched and instead attach a cost to the externality itself. That is the frame this paper adopts for Section 5's policy proposal: the entrenchment effect is real and worth naming, but the fix is to price the congestion, not to break up or cap incumbent constellations. The mechanism-design literature offers a well-developed toolkit for exactly this class of problem, and Section 3 turns to its terrestrial cousin — the holdout problem — before Section 4 introduces the specific instrument (dominant assurance contracts) this paper argues should anchor the near-term remedy.

## 3. The Terrestrial Analogue: Holdout Costs in Mid-Band Relocation

If orbital congestion shows scarcity distorted by an unpriced externality, terrestrial mid-band relocation shows the mirror-image failure: a priced externality — incumbents are explicitly compensated to clear spectrum — that still runs slowly and expensively because the compensation mechanism does not solve the underlying holdout problem. The economics are old and well understood. When a buyer needs several incumbent holdouts to agree to vacate before a resource becomes usable, and each holdout knows the deal cannot close without their individual consent, each has an incentive to withhold agreement until the last possible moment in order to extract a larger share of the surplus. The FCC's relocation regime does not eliminate that incentive; it institutionalizes a payment large enough to overcome it, tranche by tranche, and the price of doing so is now directly observable in two back-to-back band-clearing efforts.

The AWS-3 record is the cleaner illustration because the incumbents are federal agencies rather than commercial operators, which strips out profit-seeking strategic delay and still leaves a slow, expensive clearing process. When [NTIA transmitted its relocation cost estimate to the FCC in 2014](https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/R48534.html), it put the price of clearing the 1695–1710 MHz band at $527.1 million and the 1755–1780 MHz band — the larger, more contested swath — at $4.576 billion, funded through the [Spectrum Relocation Fund](https://www.ntia.gov/category/aws-3-transition) out of auction proceeds, with a statutory requirement that auction revenue cover at least 110 percent of estimated relocation cost before the fund can be tapped. The transition timelines NTIA reported alongside those estimates ranged from a few months to ten years, agency by agency and system by system — not because the physics of vacating a frequency band varies that much, but because each federal incumbent negotiates its own comparable-capability replacement system on its own schedule, with no mechanism forcing simultaneous commitment. More than a decade after that initial estimate, NTIA's own [2023 Commercial Spectrum Enhancement Act annual progress report](https://www.ntia.gov/report/2024/2023-commercial-spectrum-enhancement-act-csea-report/part-iii-1755-1780-mhz-band-aws-3) shows the Department of Defense, the Department of Energy's Bonneville Power Administration, the Department of Homeland Security, the Department of Justice, and two Interior Department components were still drawing on the Spectrum Relocation Fund to complete transitions toward "comparable capability" in the 1755–1780 MHz band, even after having transitioned actual spectrum use — a multi-year, multi-agency tail corroborated independently by [CRS's summary of the fund](https://www.everycrsreport.com/reports/R48534.html). The current upper C-band auction tranche is itself proceeding under a statutory deadline precisely because Congress has learned not to trust incumbent agencies to clear on a voluntary timeline. The reimbursement model prices actual relocation cost reasonably well; it does nothing to price the *option value* an incumbent holds by not committing early, and a decade-long tail is the observable result.

The C-band clearing for 5G, by contrast, shows what happens when the FCC tries to buy its way past holdout directly — and how much that costs. The [FCC's 2020 Report and Order](https://www.fcc.gov/auction/107) estimated satellite operators' actual relocation costs at $3.3 to $5.2 billion, to be reimbursed dollar-for-dollar by Auction 107's winning bidders. But reimbursement alone was not enough to secure an accelerated timeline, so the Commission layered on a separate instrument: [$9.7 billion in accelerated relocation payments](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4177518) to the three incumbent satellite operators — Intelsat, SES, and Eutelsat — conditioned specifically on clearing the lower 300 MHz by December 2021 rather than the default multi-year schedule. That figure is not a cost estimate; it is a premium, roughly double the actual relocation cost, paid purely to convert a slow default-track clearing into a fast one. A [Journal of Information Policy evaluation of the auction](https://scholarlypublishingcollective.org/psup/information-policy/article/doi/10.5325/jinfopoli.13.2023.0003/381377/Evaluating-the-FCC-s-10-Billion-Gamble) frames the $9.7 billion payment explicitly as a bet that a large enough incentive could overcome the coordination problem among three operators — a bet that paid off in the sense that clearing happened on schedule, but only because the FCC was willing to let winning bidders absorb a premium on top of actual cost. Auction 107 raised $81.2 billion in mobile-carrier bids, of which the accelerated payments and relocation reimbursement together consumed roughly $13–15 billion — money that did not buy any additional spectrum capacity, only faster access to capacity that already existed on paper.

The lesson from both cases is not that the FCC mispriced relocation — NTIA's and the Commission's cost estimates were, by most accounts, reasonably close to actual expenditure. The lesson is that *accurate cost reimbursement and holdout-resistant coordination are two different problems*, and the current regime only solves the first one. AWS-3 shows what slow-but-cheap holdout resolution looks like when no acceleration premium is offered: a decade-plus tail with agencies transitioning on their own idiosyncratic schedules. C-band shows what fast-but-expensive holdout resolution looks like when a large acceleration premium is offered: three operators cleared in roughly eighteen months, at a cost of billions of dollars above documented relocation expense, paid to precisely the parties whose delay was the binding constraint — the textbook signature of a holdout premium rather than a cost payment. Neither outcome is what a coordination mechanism designed specifically to solve the holdout problem, rather than to buy around it after the fact, should produce. Section 4 turns to dominant assurance contracts as exactly that kind of mechanism — one that resolves the holdout problem structurally, through a conditional-commitment and threshold-refund design, rather than through the blunt instrument of a nine-figure acceleration bonus.

## 4. Dominant Assurance Contracts as a Coordination Fix

The mechanism-design literature has had a solution to threshold holdout problems on the shelf since 1998. [Alex Tabarrok's original dominant assurance contract](https://mason.gmu.edu/~atabarro/PrivateProvision.pdf) modifies the ordinary assurance contract — pledge money toward a public good, get refunded if the pledge threshold isn't met — by adding one feature: if the threshold fails, contributors don't just get their money back, they get a *bonus* on top, funded by the entrepreneur organizing the contract. That single addition changes the game-theoretic structure entirely. In a plain assurance contract, refusing to contribute and free-riding on others' contributions is a plausible strategy, because a holdout loses nothing by waiting to see whether the project clears without them. In a dominant assurance contract, waiting is dominated: if you contribute and the threshold is met, you get the good; if you contribute and the threshold fails, you get your money back *plus a bonus*; either way, contributing weakly beats not contributing, for every participant, regardless of what anyone else does. Tabarrok's proof that contribution becomes a dominant strategy is why the mechanism carries the name it does, and empirical crowdfunding studies cited in the [Foresight Institute's summary of the mechanism](http://foresight.org/resource/dominant-assurance-contracts-alex-tabarrok-george-mason-university/) find that adding a refund bonus can raise campaign success rates by 50 percent or more, even after accounting for the cost of paying out bonuses on failed campaigns.

The [Information Technology and Innovation Foundation's April 2026 proposal](https://itif.org/publications/2026/04/29/how-to-align-incentives-to-accelerate-spectrum-productivity/) is the first attempt to map this mechanism directly onto FCC spectrum reallocation, and it does so with a concrete, numbered example rather than an abstract sketch: accelerating altimeter retrofits ahead of upper C-band deployment — the same band now moving through the FCC's 2026 auction order. The proposed threshold is $129.7 million, built from $20.2 million in accelerated equipment-design costs (funding roughly 60 additional engineer-years of work) and $109.5 million in manufacturing and supply-chain preparation, estimated at 5 percent of total retrofit cost. Each of the three major mobile carriers would be asked to contribute 34 percent of the threshold — about $43.2 million apiece — a structure chosen specifically so that the contract can only succeed if *all three* carriers commit, replicating the multilateral-holdout structure of the C-band case in Section 3 but this time with a mechanism designed to defeat it rather than buy past it. If the threshold is met, the funds finance the Fast Retrofit Plan and carriers gain accelerated access at the ordinary auction price — no acceleration premium required. If the threshold is not met, no money changes hands; instead, the pledged amounts convert into a 15 percent bidding credit at the eventual slow-track auction, so a contributor's escrowed dollar counts as $1.15 in bidding power. Crucially, the FCC itself functions as the fail-prize guarantor, using auction structure — bidding credits — rather than cash it does not have, to fund the bonus that makes the mechanism dominant-strategy-compatible.

Set against the Section 3 baseline, the contrast is stark on the metric that matters. C-band's $9.7 billion acceleration payment bought roughly eighteen months of speed at a cost of about double the underlying relocation expense, paid unconditionally to three operators regardless of whether their own actions were actually the bottleneck. The upper C-band DAC proposal targets a comparable acceleration goal — full deployment by the end of 2029 — for a threshold two orders of magnitude smaller, $129.7 million against $9.7 billion, precisely because the payment is not a premium extracted through the threat of delay but a financing instrument that only disburses against verified acceleration activity, with the fail-prize costing the FCC nothing but bidding-credit accounting. The incentive logic explains the gap: the accelerated-payment model in Section 3 had to overcome each operator's rational incentive to hold out for a larger share of a fixed pie, and the only way to overcome a strong holdout incentive with a blunt cash instrument is to make the payment large enough that even the most reluctant holdout is bought off. A DAC does not try to out-bid the holdout incentive; it restructures the payoff matrix so that holding out is never the better strategy in the first place, which means the threshold amount only has to cover actual acceleration cost, not a premium sized to overwhelm strategic delay.

None of this is free of real difficulty, and the paper takes the ITIF proposal's own candor about its assumptions as the right standard to hold the mechanism to, rather than treating DACs as a costless substitute for the status quo. Three objections deserve direct treatment. First, valuation asymmetry: the $129.7 million threshold and its component estimates are, as the ITIF report itself concedes, "subject to challenge and revision," and a mispriced threshold either overpays for acceleration or sets a bar carriers won't clear — the closest empirical anchor, Verizon's roughly $172 million payment for a two-year lower C-band acceleration, is a rough cross-check rather than a validated cost model, since auction-specific peculiarities limit comparability across bands. Second, non-monetary holdout motives: DACs solve holdout that is *purely* a bargaining-leverage problem, but an incumbent with an operational, security, or strategic reason to delay — not just a pricing motive — will not be moved by a bonus structured around monetary payoffs, meaning DACs are better suited to the AWS-3/C-band pattern of commercial operators optimizing for compensation than to federal agencies whose delay reflects genuine system-replacement timelines. Third, verification and enforcement cost: the mechanism requires the FCC to define and monitor a Fast Retrofit Plan, determine which contributors forfeit bonds if deadlines slip, and prevent partial completion from triggering a full bidding-credit award — real administrative burden that a pure cash-transfer mechanism like the C-band payment does not carry, and one that depends on carriers trusting FCC enforcement of the DAC's terms, the same trust problem that made multilateral coordination hard in the first place. These are reasons to pilot the mechanism on a bounded, well-specified problem — as Section 7 recommends — rather than reasons to dismiss it; the C-band and AWS-3 baselines already show that the status quo's own transaction costs (a nine-figure premium, or a decade-long tail) are not small either.

## 5. Applying Congestion-Based Pricing to Orbital Allocation

Section 4's fix for terrestrial holdout was domestic and near-term: the FCC can pilot a dominant assurance contract inside the existing upper C-band auction pipeline without asking anyone else's permission. Orbital congestion does not offer that shortcut. But the underlying diagnosis from Section 2 — that ITU first-come registration prices filing order, not the collision risk a filing actually imposes — points to the same class of fix mechanism-design economists have already worked out in detail: replace an administrative queue with a price. The most fully specified version of that idea is Akhil Rao, Matthew Burgess, and Daniel Kaffine's [orbital-use fee model](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7293599/), published in *PNAS*, which treats satellite deployment as a textbook open-access commons problem and derives the fee schedule that internalizes it. Their headline result is not modest: an optimally calibrated per-satellite-year fee — starting around $14,900 in 2020 and rising roughly 14 percent annually to about $235,000 by 2040 — would raise the industry's net present value from roughly $600 billion under open access to as much as $3 trillion, a four-to-sixfold gain, because pricing collision risk induces operators to substitute toward higher-throughput, lower-footprint satellites instead of simply adding hardware to a commons nobody pays to congest. Critically, Rao, Burgess, and Kaffine also model the leading non-price alternative — subsidized debris removal — and find it recovers at most 9.5 percent of the value lost to open access, and can worsen congestion through rebound effects if cheaper cleanup simply invites more launches. The comparison matters for this paper's argument: technological fixes to orbital congestion (debris removal, collision-avoidance automation) address the symptom; pricing the externality addresses the incentive that produces it, which is exactly the distinction Section 2 drew between Doyle's proposed Pigouvian charges and a structural remedy.

The economic logic here has a well-tested terrestrial analogue in aviation, where regulators faced an almost identical problem — a fixed, congestible resource (runway slots) rationed through an administrative queue (the FAA's 1968 High Density Rule) that priced arrival order, not the delay cost a given slot imposed on everyone else using the airport. The [economic literature on slot-based congestion management](https://www.economics.uci.edu/files/docs/thdworkshop/sp08/brueckner.pdf), and the Department of Justice's own position favoring auction-based allocation over administrative grandfathering, both concluded that letting price signal willingness-to-pay allocates scarce access more efficiently than a queue that mostly rewards whoever filed first — the same first-come defect Section 2 identified in ITU orbital filings. The mechanism was not merely theoretical: when Delta and US Airways swapped slot portfolios at LaGuardia and Reagan National in 2011, the [Department of Transportation ran the resulting divestiture as a competitive auction](https://www.transportation.gov/briefing-room/auction-opens-slots-new-york-and-washington-airports) open to carriers holding less than 5 percent of existing slots, converting what had been a purely administrative reassignment into a market-clearing price for scarce landing rights. Orbital allocation lacks a direct equivalent today, but the shape of the fix is visible in domestic reform proposals already on the table: the [International Center for Law & Economics' 2026 satellite-spectrum modernization proposal](https://laweconcenter.org/resources/satellite-spectrum-policy-changes-are-needed/) explicitly recommends replacing first-come-first-served interference protection with tradable shares of allowable interference within a band — an auction-and-secondary-market structure that lets operators who value additional capacity most bid it away from operators who value it least, rather than freezing allocation at whatever the filing queue produced. Layered onto Rao, Burgess, and Kaffine's fee schedule and Doyle's tradable conjunction-risk permits from Section 2, the emerging policy consensus among economists working this problem is convergent even where the specific instrument differs: price the externality, let the market reallocate around the price, and stop treating filing priority as a proxy for social value.

The harder problem, and the one that separates this section from Section 4's domestically actionable DAC pilot, is that orbital slots are not a domestic resource. Collision risk and orbital-shell capacity are transboundary by construction — a satellite launched under FCC authority imposes conjunction risk on operators licensed by every other ITU member state sharing that shell — which means a unilateral U.S. Pigouvian fee only prices the fraction of global congestion cost that U.S.-licensed operators impose on each other, while leaving foreign-licensed constellations free to keep congesting the same shells at the old, unpriced rate. That jurisdictional mismatch is not a hypothetical concern; it is playing out in real time in the ITU's own coordination machinery. The dispute over unauthorized Starlink terminal operations — which Iran escalated to the [ITU Radio Regulations Board and then onto the WRC-27 agenda as Agenda Item 1.5](https://www.techpolicy.press/global-fight-over-who-governs-communications-satellites-heats-up/) — shows that ITU member states are currently fighting over *sovereignty* questions (who authorizes cross-border satellite service) with enough intensity that adding a *pricing* question (who pays for congestion, and how much) to the same forum is unlikely to move quickly. Any credible orbital-pricing proposal has to be designed for that political reality rather than assume WRC-27, convening in Shanghai in [October 2027 with roughly 80 percent of its agenda already devoted to space issues](https://www.spaceintelreport.com/itu-wrc-27-its-agenda-heavily-laden-with-space-issues-risks-setting-aside-key-spectrum-and-space-conduct-policies/), will resolve it as a side item.

The realistic path is coalition-building rather than universal ITU consensus, and there is already a live template for it. [New America's WRC-27 priorities analysis](https://www.newamerica.org/insights/wrc-27-priorities-and-positions/) recommends the U.S. delegation build regional consensus inside CITEL — the Inter-American Telecommunication Commission — before carrying a position into the full ITU conference, on the logic that a bloc arrives with more negotiating weight than a single delegation. Japan and South Korea are running the same play on a different axis: their two regulators held a coordination meeting in June 2026 specifically to align positions on 6G and LEO satellite spectrum bands ahead of WRC-27, rather than waiting to negotiate cold in Shanghai. This paper's recommendation follows the same logic but names a different, more consequential bloc for pricing specifically: the U.S. should pursue plurilateral alignment with the EU's Radio Spectrum Policy Group and Japan's spectrum regulator on a bounded pilot — a Pigouvian charge or tradable-permit regime applied to a single, already-contested orbital shell — ahead of WRC-27, using the same EPFD-reform momentum from the FCC's 2026 satellite modernization vote that ThinkBot covered previously as the credibility anchor. A three-way coalition covering the largest satellite-operator jurisdictions outside China does not bind the full ITU membership, but it prices congestion for the operators generating most of it today, and it gives WRC-27 a working precedent to expand from rather than a blank page to negotiate from scratch. Section 6 turns to comparative regimes to show that market-based reallocation, even short of full DAC- or congestion-pricing adoption, already has real institutional precedent to build that coalition on.

## 6. Comparative International Regimes

If dominant assurance contracts and orbital congestion pricing sound like a leap from administrative allocation, it is worth establishing that the leap from administration to market mechanism has already been made once, in a different but structurally related part of spectrum policy. The UK is the clearest case. Ofcom's spectrum trading and liberalisation framework, first implemented starting in 2004 following the [Cave Review's market-based recommendations](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/siteassets/resources/documents/consultations/uncategorised/9750-spec_trad/associated-documents/sts.pdf), lets licensees transfer or lease spectrum rights and vary the terms of use rather than surrendering unused capacity back to the regulator or sitting on it until a license term expires. Ofcom's own account of the framework frames the logic in exactly the terms this paper has been building toward: liberalisation and trading give firms "greater scope to use spectrum for higher value applications or to sell it in the market to those that can use it better" — a secondary market substituting for continued administrative reassignment. Two decades on, Ofcom is still actively extending the model rather than treating it as settled: a [January 2024 review of the market-based approach to mobile spectrum management](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/spectrum/innovative-use-of-spectrum/review-of-ofcoms-market-based-approach-to-mobile-spectrum-management) and continuing work on [simplifying spectrum trading](https://www.ofcom.org.uk/consultations-and-statements/category-1/simplify) show a regulator that has spent twenty years learning that letting price reallocate spectrum after the initial grant works better than re-running an administrative process every time a license's best use changes. That is the precedent this paper leans on: trading and liberalisation are not congestion pricing or a DAC, but they are proof that a G7 telecom regulator can run exclusive-use spectrum rights through a market layer on an ongoing basis, not just at the moment of initial auction, without the allocation regime collapsing.

The EU's approach to satellite spectrum coordination sits at the opposite end of the spectrum from Ofcom's trading model, and the contrast is instructive precisely because it shows the administrative alternative is still very much alive among peer economies. The European Commission's May 27, 2026 proposal for a new Mobile Satellite Services Regulation — adopted formally as [COM(2026) 311, published on EUR-Lex](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/HTML/?uri=CELEX%3A52026PC0311) and announced via the Commission's own [press release on EU resilience and competitiveness](https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/detail/en/ip_26_1170) and [proposal library entry](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/proposal-eu-level-authorisation-systems-providing-mobile-satellite-services-mss) — replaces the current member-state-by-member-state authorization process for the 2 GHz MSS band with a single, directly applicable EU-level authorization, ahead of the May 2027 expiration of the existing Viasat and EchoStar authorizations. The proposal reserves roughly one-third of the band for governmental, security, and defense use — tied to the EU's IRIS² sovereign constellation — and two-thirds for commercial operators under EU-control eligibility conditions. This is centralization, not marketization: the Commission is solving the coordination problem Section 2 and Section 5 describe — fragmented, slow, multi-jurisdictional authorization — by consolidating decision authority in Brussels rather than by pricing the externality or restructuring incentives to make coordination self-enforcing. It is a rational response to genuine fragmentation costs, and it may well work better than the status quo it replaces, but it is a bet on better administration, not a bet on better mechanism design, and it does nothing to resolve the ITU-level first-come entrenchment dynamic Section 2 identified — it only resolves fragmentation *within* the EU's own authorization layer.

China's ITU filing behavior is the sharpest illustration of what happens when an administrative first-come regime is treated as a resource to be gamed rather than reformed. Chinese entities — led by the newly registered Institute of Radio Spectrum Utilization and Technological Innovation, joined by China Mobile, China Telecom, and other state-linked filers — submitted ITU network filings in late December 2025 covering, according to [SpaceNews's independent reporting](https://spacenews.com/china-files-itu-paperwork-for-megaconstellations-totaling-nearly-200000-satellites/), roughly 200,000 satellites across more than a dozen constellations, with the Institute's own CTC-1 (CHN2025-79441) and CTC-2 (CHN2025-79398) filings alone each claiming 96,714 satellites in 3,660 orbital planes. Under the ITU's "[use it or lose it](https://techblog.comsoc.org/2026/01/13/china-itu-filing-to-put-200k-satellites-in-orbit-fcc-authorizes-7-5k-additional-starlink-leo-satellites/)" deployment milestones — first satellite within seven years, full constellation within fourteen — the filings do not obligate China to build anything close to 200,000 satellites, a figure that dwarfs Starlink's roughly 19,000 approved satellites and quadruples SpaceX's own long-term 50,000-satellite architecture goal, a scale independent analysts note China's own launch infrastructure cannot plausibly support on that timeline. SpaceNews further reports the filings currently have no regulatory standing and have not yet been examined by the ITU — they function, at this stage, purely as claims to priority. The filings function exactly as Section 2's entrenchment logic predicts: paperwork priority claimed now, at near-zero marginal cost, to raise the future coordination burden any other operator — including, eventually, Chinese commercial entrants competing with state-linked filers — must clear before it can deploy into the same orbital shells. China is not circumventing the first-come-first-served regime; it is using the regime as designed, at industrial scale, which is the strongest available evidence that the regime itself, not any single incumbent's bad conduct, is what needs to change.

Read together, these three regimes bracket the paper's argument rather than settle it. Ofcom shows market-based reallocation surviving and expanding over two decades of real-world operation, which is the closest available proof of concept that this paper's DAC and congestion-pricing proposals are extensions of a proven family of instruments rather than a speculative leap. The EU's MSS proposal shows that even sophisticated regulators facing the exact coordination failure this paper diagnoses are still reaching first for centralized administration rather than market mechanism — a reminder that the policy path recommended here is not the path of least resistance internationally. And China's filing strategy shows the cost of leaving the first-come queue unpriced at global scale: a state actor rationally exploiting exactly the entrenchment mechanism Section 2 modeled, using a paperwork claim rather than actual deployment as the entrenchment instrument. No jurisdiction surveyed here has implemented a DAC-style conditional-commitment mechanism for spectrum or applied Pigouvian pricing to orbital slots specifically — this paper's Section 4 and Section 5 proposals remain, to date, novel applications rather than tested policy. But Ofcom's twenty-year trading record establishes that the underlying premise — markets can substitute for administrative allocation in spectrum policy without the sky falling, literally or figuratively — is no longer in serious dispute; what remains open is only how far that substitution should extend.

## 7. Policy Recommendations: A Hybrid Reallocation Framework

The preceding sections point toward two fixes operating on different clocks, and conflating them is the surest way to get neither one enacted. The terrestrial fix is legislatively actionable now, inside a pipeline Congress has already authorized. The orbital fix requires multilateral buy-in this paper has argued should be pursued through coalition-building rather than assumed away. Treating both as a single "reform spectrum policy" ask risks the DAC pilot — which needs no new international coordination and could be running before the next pipeline tranche closes — getting stuck behind the much harder ITU problem. The recommendations below are sequenced deliberately: near-term and domestic first, longer-horizon and multilateral second.

**Recommendation 1: Give the FCC explicit pilot authority to run a dominant assurance contract inside the next spectrum pipeline tranche.** The [OBBBA-mandated inventory](https://www.ntia.gov/issues/spectrum-management) already requires NTIA and the FCC to identify and schedule additional bands for auction on a statutory timeline; nothing in that structure currently permits the Commission to test a conditional-commitment financing mechanism as an *alternative* to the accelerated-payment approach used in C-band. Congress should amend the pipeline's implementing language — or the FCC should seek authority via a targeted rulemaking, as ITIF's proposal contemplates — to permit a bounded DAC pilot on a single, well-specified relocation problem: the altimeter-retrofit case ITIF has already priced at a $129.7 million threshold is the obvious candidate, since it is scoped, quantified, and tied to a deployment deadline (full upper C-band buildout by 2029) the Commission already cares about. The pilot should be capped in size and duration — a single tranche, a two-year window to hit the threshold — specifically so that if the valuation-asymmetry or enforcement-cost objections raised in Section 4 prove more binding than ITIF's estimates suggest, the failure is contained rather than structural.

**Recommendation 2: Require a comparative cost-accounting disclosure alongside any future accelerated-relocation payment.** Section 3 showed that the $9.7 billion C-band acceleration payment was, on the evidence, roughly double documented relocation cost — a holdout premium rather than a cost reimbursement — but that comparison was reconstructable only because GAO and FCC records happened to separate the two figures. Future accelerated-clearing orders should be required to publish, at the time of the order, both the documented relocation-cost estimate and the acceleration premium as separate line items, specifically so that Congress and outside researchers can evaluate whether a DAC-style alternative would have been cheaper before the next multi-billion-dollar premium is authorized. This is a transparency requirement, not a mechanism change, and it is the cheapest of the paper's recommendations to implement — it requires no new authority, only a disclosure rule.

**Recommendation 3: Direct NTIA to conduct a feasibility study on congestion-based orbital-slot pricing, reporting ahead of WRC-27.** Unlike Recommendation 1, this is not a request to implement Pigouvian orbital fees domestically — Section 5 established why unilateral U.S. pricing addresses only a fraction of a transboundary externality. It is a request to do the analytical work now so that a coalition position is ready to propose, rather than improvised, when the U.S. delegation sits down with the EU's Radio Spectrum Policy Group and Japan's regulator ahead of the [October 2027 WRC-27 conference](https://www.spaceintelreport.com/itu-wrc-27-its-agenda-heavily-laden-with-space-issues-risks-setting-aside-key-spectrum-and-space-conduct-policies/). The study should build directly on Rao, Burgess, and Kaffine's fee-schedule methodology and Doyle's tradable-permit alternative, applied to the specific orbital shells where U.S., EU, and Japanese operators already have the densest overlapping filings — the shells where a plurilateral pilot would have the most immediate effect on congestion pricing without requiring the full ITU membership's assent.

**Recommendation 4: Task the State Department and FCC's International Bureau with pursuing CITEL-style regional consensus-building as the vehicle for the plurilateral coalition, not a standalone U.S. proposal.** [New America's WRC-27 analysis](https://www.newamerica.org/insights/wrc-27-priorities-and-positions/) is right that a bloc carries more weight than a single delegation; the U.S. should treat congestion pricing the way it is already learning to treat other WRC-27 positions, by building regional alignment first. This recommendation is process rather than substance, but Section 6's China case shows what happens when a first-come regime goes unpriced and one actor is willing to exploit it at scale — the political cost of moving slowly on this front is not zero.

None of these four steps requires accepting the whole of Sections 4 and 5 on faith. Recommendation 1 is a bounded experiment; Recommendation 2 is disclosure; Recommendations 3 and 4 are preparatory rather than binding. That is the right posture for proposals this paper has been explicit are novel and untested in practice — the goal is to get the mechanism-design toolkit into the pipeline's next iteration, not to mandate its outcome in advance.

## 8. Conclusion

The two scarcities this paper opened with turn out to share a diagnosis. Orbital congestion looks like a physical limit — only so many collision-safe slots in a given shell — but Section 2 showed that the binding constraint is an unpriced externality layered on top of that physical limit: incumbents extract more capacity per unit of collision risk than entrants, and ITU first-come registration converts that asymmetry into a coordination tax that scales with how densely a shell is already filed. Terrestrial mid-band scarcity looks like a supply problem solved by moving incumbents out of the way, but Section 3 showed the AWS-3 and C-band records are holdout-cost case studies: a decade-long tail when no acceleration premium is offered, a nine-figure premium when one is. In both cases, the allocation regime is not failing to identify scarcity correctly — it is failing to price the coordination cost that scarcity generates, and defaulting instead to either slow administrative queuing or expensive ad hoc buyouts.

That reframing is what licenses the paper's central claim: mechanism design, not faster auctions or bigger relocation budgets, is the lever that actually moves time-to-clear and total coordination cost. Section 4's dominant assurance contracts attack terrestrial holdout structurally, by making early commitment a dominant strategy rather than a strategy holdouts have to be bribed into; Section 5's congestion-based orbital pricing attacks the ITU entrenchment dynamic by pricing collision risk instead of rationing filing order. Neither proposal asks regulators to break up incumbents or cap constellations — both leave existing licenses and market positions untouched and instead change the price signal incumbents and entrants face going forward, consistent with the pricing-not-structure frame this paper has held throughout. Section 6's comparative survey shows the premise is not speculative: Ofcom's two-decade trading regime demonstrates a G7 regulator running exclusive-use spectrum through a market layer without incident, even though no jurisdiction has yet paired that precedent with DAC-style conditional commitments or orbital Pigouvian pricing specifically.

The limitations are real and worth restating rather than burying. The ITIF DAC threshold rests on cost estimates its own authors call provisional; the Rao-Burgess-Kaffine fee schedule depends on collision-risk data that is, at present, better modeled than measured; and Section 5's coalition path assumes the U.S., EU, and Japan can align on pricing while WRC-27's agenda is already consumed by sovereignty disputes over satellite authorization. None of these gaps undercuts the diagnosis — they define the next research agenda. A future paper cycle should track whether the FCC's next pipeline tranche adopts pilot DAC authority, whether NTIA's feasibility study on orbital pricing materializes ahead of WRC-27, and whether better conjunction-risk telemetry narrows the valuation uncertainty this paper has flagged rather than resolved.
