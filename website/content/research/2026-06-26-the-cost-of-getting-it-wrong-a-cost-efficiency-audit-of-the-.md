## Abstract

The $42.45 billion Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment (BEAD) program was designed to resolve the most stubborn geography-driven failure in American telecommunications: rural and tribal communities that private capital would not reach at commercially viable prices. Nearly five years after the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act's enactment, BEAD has not reached operational deployment at scale, and approximately $21 billion in allocated funds remains undeployed. This paper conducts a cost-efficiency audit of BEAD's program architecture, comparing per-location federal subsidies embedded in approved state plans against the benchmarks established by the FCC's two prior competitive broadband programs — the Connect America Fund Phase II auction (Auction 903) and the Rural Digital Opportunity Fund (Auction 904). CAF II, with its lower default rate and cleaner realized-cost record, serves as the primary efficiency benchmark; RDOF provides confirmatory evidence of what competitive mechanisms can achieve when properly designed, qualified by the default experience documented in Section 5. The audit finds that BEAD's original design choices — a strong fiber technology preference, a multi-layered administrative compliance overlay, and a sequential multi-milestone plan-review process — systematically inflated per-location costs and delayed deployment. State plans reveal per-location subsidies ranging from roughly $5,000 in accessible rural terrain to more than $40,000 in the most extreme cases, against a competitive program benchmark of $1,000–$2,500. The administrative delay imposed by NTIA's plan-review cycles carries an estimated annual social cost of $4.5 to $7.8 billion in foregone rural economic welfare. The June 2025 BEAD Restructuring Policy Notice partially corrected these failures by replacing the fiber preference with a lowest-cost bid standard and eliminating the compliance overlay, but the restructuring arrived after most delay costs had been locked in and was implemented through administrative rather than statutory action. This paper proposes a five-element framework for a successor program built around technology-neutral reverse auctions, calibrated performance bonds, a statutory NTIA decision clock, tiered deployment requirements by location density, and compliance minimalism with post-deployment outcome verification.

## 1. Introduction: A $42 Billion Bet on the Wrong Architecture

Twenty-four million Americans lack access to broadband internet. The figure is not drawn from the optimistic definitions that once counted a single provider serving one household per census block as universal service, but from the Federal Communications Commission's updated [Broadband Data Collection maps](https://www.fcc.gov/broadbanddata), which use granular location-level fabric data to identify homes and businesses that remain genuinely unserved at 25/3 Mbps. The deficit is concentrated in rural and tribal areas, where the economics of wireline deployment have long been hostile and where the absence of reliable connectivity forecloses telemedicine appointments, remote-work opportunities, precision agriculture, and basic digital participation in the modern economy.

Congress's response was the largest single broadband investment in American history. The 2021 Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA) appropriated [$42.45 billion for the Broadband Equity, Access, and Deployment (BEAD) program](https://broadbandusa.ntia.gov/bead-allocation-methodology), administered by the National Telecommunications and Information Administration (NTIA). The ambition matched the problem: BEAD was designed to reach the hardest-to-serve locations that private investment would never reach on its own and that prior federal programs had left behind.

The question this paper asks is not whether that commitment was justified — it was — but whether BEAD was designed to fulfill it efficiently. The answer, on the evidence now available from four-plus years of program execution, is largely no.

BEAD's original architecture combined three design choices that, taken together, systematically inflated cost-per-location and delayed deployment. First, the program embedded a strong structural preference for fiber construction — the most expensive last-mile technology — at precisely the moment when low-earth orbit satellite providers were demonstrating the ability to serve remote locations at a fraction of the cost. Second, NTIA layered a dense administrative compliance overlay atop the base program requirements: workforce training attestations, diversity and equity certifications, civil rights audits, and contracting preferences for minority- and women-owned businesses — requirements that added overhead without improving deployment outcomes. Third, NTIA's multi-stage plan-review process — Initial Proposal, Final Proposal, NTIA approval — created resubmission cycles that consumed years before construction could begin.

The results are visible in the data. As [Pew Charitable Trusts reported in January 2026](https://www.pewtrusts.org/en/research-and-analysis/articles/2026/01/13/whats-next-for-broadband-expansion), no BEAD-funded construction had reached operational scale nearly four years after enactment. Approximately $21 billion in BEAD funds remained undeployed, [according to Congressional Research Service analysis](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48666). And the per-location cost trajectory in approved state plans — anchored by the extraordinary case of New Mexico's $40,433-per-doorstep fiber proposal — suggested that BEAD was purchasing connectivity at a steep premium compared to what competitive mechanisms have historically achieved.

NTIA's June 2025 BEAD Restructuring Policy Notice partially acknowledged these failures, stripping the compliance overlay and replacing the fiber preference with a lowest-cost bid preference. But the restructuring arrived late, and its implementation introduced new delays. Congress now confronts a live decision about those $21 billion in non-deployed funds and the design of any successor program.

This paper uses BEAD's record to construct a cost-efficiency audit — benchmarking per-location subsidies against CAF II and RDOF, quantifying the administrative burden imposed by plan-review cycles, and estimating the social cost of delayed deployment. It concludes with a framework for a successor program built around the right objective: unserved locations served per dollar, on a timeline that makes the cost of waiting defensible.

## 2. How BEAD Was Built: Architecture, Mandates, and the Fiber Preference

To understand why BEAD costs so much and moves so slowly, one must understand how it was designed — not as a coherent deployment program, but as a legislative negotiation layered with administrative precedent and political priorities that accumulated over the life of the rulemaking.

The IIJA's [Section 60102](https://www.congress.gov/bill/117th-congress/house-bill/3684/text) established BEAD's funding formula: a $100 million baseline allocation per state and territory, with remaining funds distributed proportionally based on unserved and underserved location counts derived from FCC Broadband Data Collection maps. The formula was defensible in its intent — weight resources toward states with the largest coverage deficits — but it created perverse incentives. States with larger unserved populations received larger allocations regardless of whether the per-location cost of serving them was efficient or extreme. Texas and California each received billions; New Mexico received over $500 million despite having one of the highest per-location cost profiles of any state. The formula allocated money based on the problem's size, not on the efficiency of the available solution.

NTIA's [May 2022 Notice of Funding Opportunity (NOFO)](https://broadbandusa.ntia.gov/sites/default/files/2022-05/BEAD%20NOFO.pdf) translated the statutory framework into program requirements — and this is where administrative ambition compounded the problem. The NOFO established a technology preference hierarchy that effectively mandated fiber as the technology of first resort. Under the original NOFO, states were required to give priority to "reliable broadband service" using "end-to-end fiber optic" infrastructure when selecting subgrantees. Fixed wireless was a permitted alternative only when fiber was demonstrably not cost-effective, and low-earth orbit satellite was relegated to the bottom of the preference hierarchy — suitable only for the most remote locations where nothing else would work.

The fiber preference was not an accident. It reflected real performance advantages that fiber holds over other technologies in throughput, latency, and future capacity headroom. But it was written at precisely the wrong moment: 2022 was when SpaceX Starlink was scaling its first-generation satellite constellation, and Amazon's Project Kuiper had not yet launched commercial service. The cost calculus that justified fiber's primacy was already eroding, and the NOFO locked in a preference architecture that would take three years and a formal restructuring to unwind.

Layered atop the technology preference was an administrative compliance overlay. The original BEAD NOFO required states and their subgrantees to demonstrate compliance with federal workforce development standards including registered apprenticeship programs; labor peace agreement provisions for projects of sufficient scale; diversity, equity, and inclusion reporting requirements covering subgrantee hiring practices; civil rights compliance certifications under Title VI and related statutes; and contracting preferences for minority- and women-owned business enterprises. Each requirement individually had a plausible rationale. In aggregate, they transformed what might have been a straightforward infrastructure procurement into a multi-dimensional compliance exercise — one that most rural ISPs, which tend to be small family-owned telephone cooperatives or regional wireless providers, were poorly equipped to navigate.

The procedural architecture amplified the compliance burden. BEAD did not allow states to select subgrantees and begin construction upon receiving their allocation. Instead, [NTIA's Progress Dashboard](https://www.ntia.gov/funding-programs/internet-all/broadband-equity-access-and-deployment-bead-program/progress-dashboard) reflects a multi-milestone review sequence: states first submitted an Initial Proposal demonstrating organizational capacity and planning methodology; then a Final Proposal specifying subgrantee selections, coverage areas, technology choices, and compliance plans; then NTIA review and approval of the Final Proposal before any subgrant awards could be made. Most states experienced at least one resubmission cycle at the Final Proposal stage, as [CRS Report R48666](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48666) documents. Several experienced multiple rounds. The pipeline from NTIA approval of a state's allocation to NTIA approval of that state's Final Proposal consumed, in many cases, more than three years.

The [June 6, 2025 BEAD Restructuring Policy Notice](https://www.ntia.gov/sites/default/files/2025-06/bead-restructuring-policy-notice.pdf) — issued by the Trump-era NTIA under Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick — formally reversed course on both the technology preference and the compliance overlay. As [CostQuest summarized](https://www.costquest.com/resources/articles/overview-new-and-changed-rules-for-ntia-bead-program/), the restructuring replaced the fiber priority with a lowest-cost bid preference subject to minimum performance thresholds (100/20 Mbps download/upload, at or below 100ms latency), eliminated the labor peace and DEI reporting requirements, and streamlined the compliance certification regime. [AEI noted](https://www.aei.org/technology-and-innovation/ntia-streamlines-bead-program-but-risks-further-delays/) that the restructuring was substantively correct but imposed a 90-day resubmission deadline on states to revise their plans under the new rules — a deadline that proved optimistic, and which extended approval timelines further for states that had been close to final approval under the prior framework.

The core lesson of BEAD's design history is that program architecture is cost architecture. Every administrative layer that NTIA added between appropriation and construction — every preference requirement, every certification, every resubmission cycle — was a cost imposed on deployers, passed through to per-location prices, and borne ultimately by the unserved Americans the program was designed to reach. The June 2025 restructuring acknowledged this. The question is whether Congress will design the next program to learn from it, or repeat it.

## 3. Data and Methodology

This paper's quantitative findings rest on three analytical pillars: per-location cost calculations derived from state plan data; competitive program benchmarks from FCC auction records; and social cost estimates derived from the academic broadband economics literature. Each methodology requires explicit statement.

**Per-Location Cost Calculations.** Per-location cost estimates for BEAD state plans are derived by dividing each state's total BEAD allocation (published in [NTIA's allocation methodology documentation](https://broadbandusa.ntia.gov/bead-allocation-methodology)) by the FCC's Broadband Data Collection count of unserved locations within that state eligible for BEAD funding. This approach produces an implied average federal subsidy per targeted location — a measure of cost efficiency comparable across states regardless of terrain or population. The method follows the same construction used by [Wireless Estimator in its New Mexico analysis](https://wirelessestimator.com/articles/2025/new-mexicos-40433-per-doorstep-bead-fiber-should-face-a-tech-neutral-sanity-check/). Important limitations apply: state allocations represent committed funding rather than necessarily expended costs; actual per-location costs in individual subgrant awards may differ from this aggregate implied figure; matching fund contributions from states or private partners are excluded, which understates total public investment per location; and states that serve more locations than their FCC baseline count would show lower implied costs than this method produces.

**CAF II and RDOF Benchmark Data.** Per-location cost figures for the Connect America Fund Phase II auction (Auction 903) are derived directly from [FCC Public Notices and auction results data](https://www.fcc.gov/auction/903) on the FCC's auction archive pages. The CAF II figure ($2,086 over the ten-year support period) is calculated by dividing total awarded support ($1.488 billion) by the number of winning-bid locations (approximately 713,000) as reported in the FCC's auction results. CAF II is the primary efficiency benchmark in this paper because its default rate was substantially lower than RDOF's, meaning its winning bid prices closely approximate the realized cost per actually-served location.

Per-location figures for RDOF (Auction 904) are calculated by dividing tier-specific and winner-specific authorized support amounts by winning-bid location counts, as reported in [FCC DA-21-232A1, the RDOF Phase I Final Results Public Notice](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-21-232A1.pdf). SpaceX's per-location figure ($1,377) is derived from SpaceX's total authorized support of $885.5 million across 642,925 locations in 35 states. Fiber- and cable-based provider figures ($1,156–$2,501) reflect the range from Charter Communications' $1.2 billion award across approximately 1.1 million locations ($1,156 per location) at the low end to LTD Broadband's $1.3 billion across 528,000 locations ($2,501 per location) at the upper end, as documented in [DGTL Infra's analysis of RDOF Phase I winning bidders](https://dgtlinfra.com/rdof-phase-i-winning-bidders/). RDOF figures are presented as evidence of what competitive mechanisms can achieve when properly designed; Section 5 discusses the default experience that qualifies their use as a standalone benchmark.

**Population Figures and Reconciliation.** The 24 million unserved Americans cited in the introduction reflects the FCC's Broadband Data Collection total of locations failing the 25/3 Mbps threshold across all geographic categories, including some suburban and exurban areas. The 19 million figure used in Section 7 reflects BEAD's more specific target population — rural, tribal, and remote locations outside census-designated places and urbanized areas, consistent with NTIA's BEAD eligibility framework and [location-count methodology](https://broadbandusa.ntia.gov/bead-allocation-methodology). The gap of approximately 5 million represents locations that are technically unserved under FCC definitions but fall outside BEAD's rural and tribal priority service footprint.

**Social Cost Methodology.** The annual social cost estimate of $4.5 to $7.8 billion applies employment and income effects documented in the broadband economics literature to the BEAD target population of approximately 6.5 million rural households. The calculation uses the lower-bound employment effect from the *Wired and Working?* study (1.2 percentage point rural employment increase), anchored to [rural median household income of approximately $55,000](https://data.census.gov/table/ACSST5Y2023.S1901) (U.S. Census Bureau, American Community Survey 2023 5-Year Estimates, Table S1901). The resulting per-household annual welfare loss of $700–$1,200 captures only the income effect of employment gains, intentionally excluding property value increases, health outcome improvements, and foregone remote-work opportunity. The aggregate estimate should be read as a conservative lower bound rather than a point estimate.

## 4. The Per-Location Cost Trajectory: What State Plans Reveal

The most direct measure of BEAD's efficiency is also the simplest: how much federal subsidy does the program spend per unserved location that receives service? Assembling that figure from approved state plans, NTIA allocation tables, and FCC unserved-location counts reveals a cost-per-location trajectory that, in case after case, substantially exceeds what prior competitive programs achieved — and in extreme cases, stretches the logic of public investment to its breaking point.

The anchor case is New Mexico. When the state submitted its Final Proposal, [Wireless Estimator's analysis](https://wirelessestimator.com/articles/2025/new-mexicos-40433-per-doorstep-bead-fiber-should-face-a-tech-neutral-sanity-check/) calculated an implied per-location subsidy of $40,433 — derived by dividing the state's BEAD allocation by the number of unserved locations its plan proposed to serve with fiber. The number is not primarily a terrain story. New Mexico is rural and rugged, but comparable western states with similar topography produced substantially lower per-location estimates. The $40,433 figure reflects the compounding effect of fiber mandates, compliance overhead, subgrantee administrative costs, and the premium that accrues when competitive pressure is absent from technology selection.

New Mexico is an outlier, but not uniquely so at the extreme end. At a [February 2026 Senate Appropriations CJS Subcommittee hearing](https://www.appropriations.senate.gov/hearings/a-review-of-broadband-deployment-funding-at-the-department-of-commerce), Senator John Kennedy questioned Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick on why BEAD was spending thousands of dollars per home on fiber when a Starlink subscription could connect the same household for approximately $600 in equipment costs, as [Wireless Estimator reported](https://wirelessestimator.com/articles/2026/senator-kennedy-questions-bead-math-why-not-use-600-satellite-instead-of-4000-fiber-per-home/). His framing was pointed, but the underlying question was legitimate: when low-earth orbit satellite can serve a remote location reliably at a fraction of the fiber construction cost, what is the public-interest justification for the differential?

Across states, per-location cost variation is wide — and the pattern of that variation illuminates the role of program design rather than geography alone. [The Brander Group's October 2025 analysis of BEAD awards by state](https://brandergroup.net/2025/11/bead-broadband-status-awards-by-state-october-2025/) documents allocations and projected coverage figures across states from which per-location costs can be reconstructed. The striking finding is not simply that costs vary, but that states with comparable topographic profiles show meaningfully different per-location cost trajectories depending on their technology mix choices. Montana and Wyoming — both mountainous, sparsely populated western states — show a cost differential traceable in significant part to differing subgrantee technology selections. Midwest states with similar terrain and population densities show analogous divergence: states that incorporated fixed wireless into their plans at meaningful scale produced lower implied per-location costs than states that maintained near-exclusive fiber commitments under the original NOFO preference. The Brander Group's cross-state data, while not presenting a formal regression, is consistent with the inference that technology choice — rather than terrain alone — is the primary driver of BEAD cost variance.

States with genuinely favorable rural terrain — portions of the Midwest and South where flat geography permits efficient fiber strand deployment — show per-location estimates in the $3,000–$7,000 range. States with extreme terrain show estimates in the $10,000–$20,000 range. And the handful of states that leaned heavily on fiber even in ultra-remote terrain, or that experienced multiple plan-resubmission cycles adding administrative overhead, cluster at the $20,000–$40,000 upper bound.

Breaking down the cost drivers illustrates why administrative design choices translate directly into per-location prices. Fiber materials have been volatile since 2020 — pandemic-era supply chain disruptions created shortages that elevated prices through 2023, and infrastructure bill spending across multiple federal programs created a simultaneous demand surge. Labor costs for fiber construction rose sharply in the same period; [NTIA's state plan filings](https://broadbandusa.ntia.gov/funding-programs/broadband-equity-access-and-deployment-bead-program/public-resources-related-bead-plans-and-milestones) show that workforce shortages in rural areas pushed installation wages upward as subgrantees competed for a limited pool of qualified construction crews. Permitting and right-of-way acquisition added further costs and delays; fiber deployment through federal lands, tribal territories, and state transportation corridors requires multi-agency coordination that fixed wireless and LEO satellite installation does not.

The matching fund requirement introduced a further cost dynamic. In states where matching funds came primarily from state appropriations rather than private capital, the matching requirement did not discipline subgrantee bids in the way that genuine private-risk capital would. A subgrantee bidding with state-match money faces weaker incentives to minimize cost than one whose principals have equity at stake.

Taken together, these cost drivers produced a program whose national per-location subsidy trajectory — visible in the cross-state pattern documented by the Brander Group and anchored by the New Mexico outlier — sits substantially above the $1,000–$2,500 range that competitive mechanisms have historically achieved. [Broadband Breakfast's reporting on the $21 billion in non-deployed funds](https://broadbandbreakfast.com/experts-say-21-million-remaining-bead-funds-at-risk-of-going-unused/) captures the paradox: BEAD has allocated vast resources but achieved limited deployment, in part because the cost-per-location architecture leaves too few locations served per dollar spent. The next section establishes the benchmark those costs should be judged against — the record of what competitive reverse auctions have actually produced.

## 5. The Benchmark: What Competitive Mechanisms Have Historically Achieved

To evaluate BEAD's cost performance, one needs a counterfactual: what does federal broadband subsidy spending buy when program architecture disciplines cost rather than inflating it? The FCC's two major competitive broadband programs — the Connect America Fund Phase II auction (CAF II, Auction 903) and the Rural Digital Opportunity Fund (RDOF, Auction 904) — provide that benchmark. CAF II is the cleaner of the two, for reasons this section explains; RDOF is presented as confirmatory evidence, qualified by a default experience that requires honest accounting. Together, their records illuminate the gap between what BEAD costs and what competitive price discovery can achieve.

**CAF II: The Primary Efficiency Benchmark**

[FCC Auction 903](https://www.fcc.gov/auction/903), concluded in August 2018, awarded $1.488 billion in support distributed over ten years to competitive bidders willing to deploy broadband service to unserved locations across 45 states. The auction used a reverse-auction mechanism: providers bid for the right to receive support, with lower bids winning over higher ones, subject to a performance tier and latency classification system that allowed the FCC to weight bids across technology types. Final-round bidding produced awards covering approximately 713,000 locations — implying an average per-location federal subsidy of approximately $2,086 over the ten-year support period, or roughly $209 per location per year.

That figure requires careful framing for comparison to BEAD's upfront capital grants. Because CAF II provided annual ongoing support rather than a one-time construction subsidy, the appropriate comparison basis is the net present value of the ten-year support stream. Even using OMB's preferred 7 percent real discount rate for federal infrastructure programs, the NPV of $209 per year over ten years is approximately $1,468 per location. At a 3 percent alternative rate — which produces a more generous estimate of the support's present value — the NPV rises to approximately $1,783 per location. Either figure sits well below the $5,000–$40,000 range visible in BEAD state plans.

CAF II serves as the primary benchmark in this paper for a straightforward reason: its default rate was low enough that winning bid prices closely approximate the realized cost per actually-served location. Providers that won CAF II support largely built what they bid. The competitive mechanism produced meaningful cost discipline without the default-rate distortion that complicates RDOF's use as a standalone benchmark.

**RDOF: Confirmatory Evidence, with Important Qualifications**

[FCC Auction 904](https://www.fcc.gov/auction/904), concluded in October 2020, was the largest broadband subsidy auction in FCC history — $9.2 billion awarded over ten years to deploy broadband to approximately 5.2 million locations across 49 states. The [RDOF Phase I Final Results](https://docs.fcc.gov/public/attachments/DA-21-232A1.pdf) show that reverse-auction competition drove per-location costs dramatically below BEAD levels: SpaceX won support across 35 states at an average of approximately $1,377 per location; fiber and cable-based winners ranged from roughly $1,156 per location (Charter Communications) to $2,501 per location (LTD Broadband), as documented in [DGTL Infra's analysis of Phase I winning bidders](https://dgtlinfra.com/rdof-phase-i-winning-bidders/). The aggregate result was a program that committed to serving over five million unserved Americans at a weighted average per-location cost well below any BEAD state plan figure.

The RDOF record also carries a cautionary chapter that any honest benchmark analysis must include. As [POTs and PANs documented in its February 2025 default analysis](https://potsandpansbyccg.com/2025/02/21/rdof-defaults/), a significant share of RDOF Phase I winners subsequently defaulted on their build-out commitments. This default experience creates a realized-cost problem: if a substantial share of winners failed to deploy, the winning bid prices do not represent the cost of actually connecting those locations — they represent the cost of bidding to connect them. The realized cost per *actually-served* RDOF location is higher than the aggregate bid data implies, because defaulted locations remain unserved.

Before accepting the bond-calibration remedy this paper recommends, it is worth engaging seriously with the deeper counterargument the RDOF defaults raise: that competitive reverse auctions systematically attract undercapitalized or unrealistically optimistic bidders, and that low bids reflect optimism bias rather than genuine cost efficiency. On this view, the entire competitive program model is flawed, not merely its bond mechanics. The counterargument has real force when applied to RDOF's worst-performing cohort — fixed wireless providers who bid aggressively without securing spectrum or equipment supply chains. But it does not generalize cleanly to the full RDOF record: fiber-based winners defaulted at substantially lower rates than fixed wireless bidders, and SpaceX — the largest single winner — has built out its award obligations at a rate that far exceeds the default cohort. The pattern suggests the failure was bidder-category-specific, not structural to competitive mechanisms. Performance bonds sized to create genuine default deterrence address the overbidding problem without abandoning a mechanism whose successes are documented and large. Pre-qualification requirements could supplement bond requirements as an additional screen, but the [Tech Policy Institute's analysis of reverse auction design](https://techpolicyinstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/Oh-Reverse-Auctions-Lessons-from-BTOP-Jan-2021.pdf) identifies bond calibration as the primary lever available to address overbidding, noting that pre-qualification thresholds impose their own exclusionary costs by filtering out legitimate new entrants who lack track records.

**The Benchmark Conclusion**

The joint record of CAF II (as primary benchmark) and RDOF (as confirmatory evidence, qualified by defaults) establishes a clear cost-efficiency range: well-designed competitive broadband programs produce per-location federal subsidies in the $1,000–$2,500 range, even targeting locations that private capital declines to serve. BEAD's state-plan cost trajectories — anchored by New Mexico's $40,433 outlier and broadly above $5,000 per location in the cross-state pattern — represent not the inescapable cost of reaching hard-to-serve Americans, but the predictable outcome of a program designed around formula allocation, technology mandates, and administrative compliance rather than competitive cost discovery. The gap between those benchmarks is the cost of BEAD's design choices, denominated in unserved locations that could have been connected for the same dollars.

## 6. The Administrative Burden Multiplier: Quantifying Delay

Against a competitive benchmark of $1,000–$2,500 per location, BEAD's cost trajectory is troubling enough. But the cost dimension does not account for BEAD's second major failure mode: time. The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act was signed into law on November 15, 2021. As of spring 2026, no BEAD-funded broadband network has reached operational deployment at scale. The gap between appropriation and construction — now approaching five years — is not primarily a terrain story or a contractor shortage story. It is an administrative story: a sequential accumulation of procedural requirements, each individually defensible, that compounded into a program timeline that defies the urgency of the underlying problem.

Disaggregating that five-year gap reveals where the time actually went. NTIA spent approximately eight months translating the IIJA's statutory language into a Notice of Funding Opportunity — a period consumed by interagency coordination, public comment, and the political negotiation of program requirements that ultimately produced the fiber preference and compliance overlay documented in Section 2. States then required additional time to stand up broadband offices capable of administering the program; many states had no dedicated broadband agency prior to BEAD, and building the institutional capacity to manage billion-dollar infrastructure grants was itself a multi-month undertaking.

The Initial Proposal stage — in which states demonstrated organizational readiness and planning methodology before NTIA would approve their allocations — consumed another six to twelve months for most states. [NTIA's Progress Dashboard](https://www.ntia.gov/funding-programs/internet-all/broadband-equity-access-and-deployment-bead-program/progress-dashboard) shows a wide distribution of Initial Proposal approval dates, with some states receiving approval in mid-2023 and others not until late 2024. The resubmission problem was structural: NTIA's review letters identified deficiencies that states were required to correct before proceeding, and the correction-and-resubmission cycle averaged several additional months per iteration, as [CRS Report R48666](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48666) documents across multiple state timelines. Some states experienced two or three resubmission rounds at the Initial Proposal stage alone.

The Final Proposal stage replicated this pattern at greater scale and complexity. States were now required to specify subgrantee selections, coverage area maps, technology choices, pricing commitments, compliance certifications, and workforce development plans — a document that, for large states, ran to hundreds of pages with extensive supporting appendices. [CRS Report R48666](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48666) documented the review burden: NTIA's staff was simultaneously reviewing dozens of state Final Proposals under no statutory deadline for approval, with no consequence for NTIA if review dragged on for months. The asymmetry — states under time pressure from their own legislatures and stakeholder communities, NTIA under no external deadline — reliably produced delays.

The [June 2025 Restructuring Policy Notice](https://www.ntia.gov/sites/default/files/2025-06/bead-restructuring-policy-notice.pdf) added a fresh delay mechanism of its own. States that had substantially completed their Final Proposals under the original NOFO framework were required to resubmit under the new technology-neutral rules within 90 days. [AEI noted](https://www.aei.org/technology-and-innovation/ntia-streamlines-bead-program-but-risks-further-delays/) that states which had invested years in fiber-centric planning processes faced a genuine dilemma: revise their plans to incorporate LEO and fixed wireless alternatives (extending the timeline further) or submit minimally revised plans that preserved fiber dominance and risked NTIA pushback for failing to demonstrate genuine technology neutrality. California, Illinois, and Oklahoma remained unapproved into spring 2026, as [Broadband Breakfast reported](https://broadbandbreakfast.com/ntia-planning-to-finish-bead-approvals-by-may/), with the restructuring resubmission process adding months to approval timelines that had already stretched past any reasonable definition of urgency.

The concept of an "administrative burden multiplier" makes the delay's cost concrete. Across the approximately 19 million rural Americans that BEAD is designed to reach (see Section 3 for the reconciliation of this figure with the 24 million FCC total), each additional month of procedural delay before construction begins represents roughly 1.58 million person-months of continued unserved status. That is not a rhetorical figure — it is the denominator against which every compliance certification, resubmission cycle, and review delay should be evaluated. When NTIA's Initial Proposal review process consumed, on average, eight additional months beyond what a streamlined review would have required, the program imposed on the order of 12 million person-months of unnecessary unserved time — before a single strand of fiber or satellite dish was deployed.

The burden also falls on state broadband offices directly. [Roll Call's March 2026 coverage](https://rollcall.com/2026/03/12/broadband-program-uncertainty-has-lawmakers-states-on-watch/) documented the strain: state offices managing BEAD's compliance and reporting requirements faced multi-year overhead costs that consumed resources otherwise available for deployment support, technical assistance to rural subgrantees, and post-deployment verification. [Light Reading's 2025 retrospective](https://www.lightreading.com/broadband/2025-in-review-the-bead-goes-on-) captured the institutional exhaustion: program administrators who had spent three years navigating NTIA review cycles were, by late 2025, managing a restructuring that invalidated much of that work and required them to rebuild their plans from different premises.

The administrative burden multiplier is not simply a process critique. It is an economic claim: the overhead costs embedded in BEAD's procedural architecture — measured in state staff-years, consultant fees, subgrantee compliance workload, and foregone deployment — represent a real resource consumption that does not appear in NTIA's per-location subsidy accounting but is nonetheless paid for by American taxpayers and borne by the unserved communities waiting for the connection to arrive.

## 7. Monetizing the Wait: The Social Cost of Delayed Deployment

The preceding section documented the administrative mechanics of BEAD's delay. This section asks what that delay actually costs — not in program accounting terms, but in the economic and social welfare foregone by the approximately 19 million rural Americans who remain unserved while state plans circulate through NTIA review cycles (see Section 3 for the reconciliation of this figure with the 24 million total unserved count — the approximately 5 million gap represents locations that are technically unserved under FCC definitions but fall outside BEAD's rural and tribal priority footprint). The analysis that follows is deliberately conservative: it constructs a lower-bound estimate designed to make the implicit "delay is costless" assumption in program design explicit and falsifiable.

The academic literature on broadband's economic effects has matured substantially over the past decade, moving from correlation studies toward quasi-experimental designs that isolate broadband's causal contribution from confounding economic trends. [A 2025 study published in *Telecommunications Policy*, *Wired and Working? An Evaluation of Broadband Expansion in Rural America*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0308596125001715), examines rural broadband deployment across counties receiving FCC high-cost program funding and finds statistically significant positive effects on both employment rates and household income. The study's preferred estimates imply that rural broadband access increases local employment rates by 1.2 to 2.8 percentage points and household incomes by 3 to 7 percent over a five-year window, with effects that strengthen over time as broadband becomes embedded in local economic activity.

The broader cross-national literature corroborates this magnitude. [Czernich, Falck, Kretschmer, and Woessmann (2011)](https://academic.oup.com/ej/article/121/552/505/5079498), in a landmark study published in *The Economic Journal*, exploit the expansion of DSL infrastructure across OECD countries to estimate that a 10 percentage point increase in broadband penetration raises annual per-capita GDP growth by 0.9 to 1.5 percentage points — a finding consistent with subsequent estimates from [World Bank](https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/handle/10986/23638) and [ITU Broadband Commission](https://www.itu.int/en/ITU-D/Statistics/Documents/publications/broadband/2012/bb-chapter8.pdf) analyses.

The [Fiber Broadband Association's January 2025 analysis](https://fiberbroadband.org/2025/01/28/fiber-broadband-boosts-income-investment-and-business-investment-in-rural-america/) calculates that rural broadband deployment increases property values by approximately 3.1 percent in served areas and correlates with measurable increases in new business formation — effects consistent with the broader literature on infrastructure investment and local economic development.

Translating these estimates into a social cost calculation requires several explicit assumptions, set out fully in Section 3. The BEAD target population is approximately 19 million rural and tribal Americans — concentrated in roughly 6.5 to 7 million unserved rural households. Applying the lower-bound employment effect from the *Wired and Working?* study (1.2 percentage points) and anchoring to [rural median household income of approximately $55,000](https://data.census.gov/table/ACSST5Y2023.S1901) (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2023 5-Year Estimates), each unserved rural household foregoes on the order of $700 to $1,200 in annual income attributable to broadband's absence. Aggregated across 6.5 million unserved households, that implies an annual social cost in the range of $4.5 to $7.8 billion. The uncertainty on this estimate is wide, but the directional conclusion is robust: the annual economic cost of continued unserved status is substantial relative to the program's annual expenditure.

Broadband's social returns extend beyond income and employment effects. [County Health Rankings research on broadband and health outcomes](https://www.countyhealthrankings.org/strategies-and-solutions/what-works-for-health/strategies/broadband-initiatives-for-unserved-and-underserved-areas) documents that unserved rural populations face measurable barriers to telehealth access — barriers that translate into delayed care, higher preventable hospitalization rates, and worse chronic disease management outcomes. Similarly, the remote-work preclusion imposed by broadband absence — particularly salient in a post-pandemic labor market where a substantial share of professional and managerial positions are available on a remote or hybrid basis — represents a labor market participation constraint that is difficult to price precisely but is nonetheless real.

The FCC has implicitly acknowledged these social returns in its universal service program design — the existence of high-cost support programs reflects a regulatory judgment that broadband's positive externalities justify public subsidy in areas where private investment will not reach. What program accounting has not done is make the cost of delay legible in the same terms. A BEAD plan-resubmission cycle that extends the deployment timeline by twelve months — a delay documented for multiple states — imposes on the order of $4.5 to $7.8 billion in foregone social welfare for the population that would have been served in that window. That number should appear in the cost-benefit accounting of program design choices. Currently, it does not.

The purpose of this estimate is not to assign blame but to reframe the policy question. Administrative requirements that add procedural delay to BEAD are not free — they are costly in precisely the dimension that justifies the program's existence. Congress and NTIA have historically treated delay as a neutral outcome of thorough program oversight. The social cost analysis suggests otherwise: every month of unnecessary delay carries a quantifiable economic downside that proper program accounting should register.

## 8. Technology Neutrality: Promise vs. Practice After the June 2025 Restructuring

The [BEAD Restructuring Policy Notice](https://www.ntia.gov/sites/default/files/2025-06/bead-restructuring-policy-notice.pdf) of June 6, 2025, announced a clean break from the original program's fiber preference. The new framework replaced the technology hierarchy with a single standard: states must select the lowest-cost bid capable of meeting minimum performance thresholds — 100/20 Mbps download/upload speeds and latency at or below 100 milliseconds. On paper, low-earth orbit satellite, fixed wireless, and fiber now compete on equal footing. The question this section addresses is whether that on-paper equality produced genuine competitive neutrality in practice — or whether fiber retained structural advantages that the restructuring did not reach.

The post-restructuring technology mix in approved state plans offers the most direct evidence. As [Wireless Estimator's analysis of state plans following the June 2025 changes](https://wirelessestimator.com/articles/2025/tech-neutral-in-name-only-fiber-retains-structural-edge-under-ntias-new-bead-policy/) documents — based on a snapshot of restructured and newly submitted plans compiled through early 2026 — fiber remained the dominant technology selection even after the formal preference was removed: approximately 63 percent of funded locations in restructured or newly submitted state plans are designated for fiber deployment, with LEO satellite accounting for roughly 23 percent, fixed wireless at about 12 percent, and cable at roughly 2 percent. The shift from the original architecture is real — LEO's share increased dramatically from near-zero levels visible in pre-restructuring plans — but fiber's dominance persisted well beyond what pure cost-minimization would predict given the cost differential in genuinely remote terrain.

The cost arithmetic in remote locations is stark. Analysis of NTIA state plan filings — combined with [CostQuest's per-location cost modeling](https://www.costquest.com/resources/articles/overview-new-and-changed-rules-for-ntia-bead-program/) developed in connection with the FCC's high-cost universal service programs — indicates that in terrain where the nearest existing fiber route exceeds five miles from the target location, fiber deployment typically runs between $10,000 and $40,000 per location (a range anchored at the upper bound by the documented New Mexico figure). LEO satellite service, by contrast, can be extended to the same location for a fraction of that cost: [SpaceX's Starlink Standard Kit](https://www.starlink.com/specifications) retails at $599, with professional installation adding $100 to $300. Amazon's Project Kuiper — rebranded as Amazon Leo in November 2025 and entering enterprise beta in April 2026, with commercial availability targeted for mid-2026 — has indicated terminal hardware costs under $400, suggesting a total upfront cost competitive with Starlink, though Amazon has not yet announced public retail pricing. The cost differential between fiber deployment in ultra-remote terrain and LEO satellite installation is large enough to change the public investment calculus fundamentally.

The most substantial technical objection to LEO satellite as a fiber substitute concerns network capacity under congestion. First-generation Starlink satellites operate on Ku-band and Ka-band frequencies with finite beam capacity; in densely subscribed service areas, throughput per subscriber can degrade during peak demand periods — a concern [CRS has documented](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R46896) as a real limiting factor for satellite broadband at scale. Amazon Leo's multi-orbit architecture, deploying satellites across multiple altitude shells to concentrate capacity in high-demand areas, directly addresses the interference and capacity allocation problem that first-generation LEO systems encountered. More important for this policy context, however, is the demand-density point: BEAD-eligible locations are by definition in rural and remote areas with low population density, and the congestion risk is substantially lower in areas with fewer than one household per square mile than in suburban or exurban areas where satellite subscribers compete intensively for beam capacity. In precisely the terrain categories where LEO's cost advantage over fiber is most pronounced — genuinely remote, ultra-low-density locations — the capacity constraint is least likely to bind. The appropriate policy response is post-deployment performance monitoring rather than a pre-deployment technology preference that excludes LEO from competition.

Several structural factors explain the persistence of fiber's dominance even after the formal preference was removed. State broadband offices and their consultants had invested years in fiber-centric planning processes; the institutional and political infrastructure built around fiber-focused subgrantee relationships did not disappear when the NOFO changed. Local government stakeholders — particularly rural electric cooperatives and telephone companies that had positioned themselves as BEAD subgrantees — had a strong interest in fiber awards, which represent long-duration assets and local construction employment, over LEO satellite deployments that are less capital-intensive and create fewer local jobs. These interests are legitimate, but they are interests — not technical findings.

[The Vernonburg Group's analysis of LEO satellite scalability across BEAD-eligible locations](https://www.vernonburggroup.com/blog/what-percentage-of-bead-eligible-locations-can-leo-satellite-providers-serve-at-scale) — conducted by a firm that advises LEO satellite operators and may have commercial interests in satellite-favorable findings, and should be read accordingly as one consulting firm's estimate subject to methodological uncertainty — suggests that a substantial majority of BEAD-eligible locations, potentially 70 to 80 percent, could be served cost-effectively by LEO satellite under current constellation capacities and pricing trajectories. If that estimate is approximately correct, the 63 percent fiber share in restructured state plans reflects something other than pure cost-minimization.

The measured conclusion is that the June 2025 restructuring improved technology neutrality significantly but incompletely. [Comtrend's summary of the policy changes](https://us.comtrend.com/major-bead-policy-changes-june-2025/) correctly characterizes the restructuring as a meaningful course correction. But the structural advantages fiber retains through incumbency, political economy, and state-level planning inertia mean that the lowest-cost bid preference has not, in practice, produced the full competitive discipline its text implies. Achieving genuine technology neutrality requires more than changing the stated preference; it requires auction design that enforces competitive price discovery rather than allowing pre-committed technology choices to set the bid floor.

## 9. The $21 Billion Question: What Congress Should Do Next

The arithmetic of BEAD's deployment record has produced an uncomfortable legislative situation. After more than four years of program administration, approximately $21 billion in BEAD funds remains allocated to states but not yet deployed to subgrantees — funds tied up in plans awaiting final NTIA approval, in states still navigating the restructuring resubmission process, or in allocations where no construction has begun because the planning pipeline has not yet cleared. That figure represents roughly half of BEAD's original appropriation, and it sits at the center of an escalating debate in the 119th Congress about whether the money should be reprogrammed, rescinded, or allowed to flow forward under the restructured program rules.

The rescission case has found its legislative vehicle. As [ABC17 News reported in January 2026](https://abc17news.com/stacker-news/2026/01/16/a-republican-sponsored-bill-would-take-back-21b-appropriated-for-broadband-deployment/), a Republican-sponsored bill introduced by Senators Joni Ernst and Ted Cruz would claw back the undeployed $21 billion, on the argument that BEAD's administrative failures disqualify it from additional patience. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick, testifying before the Senate Commerce Committee, pushed back — assuring senators that the funds would not be rescinded and that the June 2025 restructuring had placed BEAD on a workable path to deployment. Bipartisan resistance at the state level reinforced Lutnick's position: governors and state broadband directors across both parties, who had invested years of institutional effort in their BEAD plans, were unwilling to see the program terminated before construction began.

The rescission debate, while politically salient, is in an important sense the wrong question. Rescinding $21 billion does not connect a single unserved household to broadband — it simply removes the federal commitment. The right question is whether those funds can be redeployed through a program architecture that actually produces efficient outcomes. [Broadband Breakfast's analysis of the non-deployment funds](https://broadbandbreakfast.com/what-to-do-with-remaining-bead-funds-a-k-a-non-deployment/) makes this distinction clearly: the policy choice is not between BEAD and nothing, but between continuing to push capital through a flawed pipeline and restructuring that pipeline around the cost-efficiency principles that prior competitive programs demonstrated were achievable.

Congress has active legislative vehicles to pursue the latter approach. [H.R.6920 and its Senate companion S.3565, the SUCCESS for BEAD Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/6920) — introduced by Representative Capito in the House and Senators Wicker and Capito in the Senate — would authorize remaining non-deployed BEAD funds to be channeled through competitive subgrant processes, creating an opening for technology-neutral and performance-based deployment at scale. The bills have attracted bipartisan co-sponsors, reflecting the political reality that both parties have constituencies — rural agricultural communities, tribal broadband advocates, rural hospital networks — with a direct stake in efficient broadband deployment. Their prospects in a Congress focused on reconciliation and appropriations battles are uncertain, but they represent the most direct available vehicle for institutionalizing competitive cost discipline over the remaining undeployed funds.

Beyond these bills, the [Benton Institute's analysis of the Rural Broadband Protection Act](https://www.benton.org/blog/rural-broadband-protection-act-meets-high-cost-program-crossroads) illustrates the tension between reform advocates and incumbent program stakeholders. The Benton Institute — a broadband access advocacy organization that has consistently argued for robust fiber investment and supported BEAD's original technology-preference regime — frames the debate around long-term infrastructure durability rather than near-term cost efficiency. That framing is not incoherent; fiber's longevity and capacity headroom are real advantages. But it aligns precisely with the institutional interests of wireline incumbents — rural telephone cooperatives, NTCA, NRECA, USTelecom — who stand to lose subgrantee awards if genuine technology neutrality produces a competitive shift toward satellite and fixed wireless deployment. Congress should distinguish between arguments grounded in technical analysis and arguments grounded in program stakeholder interest.

The design criteria for a successor program should emerge directly from the failure modes this paper has documented. A program that spent five years in administrative review before reaching construction must embed enforceable NTIA review timelines — a statutory decision clock, with deemed-approval if the deadline is missed, so that the asymmetry between state urgency and federal review inertia cannot recur. A program that produced $40,000-per-location fiber plans must require competitive price discovery through reverse auction mechanisms that force technology options to compete on cost, rather than allowing pre-committed technology selections to set the bid floor. A program whose defaults (in the RDOF precedent) resulted from inadequate performance bonding must calibrate bond requirements to create genuine financial deterrence against overbidding. And a program whose compliance overlay consumed years of administrative capacity must embrace the restructuring's compliance minimalism as a permanent feature, not a temporary emergency measure.

The $21 billion question, properly framed, is not whether to rescind but how to ensure the next dollar of federal broadband investment reaches an unserved household rather than a state plan approval cycle. That reframing is where the policy design work begins.

## 10. A Framework for a Leaner Successor Program

The preceding nine sections constitute a diagnosis. This section is the prescription. BEAD's record — per-location costs running three to twenty times above competitive program benchmarks, five years of administrative delay before construction scale, a technology mandate that locked out cost-efficient alternatives, and a compliance overlay that consumed state institutional capacity without improving deployment outcomes — provides a clear map of which design choices failed and what the successor program must do differently. What follows is a five-element framework built directly from that failure map.

**Element 1: Technology-Neutral Reverse Auctions**

The foundational design choice for any successor program should be competitive price discovery through reverse auctions that require all eligible technologies to bid against a common performance floor. The [Tech Policy Institute's analysis of reverse auction design for broadband programs](https://techpolicyinstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/03/Oh-Reverse-Auctions-Lessons-from-BTOP-Jan-2021.pdf) is unambiguous on the core principle: subsidy programs that allow technology selection before cost competition produce higher per-location costs than programs that force technology options to compete for the subsidy. The minimum performance threshold — 100/20 Mbps and 100ms latency, as established by the June 2025 restructuring — is a reasonable floor. Any bid meeting that floor should compete solely on cost-per-served-location, with the lowest qualifying bid winning. Fiber, LEO satellite, fixed wireless, and cable should face identical procedural treatment. The competitive mechanism is the technology preference; stated technology hierarchies should not exist.

**Element 2: Performance Bonds Calibrated to Deter Overbidding**

The RDOF default record identified the failure mode that reverse auction design must address: providers bidding aggressively to win support, then defaulting when deployment realities exceed their projections. The solution is not to abandon competitive mechanisms but to price default deterrence appropriately. [FCC's RDOF auction design documentation](https://www.fcc.gov/implementing-rural-digital-opportunity-fund-rdof-auction) shows that Phase I performance bonds were sized at levels insufficient to alter the incentive calculation for marginal bidders. This paper recommends performance bonds of 25 to 35 percent of total award value — a range informed by the Tech Policy Institute's analysis identifying bond calibration as the primary lever for addressing overbidding — forfeited in full upon default with no partial-credit mechanism. A bidder facing forfeiture of a quarter of its award has a materially different incentive to bid only what it can actually build. Bond requirements should also be staged — an initial bond upon award, with additional tranches required as construction milestones approach — to catch financial distress early rather than after years of non-performance.

**Element 3: A Statutory NTIA Decision Clock**

The administrative burden analysis in Section 6 identified the asymmetry at the heart of BEAD's delay problem: states face continuous pressure to advance their plans, while NTIA faces no external deadline for completing reviews. [CRS Report R48666](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48666) documents the predictable result — multi-month review cycles at every milestone stage, with no mechanism to hold the federal reviewer accountable for delay. A successor program must embed a statutory 60-day decision clock for NTIA review of state plan submissions at every stage, with an automatic deemed-approved outcome if NTIA fails to act within the deadline. This provision transfers the cost of federal inaction from unserved communities to the reviewing agency, where it belongs. NTIA would retain full authority to reject noncompliant plans within the deadline; the constraint is that the agency must make a decision, not that it must approve everything.

**Element 4: Tiered Deployment Requirements by Location Density**

Not all unserved locations are economically equivalent, and program design should reflect that reality. A framework that applies wireline deployment expectations to genuinely ultra-remote locations — defined as areas below one household per square mile, or with no existing fiber route within five miles — is committing a category error. A successor program should establish a formal tiered classification: Tier 1 locations (accessible rural, where wireline economics are plausible) subject to standard competitive bidding; Tier 2 locations (remote rural, where satellite and fixed wireless are likely cost-efficient) subject to a separate competitive window with modified performance floors reflecting realistic LEO capacity; and Tier 3 locations (ultra-remote, where any deployment requires extraordinary subsidy) subject to individualized negotiation. [The Vernonburg Group's LEO scalability analysis](https://www.vernonburggroup.com/blog/what-percentage-of-bead-eligible-locations-can-leo-satellite-providers-serve-at-scale) — with the conflict-of-interest caveat noted in Section 8 — provides a starting methodology for the Tier 2 classification, though any final categorization should draw on FCC cost modeling and NTIA's location fabric data. This tiered framework is the most operationally novel element of the five: it directly addresses the category error of applying identical deployment requirements to terrain categories with fundamentally different cost structures.

**Element 5: Compliance Minimalism and Post-Deployment Verification**

BEAD's original compliance overlay — workforce attestations, DEI certifications, civil rights audits, contracting preferences — imposed pre-deployment burden without improving deployment outcomes. The [June 2025 Restructuring Policy Notice](https://www.ntia.gov/sites/default/files/2025-06/bead-restructuring-policy-notice.pdf) eliminated most of it; a successor program should make that elimination permanent and statutory. The sole compliance requirement that matters is verified delivery: did the subgrantee connect the locations it committed to serve, at the performance levels it bid? A single post-deployment buildout verification, conducted by NTIA or a delegated state agency against the FCC's location fabric, replaces the multi-stage attestation regime. Providers that verify deployment receive their full award; providers that fall short face proportional bond forfeiture. Pre-deployment process requirements serve administrators; post-deployment outcome verification serves unserved Americans. A successor program should be designed around the latter.

Taken together, these five elements constitute a program architecture in which cost discipline emerges from competition rather than mandate, delay is constrained by statutory deadline rather than administrative goodwill, technology choice is determined by economics rather than preference hierarchies, the unique cost structure of ultra-remote terrain is recognized through differentiated deployment tiers, and compliance is verified against outcomes rather than process boxes. Each element traces directly to a BEAD failure mode that would recur if the element were absent.

## 11. Conclusion: Serving Locations, Not Satisfying Checklists

There is a metric that BEAD's program administrators track obsessively — state plans submitted, initial proposals approved, final proposals under review, milestones reached — and there is a metric that matters: unserved Americans connected to broadband, per dollar spent, on a timeline that does not outlast the urgency of the problem. BEAD was designed around the first metric and built an architecture that systematically undermined the second.

The evidence assembled in this paper supports a straightforward conclusion. BEAD's original design produced per-location costs running three to twenty times above what competitive reverse auction mechanisms achieved in comparable programs, across a five-year administrative timeline that converted a 2021 appropriation into a 2026 construction ambition. The drivers of that outcome were not primarily geographic or technical — they were architectural. The fiber preference foreclosed cost-efficient alternatives at the moment LEO satellite economics made them viable. The compliance overlay imposed administrative burden on deployers without improving deployment outcomes. The multi-stage plan review created an asymmetric accountability structure in which states bore the cost of federal delay. Each of these choices was individually defensible; their cumulative effect was a program that spent its first four years managing itself rather than connecting people.

The June 2025 BEAD Restructuring Policy Notice was a meaningful course correction, and it deserves credit as such. Technology neutrality — enforced through a lowest-cost bid preference rather than a stated hierarchy — is substantively the right framework. Compliance minimalism is right. Streamlining the plan review process is right. But the restructuring arrived after years of delay had already been locked in, and it was implemented through administrative action rather than statute, which means the next administration or NTIA leadership can restore the original preference regime without congressional action. The corrections need to be codified to be durable.

Congress faces a live decision about $21 billion in non-deployed BEAD funds and the design of any successor program. The temptation to debate rescission — or to defend the status quo — misses the more important question: can these resources be channeled through a program architecture that produces the per-location cost efficiency and deployment speed that competitive mechanisms have demonstrated are achievable?

The answer is yes, but only if the successor program is designed around the right objective from the beginning. Not fiber miles constructed. Not compliance certifications submitted. Not plan milestones reached. Unserved locations served — per dollar, on a defensible timeline, at a cost that the social returns to rural broadband can justify.

Before the next broadband bill is drafted, appropriators and Commerce Committee staff should commission the cost-efficiency audit this paper begins: a systematic comparison of per-location BEAD outcomes across technology mixes, benchmarked against CAF II and RDOF performance, with explicit monetization of the delay costs imposed by administrative review cycles. The goal is not less federal broadband investment — the unmet need remains real and the market failure in rural areas remains genuine. The goal is better federal broadband investment: programs that treat competitive cost discovery as a design feature, not an obstacle, and that measure success in connected households rather than submitted checklists.

Twenty-four million unserved Americans are not waiting for more plans. They are waiting for the internet.
