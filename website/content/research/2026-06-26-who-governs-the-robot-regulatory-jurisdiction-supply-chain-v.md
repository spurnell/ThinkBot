## Abstract

The United States faces a physical AI governance challenge that is live, not speculative. Foundation-model-embedded robotic systems — humanoid platforms at automotive plants, surgical robots in clinical settings, autonomous mobile units navigating public spaces — are already in commercial operation. Yet the regulatory frameworks governing them remain fragmented, underutilized, and in several cases technically unprepared for the open-ended behavioral dynamics that distinguish modern physical AI from its deterministic predecessors.

This paper argues that the United States already possesses the regulatory infrastructure necessary to govern physical AI across its principal risk categories. The FDA, NHTSA, OSHA, CPSC, FTC, and BIS each hold statutory authority relevant to distinct deployment contexts. What the US lacks is not legislative authority but the deliberate activation of that authority, sufficient technical capacity within agencies to evaluate foundation-model-specific risks, and coordinated interagency protocols for the cross-boundary deployments that define physical AI's commercial trajectory.

Drawing on [CSET's 2026 analysis of the physical AI supply chain](https://cset.georgetown.edu/publication/physical-ai/), the paper situates its governance argument within the competitive dynamics of US-China robotics competition, showing that regulatory clarity and competitive positioning are complementary rather than competing objectives. A systematic, agency-by-agency jurisdictional analysis identifies three bounded gap types — jurisdictional seams, technical capacity deficits, and pre-market review gaps — none of which requires new legislation to address. The paper concludes with a concrete regulatory assignment framework: primary agency designations across deployment contexts, coordination prescriptions for cross-boundary deployments, and an executive-action pathway that would activate the framework without a single vote in Congress.

## 1. Introduction: Embodied Intelligence and the Governance Problem

Consider a scenario that is no longer hypothetical. A foundation-model-embedded humanoid robot operating on an automotive production line receives a visual input it has not encountered during training: an unusual part orientation, a shift supervisor stepping unexpectedly into its work envelope, a floor surface wet from a spill. The robot's large-scale neural network, trained on billions of embodied interaction examples, generates a real-time motor command. The command is not unreasonable by any narrow metric. But it is wrong in context, and the worker twenty inches away is injured.

Who is responsible? More urgently for governance purposes: who was supposed to prevent it?

The question is not hypothetical because the deployments are already underway. [Boston Dynamics unveiled its electric Atlas humanoid for commercial production at CES in January 2026](https://techcrunch.com/2026/01/05/boston-dynamicss-next-gen-humanoid-robot-will-have-google-deepmind-dna/), with Google DeepMind as a foundation-model development partner. [Figure AI's Helix system](https://interestingengineering.com/innovation/figure-launches-helix-ai-robots) employs a dual-model architecture — a 7-billion-parameter language processor and an 80-million-parameter motion controller — running on embedded low-power GPUs and processing multimodal sensory inputs to generate real-time motor commands without task-specific programming. [Agility Robotics signed a commercial Robots-as-a-Service agreement deploying seven humanoid Digit units at a Toyota Motor Manufacturing Canada assembly plant in February 2026](https://www.agilityrobotics.com/content/agility-robotics-announces-commercial-agreement-with-toyota-motor-manufacturing-canada) — the first commercial humanoid deployment at a major automaker's production facility. These are not research demonstrations. They are production deployments of systems whose behavioral envelopes cannot be fully characterized in advance by any regulator — or by their manufacturers.

This paper's central thesis is straightforward: the United States already possesses the regulatory infrastructure necessary to govern physical AI systems across their principal risk categories. The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://www.nist.gov/system/files/documents/2023/01/26/AI%20RMF%201.0.pdf) provides the voluntary governance architecture. The FDA, NHTSA, OSHA, CPSC, FTC, and BIS each hold statutory authority relevant to distinct deployment contexts. What the US lacks is not legislative authority but the deliberate activation of that authority, sufficient technical capacity within agencies to evaluate foundation-model-specific risks, and coordinated interagency protocols for the cross-boundary deployments that define physical AI's commercial trajectory.

The paper proceeds in four analytical stages. First, it characterizes what makes physical AI technically distinct from software-only AI systems — specifically, the combination of embodiment, foundation-model integration, and deployment diversity that strains existing regulatory categories. Second, it examines the supply chain dimension: drawing on CSET's February 2026 analysis of the physical AI hardware ecosystem, it maps US-China dependencies at the component level and argues that competitive cost pressure and regulatory clarity are complementary objectives, not competing ones. Third, it conducts a systematic, agency-by-agency jurisdictional analysis — identifying what existing authority genuinely covers and where the specific, bounded gaps lie. Finally, it presents a regulatory assignment framework: a concrete matrix of primary agency ownership across deployment contexts, coordination prescriptions for jurisdictional seams, and an executive-action pathway that would activate the framework without new legislation.

This paper departs from the dominant stream of AI governance scholarship in one important respect. The literature on AI regulation has overwhelmingly addressed software and algorithmic systems: recommendation algorithms, credit-scoring models, facial recognition pipelines, large language models deployed through APIs. That literature's central debates — around explainability, bias auditing, pre-deployment risk assessment, and horizontal versus sector-specific regulation — map imperfectly onto physical AI, where the operative risk is not a discriminatory output but a physical consequence in the world. The governance problem for embodied AI is not primarily one of algorithmic accountability. It is a product-safety, workplace-safety, and cross-boundary jurisdictional problem that requires a different analytical frame. This paper provides that frame — and argues that the institutions best positioned to apply it already exist.

## 2. What Makes Physical AI Different: A Technical Characterization

To understand why physical AI strains existing regulatory categories, one must first understand what distinguishes it — technically — from the software and algorithmic systems that have dominated AI governance debates. Three characteristics, taken individually, are each manageable within existing frameworks. Taken together, they create a cross-boundary regulatory challenge that no single agency was designed to address.

**Embodiment and the Product-Liability Threshold**

The most fundamental distinction is also the most obvious: physical AI systems act on the world through actuators. A large language model deployed through an API produces text. A recommendation algorithm produces a ranked list. Neither can fracture a wrist, puncture an abdominal wall, or collide with a pedestrian. A foundation-model-embedded robot can do all three.

This distinction has immediate legal significance. Product liability doctrine — governing manufacturer responsibility for defective products that cause physical harm — applies to robotic systems in ways that have no clean analogue for software. When a [Boston Dynamics Atlas unit](https://bostondynamics.com/atlas/) performs an assembly operation and a component fails, the question of whether the failure traces to hardware defect, software error, or emergent model behavior implicates a chain of potential defendants — the actuator manufacturer, the vision-sensor supplier, the foundation-model developer, the system integrator — that product liability frameworks were not designed to untangle. The [IEEE Robotics and Automation Society's technical standards for industrial robots](https://www.ieee.org/standards/robotics/) (R15.06, R15.08) address this for traditional deterministic systems. They were not written for neural-network-driven behavioral generation.

**Foundation-Model Integration and the End of the Behavioral Envelope**

Traditional industrial robots operate on behavioral trees: explicitly programmed decision graphs that specify, for every input state, the permissible output commands. A robot arm in a legacy factory has a finite, enumerable behavioral repertoire. Safety validation consists of exhaustively characterizing that repertoire under all anticipated operating conditions. That is hard engineering, but it is tractable engineering.

Foundation-model-embedded physical AI systems do not have behavioral trees in any meaningful sense. [Google DeepMind's RT-2](https://deepmind.google/discover/blog/rt-2-new-model-translates-vision-and-language-into-robot-actions/) and its successors generate motor commands through transformer-based architectures trained on web-scale data and embodied demonstration data simultaneously. The behavioral output is a function of the model's learned representations of context — and those representations respond to inputs that no manufacturer could anticipate at the time of deployment. The robot's behavioral envelope is, in a precise technical sense, open-ended.

This matters enormously for regulatory frameworks built around the assumption that a manufacturer can certify a product's behavior under specified conditions. The FDA's [predetermined change control plan (PCCP) framework](https://www.fda.gov/media/156897/download) for AI-enabled medical devices was designed for software that updates incrementally within a defined performance envelope. A foundation model that generalizes across task contexts does not have a definable performance envelope at the time of clearance. As [Stanford HAI's 2024 AI Index Report](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2024-ai-index-report) documents in its analysis of embodied AI systems, even developers cannot fully predict behavioral responses to novel environmental inputs — a property that is not a bug but an architectural feature of the generalization capacity that makes these systems commercially valuable.

**Deployment Diversity and the Jurisdictional Fragmentation Problem**

The third characteristic compounds the first two. A single foundation-model-embedded robot platform — consider Meta's [open-source robotics initiative](https://ai.meta.com/blog/open-source-robotics/) or the general-purpose humanoid architecture underlying Figure's Helix system — may be deployed in a hospital operating suite, a distribution warehouse, a public-road delivery context, and a residential elder-care setting, often running on the same underlying model with context-specific fine-tuning overlays.

Each deployment context sits in a different regulatory regime. The hospital robot is subject to FDA jurisdiction. The warehouse robot triggers OSHA authority. The delivery robot on a public sidewalk falls under NHTSA or potentially CPSC, depending on its classification. The elder-care robot at home is a consumer product. And a foundation model that bridges all four contexts — trained once, deployed everywhere — does not fit neatly into any of them.

This is the cross-boundary jurisdictional problem the paper addresses. It does not arise from regulatory failure or institutional design flaws. It arises from the specific combination of embodiment, emergent behavior, and deployment flexibility that defines the commercial value proposition of physical AI. Understanding that combination is the prerequisite to addressing it.

## 3. The Supply Chain Dimension: CSET Data and US-China Exposure

The governance problem for physical AI cannot be analyzed in isolation from the competitive and supply chain dynamics shaping how quickly these systems reach deployment. A governance framework that imposes compliance costs without understanding the cost structure of the underlying industry risks accelerating the very competitive disadvantage it should prevent.

The starting point is a cost disparity that is stark by any measure. [CSET's February 2026 analysis of the physical AI ecosystem](https://cset.georgetown.edu/publication/physical-ai/) documents that leading Chinese humanoid platforms — the [Unitree G1](https://www.unitree.com/g1/) and [Fourier GR-1](https://fourierintelligence.com/gr1/) — retail at approximately $16,000 and $55,000 respectively. Comparable US and European platforms currently price between $150,000 and $250,000 for systems with equivalent payload and mobility specifications. The gap is not marginal. It is structural, driven by three reinforcing factors: vertically integrated domestic supply chains, state procurement subsidies through China's national robotics initiative, and a domestic manufacturing ecosystem that has commoditized critical components that remain specialty items in US supply chains.

**The Component-Level Dependencies**

The cost gap traces, at the component level, to specific subsystems where Chinese manufacturers have achieved dominant market positions or where US manufacturers remain dependent on non-domestic suppliers. CSET identifies five component categories with meaningful supply chain vulnerability.

*Actuators and servo drives*: Harmonic drive actuators — the precision gear units that give humanoid robots dexterous joint control — are produced at scale by a small number of manufacturers, with Nabtesco and Harmonic Drive AG (Japan and Germany, respectively) holding dominant positions for high-precision variants. Chinese manufacturers including Leaderdrive and Han's Motor have rapidly expanded capacity for mid-precision applications. For US manufacturers competing on cost rather than surgical precision, the Chinese mid-precision market is already the price-setting tier.

*Force-torque sensors*: These components, which allow robots to sense contact forces and modulate their responses — directly relevant to worker-safety applications — are currently dominated by ATI Industrial Automation (US) and Robotiq (Canada) for high-precision variants. However, [CSET's component-level mapping](https://cset.georgetown.edu/publication/physical-ai/) identifies a growing Chinese supplier base, with several components relying on rare-earth magnetic materials sourced almost exclusively from China, creating a materials-layer dependency even for sensors assembled in the United States.

*Vision sensors and depth cameras*: The LiDAR and structured-light depth sensors that give robots spatial awareness of their environment are an area of acute US-China supply chain tension. Intel's RealSense line — a preferred component for US robotics developers — has faced production uncertainty. The dominant cost-competitive alternatives are produced by [Hesai Technology](https://www.hesaitech.com/) and [Livox (DJI)](https://www.livoxtech.com/), both Chinese manufacturers, raising dual-use concerns that intersect directly with BIS export control policy.

*Proprietary computing substrates*: The inference compute that runs foundation models at the edge — enabling real-time motor command generation without cloud connectivity — currently relies on NVIDIA's Jetson platform for most US-developed systems. This creates a dependency on TSMC's advanced node fabrication, introducing a Taiwan-concentration risk distinct from the China-source risks above. [Congressional Research Service analysis of semiconductor supply chains](https://crsreports.congress.gov/product/pdf/R/R47301) has flagged this specific dependency as a vulnerability for defense-adjacent applications.

**The Regulatory Feedback Loop**

The supply chain dimension connects directly to the regulatory design question. US physical AI manufacturers operating at a structural cost disadvantage face intense pressure to deploy systems quickly — to establish revenue, demonstrate market viability, and attract capital against better-capitalized Chinese competitors with state backing. Regulatory ambiguity amplifies this pressure: when manufacturers cannot determine in advance which agency's clearance or approval process they must navigate, or whether any prospective review is required at all, they must make deployment decisions under uncertainty.

This dynamic has a name in regulatory economics: the compliance-uncertainty premium. When regulatory requirements are ambiguous, risk-tolerant deployers gain a competitive advantage over risk-averse ones. In a market where one set of competitors (US manufacturers) bears the full weight of domestic liability and regulatory uncertainty while another (Chinese manufacturers serving non-US markets) does not, the compliance-uncertainty premium functions as an additional cost disadvantage layered on top of the structural cost gap CSET documents.

The [International Federation of Robotics' 2025 World Robotics Report](https://ifr.org/worldrobotics/) projects China accounting for approximately 40 percent of global industrial robot installations by 2027, with humanoid and collaborative robot deployments growing faster than any other segment. For US regulatory design, the implication is direct: governance frameworks for physical AI must be calibrated not only against domestic safety objectives but against the competitive environment in which US manufacturers operate. Supply chain resilience and regulatory clarity are not competing policy objectives. They are complementary — and a governance architecture that provides clarity while avoiding unnecessary compliance friction serves both simultaneously.

## 4. The Regulatory Landscape: An Inventory of Existing Authority

The United States does not have an AI regulator. It has a Food and Drug Administration, a National Highway Traffic Safety Administration, an Occupational Safety and Health Administration, a Consumer Product Safety Commission, a Federal Trade Commission, and a Bureau of Industry and Security. Each of these agencies was built around a specific risk domain, a specific statutory mandate, and a specific evidentiary tradition. None was built for AI. But six of them — across healthcare, transportation, workplace safety, consumer protection, commerce, and national security — hold authority directly relevant to the physical AI deployments now underway.

**Why Sector-Specific Regulation Is Not a Compromise**

The dominant critique of the US approach — voiced in congressional hearings, EU policy documents, and a substantial strand of academic governance scholarship — is that sector-specific regulators cannot address cross-boundary technologies. A robot that moves between a hospital and a warehouse, the argument goes, cannot be effectively governed by agencies whose authority stops at those institutional walls.

This critique is not wrong about the cross-boundary problem. It is wrong about the remedy. As the [GAO's 2021 analysis of AI governance across federal agencies](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-21-519sp) documented, the gap in US AI governance is not statutory authority — it is coordination, technical capacity, and deliberate activation of existing mandates. Regulators with deep sectoral expertise are better positioned to assess the specific risk profile of a surgical robot or an autonomous vehicle than a horizontal AI regulator with shallow knowledge spread across every domain simultaneously. The [NIST AI Risk Management Framework](https://airc.nist.gov/RMF) reflects this logic: it provides a voluntary cross-cutting structure precisely because mandatory horizontal rules would override the sectoral expertise that makes American product-safety regulation effective.

The Biden administration's [OMB M-24-10](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/M-24-10-Advancing-Governance-Use-and-Acquisition-of-AI.pdf) established the Chief AI Officers Council coordination mechanism for federal agency AI governance. Its successor, [OMB M-25-21](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-21-Accelerating-Federal-Use-of-AI-through-Innovation-Governance-and-Public-Trust.pdf) (April 2025), retained the core interagency coordination architecture while adopting a more explicitly innovation-forward posture — evidence that the sector-specific, agency-coordinated approach persists across administrations because it reflects an institutionally sound design choice. Both memos coordinate across agencies through [OSTP](https://www.whitehouse.gov/ostp/) rather than consolidating authority in a new horizontal body — a judgment about where regulatory competence actually resides.

Section 5 maps what that competence covers — and where its specific boundaries lie.

## 5. Agency-by-Agency Jurisdictional Analysis

The analytical core of this paper requires moving from the inventory of existing authority to a systematic assessment of how that authority actually performs against the specific characteristics of foundation-model-embedded physical AI. The analysis proceeds through three criteria for each agency: (1) *statutory basis* — does the agency's enabling legislation grant it jurisdiction over foundation-model-embedded systems or only their hardware predecessors? (2) *existing guidance and enforcement* — has the agency issued AI-specific guidance or taken enforcement actions that demonstrate applied authority? (3) *technical capacity* — does the agency employ staff capable of evaluating foundation-model behavioral dynamics, and if not, what institutional pathway exists to acquire that capacity?

On this standard, the six agencies do not score uniformly. The variation is not a damning indictment of the sector-specific model — it is a map of where targeted investment and coordination are needed.

### 5.1 FDA — Medical and Surgical Robots

The FDA's claim to authority over AI-embedded surgical and clinical robotics is the strongest of any agency analyzed here. The [Digital Health Center of Excellence's Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) framework](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/digital-health-center-excellence/software-medical-device-samd) — developed through iterative guidance documents from 2019 through 2024 — provides a genuine, operational regulatory pathway for AI-enabled medical devices. The Da Vinci surgical system and its competitors have navigated 510(k) clearance. As of December 2024, [FDA has cleared over 1,000 AI/ML-enabled medical devices](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12730494/) across the full suite of 510(k), PMA, and De Novo pathways. The statutory basis is unambiguous: the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (21 U.S.C. § 360) grants FDA pre-market review authority over Class II and Class III medical devices, and AI-embedded surgical robots plainly qualify.

The gap is specific. FDA's [predetermined change control plan (PCCP) framework](https://www.fda.gov/media/156897/download), finalized in 2024, was designed for software that updates incrementally within a defined performance envelope — a classifier that re-trains on new imaging data within a specified accuracy band, for example. It presupposes that a manufacturer can characterize the behavioral envelope at clearance and then describe permissible update pathways in advance. A foundation-model-embedded surgical robot does not have a stable behavioral envelope in this sense. The same model weights that guide a laparoscopic procedure today may generalize — in ways no manufacturer specified — to novel instrument configurations, unusual anatomical presentations, or unanticipated tool failures. The PCCP framework's requirement for advance characterization of "types of modifications" is architecturally incompatible with open-ended generalization. FDA must develop PCCP guidance specifically addressing foundation-model behavioral dynamics — this is the bounded gap, and it falls entirely within FDA's existing statutory authority to address.

### 5.2 NHTSA/DOT — Autonomous Mobility

The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration's authority over autonomous ground vehicles is statutory and substantial. [AV 4.0](https://www.transportation.gov/sites/dot.gov/files/docs/policy-initiatives/automated-vehicles/373971/av-4.0.pdf), issued in 2020, establishes the federal framework for autonomous vehicle safety, and FMVSS standards under 49 U.S.C. § 30111 give NHTSA authority to set performance standards for motor vehicles. For autonomous ground vehicles — self-driving cars, AV trucks, autonomous delivery platforms operating in traffic — NHTSA's authority is the appropriate primary locus.

The regulatory seam appears precisely where mobile robots depart from the motor vehicle definition. Under [49 U.S.C. § 30102](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title49-section30102&num=0&edition=prelim), a "motor vehicle" is a vehicle driven or drawn by mechanical power and manufactured primarily for use on public streets. A humanoid robot that walks on a public sidewalk — or a delivery robot operating in a bike lane — does not qualify as a motor vehicle by conventional statutory interpretation. These mobile robots fall into a gap between NHTSA (no jurisdiction over non-vehicles) and CPSC (consumer product authority, not traffic-safety authority). NHTSA could address this through notice-and-comment rulemaking extending its guidance framework to mobile robotic systems operating on public rights-of-way, or Congress could clarify the statutory definition — but absent either, a mobile robot injuring a pedestrian has no prospective federal safety standard governing its street operation.

### 5.3 OSHA — Workplace Robots

OSHA's authority is broad and directly applicable to the most consequential near-term physical AI deployment context: industrial warehouses, manufacturing floors, and logistics facilities. The General Duty Clause (29 U.S.C. § 654(a)(1)) requires employers to provide workplaces free from recognized hazards likely to cause serious harm. Foundation-model-embedded robots operating in shared human-robot workspaces constitute a recognized hazard by any reasonable interpretation — and OSHA has not been reluctant to apply the General Duty Clause to novel industrial hazards.

Existing standards reinforce this authority. OSHA's machine guarding standard (29 C.F.R. § 1910.212) requires that moving parts be guarded to prevent worker contact. The [2023 National Emphasis Program on warehousing, distribution, and high-injury industries](https://www.osha.gov/national-emphasis-programs/warehousing) put Amazon, robotics-heavy fulfillment centers, and collaborative robot deployments squarely in OSHA's enforcement crosshairs. A [2024 analysis of OSHA Severe Injury Reports](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0003687024001017) found that unexpected robot activation accounts for over 60 percent of robot-related workplace accidents — precisely the category of harm that human-robot workspace separation protocols are designed to prevent.

The gap here is prospective rather than statutory. The General Duty Clause is inherently reactive — it requires a recognized hazard, typically established through injury data, industry knowledge, or prior citations, before OSHA can act. For genuinely novel foundation-model-embedded systems, the hazard may not be "recognized" in the legal sense until workers have already been harmed. OSHA's National Emphasis Programs are the appropriate prospective mechanism: a Physical AI National Emphasis Program, modeled on the warehousing NEP, would direct inspection resources toward facilities deploying foundation-model-embedded systems before the injury record accumulates. This requires no new legislation — only an agency decision to act.

### 5.4 CPSC — Consumer Robotics

The Consumer Product Safety Commission holds statutory authority under Section 7 of the [Consumer Product Safety Act](https://www.cpsc.gov/Regulations-Laws--Standards/Statutes/Consumer-Product-Safety-Act) to establish mandatory performance standards for consumer products that pose unreasonable risks of injury. The emerging home assistant and elder-care robot market — systems like Amazon's Astro, Samsung's Bot Handy, and a growing cohort of Japanese and Korean elder-care platforms — creates a genuine consumer safety question that falls squarely within CPSC's mandate.

The problem is technical capacity. CPSC is a small agency — [congressional testimony places its full-time staff at approximately 534 employees](https://www.cpsc.gov/About-CPSC/Chairman/Alexander-Hoehn-Saric/Testimony/Testimony-of-Alexander-D-Hoehn-Saric-at-the-Energy-and-Commerce-Committee-Hearing-on-The-Fiscal-Year-2025-Consumer-Product-Safety-Commission-Budget) — with no history of evaluating AI systems. The agency has issued no AI-specific guidance. Its evaluation methodology for novel products relies heavily on voluntary standards developed through ASTM and UL processes — processes that have not produced foundation-model-specific performance standards for consumer robots. CPSC's enforcement posture is also post-injury: the agency responds to harm data through recall authority and post-market enforcement, not pre-market review. For consumer robots operating in residential settings around children, elderly users, and people with disabilities — all high-vulnerability populations — post-injury enforcement is an inadequate safety architecture.

Section 7 rulemaking authority exists and is sufficient; what is missing is the technical capacity to exercise it. NIST's [Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI)](https://fedscoop.com/nist-ai-consortium-reemerges-new-name-scope-members/) — which continues to conduct AI evaluation and measurement science — is the natural source of technical support. A formal NIST-CPSC technical assistance arrangement would address the capacity gap without new legislation.

### 5.5 FTC — Deception and Unfair Practices

The Federal Trade Commission's Section 5 authority over unfair or deceptive acts and practices applies to physical AI across two important dimensions. First, manufacturers and deployers making capability claims — marketing a surgical robot as capable of procedures it has not been validated to perform, or advertising an elder-care robot's fall-detection accuracy using unverified benchmarks — fall squarely within Section 5's deception prohibition. The [FTC's 2024 consent agreement with OpenAI](https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/cases-proceedings/232-3044-openai-inc) demonstrated that the agency is prepared to use this authority against AI companies specifically.

Second, physical AI systems generate behavioral and environmental data — about workplaces, home layouts, and user habits — in quantities that raise serious data-practice concerns under FTC's unfair practices doctrine. A humanoid robot with cameras, microphones, and force-torque sensors operating in a residential setting collects environmental data at a scale and intimacy that no prior consumer product has approached.

The limit of FTC authority is clear and should be acknowledged directly: Section 5 does not reach physical safety hazards. A robot that injures a worker because of an emergent behavioral failure is not an unfair trade practice — it is a product safety matter for OSHA, FDA, or CPSC. FTC's role in the regulatory assignment framework is real but bounded: deception, data, and capability-claim enforcement, not physical safety regulation.

### 5.6 BIS/ITAR — Export Controls and Dual-Use

The Bureau of Industry and Security administers the Export Administration Regulations (EAR) under the Export Control Reform Act of 2018. ITAR governs defense articles under the State Department's United States Munitions List. Both frameworks apply to physical AI systems with military potential, but both face the same classification challenge: foundation models embedded in commercial robots do not map cleanly onto the EAR's Commerce Control List or the USML's article categories.

The challenge is the software-hardware integration problem. A commercial humanoid robot — designed for warehouse logistics — running a foundation model capable of fine-tuning for reconnaissance, material handling in contested environments, or physical manipulation tasks with tactical applications exists simultaneously as a commercial product and a potential dual-use item. [BIS has issued advisory opinions on AI software export controls](https://www.bis.gov/regulations/ear/ear-advisory-opinions) but has not developed integrated guidance for the hardware-software stack of foundation-model-embedded robots. Updated classification guidance — specifically, advisory opinions addressing the conditions under which a commercial physical AI platform triggers EAR licensing requirements — is the targeted regulatory action needed, and BIS has authority to issue it without new legislation.

## 6. Where Existing Authority Genuinely Strains: The Bounded Gaps

The agency-by-agency analysis in Section 5 establishes the foundational claim of this paper: the United States possesses substantial, operational regulatory authority over physical AI across its principal deployment contexts. But a candid assessment requires naming the specific places where that authority is insufficient — not in the abstract sense that any regulatory framework can be improved, but in the concrete sense that identifiable categories of risk currently fall between or beyond the reach of the six agencies examined.

Three gap types emerge from the analysis. Each is bounded, specific, and addressable through an existing institutional mechanism.

**Type I: Jurisdictional Seams**

The most structurally significant gap is the public right-of-way problem introduced in Section 5.2. A mobile robot — whether a delivery unit, an elder-care companion in transit, or a commercial services robot — that operates on a public sidewalk, bike lane, or pedestrian crossing exists in regulatory no-man's-land. NHTSA's authority ends at the motor vehicle definition. CPSC's consumer product authority does not encompass traffic safety. OSHA has no jurisdiction outside the workplace. And municipal authorities, while increasingly asserting permitting requirements for sidewalk delivery robots, operate under inconsistent legal frameworks that produce patchwork rather than national safety standards.

As the [2024 analysis of OSHA Severe Injury Reports](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0003687024001017) documents, unexpected activation is the principal cause of robot-related workplace accidents — precisely the dynamic most likely to produce pedestrian harm when mobile robots operate in public environments beyond the OSHA perimeter. No federal agency has clear prospective authority to set the safety performance standards that would prevent injuries in public rights-of-way. This gap is concrete, not theoretical.

**Type II: Technical Capacity Gaps**

The second gap is not statutory but organizational. Two of the six agencies — CPSC and, to a lesser degree, OSHA — hold genuine statutory authority over their respective physical AI deployment contexts but lack the internal technical capacity to exercise that authority effectively with respect to foundation-model-embedded systems.

CPSC's situation is the more acute. With approximately 534 full-time employees and no history of evaluating AI behavioral systems, its standard-setting methodology relies on voluntary standards development through ASTM and UL — processes that have not produced foundation-model-specific performance benchmarks. As [GAO's 2022 technology assessment of AI in health care](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-22-104629) documented in the parallel medical context, agencies with formal statutory authority over AI-adjacent domains frequently lack the in-house expertise to translate that authority into effective technical standards. CPSC cannot credibly issue a Section 7 performance standard for consumer robots without access to the computational and AI evaluation capacity to define what "performance" means for a system with an open-ended behavioral envelope. NIST's Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) is the natural source of borrowed technical capacity for both agencies — but no formal technical assistance agreements currently exist.

**Type III: Pre-Market Review Gaps**

The third gap is structural: most physical AI deployment contexts outside FDA jurisdiction involve no prospective regulatory review whatsoever. A warehouse operator can deploy a foundation-model-embedded humanoid robot today without filing any application with any federal agency, without submitting any safety case, and without receiving any clearance. OSHA's General Duty Clause will respond after an injury occurs. CPSC's recall authority will engage after a consumer product causes harm. But neither agency has a pre-market checkpoint for physical AI systems in their jurisdiction.

This asymmetry matters because the harm profile of physical AI is not primarily about product defect in the traditional sense — it is about emergent behavioral failures in novel deployment contexts, which are inherently prospective in character. Post-injury enforcement is poorly suited to risks that arise from deployment diversity and open-ended generalization.

The critical observation — one that will structure the response to the horizontal-regulation argument in Section 8 — is that none of these three gaps requires new legislation to address. Each has an existing institutional mechanism available: NHTSA rulemaking to extend AV guidance to mobile robots on public rights-of-way; a formal NIST-CPSC technical assistance agreement; an OSHA Physical AI National Emphasis Program; and an executive-order-activated interagency working group to manage the seams between all six agencies. The absence of these mechanisms is not evidence of institutional failure. It is evidence that existing institutions have not yet been deliberately directed toward physical AI. That direction is an executive and administrative action, not a legislative one.

## 7. The Case for Horizontal Regulation: A Serious Engagement

The analysis in Sections 4 through 6 makes a case for the adequacy of existing sector-specific authority, subject to targeted modifications. Before advancing to that conclusion, intellectual honesty requires a genuine confrontation with the best arguments on the other side. Proponents of a new horizontal physical AI framework — a dedicated regulatory body, a comprehensive statute, or a mandatory cross-agency coordination mandate with binding rulemaking authority — advance three claims that deserve more than dismissal.

**The Regulatory Arbitrage Argument**

The most technically sophisticated case for horizontal regulation turns on the deployment-diversity characteristic described in Section 2. A single foundation-model-embedded robot platform — deployed across FDA, OSHA, and CPSC jurisdictions depending on use context — creates an opportunity for regulatory arbitrage that no single sector-specific agency can close unilaterally. A deployer who finds FDA's SaMD clearance process too burdensome for a clinical robot may rebrand the same system as a "wellness" device that escapes FDA jurisdiction entirely. A manufacturer selling into both consumer and commercial markets can navigate the seam between CPSC and OSHA authority by calibrating its marketing language rather than its product design. [EU AI Act Article 6](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32024R1689), which classifies AI embedded in safety-relevant machinery as high-risk regardless of market context, was specifically designed to prevent this kind of jurisdictional arbitrage — and the design choice has a genuine logic.

This argument is correct about the arbitrage risk. It is wrong to conclude that the remedy requires a new agency. But the risk is real and should not be minimized.

**The Coordination Failure Argument**

The second argument is institutional. Fragmented regulatory systems face endemic coordination failures: agencies develop conflicting standards, overlapping enforcement creates compliance inefficiency, and gaps persist because no agency has the mandate or incentive to address problems in another's jurisdiction. [Coglianese and Lehr's foundational 2017 work on algorithmic governance](https://scholarship.law.upenn.edu/faculty_scholarship/1734/) documents how administrative fragmentation produces inconsistent regulatory outputs even when individual agencies operate well within their own domains. For physical AI specifically, a warehouse robot deployer navigating OSHA's machine-guarding standards, NHTSA's emerging mobile-robot guidance, and CPSC's consumer product rules faces compliance requirements developed independently, which may conflict at the margin and will be enforced by agencies that do not share data or coordinate inspections. The compliance burden is real, and it falls disproportionately on smaller deployers who cannot afford dedicated regulatory affairs staff for six separate agency relationships.

This argument identifies a genuine cost of the sector-specific model. It deserves a serious answer — which Section 8 provides.

**The Multilateral Standard-Setting Argument**

The third argument is geopolitical. The EU AI Act's [Annex I](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32024R1689) classification of AI embedded in regulated machinery as high-risk, with mandatory third-party conformity assessment requirements, creates external regulatory pressure on US manufacturers serving European markets. If the US lacks a comparable framework, the EU's standards become the de facto global baseline — a pattern of regulatory export familiar from GDPR and the General Product Safety Regulation. The US cedes standard-setting authority by remaining reactive.

This argument must now be weighed against current facts. The European Commission [formally withdrew the companion EU AI Liability Directive in October 2025](https://eapil.org/2025/10/09/european-commission-withdraws-two-proposals-assignments-of-claims-regulation-and-ai-liability-directive/), after announcing the withdrawal in its February 2025 Work Programme on grounds that "no foreseeable agreement" was possible. [Bird & Bird's analysis of the withdrawal](https://www.twobirds.com/en/insights/2025/proposed-eu-ai-liability-rules-withdrawn) confirmed the companion instrument establishing cross-border civil liability for AI harm was abandoned entirely. This is not a minor implementation hiccup. It demonstrates that horizontal EU frameworks are themselves subject to political entropy, and that the "EU sets the global standard" dynamic is less automatic than proponents claim.

What remains correct in the multilateral argument is narrower but real: the US has a legitimate interest in ensuring that its sector-specific framework produces outcomes compatible with key trading partners' safety requirements. Regulatory interoperability with EU product-safety and machine-safety standards — not harmonization, but compatibility — is a reasonable policy objective, and one that an interagency coordination mechanism can pursue more agilely than a new horizontal statute. Section 8 returns to this point.

## 8. Why the Case for New Mandates Fails: Coordination Without Legislation

The three arguments catalogued in Section 7 are the strongest available case for a new horizontal physical AI framework. Each identifies a genuine cost of the sector-specific model. None survives scrutiny as a justification for new legislation — because each problem has an institutional solution that does not require displacing the sectoral expertise that makes the existing framework worth preserving.

**On Regulatory Arbitrage: Coordination Closes the Seam**

The arbitrage risk identified in Section 7 is real. A deployer who rebadges a clinical robot as a "wellness" device to escape FDA's SaMD process is exploiting a jurisdictional seam. But the remedy for a seam is not to consolidate all authority in a new body — it is to close the seam through interagency agreement that removes the arbitrage opportunity.

The mechanism exists and has been used. The [2016 Federal Automated Vehicles Policy](https://www.transportation.gov/AV/federal-automated-vehicles-policy-september-2016) coordinated across DOT components and signaled the intent for FDA-NHTSA joint guidance on vehicle-integrated medical systems — precisely because connected vehicles might carry monitoring devices under both agencies' jurisdiction. The joint framework required no new legislation; it required two agencies to agree on primary jurisdiction triggers and mutual notification protocols. A joint FDA-OSHA protocol establishing that any robotic system marketed for patient care triggers FDA's SaMD assessment regardless of how the manufacturer categorizes the commercial context would eliminate the most significant arbitrage vector — through an interagency memorandum of understanding, not an act of Congress.

A horizontal regulator would not eliminate arbitrage risk; it would simply relocate it. Any comprehensive physical AI statute would contain definitional scope limitations, and sophisticated deployers would optimize against those definitions. The advantage of the sector-specific approach is that sectoral experts — who understand the actual risk profiles of surgical robots and warehouse automation systems — are harder to game than a horizontal body with shallow technical knowledge spread across every AI application domain simultaneously.

**On Coordination Failures: The Record Contradicts the Theory**

The coordination failure argument rests on a general prediction about administrative fragmentation that the specific history of interagency coordination on cross-boundary technology risks does not support. Agencies with overlapping jurisdiction on shared risks have repeatedly developed coherent joint frameworks when given clear mandates and institutional support.

The OSHA-CPSC history on powered industrial trucks and consumer product overlaps produced joint interpretive guidance that resolved competing standard-setting claims without litigation. The [FTC-CFPB coordination framework on algorithmic credit decisions](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/newsroom/cfpb-federal-partners-confirm-automated-systems-advanced-technology-not-an-excuse-for-lawbreaking-behavior/), announced in 2023, established a working model for AI-specific interagency enforcement that neither agency could have constructed unilaterally. As [RAND's AI governance research](https://www.rand.org/well-being/justice-policy/projects/artificial-intelligence-governance.html) documents, the institutional conditions under which interagency coordination succeeds are identifiable: a designated lead agency for each risk domain, a joint technical working group with standing, shared data-reporting obligations, and a clear escalation mechanism for disputes. These are administrative design choices, not legislative requirements.

The coordination failure prediction also mischaracterizes the cost structure. A new horizontal physical AI agency would not reduce coordination costs — it would add a new actor to the coordination problem. An agency with nominal authority over surgical robots, warehouse automation, consumer home robots, and autonomous vehicles simultaneously would face constant jurisdictional conflict with the FDA, OSHA, CPSC, and NHTSA staff who hold the substantive expertise it lacks. The [GAO's 2021 AI governance assessment](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-21-519sp) found that the federal AI governance gap was not institutional turf — it was technical capacity and deliberate activation of existing mandates. Adding a new institution does not solve a capacity and activation problem.

**On EU Pressure: The Horizontal Model Is Visibly Struggling**

The multilateral argument has been overtaken by events. EU AI Act implementation data tells a sobering story about horizontal governance at scale: [only 8 of 27 EU member states met the August 2025 deadline](https://www.aiacto.eu/en/blog/ai-act-member-states-national-authorities-2026) for designating national competent authorities, while roughly 6 member states had yet to designate any competent authority at all. The Commission and Parliament reached a [provisional agreement in May 2026 to simplify and streamline AI Act requirements](https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/press/press-releases/2026/05/07/artificial-intelligence-council-and-parliament-agree-to-simplify-and-streamline-rules/) — explicitly deferring the high-risk AI deadlines that had been driving compliance pressure — an acknowledgment that the original horizontal framework was generating compliance burdens that were neither necessary nor proportionate. Formal adoption of those simplifications remains pending. The EU AI Liability Directive, the companion instrument that would have established cross-border civil liability for AI harm, was withdrawn entirely.

This is not a minor implementation hiccup. It is evidence that the architecture of comprehensive horizontal AI regulation produces the coordination fragmentation it was designed to prevent — just at the member-state level rather than the agency level. Importing that architecture into the United States would add compliance costs falling disproportionately on smaller US physical AI manufacturers while generating the same divergence, now across fifty states and six federal agencies simultaneously.

The affirmative case for targeted coordination is not a counsel of regulatory minimalism. It is a judgment about institutional design under technological uncertainty. The sector-specific model preserves the one resource that no new statute can create: decades of domain expertise in the agencies that already govern the risk categories physical AI creates. The targeted modifications — interagency MOUs for jurisdictional seams, NIST technical support for CPSC, a new OSHA National Emphasis Program, updated BIS classification guidance — are achievable through executive coordination and agency rulemaking on a timeline measured in months, not the years any new comprehensive statute would require. In a market where Chinese competitors are already shipping commercial humanoid platforms, regulatory response time is not an abstraction.

## 9. Historical Precedent: How Interagency Mechanisms Have Handled Cross-Boundary Risks

The case made in Section 8 — that coordination without new legislation is achievable — rests on a claim about institutional capability. The question is not whether interagency coordination can work in theory, but whether the US administrative state has actually produced it for cross-boundary technology risks with characteristics similar to physical AI. Three historical cases provide the empirical foundation.

**Case 1: FDA-NHTSA and the Connected Vehicle Medical Interface**

The [2016 Federal Automated Vehicles Policy](https://www.transportation.gov/AV/federal-automated-vehicles-policy-september-2016) explicitly flagged the jurisdictional interface between NHTSA's vehicle-safety authority and FDA's medical device authority as an area requiring coordinated guidance. The trigger was concrete: connected and autonomous vehicles were incorporating biometric monitoring systems — driver fatigue detection, cardiac event recognition, glucose monitoring interfaces — that qualified simultaneously as vehicle-safety systems under NHTSA's FMVSS authority and Software as a Medical Device under FDA's 21 U.S.C. § 360 jurisdiction. A single integrated system could generate simultaneous regulatory obligations from two agencies with different evidentiary standards, different pre-market review timelines, and different definitions of what constitutes a safety-relevant software update.

The response was not new legislation. It was joint policy signaling, followed by agency-level coordination agreements establishing primary jurisdiction triggers: FDA took lead authority when a system's primary function was diagnostic or therapeutic; NHTSA took lead when the primary function was vehicle control or driver monitoring for traffic safety. The dual-jurisdiction scenario was addressed through a notification protocol requiring simultaneous submission to both agencies. No statute was required. The coordination produced functional clarity within eighteen months of the Federal AV Policy's release.

**Case 2: OSHA-CPSC and the Commercial/Consumer Product Boundary**

The oldest and most durable interagency coordination precedent in US product-safety law is the [OSHA-CPSC Memorandum of Understanding, executed in April 1974](https://www.osha.gov/laws-regs/mou/1974-04-11) — within months of both agencies' establishment. The core problem was a category of products that moved between consumer and commercial settings: powered industrial trucks (forklifts) used in warehouses that also appeared in retail distribution environments accessible to consumers; hand tools sold through consumer channels but used in commercial workplaces; household chemical products that entered industrial settings. Both agencies had plausible statutory authority; neither had exclusive jurisdiction; the risk of duplicative or conflicting standards was immediate.

The MOU established four governing principles that remain instructive today: coordinated long-range planning to avoid duplicative standards development; shared legal and policy analysis on statutory authority questions at the jurisdictional margin; joint communications protocols for educational and safety materials; and a designated contact mechanism for interagency escalation when conflicts arose. The framework has operated for more than fifty years without legislative modification. It has not prevented all conflicts, but it has channeled them into resolvable administrative processes rather than litigation or regulatory paralysis.

**Case 3: FTC-CFPB-DOJ-EEOC and Algorithmic Enforcement Coordination**

The most recent and most directly AI-relevant precedent came in April 2023 when the [FTC, CFPB, DOJ Civil Rights Division, and EEOC issued a joint statement on enforcement against discrimination and bias in automated systems](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/newsroom/cfpb-federal-partners-confirm-automated-systems-advanced-technology-not-an-excuse-for-lawbreaking-behavior/). The four agencies had overlapping but non-identical authority over algorithmic credit decisions, automated hiring tools, and AI-driven pricing systems. Rather than waiting for Congress to delineate jurisdiction or for litigation to establish precedent through costly enforcement conflicts, the agencies jointly declared their intent to enforce existing laws — ECOA, FCRA, Title VII, Section 5 — against AI-driven disparate impact, and established shared evidentiary standards for algorithmic bias analysis and an informal notification protocol when one agency opened an investigation with potential cross-agency implications.

**The Institutional Conditions**

Across all three cases, four conditions correlate with successful interagency coordination: (1) a clear delineation of primary jurisdiction by risk domain or product type, so each agency knows when it leads and when it defers; (2) a joint technical working group or regular interagency contact with designated staff — not ad hoc engagement at the leadership level; (3) a shared data-reporting or notification obligation that prevents agencies from developing conflicting standards in isolation; and (4) an escalation mechanism for genuine jurisdictional disputes, routing them to OMB or OSTP rather than leaving them unresolved.

These conditions are not exotic institutional innovations. They are the standard operating architecture of successful interagency coordination, and they can be established for physical AI through a single executive action — an OMB-coordinated Physical AI Working Group with defined lead-agency assignments, a joint technical secretariat housed at NIST, and a two-year deliverable schedule. No new statute is required.

## 10. The Regulatory Assignment Framework

The analysis conducted in Sections 4 through 9 yields a concrete conclusion: the United States does not need a new physical AI statute, a new regulatory body, or a comprehensive horizontal framework. It needs a deliberate assignment of existing regulatory authority to specific agencies, targeted modifications to fill the bounded gaps identified in Section 6, and a single coordinating executive mechanism to manage the jurisdictional seams. This section presents that framework.

**Primary Assignments**

*FDA — Surgical, Clinical, and Elder-Care Robots.* FDA holds the clearest and most developed regulatory authority of any agency examined here. The primary assignment is unambiguous: any AI-embedded robotic system whose primary function is diagnostic, therapeutic, or clinical-assistance falls under [SaMD jurisdiction](https://www.fda.gov/medical-devices/digital-health-center-excellence/software-medical-device-samd) via the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act. The targeted modification: FDA must issue updated [PCCP guidance](https://www.fda.gov/media/156897/download) that addresses foundation-model behavioral envelopes specifically — establishing what constitutes a "performance envelope" for a non-deterministic generative model and what change-notification obligations attach when foundation model weights are updated post-clearance. This is a guidance document, not a rulemaking, and it falls entirely within FDA's existing statutory authority.

*NHTSA — Autonomous Ground Mobility.* NHTSA owns autonomous ground vehicles under AV 4.0 and FMVSS. The targeted modification: NHTSA should initiate a notice-and-comment rulemaking extending its mobile-robot guidance framework to non-vehicle robotic systems operating on public rights-of-way — pedestrian-zone delivery robots, sidewalk autonomous units, and mobile humanoid platforms in transit. The statutory vehicle is NHTSA's authority under [49 U.S.C. § 31136](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title49-section31136&num=0&edition=prelim) to issue minimum safety standards for motor carriers and, through interpretive extension, for automated systems operating in traffic environments. This is a rulemaking, not a statute — achievable within the existing NHTSA regulatory calendar.

*OSHA — Workplace Deployments.* OSHA owns worker safety across all physical AI deployments in commercial and industrial settings via the General Duty Clause and existing machine-guarding standards. The targeted modification: the agency should designate a Physical AI National Emphasis Program, modeled explicitly on the [2023 warehousing NEP](https://www.osha.gov/national-emphasis-programs/warehousing), directing inspection resources toward facilities deploying foundation-model-embedded collaborative robots and establishing inspection protocols that assess human-robot workspace separation and behavioral-envelope documentation. This requires no rulemaking — an NEP is an administrative targeting decision.

*CPSC — Consumer Home Robots.* CPSC holds Section 7 authority to establish mandatory performance standards for consumer robots. The targeted modification: CPSC should initiate Section 7 rulemaking for home-assistant and elder-care robotic systems, with formal technical support from NIST's Center for AI Standards and Innovation, backed by the interagency AI coordination framework under [OMB M-25-21](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/M-25-21-Accelerating-Federal-Use-of-AI-through-Innovation-Governance-and-Public-Trust.pdf). A formal NIST-CPSC technical assistance agreement would provide CPSC the AI evaluation capacity it currently lacks without requiring new hiring or new legislative authority.

*FTC — Deceptive Claims and Data Practices.* FTC's existing Section 5 authority is sufficient for its assigned role: enforcement against deceptive capability claims, unverified benchmark advertising, and data-collection practices by embodied systems. No modification is required — only sustained enforcement priority, building on the OpenAI consent-agreement precedent.

*BIS/ITAR — Dual-Use Export Screening.* BIS should issue updated classification guidance — specifically, advisory opinions addressing the conditions under which a commercial physical AI platform with foundation-model integration triggers EAR licensing requirements. The software-hardware integration problem is solvable within the existing Commerce Control List framework through targeted advisory opinions; no statutory revision to the Export Control Reform Act is required.

**Seam Prescriptions**

Two jurisdictional seams require specific coordination prescriptions. First, medical robots used in patient transport — systems under FDA clinical jurisdiction that operate in NHTSA-adjacent mobility contexts — require an FDA-NHTSA joint guidance document establishing that FDA's SaMD clearance takes primacy for the clinical function, with NHTSA's mobility safety standards applying as a floor to the transport function. The [2016 Federal AV Policy's interagency signaling](https://www.transportation.gov/AV/federal-automated-vehicles-policy-september-2016) provides the template.

Second, robots that operate in both commercial and residential settings require an OSHA-CPSC joint enforcement protocol — explicitly designating OSHA as the lead when the system operates in a commercial workplace and CPSC as lead when it is marketed to residential consumers, with a shared notification obligation when enforcement action reveals cross-jurisdictional implications.

**The Activating Executive Action**

The entire framework can be activated through a single executive action: an OMB-coordinated interagency Physical AI Working Group, established under the President's coordination authority in [3 U.S.C. § 301](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title3-section301&num=0&edition=prelim), with delegated rulemaking authority to each lead agency and a two-year deliverable schedule. The Working Group's charter should assign NIST a joint technical secretariat role — housing the foundation-model evaluation capacity that CPSC and OSHA require — and designate [OSTP](https://www.whitehouse.gov/ostp/) as the escalation authority for unresolved jurisdictional disputes. The NSTC's existing AI coordination infrastructure, which has convened interagency AI working groups across multiple administrations, provides the institutional home.

The framework's animating logic is straightforward: assign authority where expertise already resides, fill capacity gaps through technical assistance rather than new institutions, and connect the seams through administrative coordination that executive action can accomplish without a single vote in Congress. That is not regulatory minimalism. It is regulatory precision — which is what the pace of physical AI deployment actually demands.

## 11. Conclusion: Specific Gaps, Targeted Fixes, and the Cost of Getting It Wrong

The argument of this paper can be stated plainly. The United States faces a physical AI governance challenge that is real, urgent, and already live in commercial deployments. The challenge has a specific character: not the absence of regulatory authority, but the fragmentation, underutilization, and technical-capacity deficits of authority that already exists. The response to that specific challenge should be specific in turn — not a new comprehensive statute, not a new horizontal regulatory body, but a deliberate activation of the six agencies that already govern the risk domains physical AI enters, with targeted modifications for the bounded gaps and a single coordinating executive mechanism for the seams between them.

Return, finally, to the supply chain dimension with which Section 3 opened. The cost gap between US and Chinese physical AI platforms is not a static fact — it is a dynamic one, and the dynamic runs in the wrong direction. [CSET's February 2026 analysis](https://cset.georgetown.edu/publication/physical-ai/) documents an 80–90 percent price differential driven by vertically integrated Chinese supply chains, rare-earth materials dependencies, and state-subsidized production scale that US manufacturers cannot replicate in the near term. The [International Federation of Robotics' 2025 World Robotics Report](https://ifr.org/worldrobotics/) projects China accounting for forty percent of global industrial robot installations by 2027. Against that competitive backdrop, the compliance-uncertainty premium imposed by regulatory ambiguity falls entirely on US deployers — Chinese manufacturers serving non-US markets bear none of it. Every month that the jurisdictional seams between NHTSA, CPSC, and OSHA remain unresolved, every quarter that FDA's PCCP guidance leaves foundation-model clearance pathways undefined, is a period in which US physical AI manufacturers bear costs their primary competitors do not.

The governance architecture proposed in this paper is calibrated to that reality. Activate FDA's SaMD authority through updated PCCP guidance. Extend NHTSA's mobile-robot framework through rulemaking. Stand up an OSHA Physical AI National Emphasis Program. Initiate CPSC Section 7 rulemaking with NIST-CAISI technical support. Direct FTC enforcement priority at deceptive capability claims. Issue BIS advisory opinions on the software-hardware export-control interface. Connect all six through an OMB-coordinated interagency working group with NIST as joint technical secretariat. The framework creates no new regulatory bodies and avoids duplicative compliance overhead. The targeted mechanisms it activates — rulemaking, NEPs, guidance updates, advisory opinions — impose proportionate and predictable compliance costs, replacing costly regulatory ambiguity with standards that deployers can plan against. That trade is worth making.

The cost of getting it wrong runs in both directions. Underregulation that leaves the jurisdictional seams unaddressed creates the liability uncertainty that slows responsible US deployment. Overregulation — a horizontal physical AI statute creating a new body with no sectoral expertise — would add compliance drag, displace the institutional knowledge residing in the FDA, OSHA, and NHTSA, and hand Chinese competitors the advantage that only US legislative dysfunction can reliably provide. The worst regulatory outcome and the worst competitive outcome are, in this domain, the same outcome.

The right model is targeted, coordinated, and agency-led. It is achievable through executive action and administrative rulemaking — without a single new law.
