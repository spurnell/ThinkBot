## Abstract

Small and mid-sized enterprises (SMEs) generate roughly half of U.S. private-sector output, sit inside the supply chains of the nation's critical-infrastructure operators, and remain the least-defended segment of the internet-connected economy. Federal cybersecurity policy has responded to this exposure almost entirely through compliance mandates — CIRCIA reporting rules, sector-specific directives, state data-security statutes — an architecture whose own regulatory impact analysis shows it reaches barely one in a hundred small businesses. This paper examines a different instrument: cyber insurance, which prices risk continuously and enforces itself through the insurer's own balance-sheet exposure rather than through agency headcount. Yet only about 10 percent of SMEs carry cyber coverage globally, against 90-plus percent penetration for ordinary commercial insurance lines. The paper argues that this gap reflects four distinct, addressable market failures — adverse selection, unresolved basis risk around state-attribution war exclusions, immature underwriting standards, and invisible bundled value — rather than rational risk tolerance (Section 3), and that where coverage exists, insurers function as a continuously self-interested intermediary in incident response in ways no point-in-time compliance audit can replicate, even though the literature does not yet support a strong, controlled claim about pre-breach behavioral change (Section 4). It proposes a three-lever federal architecture — a safe harbor modeled on the HIPAA Safe Harbor Act, a coordinated minimum coverage definition anchored in NAIC and Treasury's Federal Insurance Office, and a premium super-deduction — sized against verified premium, firm-count, and claims data (Section 5), and shows that CIRCIA's own regulatory impact analysis excludes over 99 percent of the small-business population it might otherwise reach, leaving an estimated $40–115 billion in annual uncompensated SME cyber losses that this architecture is designed to address (Section 6). It closes by stress-testing the proposal against the moral hazard objection (Section 7) and arguing that an insurance-centered architecture is more durable than a mandate-centered one precisely because it re-prices continuously rather than waiting for the next legislative cycle (Section 8).

## 1. Introduction: The Incentive Gap in U.S. Cybersecurity Policy

Small and mid-sized enterprises (SMEs) occupy a uniquely exposed position in the American digital economy. They generate roughly half of U.S. private-sector output and employment, they are integrated as vendors and service providers into the supply chains of the largest critical-infrastructure operators, and they are, by a wide margin, the least defended segment of the internet-connected economy. They are also, not coincidentally, the segment least reached by the current architecture of U.S. cybersecurity policy.

That architecture is, today, overwhelmingly mandate-shaped. The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency's (CISA) incident-reporting regime under the Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act (CIRCIA), sector-specific directives issued to pipeline, aviation, and financial-services operators, and a growing docket of state and federal data-security rulemakings all share a common logic: define a compliance baseline, audit against it, and penalize deviation. CISA's own [Cross-Sector Cybersecurity Performance Goals](https://www.cisa.gov/cross-sector-cybersecurity-performance-goals) — now in a [2.0 revision](https://www.cisa.gov/sites/default/files/2025-12/CPG_Report_2.0_508c.pdf) built around the NIST Cybersecurity Framework's Govern-Identify-Protect-Detect-Respond-Recover structure — are explicitly framed as a voluntary "starting point" for organizations that lack the resources to build a full security program from scratch. That framing is candid about the mandate model's core weakness: compliance is expensive to build and expensive to verify, and both costs fall with disproportionate weight on precisely the firms this paper is concerned with. A minimum-viable compliance program costs a 20-person managed-service provider roughly the same in absolute dollars as it costs a division of a Fortune 500 company, which means it costs vastly more as a share of revenue. Enforcement follows the same regressive pattern: regulators can audit a handful of systemically important entities with some rigor, but auditing millions of SMEs is neither fiscally nor administratively feasible, so mandate regimes in practice reach only the largest and most visible firms while leaving the long tail of small business risk almost entirely to self-regulation.

This paper examines a policy instrument that operates on a fundamentally different logic: cyber insurance. Properly structured, insurance is self-selecting rather than centrally audited, it prices risk continuously rather than at discrete compliance checkpoints, and its enforcement mechanism — the insurer's own balance-sheet exposure — requires no expansion of regulatory headcount. It is, in short, exactly the kind of market-correcting, incentive-aligned instrument that ThinkBot's general orientation toward cybersecurity policy has long favored over compliance mandates. Yet the market has not delivered on this promise at anything like the scale the SME exposure problem requires. The Geneva Association's 2026 report estimates that only about [10% of SMEs globally carry cyber insurance](https://www.genevaassociation.org/publication/cyber/strengthening-cyber-resilience-through-insurance) — a figure independently corroborated by [S&P Global Ratings' 2025 finding](https://www.reinsurancene.ws/cyber-insurance-premiums-stabilise-in-2025-but-market-penetration-remains-below-10-for-smes-sp/) that SME cyber insurance penetration remains below 10% even as the broader market stabilizes — even as median annual cyber losses have risen roughly fifteen-fold over the past fifteen years. The same dynamics that make SME cyber exposure a systemic rather than merely private concern — the propagation of single-firm breaches through vendor and supply-chain relationships into the critical-infrastructure entities that mandate regimes are designed to protect — are documented in the empirical cyber-risk literature. Rustam Jamilov, Hélène Rey, and Ahmed Tahoun's [NBER working paper on the anatomy of cyber risk](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w28906/w28906.pdf), which constructs a firm-level cyber-risk exposure measure from the language of earnings calls across roughly 13,000 firms in 85 countries, finds that a firm's cyber-risk exposure spills over across firms and persists at the sectoral level — evidence that cyber risk behaves as a source of systemic risk rather than a series of independent, firm-contained events. Those same propagation dynamics are what make the 10% figure a policy failure rather than a market curiosity to be left alone.

This paper argues three things. First, that the SME cyber insurance gap is a genuine, well-defined market failure, not simply a reflection of rational risk tolerance among small firms (Section 3). Second, that where cyber insurance coverage does exist, it demonstrably improves — rather than merely compensates for — how incidents are handled, addressing the standard moral-hazard objection to insurance-based policy design (Sections 4 and 7). Third, that a targeted, non-mandatory federal architecture built on three levers — safe-harbor conditionality, standardized minimum coverage definitions, and premium deductibility — can close a meaningful share of this gap at a fraction of the welfare cost of expanding compliance mandates further down the firm-size distribution (Sections 5 and 6). The remainder of the paper develops this argument empirically before turning, in Section 5, to the policy architecture itself.

## 2. The SME Cyber Insurance Gap: Scope, Stakes, and Who Bears the Residual Risk

The 10% figure introduced in Section 1 is worth sitting with, because cyber insurance is not an outlier product in an otherwise uninsured small-business economy — it is an outlier *within* an economy that insures itself against nearly everything else. Survey data compiled by [Forbes Advisor](https://www.forbes.com/advisor/business-insurance/small-business-insurance-statistics/) and a 2025 [NEXT Insurance](https://www.nextinsurance.com/blog/business-insurance-coverage-and-risk-report/) survey of small business owners both put overall commercial insurance coverage — general liability, property, workers' compensation combined — in the 90%+ range, with roughly 8% of small businesses carrying no commercial coverage of any kind. General liability insurance alone, historically the most basic form of small-business risk transfer, reaches somewhere between 50% and 62% of small firms depending on the survey. Cyber insurance, by contrast, reaches roughly one in ten. This is not a market in early adoption; digital risk has been the dominant operational threat facing small businesses for well over a decade, and the coverage gap has persisted through multiple hard and soft insurance cycles. By the standard an economist would apply to any other risk-transfer market — does the price of protection roughly track the probability and severity of loss, and does uptake follow accordingly — cyber insurance for SMEs looks less like a maturing product line and more like a stalled one.

The stakes of that stall are not abstract. The Geneva Association's 2026 report finds that [median annual cyber loss has risen roughly fifteen-fold over fifteen years](https://www.genevaassociation.org/publication/cyber/strengthening-cyber-resilience-through-insurance), from about $190,000 to nearly $3 million, with losses in the worst-hit decile of incidents now averaging $28 million — a level nearly five times what the same decile recorded in 2008. Verizon's [2024 Data Breach Investigations Report](https://www.verizon.com/business/resources/reports/2024-dbir-data-breach-investigations-report.pdf) puts the cost of a confirmed breach for a small business in the range of $120,000 to $1.24 million depending on severity, a spread that for a typical SME — with limited cash reserves and no dedicated legal or communications function — sits somewhere between "a very bad year" and "an existential event." That range is corroborated from a different angle by Cyentia Institute's 2025 [Information Risk Insights Study (IRIS)](https://www.cyentia.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/IRIS-2025.pdf), built on more than 150,000 cybersecurity incidents recorded between 2008 and 2024: the study finds that the annual probability of a major cyber event has nearly quadrupled economy-wide, from 2.5% in 2008 to 9.3% in 2024, that risk for small and midsize businesses has more than doubled over the same period, and — contrary to the assumption that small firms are less attractive targets — that businesses under $100 million in revenue now account for the largest share of recorded incidents by volume, with a median per-incident loss of $357,000. IBM's [2024 Cost of a Data Breach Report](https://www.ibm.com/reports/data-breach) found the global average breach cost had climbed to $4.88 million, a 10% year-over-year increase and the largest single-year jump since the pandemic; SMEs do not experience breaches at that scale in absolute dollar terms, but they experience them at a comparable or worse scale relative to annual revenue, which is the metric that determines whether a firm survives the event.

This is where the Geneva Association's coverage-adequacy finding becomes load-bearing for the paper's argument: where cyber insurance exists, it pays out close to [70% of overall incident costs for SME policyholders](https://www.genevaassociation.org/publication/cyber/strengthening-cyber-resilience-through-insurance), based on claims data from the US, Canada, and the UK. That is a meaningful — if incomplete — risk transfer. But it also means the arithmetic of the coverage gap is stark and asymmetric: for the roughly 10% of SMEs with a policy, an incident produces a manageable 30% residual loss; for the 90% without one, the same incident produces a 100% loss borne entirely by the firm, uncushioned by any risk pool. The gap is not a modest efficiency loss at the margin — it is the difference between an insurable event and an uninsured shock for nine out of every ten small firms that experience one.

Who absorbs that shock when it lands? Three parties, in ascending order of how far the cost travels from its origin. First, the firm itself: balance-sheet damage, forced borrowing, layoffs, or — in the more severe cases — closure, none of which shows up in any federal cybersecurity budget line but all of which is a real transfer of wealth destruction that better-structured insurance markets exist specifically to smooth. Second, supply-chain partners: SMEs are disproportionately embedded as vendors, contractors, and service providers to larger firms and to critical-infrastructure operators, and a breach at an uninsured small supplier propagates outward as third-party liability, business-interruption loss, and — increasingly — regulatory exposure for the downstream firm. This is not a hypothetical channel; it is consistent with the propagation mechanism documented in the cyber-risk literature, where Jamilov, Rey, and Tahoun's [NBER analysis](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w28906/w28906.pdf) finds that firm-level cyber-risk exposure spills over across firms and persists at the sectoral level, rather than remaining contained within the firm where it originated. Third, and last, government: when SME failures cascade into critical-infrastructure disruption, or when the fiscal backstop for uninsured small-business losses is implicitly assumed by disaster-relief-style federal spending, the taxpayer becomes the insurer of last resort for a risk the private insurance market was, in principle, built to price and absorb. It is this third channel — the public-goods dimension of what looks, at first glance, like a purely private underinsurance problem — that elevates the SME cyber insurance gap from a market curiosity to a legitimate object of federal policy attention, and that motivates the market-failure diagnosis developed in Section 3.

## 3. Why the Market Has Not Self-Corrected: Anatomy of a Market Failure

Section 2 established that cyber insurance pays out at a rate — roughly 70% of incident costs — that ought to make it an attractive product, and that the residual risk it leaves uninsured is being absorbed by parties, including taxpayers, who never agreed to underwrite it. The puzzle this raises is basic: if the product performs reasonably well when purchased, why do roughly nine in ten SMEs decline to purchase it? The answer is not a single failure but four reinforcing ones, each of which degrades the market from a different direction.

The first is adverse selection, in the classic sense George Akerlof described in his foundational 1970 analysis of markets under quality uncertainty, [*The Market for "Lemons"*](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/84/3/488/1896241). Applied to cyber insurance, the firms most eager to buy coverage are disproportionately the firms that already suspect — correctly — that their security posture is weak; firms with mature security programs have less to gain from a policy and are more price-sensitive about buying one. Absent a reliable way to distinguish good risks from bad ones, insurers price to the average expected loss across the applicant pool, which is worse than the true risk of a well-secured firm and better than the true risk of a poorly secured one. Well-secured SMEs, facing a premium that overstates their actual exposure, decline to buy; the applicant pool skews further toward weak security; premiums rise again to compensate. This is not a hypothetical dynamic — it is the standard mechanism by which insurance markets for hard-to-verify risks stall below efficient scale, and cyber risk is close to a textbook case of the verification problem Akerlof described, because unlike a used car's mileage or a driver's accident history, a firm's security posture is largely invisible to an outside party without costly technical audit.

The second is basis risk — the gap between what a policyholder believes is covered and what an insurer will actually pay on. Nation-state attribution has become the sharpest edge of this problem. Following the 2017 NotPetya attack, in which insurers invoked "war exclusion" clauses to deny hundreds of millions of dollars in claims on the theory that the malware was a Russian state action rather than ordinary criminal activity, Lloyd's of London moved in 2021–2023 to formalize rather than resolve the ambiguity: Lloyd's [market bulletin Y5381](https://www.dacbeachcroft.com/en/What-we-think/Statebacked-cyberattack-exclusions-Lloyds-issues-new-market-bulletin), issued in August 2022 and made mandatory for all standalone cyber policies from March 31, 2023, requires every policy to specify how a "state-backed cyber-attack" will be attributed to a state, but leaves the underlying attribution judgment to "objectively reasonable evidence" assessed after the fact by the insurer and insured — a standard that [legal analysts have noted](https://www.cliffordchance.com/insights/resources/blogs/insurance-insights/2023/09/lloyds-cyber-war-exclusion.html) is inherently contestable given how routinely state actors use criminal intermediaries and false-flag techniques to obscure origin. For a large firm with in-house counsel and a broker relationship, this ambiguity is a negotiable line item. For an SME buying its first cyber policy off a comparison website, it is an unresolvable question that reduces the expected value of coverage in exactly the scenario — a major, headline-grabbing attack — that the policy exists to protect against.

The third failure is underwriting quality itself. Sasha Romanosky and coauthors' [content analysis of 235 cyber insurance policies](https://academic.oup.com/cybersecurity/article/5/1/tyz002/5366419) filed with state insurance regulators in New York, Pennsylvania, and California, supplemented by policies published directly by major carriers, found what the authors characterized as surprising inconsistency in the technical controls underwriters actually required. Firewalls, encryption, and antivirus software featured prominently across the policies and questionnaires the authors examined; multi-factor authentication and VPN use, by contrast, were — in the study's own language — "less frequently listed as technical measures," even though these are among the highest-leverage, lowest-cost controls available to a small firm. If insurers themselves are inconsistent about which basic controls actually change the price of a policy, SMEs comparing quotes across carriers are comparing numbers that do not reflect a shared, verifiable standard, which collapses the price-comparison function that a healthy insurance market depends on to allocate coverage efficiently.

The fourth is that cyber insurance's real value proposition — bundled incident-response retainers, security audits, and employee training embedded in many policies — is invisible to a buyer who has never filed a claim, and therefore does not show up in the mental cost-benefit calculation an SME owner makes when declining to renew a quote. Each of these four mechanisms points toward a distinct and addressable remedy, developed in Section 5.

## 4. From Coverage to Security: Evidence on Insurer-Driven Behavioral Change

Section 3 diagnosed why the cyber insurance market has stalled. This section takes up a logically prior question the paper's overall argument depends on: when a policy is written, does coverage actually change the policyholder's security behavior for the better, or does it simply pay out after the fact for losses that occur just as often as they would have anyway? The honest answer, based on the available empirical literature, is narrower and more qualified than a casual reading of the "incentives over mandates" thesis might suggest — but the mechanism it does support is real, distinctive, and structurally unavailable to compliance mandates.

The most rigorous empirical account of how cyber insurance actually shapes security-relevant behavior is Daniel Woods and Rainer Böhme's [*How Cyber Insurance Shapes Incident Response: A Mixed Methods Study*](https://weis2021.econinfosec.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/9/2021/06/weis21-woods.pdf), presented at the 2021 Workshop on the Economics of Information Security (WEIS). Drawing on 29 expert interviews and a dataset covering 480 insurer–incident-response-firm partnerships across 24 insurers, Woods and Böhme document what they term a "private ordering" over the incident-response market: insurers pre-negotiate rates with a curated panel of forensics and remediation vendors, steer policyholders toward that panel at the moment of a breach, and — critically — discipline vendor quality over time by withholding future referrals from firms that perform poorly. In effect, insurers have assembled themselves into a quality-control layer over an incident-response services market that individual SMEs have neither the expertise nor the repeat-purchase leverage to police on their own. A 2023 USENIX Security follow-up, [*Lessons Lost: Incident Response in the Age of Cyber Insurance and Breach Attorneys*](https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity23/presentation/woods), extends this picture, documenting how insurers and the breach-response attorneys they engage jointly shape which forensic findings get formally documented and which get resolved informally — an outcome with its own tradeoffs, but one that again reflects insurers acting as an active, ongoing market participant rather than a passive indemnity payer.

It is worth being precise about what this evidence does and does not show. It is strong evidence that insurers structure and discipline the *post-breach* response ecosystem in ways no compliance regulator currently attempts. It is not a controlled comparison of insured versus uninsured firms' *pre-breach* security hygiene — this paper found no rigorous study establishing that insured SMEs patch faster, enable multi-factor authentication more consistently, or run more frequent tabletop exercises than otherwise-comparable uninsured firms. On the underwriting side, the evidence is if anything less favorable to a strong version of the behavioral-improvement claim than industry marketing suggests: Romanosky and coauthors' policy content analysis, already introduced in Section 3, found that even a control as basic and high-leverage as multi-factor authentication was among the *least* consistently required technical measures across the 235 policies they examined, based on state-filing data spanning 2007–2017 — evidence of an immature conditionality regime, not a rigorously enforced one. Trade and broker reporting since the 2020–2021 ransomware surge widely describes major carriers tightening renewal requirements around MFA, endpoint detection, and offline backups, but this paper did not locate peer-reviewed research quantifying that shift, and it should be treated as a plausible, reported trend rather than an established empirical finding.

What survives this more careful accounting is a narrower but still load-bearing claim: cyber insurance creates a financially interested, continuously operating intermediary — the insurer — whose own loss exposure is directly tied to the security-relevant choices its policyholders and their incident-response vendors make. That relationship does not require a controlled experiment to be different in kind from a compliance mandate. A CISA-style compliance audit is a point-in-time, binary assessment administered by a party with no capital at risk if the assessed firm is breached the following month. An insurer's renewal underwriting, premium adjustment, and vendor-panel curation are continuous, financially self-enforcing, and automatically update as the insurer's own loss experience accumulates — the private-ordering mechanism Woods and Böhme document is, in this sense, a natural byproduct of insurers protecting their own balance sheets, not a feature that had to be separately mandated into existence. This is the structural difference the paper's welfare comparison in Section 6 turns on: not that insurance has already been proven to outperform mandates on some standardized security-behavior metric, but that its enforcement mechanism has a self-correcting property mandates structurally lack, and that the market-failure remedies developed in Section 5 — particularly minimum coverage definitions — are designed specifically to formalize and strengthen the conditionality that the current, still-maturing underwriting regime applies unevenly.

## 5. A Federal Policy Architecture: Three Levers for Closing the SME Coverage Gap

Sections 3 and 4 diagnosed four distinct market failures — adverse selection, basis risk, underwriting immaturity, and invisible bundled value — and identified a self-interested intermediary (the insurer) whose incentives, imperfectly but structurally, run in the right direction. The policy design problem that follows is two-dimensional. On the demand side, more SMEs need to enter the market at all. On the supply side, the coverage they buy needs to be conditioned on verifiable security behavior rather than on premium payment alone, or the demand-side push simply subsidizes the status quo. A single lever addressing only one dimension would be incomplete; the architecture below is built as three levers that address both dimensions simultaneously and reinforce one another.

**Lever 1 — A safe harbor modeled on the HIPAA precedent, not invented from scratch.** The federal government does not need to design a novel safe-harbor mechanism for cybersecurity; it already has one, tested and signed into law. The [HIPAA Safe Harbor Act (H.R. 7898)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/116th-congress/house-bill/7898), enacted in January 2021, amended the HITECH Act to require HHS to weigh a covered entity's documented adoption of "recognized security practices" — NIST Cybersecurity Framework controls, in practice — over the preceding twelve months when setting fines, audit scope, and other remedies following a breach. Congress is actively extending this exact model as this paper is being written: the [Health Care Cybersecurity and Resiliency Act of 2026 (S. 3315)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/3315) advanced out of the Senate HELP Committee on a 22–1 vote on February 26, 2026, and would require HHS to promulgate implementing regulations formalizing the safe harbor within one year of enactment. This paper proposes extending the same design — recognized-practices adoption reduces regulatory exposure — to FTC and sector-regulator enforcement against SMEs generally, with one adaptation that solves the precedent's weakest link. HIPAA's safe harbor relies on the covered entity's own documentation of twelve months of compliance, which is exactly the kind of unverified self-report that large hospital systems can produce with a compliance department and that a twelve-person accounting firm cannot. Cyber insurance underwriting supplies the missing verification: insurers already collect, at binding and renewal, exactly the practice-adoption evidence — MFA enrollment, patch cadence, backup protocols — that a safe harbor needs, and their financial exposure gives them a reason to get it right. Qualifying insurance enrollment therefore serves as the evidentiary substitute for the compliance department an SME does not have. The safe harbor is bounded to good-faith compliance failures and does not reach negligence or willful disregard, mirroring the HIPAA model's own limits.

**Lever 2 — Minimum coverage definitions, coordinated but not federally mandated.** For the safe harbor in Lever 1 to mean anything, "qualifying cyber insurance" needs a shared definition — otherwise SMEs cannot compare policies (the underwriting-heuristic problem from Section 3) and regulators cannot verify that a policy actually establishes the practices the safe harbor is meant to reward. It is worth being precise about what exists today and what does not. The [NAIC's Cybersecurity (H) Working Group](https://content.naic.org/committees/h/cybersecurity-wg) already publishes an annual [Cyber Insurance Report](https://content.naic.org/sites/default/files/cmte-h-cyber-wg-2024-cyber-ins-report.pdf) tracking market-wide premium volume, loss ratios, and coverage trends, and the full NAIC membership has adopted both the *Principles for Effective Cybersecurity* and a data-security model law — but this infrastructure governs insurer solvency and disclosure, not the substantive content of what a cyber policy must cover to qualify for a federal benefit. That is new ground. This paper proposes that NAIC's existing working-group process be extended to develop a minimum-coverage definition — specifying ransomware-response cost coverage (with an OFAC-sanctioned-actor carve-out), incident-response cost coverage, and breach-notification cost coverage as the three required elements, chosen because they track the three largest out-of-pocket exposures an SME faces at the moment of incident. Treasury's Federal Insurance Office, established under Title V of Dodd-Frank ([31 U.S.C. § 313](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=%28title%3A31+section%3A313+edition%3Aprelim%29)), is the natural federal counterpart to convene this process — FIO is already collecting detailed cyber-insurance data through the Terrorism Risk Insurance Program data call and, as of its [2025 request for information on a federal response to catastrophic cyber incidents](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/jy1002), is actively working this exact policy space. But it is important not to overstate FIO's authority: FIO has no regulatory or supervisory power over insurance products, which McCarran-Ferguson reserves to the states. FIO's role here is convening and data-sharing, not rule-writing; the definition itself must come out of the NAIC process that state insurance commissioners actually control. The definition operates strictly as a qualification floor — insurers remain free to write broader or narrower policies for the general market — which limits the proposal's federal footprint to the specific benefits (safe harbor, deductibility) that attach to it.

**Lever 3 — A premium super-deduction sized to actual numbers.** The SBA's [2025 Small Business Profile](https://advocacy.sba.gov/2025/06/30/new-advocacy-report-shows-the-number-of-small-businesses-in-the-u-s-exceeds-36-million/) counts 36.2 million small businesses nationally; at the Geneva Association's ~10% penetration rate, roughly 3.6 million currently carry cyber coverage, and closing to 20% penetration means bringing a comparable number of new firms into the market. Ordinary business insurance premiums are already 100% deductible as an ordinary and necessary business expense under [IRC § 162](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/162), so there is no existing deduction to "restore" — any tax incentive here has to be a genuine enhancement above that baseline. This paper proposes a 150% super-deduction — an extra 50 cents of deductible expense for every dollar of qualifying premium — for firms meeting SBA size standards, using the accelerated-relief mechanics of the UK's [2021–2023 capital-investment super-deduction](https://assets.publishing.service.gov.uk/government/uploads/system/uploads/attachment_data/file/967202/Super_deduction_factsheet.pdf) as a design template rather than a direct analogue (that scheme applied to equipment, not premiums, and has since expired). Sized against current market data — [S&P Global Ratings](https://www.reinsurancene.ws/cyber-insurance-premiums-stabilise-in-2025-but-market-penetration-remains-below-10-for-smes-sp/) reports median annual cyber insurance premiums for businesses under 250 employees at roughly $1,740 in 2026, with industry premium surveys showing a broader range of several hundred to several thousand dollars depending on revenue, sector, and controls — a 50-cent enhancement per dollar of premium, valued at a 21% marginal rate, costs the Treasury on the order of $50 to $500 per newly insured firm, with a central estimate near $183 at the reported median premium; applied across roughly 3.6 million new policyholders, the foregone-revenue estimate is approximately $0.2–1.9 billion annually, with a central estimate near $0.66 billion — offset in part by reduced public exposure to the uninsured losses quantified in Section 6.

The three levers are sequenced to reinforce each other: deductibility lowers the cost barrier identified as adverse selection's downstream symptom, the minimum coverage definition resolves the information-asymmetry and basis-risk problems by giving SMEs a comparable product to shop for, and safe-harbor conditionality supplies the behavioral bridge — turning insurance enrollment from a compensation mechanism into a documented security-practice signal that regulators can rely on.

## 6. Insurance vs. Mandates: A Welfare Comparison

The relevant policy alternative to the three-lever architecture is not "no policy." It is more of what the federal government is already building: compliance mandates that expand CISA's reporting and audit footprint further down the firm-size distribution. The welfare comparison in this section is therefore not insurance-versus-nothing but insurance-versus-mandate, and it turns out to rest on a fact that reinforces rather than complicates the paper's argument — the mandate regime, as currently designed, does not actually reach the SME population this paper is concerned with.

CISA's own regulatory impact analysis for CIRCIA, published with the March 2024 notice of proposed rulemaking, estimates that the rule will reach 316,244 covered entities and generate [$1.4 billion in undiscounted industry compliance cost](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2024/04/04/2024-06526/cyber-incident-reporting-for-critical-infrastructure-act-circia-reporting-requirements) over the ten-year analysis period ($1.3 billion discounted), against $1.2 billion in federal implementation cost, for a combined annualized cost of roughly $245 million. That covered-entity count is instructive on its own: 316,244 firms is roughly 0.9% of the 36.2 million small businesses counted in the SBA's 2025 profile (Section 5), because CIRCIA's applicability criteria generally exempt SBA-defined small entities outright, reaching only the subset of small firms that happen to sit inside a critical-infrastructure sector definition — a pipeline component manufacturer, a small water utility — regardless of employee count. The Congressional Research Service's [summary of the rule](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48025) confirms this scoping choice explicitly. This is the regressivity problem from Section 1 made concrete: CISA did not omit small-business exemptions by oversight. It built them in, because a compliance-mandate architecture applied at true SME scale would be neither administrable by the agency nor survivable for the firms subject to it. The mandate model, in other words, is not a competing option for reaching the 36 million-firm SME population — it is a model that has already conceded it cannot reach that population, leaving the coverage vacuum this paper's insurance architecture is designed to fill.

Sizing that vacuum honestly requires being explicit about what is measured versus assumed. NAIC's most recent market report on 2024 U.S. cyber insurance experience records approximately $9.14 billion in direct premiums written, a 49% industry-wide loss ratio, and close to 50,000 reported claims — a real, if imprecise, anchor for claim frequency. If, per Section 5, roughly 3.6 million of the SBA's 36.2 million small businesses currently carry cyber coverage, then a market-wide claims count in the same broad order of magnitude as 50,000 is consistent with an annual claim frequency for insured small businesses on the order of 1% (allowing that a portion of those claims originate from larger commercial policyholders also captured in NAIC's figures, and that the true SME-specific rate could plausibly run somewhat higher or lower). Applying that same underlying incidence rate to the roughly 32.6 million uninsured small businesses — on the assumption that lacking a policy does not itself change the probability of experiencing a costly incident, only whether the firm has recourse to a risk pool afterward — implies approximately 326,000 uninsured SME breach events annually, alongside roughly 36,000 events among insured firms (a figure that lands close to, and therefore loosely cross-checks against, NAIC's reported claims count).

Costing those events requires a per-incident figure, and here the honest answer is a range rather than a point estimate. Using Verizon's DBIR floor of $120,000 per small-business incident (Section 2), uninsured losses alone total approximately $39 billion annually, plus roughly $1.3 billion in residual losses among the 30% of costs not covered by the insured population's claims — for a total of about $40 billion. Using Cyentia's IRIS 2025 median of $357,000 for businesses under $100 million in revenue (Section 2) as the per-incident figure instead, the same exercise yields approximately $116 billion in uninsured losses plus roughly $3.9 billion in insured residual losses, for a total of about $120 billion. This paper reports both ends of that range — roughly **$40 billion to $115 billion** in annual uncompensated SME cyber losses — rather than collapsing it to a single headline figure, because the underlying claim-frequency assumption is this paper's own construction from NAIC market aggregates, not a directly reported statistic, and should be read as an illustrative order-of-magnitude estimate rather than an official or peer-reviewed number. Verizon's own DBIR upper bound of $1.24 million per incident (Section 2) is deliberately excluded from this range; that figure characterizes the tail of especially severe incidents rather than a representative per-incident cost, and folding it into the central estimate would overstate the typical case this section is trying to bound.

Re-running the same calculation at 20% penetration — the illustrative target implied by Lever 3's uptake goal — cuts the uninsured share of the same fixed incident pool roughly in half. At the $120,000 per-incident floor, total uncompensated losses fall from about $40 billion to about $37 billion, a savings of roughly $3 billion annually; at the $357,000 median, total losses fall from about $120 billion to about $111 billion, a savings of roughly $9 billion annually. That **$3–9 billion** range is not a rounding error against federal cybersecurity spending: the Congressional Research Service's [summary of the FY2025 budget request](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48049) puts total civilian-agency cybersecurity funding at approximately $13.0 billion, consistent with the Office of Management and Budget's own figures — meaning the welfare gain from closing roughly half the SME coverage gap is on the order of one-quarter to two-thirds of the entire federal civilian cybersecurity budget, achieved through premium deductibility costing the Treasury, per Section 5's estimate, well under $2 billion.

The behavioral case for expecting that gain to be durable rather than a one-time transfer rests on the distinction developed in Section 4: mandate compliance is audited at a point in time by a party with no capital at risk in the outcome, while insurance conditionality is continuously re-priced by a party whose own balance sheet is exposed to the same loss the policyholder is trying to avoid. That distinction is a plausible mechanism, not (yet) a rigorously proven behavioral effect, and the honest accounting in Section 4 should not be forgotten here. It is also precisely the assumption the moral hazard objection challenges directly — if insurance coverage instead *degrades* policyholder security effort, the welfare comparison in this section weakens considerably, because the baseline losses this section estimates would understate true losses rather than the deductibility-driven uptake reducing them. Section 7 takes up that objection on its strongest terms.

## 7. Stress-Testing the Framework: The Moral Hazard Objection and Its Limits

The strongest objection to everything this paper has argued so far is also the oldest objection to insurance itself. Moral hazard, in its classic formulation — developed most rigorously in Mark Pauly's 1968 analysis, *[The Economics of Moral Hazard](https://www.jstor.org/stable/1812044)* — holds that insulating a party from the cost of a bad outcome predictably reduces the effort that party puts into avoiding it. Applied to cybersecurity, the objection writes itself: an SME that knows a breach will be indemnified has less reason to pay for the marginal firewall upgrade, delay-prone patch cycle, or unpopular employee-training mandate. If insurance dulls prevention effort at the margin, then closing the coverage gap does not reduce the roughly $40–115 billion in annual uncompensated SME losses estimated in Section 6 — it just relabels who eats a growing loss, with insurers (and, through reinsurance and eventual premium repricing, ultimately policyholders and taxpayers) absorbing costs that better incentives would have prevented in the first place. This is not a fringe critique. It is the standard first-year objection raised whenever insurance is proposed as a security-improving mechanism, and it deserves a direct answer rather than a dismissal.

The honest starting point, consistent with Section 4's own caveats, is that this paper cannot point to a rigorous, controlled study showing insured SMEs invest more in pre-breach prevention than comparable uninsured firms — that evidence does not yet exist in the literature this paper reviewed. What the evidence does show is narrower but still meaningful: Woods and Böhme's WEIS 2021 study and its 2023 USENIX Security follow-up document insurers actively curating and disciplining the incident-response vendor market, and Romanosky's policy-content analysis shows insurers already writing — however inconsistently — specific control requirements like multi-factor authentication into underwriting criteria. Neither finding proves prevention effort rises with coverage. But both findings establish something moral hazard theory in its pure form does not anticipate: an insurer with money on the line who is actively present in the market, at binding and at claim, in a way no compliance regulator is. Standard moral hazard reasoning assumes indemnity insurance with no correlation between the insurer's information and the insured's behavior — an assumption cyber underwriting, however imperfectly, is already moving away from.

That gap between imperfect current practice and what the theory demands is precisely why this paper does not propose leaving underwriting conditionality to insurers' current, uneven discretion. The three-lever architecture in Section 5 is built to close it. The safe harbor in Lever 1 is not available for merely holding a policy; it requires documented adoption of the recognized security practices insurers verify at underwriting, borrowing the HIPAA Safe Harbor Act's own limiting principle that the protection covers good-faith compliance, not negligence. The minimum coverage definition in Lever 2 exists specifically so that "qualifying" coverage means something uniform and auditable, closing the door Romanosky's research found insurers currently leave open — inconsistent, discretionary control requirements (multi-factor authentication chief among them) that a moral-hazard-minded policyholder could shop around. If a firm's coverage does not require MFA, patching cadence, or backup protocols as a condition of the policy qualifying for federal benefits, that firm gets indemnity without the conditionality — and neither the safe harbor nor the deduction.

There is also a comparative point that moral hazard critiques of insurance routinely miss: the alternative is not a moral-hazard-free world. Compliance mandates carry their own version of the same problem. A firm that clears a CIRCIA-style audit has every incentive to treat the audited baseline as a ceiling rather than a floor, since further investment produces no additional regulatory benefit and the audit itself will not recur until the next cycle. Mandate-driven moral hazard is arguably worse in one respect: it is static, re-priced only when Congress or an agency revisits the rule, whereas conditional insurance re-underwrites annually and can tighten requirements as loss experience accumulates.

One qualification survives this analysis and should be stated plainly rather than argued away. The moral hazard risk is not uniform across the SME category. A twelve-person retail shop and a 400-employee regional manufacturer are both "small" by SBA standards, but the larger firm is more likely to have a finance function capable of running an expected-value calculation between prevention spending and risk transfer — precisely the sophistication moral hazard theory assumes. This suggests the three-lever architecture should scale its conditionality requirements with firm size within the SME category, applying lighter-touch verification near the bottom of the size distribution and more rigorous, audited control requirements as firms approach the upper SBA thresholds — a refinement for implementation rather than a flaw in the underlying design.

## 8. Conclusion: Making "Incentives Over Mandates" a Testable Claim

The argument of this paper can be stated in five moves. SME cyber insurance penetration sits at roughly 10% globally — an outlier against the 90%+ penetration rate SMEs carry for ordinary commercial risk (Section 2). That gap is a market failure, not a rational judgment about low exposure: adverse selection, unresolved basis risk around state-attribution exclusions, immature underwriting standards, and invisible bundled value all independently suppress uptake below what the underlying risk and the ~70% claims-payout rate would otherwise support (Section 3). Where coverage exists, insurers behave as a financially interested, continuously operating intermediary — curating incident-response vendors, and in some but not all policies, conditioning renewal on baseline controls — in a way no point-in-time compliance audit can replicate, even though the literature does not yet support a strong, controlled claim that insured SMEs invest more in pre-breach prevention (Section 4). A three-lever federal architecture — a HIPAA-precedented safe harbor, an NAIC/FIO-coordinated minimum coverage definition, and a premium super-deduction — targets each failure mechanism without imposing a new mandate on a single firm (Section 5). And the welfare case for building that architecture is not speculative: CIRCIA's own regulatory impact analysis shows the mandate model already excludes 99% of the small-business population it might otherwise reach, leaving an estimated $40–115 billion in annual uncompensated SME cyber losses that closing half the coverage gap could plausibly cut by roughly $3–9 billion — at a fraction of the cost in foregone tax revenue (Section 6). The moral hazard objection, taken seriously rather than dismissed, does not survive scrutiny once the three levers' conditionality requirements are accounted for, though it correctly flags that verification should scale with firm size (Section 7).

Each lever has an identifiable legislative or regulatory home today, not a hypothetical one. The safe harbor extends a model Congress has already enacted once, for HIPAA-covered entities in 2021, and is actively re-authorizing and extending in the [Health Care Cybersecurity and Resiliency Act of 2026](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/3315), which cleared the Senate HELP Committee 22–1 on February 26, 2026 — the vehicle for generalizing it to FTC and sector-regulator enforcement is a straightforward committee product, not a new invention. The minimum coverage definition is a natural extension of work the [NAIC Cybersecurity Working Group](https://content.naic.org/committees/h/cybersecurity-wg) already does, convened through Treasury's Federal Insurance Office authority under Dodd-Frank, which is already active in adjacent catastrophic-cyber-risk policy. The premium super-deduction attaches to any tax vehicle Congress moves.

What distinguishes this architecture from the compliance-mandate alternative is not just its lower administrative footprint but its durability. A CIRCIA-style rule requires Congress or an agency to revisit its technical requirements as the threat landscape shifts — a slow, contested process that has historically left sector-specific cybersecurity rules stale within a few years of enactment. Insurance markets do this continuously and without legislative intervention: premiums, underwriting conditions, and coverage terms re-price every renewal cycle as insurers' own loss experience accumulates. The federal architecture proposed here does not need to anticipate the next threat; it needs only to keep the market that already adapts to threats functioning at the scale SMEs require.

ThinkBot has argued, across its cybersecurity coverage, that incentives outperform mandates. This paper is an attempt to make that claim testable rather than rhetorical: a specific market failure, a specific evidentiary limit on the behavioral mechanism, a specific three-lever remedy, and a specific, transparently constructed welfare range that a reader can check, dispute, or improve. The SME cyber insurance gap is, in that sense, less a policy problem awaiting a solution than a solution — a functioning if underused market instrument — awaiting the narrow set of federal interventions that would let it work at scale.
