## Abstract

The Supreme Court's June 2025 decision in *Free Speech Coalition, Inc. v. Paxton* has generated the most significant wave of online age-restriction legislation in the three decades since *Reno v. ACLU* established strict scrutiny as the constitutional baseline for internet speech. At least two dozen states and multiple federal legislative vehicles have advanced social media age-restriction or age-verification mandates citing *FSC* as controlling authority. The premise is wrong.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it identifies the specific doctrinal misreading driving the post-*FSC* legislative surge: legislators, advocates, and commentators have treated the ruling as a categorical license for social media age-gating, when the Court's application of intermediate — not strict — scrutiny rested on a single pivotal element: the *Ginsberg v. New York* obscenity-to-minors classification. Laws that lack that classification cannot access the *FSC* intermediate scrutiny pathway. They remain subject to the *Reno* strict scrutiny baseline, which no online age-restriction law has survived in thirty years of litigation.

Second, the paper develops the first comprehensive doctrinal classification framework for evaluating post-*FSC* age-restriction legislation. The framework organizes pending bills into three structural models — *FSC*-footprint statutes, design-feature reframe laws, and blanket platform age-gating — and applies a three-stage constitutional analysis: a gateway inquiry, an intermediate scrutiny tailoring analysis, and a privacy-architecture assessment derived from *McIntyre v. Ohio* and its progeny.

Third, the paper finds that most pending legislation fails the framework's gateway analysis and will not survive First Amendment challenge. Durable child-protection law is constitutionally achievable; most current drafts are not that law.

## Introduction: The Blank-Check Misreading

In the weeks following the Supreme Court's June 2025 decision in [*Free Speech Coalition, Inc. v. Paxton*](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/24pdf/23-1122_3e04.pdf), legislative activity around minor protection online was extraordinary in volume and notable in uniformity. State press releases described the ruling as "clearing the path" for social media age-gating. Congressional sponsors of pending bills declared that the Court had "upheld the authority of legislatures to require age verification across the internet." Within six months, the Congressional Research Service would count over two dozen states that had enacted or introduced age-verification or social media age-restriction legislation in *FSC*'s wake, with legislators in most cases offering the decision as controlling constitutional authority — proof that the old *Reno v. ACLU* barriers had fallen. [CRS Report LSB11354](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11354) summarized the legislative response as proceeding on the widespread assumption that *FSC* had "broadly authorized" state and federal age-restriction mandates for online platforms.

That assumption is wrong. It is wrong not as a matter of policy preference but as a matter of constitutional law, and understanding exactly how it is wrong is the analytical contribution this paper makes.

The *FSC* holding is precise and bounded. The Court upheld a Texas statute — HB 1181 — that required age verification before access to content classifiable as "harmful to minors" under the *Ginsberg v. New York* obscenity-to-minors standard. The majority's application of intermediate rather than strict scrutiny rested on a single doctrinal pivot: that standard made HB 1181's access restriction a conduct-based regulation of a legally pre-classified category, not a content-based restriction on protected speech. Remove the *Ginsberg* classification, and the pivot fails. Laws that target social media platforms as a category, laws whose content definitions exceed the *Ginsberg* boundary, and laws whose mechanism-focus tracks a legislative theory of harm rather than a recognized legal classification cannot use the *FSC* pathway. They remain subject to the strict scrutiny baseline established in *Reno v. ACLU* — a standard that, in thirty years of internet speech litigation, no age-restriction law has survived.

The specific misreading this paper addresses is the conflation of *FSC*'s holding with categorical authorization for social media age-gating. Legislators advancing blanket access-restriction statutes — conditioning platform membership on age verification — cite *FSC* as controlling authority for laws whose structure is entirely inconsistent with the majority's reasoning. Sponsors of design-feature legislation targeting recommendation algorithms — the Kids Off Social Media Act and its state-level analogs — invoke *FSC* for a content-neutrality argument the ruling's *Ginsberg* dependence does not support. Even the statutes most closely tracking HB 1181 are routinely advanced with content definitions and compliance architectures that the intermediate scrutiny tailoring analysis will defeat.

This paper offers the first comprehensive doctrinal classification framework for distinguishing which age-restriction bill designs enter the *FSC* intermediate scrutiny pathway and, among those that do, which are likely to survive it. The framework proceeds in three analytical stages: a gateway analysis identifying which laws remain subject to *Reno*-era strict scrutiny; an intermediate scrutiny analysis identifying which laws within the *FSC* pathway survive tailoring; and a privacy-architecture analysis identifying how technically compliant laws may nonetheless impose a distinct First Amendment burden on anonymous speech that courts can reach as a separate constitutional challenge.

The paper is addressed to three audiences: legislative counsel finalizing age-restriction bill language for the fall 2026 markup season; platform policy teams assessing litigation exposure under enacted and pending statutes; and First Amendment litigators building the factual record for facial and as-applied challenges. A note on framing is necessary at the outset. This is not a how-to guide for enacting age-restriction legislation. It is a constitutional constraint analysis. The framework's primary value is identifying which designs are indefensible — and its most important finding is that most pending legislation, as drafted, falls into that category. Legislators who want to protect children online are best served by understanding what the Constitution actually requires, not by repeating a political narrative the courts will correct at their constituents' expense.

## Section I: Strict Scrutiny and Its Internet Baseline — *Reno* Through *Packingham*

Before *Free Speech Coalition v. Paxton*, the constitutional law of online speech and minor protection had been settled for nearly three decades by a string of decisions sharing a single structural logic: the government may not protect children from online speech by restricting the speech rights of adults, and any law that does so — regardless of legislative intent — must survive strict scrutiny and almost never will.

The starting point is [*Reno v. ACLU*, 521 U.S. 844 (1997)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/521/844/), the Court's first major internet speech decision and still its most consequential. Congress enacted the Communications Decency Act of 1996 in response to public concern about minors accessing pornographic and "indecent" material online. The CDA's challenged provisions made it a federal crime to knowingly transmit "obscene or indecent" communications to anyone under eighteen, and to knowingly use a telecommunications device to send a minor any communication depicting or describing "sexual or excretory activities or organs" in terms that are "patently offensive." The statute provided an affirmative defense for speakers who could demonstrate that they had taken "good faith, reasonable, effective" steps to restrict minor access — including credit card verification.

Justice Stevens, writing for a unanimous Court, rejected the government's attempt to analogize the internet to broadcast radio and television — the only medium the Court had ever subjected to reduced First Amendment scrutiny, on the ground that broadcast spectrum is scarce, government-licensed, and intrusive into the home. The internet is nothing like that. It is an "international network of interconnected computers" through which "any person with a phone line can become a town crier with a voice that resonates farther than it could from any soapbox." More analogous to print than to broadcast, it commands the highest First Amendment protection the Constitution affords.

Because the CDA restricted speech based on its content, it triggered strict scrutiny: the government must demonstrate that the law serves a compelling interest through the least restrictive means available. The government's interest in protecting children from harmful content was conceded to be compelling. But the CDA was not the least restrictive means. Filtering technology and parental controls — mechanisms protecting children without restricting adult access — were already available and less speech-suppressive. The CDA failed.

*Reno* established three principles that would govern internet speech law for the next three decades. First, the internet is a high-protection medium. Second, laws restricting online content to protect minors face strict scrutiny and must be narrowly tailored through the least restrictive means available. Third, the existence of less-restrictive technological alternatives is a powerful constitutional argument against broad content restrictions.

Congress tried again with the Child Online Protection Act of 1998. COPA was more carefully drafted — narrowed to target "material that is harmful to minors" modeled on the *Ginsberg v. New York* standard for print material. Congress believed this narrowing would allow COPA to survive where the CDA had failed.

The Supreme Court disagreed, twice. In [*Ashcroft v. ACLU*, 542 U.S. 656 (2004)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/542/656/), the Court affirmed the preliminary injunction against COPA and remanded for further proceedings. Justice Kennedy's plurality accepted that COPA's content definition was narrower than the CDA's. But narrowing the definition does not, by itself, render a statute the least restrictive means of protecting minors. Filtering software — which COPA's own legislative history confirmed was already effective at blocking most minor access to covered material — remained a less restrictive alternative. The government had not carried its burden.

*Ashcroft* reinforced the *Reno* framework with a critical refinement: the narrow tailoring inquiry requires the government to demonstrate not merely that its chosen mechanism works, but that no less restrictive alternative works equally well or better. This less-restrictive-alternative discipline remains the doctrinal pressure point that most pending age-verification legislation fails to address.

[*Packingham v. North Carolina*, 582 U.S. 98 (2017)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/582/98/), extended the strict scrutiny baseline from content-restriction laws to access-restriction laws affecting social media specifically. North Carolina's statute made it a felony for registered sex offenders to access social media platforms where minors might be present. The Court unanimously rejected the state's characterization of this as a conduct restriction. Writing for eight Justices, Justice Kennedy treated social media platforms as the "modern public square" — spaces where citizens engage in political discourse, access government information, and participate in civic life. A law that categorically excludes a class of persons from accessing those spaces restricts speech, not merely conduct, and must pass strict scrutiny. North Carolina's blanket prohibition failed because it was far broader than necessary: it barred sex offenders from platforms where minors *might* be present, not platforms they were actually using to contact minors.

*Packingham*'s significance for present purposes is twofold. First, it confirms that access to social media platforms — not just the speech hosted on them — receives First Amendment protection. Second, it demonstrates that categorical exclusion from access fails strict scrutiny even when the excluded class is sympathetically chosen: sex offenders, not minors. If North Carolina could not categorically bar convicted sex offenders from social media, the structural analogy for laws categorically restricting minor access to entire platforms is deeply unfavorable.

Taken together, *Reno*, *Ashcroft*, and *Packingham* established a coherent pre-*FSC* baseline. Online speech is entitled to the highest First Amendment protection. Laws conditioning access to that speech on age, status, or verification requirements are content-based restrictions triggering strict scrutiny. Less restrictive alternatives — filtering, parental controls, platform design — consistently undercut the government's necessity argument. What *Free Speech Coalition v. Paxton* changed — and, critically, what it did not change — is the subject of Section II.

## Section II: What *Free Speech Coalition v. Paxton* Actually Held

The Supreme Court's June 2025 decision in [*Free Speech Coalition, Inc. v. Paxton*](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/24pdf/23-1122_3e04.pdf) is simultaneously narrower than its supporters claim and more consequential than its critics acknowledge. The narrowness lies in the statute the Court actually reviewed; the consequence lies in the doctrinal mechanism the majority used to uphold it.

### The Texas Statute: HB 1181

Texas House Bill 1181, enacted in 2023, targets a specifically defined category of content: "sexual material harmful to minors." The definition tracks the *Ginsberg* standard — material that, taken as a whole, appeals to the prurient interest of minors in nudity, sex, or excretion; that is patently offensive to prevailing standards regarding what is suitable for minors; and that, taken as a whole, lacks serious literary, artistic, political, or scientific value for minors. Commercial websites that host or distribute such material are required to use "reasonable age verification methods" to confirm that a user is eighteen or older before providing access. Failure to implement compliant verification exposes the operator to civil liability.

Three structural features of HB 1181 are worth isolating because the majority found them legally significant — and most pending legislation does not share all three. First, the statute's scope is limited to content already classified as harmful to minors under *Ginsberg*'s obscenity-adjacent standard; it does not sweep in constitutionally protected speech that adults may access freely. Second, the compliance obligation falls on website operators, not the government restricting the content's existence. Third, the statute does not bar verified adult access; it conditions access on age confirmation, leaving the content available to its legally cognizable audience.

### The Doctrinal Question: Content-Based or Content-Neutral?

The central question before the Court was not whether Texas's interest in protecting children from sexually explicit material was compelling — that has never been seriously disputed. The operative question was how to characterize HB 1181 for First Amendment purposes.

Under established doctrine, a law is content-based if it restricts speech based on its communicative content — its subject matter, viewpoint, or message. Content-based laws are presumptively unconstitutional and survive only if the government proves they are narrowly tailored to serve a compelling interest. Laws that regulate conduct associated with accessing speech — without regard to its subject matter as such — face only intermediate scrutiny: the government must demonstrate that the law serves a significant interest and is narrowly tailored, without proving it chose the least restrictive means.

Free Speech Coalition argued that HB 1181 was plainly content-based: its coverage was determined entirely by the subject matter of the speech on regulated websites. A statute whose legal trigger is the content those websites publish is, by definition, content-based. Under *Reno* and *Ashcroft*, that analysis would end the inquiry — strict scrutiny, least-restrictive-means, and likely failure.

### The Majority's Core Doctrinal Move: Conduct of Access, Not Content of Speech

The majority declined to follow that path. It characterized HB 1181 not as a restriction on the content of speech, but as a regulation of the conduct of accessing a legally pre-classified category of content. On this view, the statute does not restrict what websites may publish or what speakers may say; it regulates who may access a specific class of material that has already been classified — through the *Ginsberg* obscenity-to-minors standard — as outside the First Amendment's protection relative to minor audiences.

This characterization draws on a distinction with deep roots in obscenity law. [*Ginsberg v. New York*, 390 U.S. 629 (1968)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/390/629/), upheld a New York statute prohibiting the sale of "girlie" magazines to persons under seventeen, notwithstanding that the same material was constitutionally protected for adults. Justice Brennan's majority opinion located the state's authority in the legitimate interest in protecting minors' well-being — sufficient to sustain regulations of material that is obscene as to children but not as to adults. The critical doctrinal point: *Ginsberg* treated the underlying legal category — material harmful to minors — as *preexisting* the statute. New York was not creating a new speech restriction; it was enforcing a boundary the Court itself recognized.

The *FSC* majority extended *Ginsberg*'s logic to digital distribution. HB 1181 does not regulate the existence or publication of content harmful to minors; that content remains publishable and accessible to verified adults. The statute regulates the act of *providing* that content to a minor — a conduct-based restriction on the distribution relationship between operator and user, not a content-based restriction on the speech itself. Because the regulatory trigger is the age of the recipient and the pre-classified status of the content under *Ginsberg*, the majority held it appropriate to treat HB 1181's content reference as incidental to the conduct regulation rather than constitutive of a content-based restriction.

The *Ginsberg* classification is the load-bearing element of the majority's reasoning — not a label the majority itself used in those words, but the best account of what distinguishes HB 1181 from laws the ruling does not authorize. Without it, the conduct/content recharacterization has no foundation in precedent. The analytical significance of this framing becomes clear when courts and legislators ask what other laws *FSC* authorizes: the answer depends entirely on whether the proposed law also rests on the *Ginsberg* classification as its trigger.

### Applying Intermediate Scrutiny: The Thomas Framework

With that characterization in place, Justice Thomas's majority turned to the intermediate scrutiny analysis. The operative standard is worth quoting precisely: a law survives intermediate scrutiny if it "advances important governmental interests unrelated to the suppression of free speech and does not burden substantially more speech than necessary to further those interests." Three factors; no least-restrictive-means requirement.

On the first factor, the majority found no difficulty. Texas's interest in preventing minors from accessing content obscene-to-minors is a traditionally recognized governmental power — the same interest that animated *Ginsberg*'s approval of in-person age verification at newsstands half a century earlier. Justice Thomas drew an explicit analogy: the law extends to the digital environment the same category of conduct-regulation the state has long exercised for alcohol, tobacco, lottery tickets, and other age-gated transactions.

On the second factor — whether the law burdens substantially more speech than necessary — the majority held that HB 1181's burden on adult speech was incidental rather than substantial. Adults who wish to access covered content may do so after age verification; the statute does not restrict the content's existence, availability, or the right of verified adults to receive it. The compliance mechanism — government-issued identification or transactional data — was analogized to age-verification requirements in analogous offline contexts that courts have consistently accepted. Because the law's coverage is confined to the *Ginsberg* category, it does not sweep in speech that adults may access without restriction on other topics. The majority concluded the tailoring was sufficient.

### The Kagan Dissent and Its Doctrinal Stakes

Justice Kagan, joined by Justices Sotomayor and Jackson, would have applied strict scrutiny — and her dissent identifies precisely where the majority's analytical move fails, in terms that will shape lower court application of *FSC* for years.

Kagan rejected the conduct/content recharacterization at its root. Texas's law is "a direct regulation of speech, triggered by the amount of sexually explicit expression on a commercial website." The fact that the majority labels the regulatory mechanism as conduct-based does not change what the law actually does: it conditions the ability of adults to access constitutionally protected speech — material lawful for adults, prohibited only for minors — on surrender of personal identification. The Court had applied strict scrutiny "on no fewer than four prior occasions" to laws that "impede adults from viewing a class of speech protected for them (even though not for children) and defined by its content."

The dissent's most practically significant argument concerns the chilling effect that the compliance architecture itself produces. Kagan identified a First Amendment harm the majority's framework did not adequately account for: "turning over information about yourself and your viewing habits — respecting speech many find repulsive — to a website operator" who might sell the data, be hacked, or be subpoenaed imposes a deterrent on constitutionally protected speech independent of the law's formal permit/deny structure. This argument — that the privacy costs of technically compliant verification architectures are themselves First Amendment burdens — is not resolved by the majority's intermediate scrutiny analysis and remains available to lower courts as a distinct constitutional challenge. The paper returns to this point in Section V.

### What the Court Held — and What It Did Not

The precise statement of *FSC*'s holding is essential to the paper's entire framework. The Court held that a law requiring age verification before access to content classifiable as obscene-to-minors under *Ginsberg*, implemented through a conduct-based mechanism that does not restrict adult access once verified, is subject to intermediate rather than strict scrutiny — and that HB 1181 survives that standard.

What the Court did not hold is equally important. *FSC* does not establish that intermediate scrutiny applies to all age-restriction laws affecting online speech. The *Ginsberg* classification is the crucial pivot — remove it, and the conduct/content recharacterization collapses. Laws that restrict access to content beyond the obscene-to-minors category, laws that restrict access to platforms rather than specific classified content, and laws whose compliance mechanisms create verification burdens not analogous to the ID-check model Texas employed remain subject to the *Reno*-era strict scrutiny baseline. The classification framework in Section IV is organized around this boundary.

## Section III: The Three Legislative Models — Mapping the Post-*FSC* Landscape

The Supreme Court handed down *Free Speech Coalition v. Paxton* on June 27, 2025. Within six months, the [National Law Review reported that 25 states had enacted or introduced age-verification laws targeting minors' access to online content](https://natlawreview.com/article/new-age-verification-reality-compliance-rapidly-expanding-state-regulatory), with Alabama, South Carolina, Idaho, and other states among those whose statutes most closely tracked HB 1181's structure. That figure does not include the separate wave of social media age-gating bills — categorical access restrictions for minors — advancing through a different legislative theory. Nor does it capture the federal legislative activity: the [Kids Off Social Media Act (S. 278)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/278) advanced out of Senate Commerce Committee by voice vote with broad bipartisan support on February 5, 2025, and the [KIDS Act (H.R. 7757)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/7757/text) — a broader House vehicle incorporating KOSA provisions and age-verification mandates for mature content — was introduced in March 2026.

The legislative response to *FSC* is voluminous and heterogeneous. States and Congress are pursuing child-protection online through at least three structurally distinct legislative models, each with a different regulatory mechanism, a different theory of constitutional authorization, and — as Section IV will demonstrate — a different constitutional fate. The purpose of this section is to map those models descriptively and precisely.

### Model A: The *FSC* Footprint

Model A laws target content within the *Ginsberg* classification — "material harmful to minors," defined by reference to the obscenity-to-minors standard — and require website operators to implement age-verification mechanisms before granting user access to that content. The regulatory trigger is the category-status of the content, and the compliance obligation runs to the operator-access relationship between the platform and the user.

[Alabama's HB 164](https://legiscan.com/AL/text/HB164/2024) (enacted April 2024, effective October 2024) and [South Carolina's HB 3424](https://legiscan.com/SC/text/H3424/id/2991247) (effective January 1, 2025) are among the clearest examples: both require age verification before access to websites where a substantial portion of content qualifies as harmful to minors under each state's obscenity-to-minors definition. The verification options they permit — government-issued identification, commercial third-party verification systems, transactional data — mirror the Texas menu. [Idaho's HB 498](https://legiscan.com/ID/text/H0498/id/2918383) (effective July 2024) operates on the same structural template.

Model A legislation is self-consciously designed to occupy the *FSC* pathway. Its authors understand the *Ginsberg* classification as the doctrinal pivot and have taken care to preserve it. The underlying theory is simple: *FSC* held that HB 1181-type laws survive intermediate scrutiny; our law is an HB 1181-type law; therefore our law survives intermediate scrutiny. Whether that syllogism holds — whether each Model A bill in fact preserves the structural features the *FSC* majority found decisive — is a question Section IV takes up in detail. The answer is that structural fidelity varies more than legislators have assumed.

### Model B: The Design-Feature Reframe

Rather than targeting content already classified as obscene-to-minors, Model B laws target the mechanisms by which platforms deliver content to minor users — principally, personalized algorithmic recommendation systems — regardless of whether the content surfaced by those algorithms falls within any obscenity-adjacent classification.

The [Kids Off Social Media Act (S. 278)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/278) is the most consequential pending example. The bill prohibits social media platforms from deploying personalized recommendation systems to deliver content to users under the age of seventeen. The regulatory trigger is the algorithmic mechanism, not the content classification: a platform that recommends constitutionally protected speech — news, political commentary, social communication — to a fifteen-year-old using a personalized algorithm is within scope, regardless of whether any specific piece of content would qualify as harmful to minors under *Ginsberg*. The theory of harm is not that the content is obscene; it is that the delivery mechanism is addictive and developmentally harmful.

Model B's constitutional theory rests on content-neutrality by design: if the law's trigger is the platform mechanism rather than the content classification, the argument goes, the law does not regulate speech based on subject matter and should be characterized as a structural or conduct-based regulation. California's [Age-Appropriate Design Code (AB 2273)](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billTextClient.xhtml?bill_id=202120220AB2273) pursued an analogous design-first theory — requiring platforms to implement age-appropriate features regardless of content classification — before [Judge Beth Labson Freeman struck it down in September 2023](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2023/09/federal-judge-strikes-down-california-age-appropriate-design-code) on First Amendment grounds, holding that its vague "best interests" standard and compelled disclosure requirements constituted content-based restrictions on editorial choices. The AADC was a precursor to Model B's current legislative family; its failure in court is a data point that Model B's architects have largely declined to absorb.

The central constitutional vulnerability of Model B is that its content-neutral characterization may be formal rather than functional. A law that bans algorithmic personalization for minors does not target a content-neutral mechanism the way a noise ordinance targets volume rather than lyrics; it targets the mechanism by which platforms decide what content users see. The practical effect is functionally identical to restricting which content minors may receive. Whether the *FSC* majority's conduct/content recharacterization — grounded as it was in *Ginsberg*'s pre-existing obscenity classification — extends to design-feature laws whose mechanism-focus lacks any analogous legal foundation is an open question that lower courts are only beginning to reach.

### Model C: Blanket Social Media Age-Gating

Model C laws do not target specific content classifications or specific delivery mechanisms; they restrict minor access to social media platforms as a category, regardless of the specific content or design features those platforms employ.

[Florida SB 868](https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2025/868), signed into law in March 2025, prohibits children under fourteen from holding social media accounts and requires parental consent for users aged fourteen and fifteen. The law applies to platforms with enumerated features — algorithmic recommendation feeds, private messaging, infinite scroll — but the operative compliance obligation is account creation, not content access. A minor cannot hold an account on a covered platform regardless of whether that minor's proposed use involves any content qualifying as harmful under *Ginsberg* or any other legal standard. The theory of harm is holistic: social media itself, as a platform category, is developmentally harmful to minors.

Federal analogs include access-restriction provisions in STOP CSAM and SHIELD Act vehicles that condition safe harbor protections on platform-wide age-verification gates — effectively mandating that platforms verify user age or face enhanced liability exposure for all content, not merely content within the *Ginsberg* classification.

Model C is precisely the kind of legislation the *FSC* holding does not authorize. Without the *Ginsberg* classification as its doctrinal pivot, the conduct/content recharacterization fails, and categorical restrictions on minor access to social media return to strict scrutiny under *Reno* and *Packingham*. A federal court reached exactly that conclusion in December 2025, [striking down Louisiana's social media access law](https://lailluminator.com/2025/12/18/louisiana-social-media-2/) as unconstitutional because the state had not shown that restricting minor access to entire platforms was the least restrictive means of protecting children from harm.

### The Map and Its Uses

All three models share a common political narrative: legislators describe all three as constitutionally authorized by *Free Speech Coalition v. Paxton*. The mapping here demonstrates why that narrative cannot be uniformly correct. Model C's structure is categorically inconsistent with the *FSC* holding's doctrinal predicate. Model B's structure presents an unresolved doctrinal question the ruling does not answer. The classification framework in Section IV operationalizes these distinctions, providing the analytical tools to assess any specific bill's constitutional position with precision rather than inference.

## Section IV: The Classification Framework — What Fails, and What Might Survive

The classification framework developed in this section has one primary purpose: identifying which age-restriction bill designs are constitutionally indefensible. That ordering is deliberate. The framework is not a compliance guide for legislators seeking to enact age-gating; it is a constitutional map whose most important features are the barriers that most pending legislation cannot cross.

### Part A: The Gateway Question — Does the Law Enter the *FSC* Pathway?

The first question any age-restriction law must answer is doctrinal: does it enter the *FSC* intermediate scrutiny pathway, or does it remain subject to the *Reno*-era strict scrutiny baseline? The answer determines the operative constitutional standard. A law that enters the *FSC* pathway faces intermediate scrutiny — a demanding but survivable standard. A law that does not enter the pathway faces strict scrutiny — a standard that, in thirty years of internet speech litigation, no age-restriction law has survived.

The gateway is defined by three disqualifiers, each drawn directly from the *FSC* majority's reasoning about what made HB 1181 eligible for content-neutral treatment. Any single disqualifying feature, present in a proposed law's text or structure, is sufficient to push that law back into *Reno* strict scrutiny. Most pending legislation fails on at least one; many fail on two or three.

**Disqualifier 1: Content-based regulatory trigger.** The first disqualifier is a regulatory trigger defined by the legislature's own content judgment rather than by reference to an existing legal classification. HB 1181's trigger was the *Ginsberg* category — "material harmful to minors" as a pre-existing obscenity-to-minors classification with constitutional pedigree extending to 1968. The law did not define a new category of disfavored content; it enforced an access-restriction on content the Constitution already treated differently relative to minor audiences.

Laws that define their own content categories — specifying which topics, subject matters, or types of speech trigger the age-verification requirement — are content-based on their face. The First Amendment's content-discrimination principle, most recently applied in [*Moody v. NetChoice*, 603 U.S. 707 (2024)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/603/22-277/), treats laws that single out specific content for regulatory burden with heightened suspicion precisely because the government's content judgments are themselves constitutionally suspect. A law that requires age verification before accessing "content that promotes eating disorders" or "content harmful to minors' mental health" does not invoke a pre-existing obscenity-adjacent classification; it substitutes the legislature's judgment about harmful content for a legal standard developed through decades of First Amendment litigation. Such laws face strict scrutiny — and on the question of least restrictive means, the filtering alternative that defeated COPA in [*Ashcroft v. ACLU*](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/542/656/) remains available and decisive.

**Disqualifier 2: Scope extending beyond the *Ginsberg* category.** The second disqualifier is scope. *FSC*'s intermediate scrutiny analysis was bounded by the *Ginsberg* classification; the Court did not hold that intermediate scrutiny applies to age-verification laws generally, but specifically to laws restricting access to material that is, by pre-existing constitutional doctrine, not fully protected as to minor audiences. A law that reaches beyond that classification — sweeping in constitutionally protected speech that adults and minors may alike access — is restricting protected speech, which is precisely what the *Reno* framework was designed to address.

Overly broad content definitions that capture literary fiction, journalistic reporting on sexual health, or LGBTQ informational resources are the clearest manifestation of this disqualifier in current pending legislation. State analogs to HB 1181 that substitute "inappropriate for minors" or "harmful to minors' wellbeing" for the *Ginsberg* three-part test fail here: those formulations are broader than *Ginsberg* and substantially less precise, capturing constitutionally protected speech without meeting the threshold of lacking serious value for minors and appealing to prurient interest. Courts reviewing post-*FSC* statutes — including the Congressional Research Service's post-decision litigation survey in [CRS Report LSB11354](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/LSB11354) — have flagged overbroad content definitions as the single most common vulnerability in enacted state statutes.

**Disqualifier 3: Platform-level rather than content-specific restriction.** The third and most commonly triggered disqualifier is structural: the law restricts access to a platform or service as a whole, rather than to a specific category of content hosted on that platform. [*Packingham v. North Carolina*](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/582/98/) established that categorical restrictions on access to platforms where speech occurs are themselves speech restrictions requiring strict scrutiny — even when the restricted class is sympathetically chosen. A law requiring age verification before accessing social media as a category does not invoke *Ginsberg*'s limited obscenity-to-minors classification; it restricts access to a medium where enormous amounts of constitutionally protected speech — political commentary, news, civic organizing, personal communication — occur alongside any material that might be obscene-to-minors. Under *Packingham*'s logic, that restriction is not a conduct-regulation on access to classified content; it is a limitation on access to the modern public square.

Model C legislation — Florida SB 868 and its federal analogs — fails on Disqualifier 3 alone. Laws whose compliance burden extends to the entire platform relationship (account creation, general access) rather than to specific content categories within the platform cannot enter the *FSC* pathway, regardless of how their proponents characterize them.

### Part B: Applying Intermediate Scrutiny to Laws Within the *FSC* Pathway

A law that survives all three gateway disqualifiers has entered the *FSC* intermediate scrutiny pathway. That passage is not a constitutional clearance; it is permission to be evaluated under a less demanding standard. Intermediate scrutiny under *FSC* requires that the law advance important governmental interests unrelated to the suppression of free speech, and that it not burden substantially more speech than necessary. Model A statutes are the principal candidates for this pathway — and many of them fail intermediate scrutiny's second prong anyway, on tailoring grounds that drafters have systematically underestimated.

**The Significant-Interest Factor.** No Model A statute faces difficulty on the first prong. Protecting minors from material classified as obscene-to-minors is a governmental interest of long constitutional standing — established in *Ginsberg*, reaffirmed in [*United States v. Playboy Entertainment Group*, 529 U.S. 803 (2000)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/529/803/), and treated by the *FSC* majority as an uncontested predicate. Courts reviewing Model A statutes do not linger here; the interest is given.

**The Narrow Tailoring Factor — Where Model A Statutes Fail.** The operative constitutional pressure on Model A legislation falls entirely on intermediate scrutiny's second prong: does the law burden substantially more speech than necessary? Three specific design features, appearing with regularity in Model A statutes enacted since *FSC*, cause failures at this stage.

*Overbroad content definitions.* HB 1181's content definition tracked *Ginsberg*'s three-part obscenity-to-minors test with care. Many state-level imitators have not. Laws defining covered content to include material that is "inappropriate for minors" — a standard both broader than *Ginsberg*'s "harmful to minors" test and substantially less precise — capture constitutionally protected speech without meeting the *Ginsberg* threshold. On this ground alone, several enacted Model A statutes face successful facial challenges that their HB 1181 pedigree does not protect against.

*Verification mechanisms that condition access to protected content on government-issued ID.* Even a law with a properly bounded *Ginsberg* content definition may fail tailoring if its compliance architecture imposes a disproportionate burden. [*United States v. Playboy Entertainment Group*](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/529/803/) applied this logic in a related context: the Court struck down a cable signal-scrambling mandate in part because less burdensome alternatives were already available at lower cost to speech. The same reasoning applies to verification architecture. A law that mandates government-issued identification as the primary or exclusive compliance option imposes a distinct deterrent: adults wishing to access constitutionally protected speech within the *Ginsberg* zone must link their government identity to their viewing habits. The chilling effect this produces is a recognized First Amendment harm to which [*Watchtower Bible & Tract Society v. Village of Stratton*, 536 U.S. 150 (2002)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/536/150/) and [*McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Commission*, 514 U.S. 334 (1995)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/514/334/) speak directly. Section V develops this argument in full.

*Platform-scope provisions that apply to hosting any minor-harmful content regardless of primary use.* A final tailoring problem appears in statutes that apply verification requirements to any website hosting any quantity of covered content, regardless of whether that content is central or incidental to the platform's primary purpose. A news website covering a sexual-health story, a medical information provider addressing reproductive health, or a literary archive that includes explicit fiction is drawn into the verification regime on the same terms as a pornographic platform for which such content is the primary commercial offering. Intermediate scrutiny's tailoring requirement is violated by a one-size-fits-all coverage rule that treats these categorically different platforms identically.

The practical implication is clear: Model A statutes are not constitutionally safe merely by virtue of copying HB 1181's surface structure. Tailoring failures arise after the law has already entered the *FSC* pathway. Legislators who believe they have satisfied the Constitution by invoking the *Ginsberg* classification and adding "age verification required" have addressed the gateway question — and may still fail intermediate scrutiny's more granular demands.

### Part C: The Kids Off Social Media Act as a Test Case

The [Kids Off Social Media Act (S. 278)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/278) is the most consequential pending piece of age-restriction legislation because it advances the Model B constitutional theory at federal scale, with bipartisan support and genuine legislative momentum. It is also the best available test case for whether the design-feature reframe produces content-neutral legislation under the *FSC* framework — or whether the content-neutrality is formal rather than functional.

KOSMA prohibits social media platforms from using "algorithmic content recommendations" to deliver content to users under the age of seventeen. The prohibition is mechanism-focused: the law targets the recommendation algorithm, not the content the algorithm surfaces. A platform that delivers content chronologically is outside scope. The same platform delivering the same content through a personalized feed trained on the minor's engagement history is within scope. The constitutional theory: because the trigger is the mechanism, not the content classification, the law does not regulate speech by subject matter and should face intermediate scrutiny.

Working this claim through the gateway analysis reveals its vulnerability. KOSMA passes Disqualifier 1 on its face — its trigger (personalized recommendation) makes no reference to any content category at all. But passing the first disqualifier does not complete the analysis. The second disqualifier exposes KOSMA's structural problem: the *FSC* majority's conduct/content recharacterization depended on *Ginsberg* as the constitutional pivot. Without a *Ginsberg* anchor, there is no legal foundation for treating KOSMA's algorithmic restriction as a conduct-regulation rather than a restriction on the editorial decisions platforms make about what content to show minor users. The practical effect of prohibiting personalized recommendations is that minors receive less speech — specifically, less speech of the kind recommendation algorithms would have surfaced based on their interests. That effect is indistinguishable, in constitutional terms, from restricting which content minors may receive. [*Moody v. NetChoice*'s](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/603/22-277/) animating principle — that platforms' editorial choices about content curation are protected speech acts — further complicates KOSMA's constitutional posture.

KOSMA's most likely constitutional fate is failure at the gateway: a court will conclude that the law's mechanism-focus does not produce genuine content-neutrality because the *FSC*/*Ginsberg* foundation for that characterization is absent. Under strict scrutiny, the filtering and parental-control alternatives that defeated COPA are again available. The empirical record on whether recommendation-algorithm restrictions actually reduce the harms KOSMA targets — rather than redirecting minor attention to different platforms — is, as of this writing, thin. That evidentiary gap is decisive.

### Part D: A Practical Checklist for Legislative Drafters

The framework's analytical conclusions can be translated into seven operational characteristics that determine whether a proposed age-restriction bill is constitutionally durable under post-*FSC* doctrine.

**1. Does the law's content trigger reference the *Ginsberg* obscenity-to-minors classification?** If the law defines covered content by reference to a new legislative judgment rather than the *Ginsberg* standard, it is content-based and subject to strict scrutiny.

**2. Does the law's content definition stay within the *Ginsberg* boundary?** Even laws that invoke *Ginsberg* may sweep beyond it through imprecise drafting. "Inappropriate for minors" and "harmful to minors' wellbeing" are overbroad unless construed to track *Ginsberg*'s three-part test precisely.

**3. Does the access restriction target specific content categories, or the platform as a whole?** Laws that gate platform access — rather than access to a specific classified content category within the platform — fail Disqualifier 3 and face *Packingham*-grounded strict scrutiny.

**4. Does the law provide privacy-preserving compliance options as co-equal alternatives to government-issued ID?** Laws that treat government-ID verification as the mandatory or default mechanism impose a deterrent on anonymous speech triggering a separate First Amendment burden under *McIntyre* and *Watchtower*.

**5. Does the law apply proportionately to platforms based on the volume and centrality of covered content?** One-size-fits-all coverage rules that impose equal obligations on incidental and primary content hosts create an overbreadth problem at the tailoring stage.

**6. Has the legislature engaged with less-restrictive alternatives in the legislative record?** Under *Ashcroft*'s refinement of the least-restrictive-means test, and by analogy under intermediate scrutiny's tailoring prong, a thin legislative record on alternatives strengthens the plaintiffs' burden argument.

**7. Does the mechanism-focus of the law track a recognized legal classification, or substitute the legislature's own theory of harm?** Design-feature laws that target recommendation algorithms, infinite scroll, or push notifications without *Ginsberg*-grounded content classification cannot use the *FSC* pathway.

A bill that satisfies all seven characteristics is the most constitutionally defensible age-restriction design currently available under post-*FSC* doctrine. Most pending legislation fails at least two. Legislators who want durable child-protection law — law that survives the first federal injunction and holds through appellate review — must build from this checklist, not from the political narrative that *FSC* authorized the full range of proposals currently advancing through committee.

## Section V: The Privacy-Speech Tradeoff — When Compliance Is Also a Burden

The Section IV framework identifies which age-restriction laws survive constitutional analysis on their face. But there is a constitutional dimension that facial analysis alone does not capture, and that the *FSC* dissent identified with precision: the compliance architecture mandated by a facially valid law may itself impose a First Amendment burden independent of the law's textual content restriction.

The argument is grounded in an established line of anonymous-speech cases. [*McIntyre v. Ohio Elections Commission*, 514 U.S. 334 (1995)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/514/334/), held that the First Amendment protects the right to engage in anonymous political speech, recognizing that compelled identification deters speakers who "might otherwise remain silent if forced to attach their name" to their expression. [*Watchtower Bible & Tract Society v. Village of Stratton*, 536 U.S. 150 (2002)](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/536/150/), extended that logic beyond political speech, holding that an ordinance requiring canvassers to register with local government before speaking door-to-door violated the First Amendment because compelled identification deters speech even when the underlying speech is entirely protected. The operative principle: the government may not condition the exercise of First Amendment rights on the surrender of personal identifying information, unless that condition is itself narrowly tailored to a substantial governmental interest. What applies to speakers applies also to receivers of speech — a reader who must identify herself to access a library book faces the same deterrent as a writer compelled to sign her pamphlet.

### Three Compliance Architectures and Their Constitutional Costs

Model A statutes that survive the Section IV framework do not specify a single verification technology; they require "reasonable age verification methods" and leave compliance architecture to the website operator. In practice, the post-*FSC* regulatory landscape has produced three categories of technically compliant architectures, each with distinct privacy profiles and distinct First Amendment consequences.

**Government-issued identification with third-party processor storage** is the compliance mechanism most state statutes treat as the primary option. Under this architecture, users submit government ID documents to a third-party verification service that confirms age and transmits the result to the platform. The constitutional problem is not formal but functional: as the [EFF's 2025 surveillance review](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/12/year-states-chose-surveillance-over-safety-2025-review) documents, "every age-verification system is, at its core, a surveillance system." The user wishing to access content constitutionally protected for adults must link her government identity to her viewing history, creating a data record whose subsequent uses — sale, breach, subpoena — she cannot control. *McIntyre* and *Watchtower* recognize exactly this deterrent. Adults who might access the content freely will decline to identify themselves to do so — and that deterrence is a burden on anonymous reception of protected speech that the intermediate scrutiny framework must account for, not assume away.

**Age-estimation biometric systems** represent a growing compliance option: software that analyzes facial geometry to estimate a user's age without requiring document submission. The privacy profile differs from ID collection in kind rather than degree. The user does not submit a name-linked government document; she submits her face. But biometric data presents its own surveillance risk: facial geometry cannot be changed if compromised, and biometric databases accumulated by third-party verification vendors are high-value targets for breach. The [Center for Democracy & Technology's analysis of verification privacy tradeoffs](https://cmuplr.org/2025/09/23/privacy-vs-protection-the-tradeoffs-of-age-verification/) notes that biometric architectures require data minimization commitments — immediate deletion of captured geometry after estimation — that most current vendor contracts do not reliably provide.

**Platform-managed account-based attestation** is the least privacy-invasive technically compliant architecture: the platform maintains age-verified account status, verified once at registration, and the underlying credential is not shared with third parties. Some platforms operating under this model store only an age-confirmed flag. This architecture reduces, though does not eliminate, the anonymous-speech deterrent — users still link a government identity to an account — but confines the data to a relationship the user has already voluntarily entered.

### The Question of Privacy-Preserving Alternatives

The availability of architectures that minimize identity exposure is constitutionally significant. Zero-knowledge proof systems, on-device age estimation without data transmission, and cryptographic age-attestation protocols can, in theory, allow a user to prove she meets an age threshold without revealing her identity to a third party. The EFF's [July 2025 assessment](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/07/zero-knowledge-proofs-alone-are-not-digital-id-solution-protecting-user-privacy) cautions that zero-knowledge proofs alone do not resolve the surveillance problem — a user must still obtain a credential from an established trust anchor — but as a component of a broader privacy-preserving system they reduce the identity-disclosure burden compared to raw ID submission. New America's [examination of privacy-preserving age verification architectures](https://www.newamerica.org/insights/exploring-privacy-preserving-age-verification/) identifies these technologies as viable and evaluates their privacy properties in detail.

Laws that permit privacy-preserving architectures as co-equal compliance options alongside ID submission impose a materially smaller *McIntyre*-cognizable burden than laws that treat ID collection as the default. The distinction is not merely a policy preference; it is a constitutional variable that affects whether the law survives the tailoring prong when the full cost of compliance — including its chilling effect on anonymous speech — is factored into the analysis.

### The Empirical Record on Effectiveness

A technically compliant law that imposes substantial privacy costs on adult speech might still satisfy intermediate scrutiny's tailoring prong if the compliance costs are proportionate to a demonstrated protective benefit. The empirical record on that question is more complicated than either proponents or opponents acknowledge.

[Internet Matters survey data from October–November 2025](https://www.internetmatters.org/hub/research/data-shows-no-rise-childrens-vpn-use-amid-online-age-checks/) found no statistically significant increase in children's VPN use following the UK's implementation of Online Safety Act age checks — evidence that some deterrence effect on minor access exists and does not simply displace minors to evasion tools. However, [Shufti telemetry](https://shuftipro.com/blog/age-verification-evasion-in-2025-how-minors-outsmart-weak-age-gates-and-how-to-fight-back/) reports that nearly one in four would-be sign-ups at age-gated sites involve suspected minors — suggesting that verification failures remain substantial even under robust implementations. A [Phoenix Center analysis](https://www.phoenix-center.org/perspectives/Perspective25-06Final.pdf) examining unintended consequences of age-verification laws found that minor access displacement — users routing to unverified platforms — compounds the problem by pushing minors toward less moderated environments.

The intermediate scrutiny question this empirical uncertainty raises is pointed: if the privacy cost of the compliance architecture is real and immediate, but the protective benefit is partial and uncertain due to bypass, does the law burden substantially more speech than necessary to serve its interest? A law whose privacy deterrent suppresses adult access to protected content while leaving meaningful minor access intact through evasion channels may fail intermediate scrutiny's proportionality demand even after surviving the Section IV threshold analysis.

## Conclusion: A Constitutional Map for the 2026 Markup Season

The classification framework this paper has developed is, at its core, a service to legislators who want their child-protection laws to survive. *Free Speech Coalition, Inc. v. Paxton* changed the constitutional landscape in a meaningful but precise way: it established that laws regulating the conduct of accessing content pre-classified as obscene-to-minors under *Ginsberg* are subject to intermediate rather than strict scrutiny. That shift matters. No online speech restriction had survived strict scrutiny in thirty years.

But the shift is narrower than the post-*FSC* legislative response has assumed. The *Ginsberg* classification is the doctrinal pivot that makes HB 1181's access restriction a conduct-regulation rather than a content-restriction. Laws that lack it remain exactly where they were before the ruling: subject to a strict scrutiny standard they will not survive. This is not a matter of policy preference. It is a structural feature of the majority's reasoning that legislators, platform counsel, and First Amendment litigators must understand precisely.

The three-model taxonomy developed in Section III makes the consequence plain. Model A statutes — those that track HB 1181's structure, confine their scope to the *Ginsberg* category, and target classified content rather than entire platforms — have entered the *FSC* pathway. But entering the pathway is not the same as surviving it. Most Model A statutes will fail intermediate scrutiny's tailoring prong because of three recurring drafting failures: content definitions that exceed the *Ginsberg* boundary; verification mechanisms that default to government-issued ID and thereby impose *McIntyre*-cognizable deterrence on anonymous reception of protected speech; and platform-scope provisions that apply equally to incidental and primary content hosts.

Model B statutes — design-feature laws like the Kids Off Social Media Act — advance a content-neutral theory of authorization that the *FSC* holding does not supply. Without the *Ginsberg* anchor that made HB 1181's characterization plausible, a law prohibiting algorithmic personalization for minors cannot use the *FSC* conduct/content recharacterization as its constitutional hook. It faces strict scrutiny. The filtering and parental-control alternatives that defeated COPA in *Ashcroft* are still available, and the empirical record on whether recommendation-algorithm restrictions reduce the targeted harms is too thin to satisfy the least-restrictive-means standard.

Model C statutes — blanket social media age-gating laws like Florida SB 868 — do not survive the gateway analysis at all. *Packingham* established that categorical exclusion from the modern public square requires strict scrutiny. Nothing in *FSC* disturbs that analysis, because *FSC*'s entire doctrinal move depended on the *Ginsberg* classification that Model C laws do not invoke.

The plain finding is this: the majority of age-restriction legislation advancing through state legislatures and Congress in the 2025–2026 cycle, as drafted, will not survive First Amendment challenge. The political narrative that *FSC* authorized the full menu of pending proposals is wrong as a constitutional description. Courts applying the *FSC* framework with precision will reach this conclusion regardless of what this paper argues; the analysis is offered to help legislators reach it first, before their constituents' resources are spent on litigation rather than child protection.

For legislative counsel finalizing bill language before the fall 2026 markup season, the Section IV checklist provides the operational tool: seven characteristics that determine whether a proposed bill is constitutionally durable. Bills that reference the *Ginsberg* classification with precision, confine their scope to that category, restrict content-specific access rather than platform access, permit privacy-preserving compliance alternatives, apply proportionately to platforms based on the centrality of covered content, document alternatives in the legislative record, and ground any mechanism-focus in a recognized legal classification rather than a legislative theory of harm are the candidates for durable law.

For platform counsel, the framework's primary value is identifying the weakest statutes — those that will generate preliminary injunctions — and distinguishing them from those that require a full merits assessment. Model C statutes are the clearest candidates for facial challenge; the gateway analysis defeats them before intermediate scrutiny is reached. Model A statutes with overbroad content definitions or ID-mandate compliance architectures present strong tailoring arguments even within the *FSC* pathway. Model B statutes present the most uncertain litigation posture, because the gateway question — whether the design-feature mechanism produces genuine content-neutrality — remains an open doctrinal issue that lower courts are only beginning to reach.

None of this analysis is an argument against child protection. The *Reno* Court acknowledged that protecting minors from harmful online content is a compelling governmental interest. *Ginsberg* has recognized the state's authority in this space for more than fifty years. The Constitution does not prohibit legislatures from acting; it requires that when they act, they act with precision. Durable child-protection law is achievable within the post-*FSC* constitutional framework. Most pending legislation, as drafted, is not that law.
